## January

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in January | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** January foraging in the UK: velvet shank, oyster mushroom, coastal shellfish and winter seaweeds. What's out there and where to find it.

---

January strips things back. The woodland floor is bare, the hedgerows emptied. The coast opens up, and the winter fungi are fruiting on dead wood and fallen branches across the country. This is a month for knowing where to look rather than having many places to look.

### Highlights at a glance

- Velvet Shank fruits through frost and snow on dead elm and beech. One of the few fungi that genuinely prefers cold
- Oyster Mushroom flushes on dead beech in wet, mild spells. Check fallen logs after rain
- Mussels, winkles, and cockles are in peak condition. Cold water keeps them firm and clean
- Jelly Ear is findable year-round on elder but prominent now the leaves are down
- Scarlet Elf Cup begins appearing in late January on rotting wood in damp woodland
- Seaweeds, laver, carrageen, bladderwrack, kelp, are available all year and often at their cleanest in winter

### What's worth finding this month

**[Velvet Shank](/species/velvet-shank)**

Velvet Shank (*Flammulina velutipes*) is the fungi to look for in January. It grows in tight clusters on dead and dying deciduous wood, most commonly elm and beech, and fruits through the coldest months when almost everything else has stopped. The cap is orange-brown and glossy, 2–8cm across. The stem is distinctly velvety toward the base. Touch it and you know it immediately. It withstands frost: find a cluster frozen solid on a cold morning and it will look exactly the same after it thaws. A mild spell mid-month will bring new flushes on logs that looked spent. Get out after rain that follows a cold snap.

**[Oyster Mushroom](/species/oyster-mushroom)**

Oyster Mushroom (*Pleurotus ostreatus*) grows in overlapping shelves on dead or dying broadleaf trees, most often beech. The caps are grey to grey-brown, fan-shaped, 5–25cm across, with crowded white gills that run down a short off-centre stem. January's wet, mild spells bring flushes on fallen logs and standing deadwood. Competition from other species is minimal and the cold keeps flushes fresh for longer than in autumn. Pick young, firm caps. Find a productive tree and check it every few weeks through winter. This is a good species for complete beginners: large, distinctive, and easy to identify once you have seen it.

**[Mussel](/species/mussel)**

Common Mussel (*Mytilus edulis*) is at its best through winter. Cold water keeps them firm and in peak condition and January is as good a month as any to be on the coast. Mussels cling to rocks, pier legs, and rope in dense colonies on exposed and semi-exposed coastline. Always check the water quality classification of any site before picking. The Shellfish Association of Great Britain publishes this data and it matters. Wild mussels need proper preparation. The species page has the detail.

### Arriving and finishing

Three-cornered Leek (*Allium triquetrum*) shoots are findable in mild areas of the south and west from January onward. Scarlet Elf Cup is just beginning in sheltered southern woodland. January is the floor of the foraging year. Things are arriving, not leaving.

## February

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in February | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** February foraging UK: Alexanders, Scarlet Elf Cup, Miner's Lettuce and the first coastal greens. What's worth finding before spring arrives.

---

February is the month of false starts. A warm week mid-month can bring the first wild garlic shoots and the earliest violets, then cold snaps everything shut again. The coast is your most productive ground this month. Alexanders is already thick on clifftops and south-facing banks, and there is more happening out there than February gets credit for.

### Highlights at a glance

- Alexanders is thick and ready on coastal banks and hedgerows. The earliest substantial wild vegetable of the year and largely ignored
- Scarlet Elf Cup peaks through February on rotting wood in wet woodland
- Cleavers shoots are young and at their most edible now, before they become too sticky and stringy to enjoy
- Sweet Violet is flowering. The flowers are good raw in salads and hold their colour well
- Three-cornered Leek is already up and edible in the south and west
- Oyster Mushroom and Velvet Shank continue through mild spells

### What's worth finding this month

**[Alexanders](/species/alexanders)**

Alexanders (*Smyrnium olusatrum*) is the find of the late winter coast. It grows in thick stands on sea cliffs, coastal hedgerows, and roadsides within a few miles of the sea, particularly in southern England, Wales, and Ireland. In February the stems are thick, bright green, and at their best. The plant flowers and the stems hollow and toughen toward April, so pick now. The flavour is celery-like but more aromatic, with a slight bitterness that cooking softens. Pick the young stems and leaves. It is a substantial wild vegetable available at a time when almost nothing else is, and almost nobody picks it.

**[Scarlet Elf Cup](/species/scarlet-elf-cup)**

Scarlet Elf Cup (*Sarcoscypha austriaca*) is a visually striking find in the British fungal year. The cups are vivid scarlet inside, cream to white on the outside, 2–6cm across, growing in clusters on fallen branches half-buried under leaf litter in damp woodland. February is when they are most abundant and at their freshest. Check rotten wood and half-buried branches in wet, shaded woodland near streams. They favour alder and hazel. As a find on a grey February morning they are hard to beat.

**[Miner's Lettuce](/species/miners-lettuce)**

Miner's Lettuce (*Claytonia perfoliata*) is a North American introduction now naturalised across Britain, most commonly on sandy soils in gardens, parks, and disturbed ground. The round leaves appear to have the stem growing through their centre. That is the unmistakable feature. They are mild, succulent, and very good raw in a winter salad. February and March are the prime months, before the plant sets seed and the leaves diminish. A good beginner species: straightforward to identify, no preparation needed, and available when the calendar feels thin.

### Arriving and finishing

Wild Garlic (*Allium ursinum*) shoots are visible in the last week of February in sheltered southern woodland. Primrose (*Primula vulgaris*) is flowering from mid-month. Scarlet Elf Cup begins to thin toward the end of February as temperatures rise. Velvet Shank is on its way out by late February in most years.

## March

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in March | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** March foraging UK: wild garlic, young nettles, wood sorrel and hawthorn buds. The foraging year starts properly. What to find and when.

---

March announces itself before you arrive. Walk into the right woodland on a warm morning and the smell of wild garlic hits you 20 metres before you see the plants. This is the first month of genuine abundance. The month that turns people who have been meaning to start for years into people who are actually out there.

### Highlights at a glance

- Wild Garlic peaks through March in damp deciduous woodland. Pick leaves before flowering for the best flavour. The smell is unmistakable and is your primary identification
- Young nettles are best this month, before they toughen. Pick the top four leaves from plants under 20cm
- Hawthorn buds are opening on sheltered south-facing hedgerows and good eaten straight from the branch
- Wood Sorrel carpets damp woodland floors alongside wild garlic. Tart, bright, and good raw
- Ground Elder, Sweet Cicely, and Hogweed are all shooting. Identifiable and edible while young
- Morel is in season from late March. Find your spots, read the species page

### What's worth finding this month

**[Wild Garlic](/species/wild-garlic)**

Wild Garlic (*Allium ursinum*) is the defining find of British spring and March is its month. The broad, lance-shaped leaves carpet ancient woodland floors. Wherever the conditions are right, they are there in quantity and the season is long enough to pick properly without rushing. Pick leaves before the white star-shaped flowers open in April. Once the plant flowers, the leaves toughen and strengthen. Use the leaves like basil: raw in a spring pesto, wilted briefly into butter, blended into soups. The flavour is soft garlic without the heat and it suits almost anything. Before you pick anything, read the species page. There are plants on the same woodland floor that matter.

**[Common Nettle](/species/common-nettle)**

Young Nettle (*Urtica dioica*) tops in March are as good as the plant gets. Pick the top four leaves only from plants under 20cm. Beyond this height the leaves develop compounds that cooking does not fully address. Wear gloves. The sting is completely neutralised by heat. The flavour is deep green and mineral, and nettle does things in the kitchen that spinach cannot quite match. March tops have more character than any other month. By June the plant is too coarse to bother with.

**[Wood Sorrel](/species/wood-sorrel)**

Wood Sorrel (*Oxalis acetosella*) grows low across the forest floor in the same damp deciduous woodland as wild garlic. The leaves are three-lobed, heart-shaped, bright green, and fold downward in strong light. The flavour is clean, precise, citrusy acidity. Sharper than Common Sorrel and more delicate. It is best raw, in small quantities, used as a leaf rather than a bulk green. A handful through a spring salad does something that nothing else quite replicates. Pick lightly from abundant patches.

### Arriving and finishing

Sorrel, Jack-by-the-Hedge, and Scurvygrass are all emerging through March. Hawthorn blossom opens on sheltered south-facing branches at the very end of the month in the south. Scarlet Elf Cup finishes in the first two weeks of March. Alexanders peaks in early March and starts to flower and toughen toward the end of the month. Pick it now if you want the best of it.

## April

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in April | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** April foraging UK: hawthorn blossom, sorrel, sea kale and wild garlic flowers. Full spring in the hedgerows. What to find and how.

---

April is when the hedgerows catch up with the woodland. Wild garlic is still going, the hedgerow mustards and sorrels are at their peak, and the blossom season begins properly. The light is better. The ground is warm enough to kneel on. This is the month most people discover they have been walking past food for years.

### Highlights at a glance

- Hawthorn blossom opens from mid-month. Petals and young leaves are both edible and worth picking
- Wild garlic is flowering. The flowers are edible and worth using before the plant collapses in May
- Sorrel is at its most tender this month. Sharp, bright, and excellent raw or cooked
- Sea Kale is shooting on shingle beaches in the south. A coastal vegetable of genuine quality
- Sweet Woodruff and Lady's Smock are both flowering. Sweet Woodruff is particularly good for cold infusions
- St George's Mushroom is in season on chalky grassland from late April. Read the species page before picking

### What's worth finding this month

**[Hawthorn Blossom](/species/hawthorn-blossom)**

Hawthorn blossom (*Crataegus monogyna*) covers every hedgerow in Britain through April and May and almost nobody picks it. The petals have a faint almond quality and a floral character that holds up well in a cold-infused cordial or vinegar. The young leaves, which appear just before the blossom, are mild and good eaten straight from the branch. An old country habit worth keeping. Pick blossom from branches well away from busy roads and use within a day. The flower heads wilt fast once picked and lose their fragrance quickly. Pick in the morning, use the same afternoon.

**[Sorrel](/species/sorrel)**

Common Sorrel (*Rumex acetosa*) is at its most tender and flavourful in April, before the stems elongate and the leaves toughen toward summer. The flavour is clean, bright acidity. A lemon without the sweetness. Raw it lifts a green salad or a plate of dressed leaves. Cooked, the acidity mellows considerably and it becomes a sauce or a soup base worth making. Pick the young leaves from the centre of the plant and leave the outer leaves. It regrows quickly and a single patch will give you several pickings through spring.

**[Sea Kale](/species/sea-kale)**

Sea Kale (*Crambe maritima*) shoots on shingle beaches in southern and western Britain from April through June. The young blanched shoots, those still pale beneath the shingle, are the prize. They emerge as thick, fleshy spears with crinkled blue-green leaves just beginning to unfurl. The plant is not common and should be picked sparingly from large colonies only. The shoots are at their best briefly. Pick them young and use them quickly.

### Arriving and finishing

Elderflower opens in sheltered spots in the south toward the end of April in warm years. Chicken of the Woods (*Laetiporus sulphureus*) begins appearing on oak and sweet chestnut from mid-month. Wild garlic is coming to the end of its leaf season. The flowers are still good and the green seed heads forming now are excellent pickled. Morel season is closing by mid-April.

## May

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in May | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** May foraging UK: elderflower, Chicken of the Woods, wild garlic seed heads and the last spring greens. What to pick before spring turns to summer.

---

May is two months at once. The spring greens are still good in the first two weeks, then the elderflower opening in the second week changes everything. From that point you are split between finishing what spring started and chasing what summer has just begun. There is almost too much to do in May, which is the right kind of problem.

### Highlights at a glance

- Elderflower opens from mid-May in the south. Pick first thing in the morning on a dry day, fully open but before the tiny florets start to drop
- Chicken of the Woods fruits on oak and sweet chestnut. Young growth at the outer edge only. Old brackets toughen fast
- Wild garlic leaves are finishing but the green seed heads forming now are excellent pickled
- Marsh Samphire is beginning to shoot in estuaries. Small and tender, the very start of its season
- Bistort is up in northern meadows. Worth finding if you are in the Pennines
- Crow Garlic, Sow Thistle, and Brooklime are all in season and worth knowing

### What's worth finding this month

**[Elderflower](/species/elderflower)**

Elderflower (*Sambucus nigra*) needs precise timing. The flowers are at their best for a short window: fully open, fragrant, and before the tiny petals begin to fall. Pick on a dry morning, away from busy roads. The heads should smell floral and distinctly musky. If they have started to drop or smell stale, they are past it. Elderflower cordial is the obvious use and a genuinely good one, but the flowers work equally well in cold-infused vinegars and syrups. The season lasts two to three weeks in the south and follows the warmth northward through June. Read the species page before picking. It matters for elder.

**[Chicken of the Woods](/species/chicken-of-the-woods)**

Chicken of the Woods (*Laetiporus sulphureus*) is a hard-to-miss find in the British countryside: overlapping shelves of vivid orange and yellow on the trunks of oak, sweet chestnut, and other species, from May through October. Pick young brackets only. The outer edge, soft and pale yellow, giving slightly under pressure, is what you want. Older growth is dry, chalky, and not worth taking. It flushes several times on the same tree through the season. Find it once and check back every few weeks. The species page has the detail on host trees that matter for this one.

**[Wild Garlic Seed Heads](/species/wild-garlic)**

The green seed heads that form on Wild Garlic (*Allium ursinum*) in May are among the most overlooked picks of the spring. Once the white flowers fade, round green seed capsules develop on the flower stems. Pick them while still firm and green, before they dry and split. Preserved in brine or cider vinegar they keep for months and deliver a sharper, more pungent flavour than the fresh leaves ever do. A way to extend the wild garlic season well beyond the point when the leaves have gone.

### Arriving and finishing

Wild Strawberry (*Fragaria vesca*) is flowering in woodland edges and clearings. Dog Rose is in bud. Dulse and Sea Lettuce are available on the coast from May onward. Wild Garlic leaves are finished by the third week. Morel season is over for another year.

## June

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in June | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** June foraging UK: marsh samphire, meadowsweet, dog rose and the last elderflower. The coastal and flower month. What to pick and when.

---

June rewards those who know where to go. The spring rush is over and the autumn abundance is some way off. What June offers is precise: a specific run of flowers and coastal plants with short windows, unmissable if you know about them and easy to miss if you do not. The coast in June is worth a dedicated trip.

### Highlights at a glance

- Marsh Samphire is shooting in English estuaries and at its most tender now. The finest wild vegetable the calendar offers at this point
- Dog Rose petals are open on every hedgerow. Pick in the morning for cold-infused cordial or vinegar
- Meadowsweet is flowering in damp meadows and along riverbanks. Cold-infuse the heads in cream overnight
- Lime Blossom from Common and Small-leaved Lime makes the finest flower tea in Britain. Pick when fully open
- Elderflower is finishing in the south. Last call. Still going in the north through June
- Fat Hen and Good King Henry are up on disturbed ground. Straightforward cooked greens

### What's worth finding this month

**[Marsh Samphire](/species/marsh-samphire)**

Marsh Samphire (*Salicornia europaea*) grows in dense bright green carpets across the mudflats and saltmarshes of English estuaries. In June the plants are young, slender, and at their most tender. Pick stems from the upper saltmarsh where they are cleanest, rinse thoroughly, and blanch briefly in unsalted water. It needs nothing more than butter. The flavour is bright and saline and distinctly its own. The plants reach their full size in July, but June growth is more delicate and the estuaries are quieter.

**[Meadowsweet](/species/meadowsweet)**

Meadowsweet (*Filipendula ulmaria*) grows in damp meadows, along riverbanks, and in wet ditches through June and July. The flowers are creamy-white, held in dense frothy heads, and smell of almonds with something harder to name. A scent that is immediately recognisable once you know it. Cold infusion in cream or milk is the method that serves the flavour best: place fresh flower heads in cold liquid and refrigerate overnight. Strip the flowers from the stalks before infusing. The scent fades quickly once picked so use it the same day.

**[Dog Rose](/species/dog-rose)**

Dog Rose (*Rosa canina*) covers British hedgerows and the pale pink five-petalled flowers are at their brief peak in June. They drop quickly after opening and are best picked in the morning on a dry day. The flavour is mild and floral. Use fresh petals in cold-infused vinegars or syrups and use them the same day. They do not survive heat well. Cooking with them loses most of what makes them worthwhile. The hips that follow in September are a completely different prospect and a fine autumn find. For now, it is the flowers.

### Arriving and finishing

Wild Strawberry (*Fragaria vesca*) is fruiting in the second half of June. Chanterelle appears in the best years in sheltered mossy woodland from late June after sustained rain. Wild Cherry and Bilberry are beginning. Elderflower is finished in the south by mid-June. Do not leave it.

## July

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in July | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** July foraging UK: chanterelle, bilberry, bay bolete and marsh samphire at its best. Summer fungi and fruit. What to find and where.

---

July is when two threads of the foraging year first run together: summer fruit and the start of the fungi season. Bilberry and raspberry are ready on moorland and in clearings. Chanterelle is fruiting in damp mossy woodland after rain. Go out early in July and go out after rain.

### Highlights at a glance

- Chanterelle peaks through July and August in damp mossy broadleaf woodland. Find them under beech and oak after rain
- Bilberry is ripening on moorland and heathland. Small, intensely flavoured, and worth picking in quantity
- Wild Cherry is ready in July in most years. A brief window. The birds move fast
- Bay Bolete is fruiting under pine, larch, and spruce. A reliable and genuinely good summer find
- Marsh Samphire is at its full best this month. The peak of the coastal green season
- Raspberry is in woodland clearings and scrub from July. Wilder and less sweet than the garden variety

### What's worth finding this month

**[Chanterelle](/species/chanterelle)**

Chanterelle (*Cantharellus cibarius*) is the find that defines the summer forager. It grows in damp, mossy broadleaf woodland, most often under beech and oak, from July through October. The cap is golden-yellow to egg-yolk orange, 3–12cm across, wavy-edged and irregular. The underside has forking ridges rather than true gills, running down the stem. The whole fungus smells faintly of apricots. They grow in the same spots year on year. Find your location once and you have it for as long as the woodland stands. Sautéed in butter it needs almost nothing else. Read the species page before picking your first.

**[Bilberry](/species/bilberry)**

Bilberry (*Vaccinium myrtillus*) grows on acid moorland, heathland, and open woodland across Britain, fruiting through July and August. The berries are smaller and darker than cultivated blueberries and the flavour is considerably more concentrated. Tart, deeply fruited, with none of the watery sweetness of the shop version. They stain everything they touch a deep purple. Pick in quantity because it takes time to collect enough for anything useful. Find bilberry by finding the right habitat: acid soil, heathery moorland, open birch woodland on higher ground.

**[Marsh Samphire](/species/marsh-samphire)**

July is the peak month for Marsh Samphire (*Salicornia europaea*) and the plants are now at their full size and best. The stems are bright green, firm, and intensely saline. Pick from the upper saltmarsh on a low tide, rinse well, and use quickly. It deteriorates within a day or two of picking. This is a wild vegetable that has made a genuine mark on restaurant cooking, and the version collected at its peak from a good estuary is better than anything sold in a supermarket.

### Arriving and finishing

Blackberry (*Rubus fruticosus*) starts fruiting in sheltered spots from late July in the south. Cep (*Boletus edulis*) begins appearing under pine and spruce in the Scottish uplands in the second half of July in good years. Lime Blossom is finished by mid-July. Wild Gooseberry is ready in hedgerows and scrub through July.

## August

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in August | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** August foraging UK: cep, blackberry, elderberry and the peak of the chanterelle season. Summer and autumn overlapping. What's at its best right now.

---

August is when autumn arrives in the fungi before it arrives anywhere else. The Cep is up under pine and spruce. Blackberries are ripening on every hedgerow. Elderberries are nearly there. Everything is happening at once and the month rewards going out often rather than saving it for one long day.

### Highlights at a glance

- Cep is fruiting under pine, spruce, and beech from early August in a good year. Rain followed by warmth. That is the moment
- Blackberries ripen from early August in the south. The best fruit is in full sun on south-facing hedgerows
- Elderberry clusters are darkening through August and will be fully ready by month's end in most areas
- Chanterelle continues strongly through August. Often the best month for it
- Hedgehog Mushroom begins appearing in woodland from late August
- Giant Puffball appears in old pasture and meadow edges. Read the species page before picking

### What's worth finding this month

**[Cep](/species/cep)**

Cep (*Boletus edulis*) is the most sought-after fungus in Britain and the early season starts in August under pine and spruce in Scotland and northern England before moving south. The cap is brown and smooth, 8–25cm across. The colour of a baked bread crust. The pores beneath are white, turning yellow-green with age. The stem is pale, club-shaped, with a fine raised network of white lines. Find Cep in a location once and return every August. It fruits in the same spots with reliable consistency in good years. Cep dries better than almost any other fungus. Sliced thinly and dried low and slow, the flavour concentrates and deepens considerably.

**[Blackberry](/species/blackberry)**

Blackberry (*Rubus fruticosus*) needs no introduction. The bramble covers every hedgerow, field margin, and scrubby edge in Britain and the fruit in August and September is among the great free things available to anyone who goes outside. The best blackberries are in full sun on south-facing banks, away from paths where picking pressure is highest. Fruit in deep shade tends to be smaller and more acid. Pick fully ripe: black all over, yielding slightly to pressure, coming away cleanly from the plug.

**[Elderberry](/species/elderberry)**

Elderberry (*Sambucus nigra*) clusters hang heavy and dark purple through August, ripening to near-black by month's end. Pick them fully dark. Underripe berries are not worth taking. Elderberry cordial, jelly, and wine are the classic uses and there are good reasons they are classic. The species page covers the full detail on elder, including which parts of the plant are not the berries.

### Arriving and finishing

Sloe (*Prunus spinosa*) berries are forming on blackthorn but not yet ready. Rosehip is colouring on dog rose. Hazelnut is in the shell but not fully ripe. The green cases begin to split in late August. Bilberry is finishing on higher ground by mid-August. Marsh Samphire is toughening and starting to redden. The picking window is closing.

## September

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in September | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** September foraging UK: cep, hedgehog mushroom, hazelnut, sloe and sweet chestnut. The richest month in the British foraging calendar.

---

September is the month. No other month comes close for sheer variety and quality. The woodland floor is alive with fungi. Hedgerows are loaded with sloe, rosehip, and the last blackberries. The nut season begins. If you go out once in the foraging year, September is when to go.

### Highlights at a glance

- Cep peaks through September in broadleaf and mixed woodland. A week of rain followed by warm days is the trigger
- Hedgehog Mushroom starts fruiting from September. A beginner-friendly edible fungi with no dangerous lookalikes in Britain
- Hazelnut drops from the shells in late September. Get there before the squirrels
- Sweet Chestnut falls in southern England from mid-September. A proper food, not a novelty
- Rosehip is at full colour and full flavour on every hedgerow
- The coastal shellfish season restarts properly from September. Mussels, winkles, and cockles are good again

### What's worth finding this month

**[Hedgehog Mushroom](/species/hedgehog-mushroom)**

Hedgehog Mushroom (*Hydnum repandum*) is a safe and beginner-friendly edible fungi in Britain. Instead of gills or pores, the underside of the cap bears cream-coloured spines or teeth, 3–6mm long. The cap is cream to pale tan, wavy and irregular, 4–15cm across. The stem is pale, solid, and stocky. It grows in broadleaf and mixed woodland, often in loose groups, from September through November. There is nothing in Britain that looks like it and is dangerous. A slight bitterness in the flesh fades with cooking. Find a productive woodland in September and check it every few weeks through to November.

**[Hazelnut](/species/hazelnut)**

Hazelnut (*Corylus avellana*) ripens from September in hedgerows, coppice woodland, and scrub across Britain. The nuts are ripe when the green case begins to yellow and the nut separates cleanly, or simply when you find them on the ground. The competition is serious: squirrels, dormice, and woodpeckers all know exactly when the nuts are ready and work faster than most foragers. Get out in the first two weeks of September in a good year. The flavour of a fresh hazel from the shell is far removed from the dried nuts sold commercially.

**[Sweet Chestnut](/species/sweet-chestnut)**

Sweet Chestnut (*Castanea sativa*) begins falling in September in southern England and peaks through October. The spiny cases split on the tree or on impact with the ground, releasing one to three nuts. Collect from the ground under productive trees: large, mature specimens in parks, estate woodlands, and southern hedgerows. The nuts do not store long raw. Use them within a week or freeze them cooked and peeled. Roasted, braised, or ground into flour, this is a substantial wild food that repays the effort of finding good trees.

### Arriving and finishing

Winter Chanterelle (*Craterellus tubaeformis*) starts appearing in wet conifer woodland from September. Wood Blewit (*Lepista nuda*) begins fruiting at woodland edges in the second half of the month. Blackberry quality declines quickly toward month's end. The coastal shellfish season restarts. Mussels, winkles, and cockles are good again after the summer.

## October

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in October | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** October foraging UK: wood blewit, hedgehog mushroom, sloe after first frost and sweet chestnut. Deep autumn in the British countryside.

---

October is the month of transitions. The summer boletes are on their way out. The winter fungi, Oyster Mushroom, Velvet Shank, Winter Chanterelle, are arriving. Sloe after the first frost is a ritual for a reason. The woodland floor smells of decay in the best possible sense: fungi, wet leaves, turned earth. Go out in the mornings.

### Highlights at a glance

- Wood Blewit is at its best in October in deciduous woodland and compost-rich gardens. The blue-lilac colouring is distinctive
- Hedgehog Mushroom peaks through October. Large, clean, and straightforward to identify
- Sweet Chestnut is still falling and often better now than in September
- Sloe is at its best after the first frost, which softens the skins and reduces bitterness
- Oyster Mushroom arrives on dead beech logs and stumps in the second half of the month
- Winter Chanterelle is appearing in mossy conifer woodland from October onward

### What's worth finding this month

**[Wood Blewit](/species/wood-blewit)**

Wood Blewit (*Lepista nuda*) is a visually striking autumn fungi: the cap, gills, and stem are all suffused with a distinctive blue-lilac colour, fading to tan as the mushroom ages. It grows in deciduous woodland, hedgerows, and gardens in deep leaf litter and compost-rich soil, from October through December. The smell is strong and floral, almost perfumed. Find it in the same spots year on year. It is a consistent returner to productive locations. Worth learning properly before picking. The species page covers what you need.

**[Sloe](/species/sloe)**

Sloe (*Prunus spinosa*) is ready when it is ready, not when the calendar says. Most foragers wait for the first frost, which softens the skins and reduces the mouth-puckering tannins that make raw sloes effectively inedible. In mild autumns, pricking each berry before using achieves the same result. The berries are deep blue-black and covered in a fine bloom. Sloe gin is the classic use and needs no improvement. They also make a good jelly with crab apple and are worth picking in quantity when conditions are right.

**[Hedgehog Mushroom](/species/hedgehog-mushroom)**

October is the peak month for Hedgehog Mushroom and the best time to find large, clean specimens in quantity. They fruit in loose groups in broadleaf and mixed woodland through October and November, often in the same spots as Chanterelle. The identifying feature, cream-white spines or teeth on the underside of the cap rather than gills, is immediately visible and unmistakable once you have seen it. Worth picking in volume in October. They dry well and the season is still at its best.

### Arriving and finishing

Velvet Shank (*Flammulina velutipes*) is appearing on dead elm and beech from late October. The Cep season is effectively over in most areas by mid-month, though cold and dry conditions can extend it in the uplands. Giant Puffball is finished. Chanterelle tapers off through the month, replaced by Winter Chanterelle in wet conifer sites.

## November

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in November | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** November foraging UK: velvet shank, winter chanterelle, oyster mushroom and coastal shellfish. The winter fungi season begins properly.

---

November looks like the end of the foraging year. It is not. Most people stop going out this month, which means quieter woodland and less competition for those who do not. The winter fungi are at their best. The coast is fully productive. There are fewer species than October, but the ones that are here are genuinely worth going out for.

### Highlights at a glance

- Velvet Shank is fully into its season on dead wood and will continue through to March
- Winter Chanterelle peaks in November in mossy wet conifer woodland. Underrated and worth finding
- Oyster Mushroom continues on beech deadwood and stumps. Cold weather suits it and slows the competition
- Coastal shellfish, mussel, winkle, cockle, are in prime condition. Cold, clean winter water
- Rosehip holds well on the branches into November in cold, dry conditions. Good for syrup and jelly
- Burdock root can be dug now from first-year plants. A substantial wild root vegetable available when little else is

### What's worth finding this month

**[Winter Chanterelle](/species/winter-chanterelle)**

Winter Chanterelle (*Craterellus tubaeformis*), also called the Yellowleg or Trumpet Chanterelle, is an underappreciated fungi in the British calendar. It grows in dense groups in wet, mossy conifer woodland, particularly under spruce and pine, from September through December and into January. The cap is small, 1–5cm, brown and funnel-shaped with a wavy margin. The stem is distinctly yellow to orange-yellow and hollow. The flavour is earthy and direct and it dries exceptionally well. Find a productive spot in November and revisit it every two or three weeks throughout the season.

**[Velvet Shank](/species/velvet-shank)**

November marks the beginning of Velvet Shank's proper season. The clusters that appear on dead elm, beech, and ash from now through March are at their freshest. The glossy orange-brown cap, 2–8cm, and distinctly velvety lower stem are the identifying features. This fungus survives frost: it freezes solid and revives without damage when temperatures rise. A hard November frost does not end the season. It simply pauses it. Find it on standing deadwood and large fallen logs in wet, shaded woodland.

**[Mussel](/species/mussel)**

November through March is the best window for wild mussels and the quality now is at its peak. Cold water keeps them firm and in excellent condition. Pick only from fast-moving, clean tidal water and always check the water quality classification of any site you are not certain about. The Shellfish Association of Great Britain publishes this data. Wild mussels collected at this time of year are a different thing from what you buy.

### Arriving and finishing

The coastal foraging season is deepening into winter. Burdock root (*Arctium minus*) comes into its best in November and December as the above-ground growth dies back. Jelly Ear (*Auricularia auricula-judae*) is findable on elder through the year and prominent now the leaves are down. The autumn berry and nut season is effectively over by the end of the month.

## December

**SEO Title:** What to Forage in December | The Foragers
**SEO Description:** December foraging UK: oyster mushroom, velvet shank, coastal shellfish and laver. The coastal month. What's worth going out for in winter.

---

December is a coastal month. Inland, the options narrow to what grows on dead wood and what persists in the ground. The coast gives you the best shellfish of the year and seaweeds available on every low tide. If you have been putting off coastal foraging all year, stop putting it off.

### Highlights at a glance

- Mussels, winkles, and cockles are in peak condition. Cold, clean water and none of the water quality concerns of summer
- Oyster Mushroom flushes on beech deadwood and stumps after mild, wet spells
- Velvet Shank continues and will do so through January and February. Find it on elm, beech, and ash
- Wood Blewit is still going in mild conditions through the first half of the month
- Burdock root is worth digging from first-year plants. Starchy, earthy, and genuinely filling
- Seaweeds, laver, carrageen, bladderwrack, kelp, are available and at their cleanest

### What's worth finding this month

**[Oyster Mushroom](/species/oyster-mushroom)**

December is as reliable a month for Oyster Mushroom (*Pleurotus ostreatus*) as any in the calendar. A week of wet, mild weather following colder conditions is the trigger. Inspect beech deadwood, fallen logs, and standing stumps in deciduous woodland after rain. The overlapping grey-brown fans, 5–25cm, with crowded white gills running down a short off-centre stem, are hard to miss once you know them. Specimens in December tend to be firmer and cleaner than summer finds. Pick young and use within two days.

**[Burdock](/species/burdock)**

Burdock root (*Arctium minus*) is available through autumn and winter and December is a good month to dig it before the ground freezes. Find first-year plants. They are identifiable by very large, round leaves up to 50cm across, which have died back but left visible dead stalks at ground level. The root of a first-year plant grows straight down and can reach 30–60cm in good soil. Bring a fork and patience. Scrub it, peel it, and roast or braise it. The flavour is earthy and lightly sweet, somewhere between parsnip and artichoke. This is the root in traditional dandelion and burdock.

**[Laver](/species/laver)**

Laver (*Porphyra umbilicalis*) is available year-round on rocky coastlines but December's low tides and clean winter water make it a particularly good month to collect it. The fronds are thin, translucent red-purple sheets, 10–20cm, attached to rocks in the mid-to-lower intertidal zone. Collect from clean, wave-washed rock and rinse thoroughly before use. Slow-cooked into laverbread, dried and crumbled as a seaweed seasoning, or used in sauces: it is the closest thing in British waters to Japanese nori, which it closely resembles.

### Arriving and finishing

Nothing of note is arriving in December. The foraging year is at its quietest point, but it is not finished. Three-cornered Leek shoots appear in mild spots from January. Scarlet Elf Cup begins in sheltered southern woodland toward the end of January. The year is already turning.
