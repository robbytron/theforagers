## 81. Beechnut

**Species Name:** Beechnut
**Latin Name:** Fagus sylvatica
**Type:** Nuts
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** beechnut
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
Beech is one of Britain's most recognisable trees. In a good mast year, the ground beneath a mature beech turns brown with fallen nuts in October. The small, triangular kernels are mild, sweet, and slightly oily — worth picking when the crop is good.

---

**Full Description:**
Beech is a dominant tree across much of southern and central England, forming some of the most spectacular woodland in the country on the chalk downs of the Chilterns and the South Downs. In a mast year — which occurs irregularly, roughly every three to five years — a single mature tree can drop thousands of nuts.

The nuts are enclosed in a spiny, four-lobed husk that splits open on the ground as the nuts ripen. Each husk usually contains two or three triangular nuts, though one or both may be empty or poorly developed. Testing as you go is part of the process: squeeze the nut between finger and thumb — a full nut resists; an empty one collapses.

The flavour is mild and pleasant, with a slight sweetness and a gentle nuttiness close to hazelnut. In lean years the crop is negligible. In mast years it is abundant enough to make serious picking worthwhile. Mast years correlate loosely with a hot, dry summer the previous year.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Beech is a large, smooth-barked deciduous tree reaching 25–40m. The bark is distinctive: pale grey, very smooth even on old trees, occasionally marked with old scars or graffiti. Leaves are 4–9cm, oval with a wavy margin and silky hairs on the veins, turning copper-gold in October before falling. The husks are 1.5–2.5cm, covered in soft, recurved spines. The nuts themselves are 1–1.5cm, sharply three-angled, brown, and glossy. The combination of smooth grey bark, copper autumn leaves, and spiny four-lobed husks is specific to beech and unmistakable in British woodland.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Beech has no dangerous lookalikes among British trees producing similar husked nuts. Sweet chestnut (*Castanea sativa*) has much larger, more fiercely spined husks and larger nuts; the leaves are entirely different. Horse chestnut (*Aesculus hippocastanum*) produces conkers in a thick, warty green husk — quite different from beech, and its "nuts" are toxic.

The main risk with beechnuts is eating too many raw. Raw beechnuts contain saponins and a compound called fagin, which in large quantities can cause nausea, headaches, and stomach cramps. Roasting or blanching removes this. Eat only small quantities raw. Children are more susceptible than adults.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Beech woodland on chalk, limestone, and free-draining sandy soils throughout southern and central England. Most productive on the chalk escarpments of the Chilterns, Cotswolds, and South Downs. Also found in beech plantations throughout Wales and Scotland, though native beech is largely restricted to England. Isolated parkland trees can be very productive in mast years. Look for the carpet of spiny husks and copper leaves on the woodland floor in October.

---

**In Season:**
Nuts fall from mid-September, with the main drop in October. In a good mast year, collect promptly — squirrels, jays, and wood mice clear productive sites quickly. By November, most fallen nuts have been taken or have begun to deteriorate. The crop is highly variable year to year: in a poor year, most husks contain empty or shrivelled nuts. Check a handful before committing to a long session.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw: eat in small quantities, straight from the husk — mild and pleasant. Roasted: spread on a tray, roast at 180°C for 10–12 minutes until the papery inner skin loosens. Peel while warm. Beechnut oil: press or grind roasted kernels and extract the oil; it was historically used as a cooking oil in France. The flavour is mild and versatile. Beechnut flour: dry roasted kernels fully, then grind — use as a partial flour substitute in bread and biscuits. The yield per nut is small; budget for significant processing time relative to the quantity of food produced.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fallen beechnuts from public woodland and commons for personal use is permitted. Nuts from private woodland are technically private property. Beech mast on Forestry England land can be collected for personal use without a licence. Large-scale commercial collection requires a permit.

---

## 82. Acorn

**Species Name:** Acorn
**Latin Name:** Quercus robur / Quercus petraea
**Type:** Nuts
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** acorn
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
Acorns are abundant and free across almost all of Britain, but they cannot be eaten straight from the tree. Raw acorns contain tannins that make them bitter and, in quantity, harmful. Leaching — repeatedly rinsing the ground meal in water — removes the bitterness and leaves a mild, starchy flour with real culinary uses.

---

**Full Description:**
Oak is Britain's most iconic tree. *Quercus robur*, the pedunculate oak, and *Quercus petraea*, the sessile oak, both produce edible acorns after processing. The two species hybridise freely and for foraging purposes are interchangeable.

The fundamental thing to know about acorns is that preparation is not optional. Raw acorns contain water-soluble tannins that make them astringent and bitter, and that cause gastrointestinal irritation and, in large quantities, kidney damage. All of this is removed by leaching. The process requires time but not skill.

Acorn flour, once leached, is mildly sweet and starchy, with a flavour that sits somewhere between chestnut flour and wholemeal wheat. It has been used as a staple food throughout human history — across native North America, prehistoric Europe, and into historical times in parts of southern Europe and Korea. As a foraging project it rewards patience.

---

**Identification Notes:**
The two native oaks are both large, long-lived deciduous trees reaching 20–40m. *Quercus robur* has acorns on long stalks (peduncles) of 2–8cm; the leaves are short-stalked. *Quercus petraea* has acorns directly on the branch or on very short stalks; the leaves have longer stalks. Both produce the classic lobed oak leaf, 7–14cm long. Acorns are 1.5–3cm, oval to cylindrical, set in a shallow, scaly cup covering roughly a third of the nut. The cup and the lobed leaf together make acorns completely unmistakable. Turkey oak (*Quercus cerris*), a common introduced species, produces acorns with distinctively shaggy, mossy cups — also edible after leaching, though more bitter.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No British tree produces a fruit that could be confused with an acorn. The scaly cup is specific to oaks.

The danger with acorns is not misidentification. It is eating them raw or insufficiently leached. Raw acorns cause gastrointestinal pain, vomiting, and diarrhoea. Repeated or large consumption of raw or under-leached acorns can cause kidney damage. Always leach the flour fully before use — taste as you go, and continue until all bitterness has gone.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Oak woodland, hedgerows, parkland, and wood pasture throughout Britain. Both native oaks are widespread from Cornwall to the Scottish Highlands, though *Q. robur* dominates lowland England and *Q. petraea* is more common in the upland west and north. Ancient oak parkland — country estates, the New Forest, Epping Forest, the Forest of Dean — can be extremely productive in a good year. Urban parks frequently contain mature oaks.

---

**In Season:**
Acorns fall from late September through October and into November after strong winds. Collect from the ground; green acorns still on the tree are immature and even more bitter. Avoid any that are mouldy, have exit holes from weevil larvae, or feel hollow. The outer green skin of freshly fallen acorns comes off easily. Allow collected acorns to dry for a few days before processing.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Acorn flour: shell the acorns, remove the papery inner skin, grind raw in a food processor to a coarse meal. Leach by placing in a jar or cloth, covering with cold water, and changing the water twice daily for three to seven days — or run under cold running water for several hours. Taste regularly. When the bitterness is gone, drain, dry in a low oven (80°C) until completely dry, then regrind to a finer flour. Use in flatbreads, porridge, and baked goods — blend with wheat flour as acorn flour has no gluten. Acorn coffee: roast whole shelled acorns at 180°C until dark brown, grind, and brew as a caffeine-free substitute. The flavour is earthy and robust.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Collecting fallen acorns from public woodland and commons for personal use is permitted. Large-scale collection from Forestry England land requires a licence. Acorns in private woodland are private property. Most landowners are relaxed about personal-scale collection from woodland paths and edges.

---

## 83. Marsh Samphire

**Species Name:** Marsh Samphire
**Latin Name:** Salicornia europaea
**Type:** Coastal & Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** marsh-samphire
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
Marsh samphire grows in saltmarshes around the British coast and is one of the few wild plants now common enough in supermarkets to have become a mainstream ingredient. The flavour is intensely salty, crisp, and faintly grassy. Pick it in summer before it turns woody and red.

---

**Full Description:**
Marsh samphire — also called glasswort — grows in the upper intertidal zone of saltmarshes, in the channels and pans where tidal water floods and drains regularly. It is a plant of high salinity: its jointed, fleshy stems accumulate salt from the surrounding mud.

Despite the supermarket presence, picked fresh from a saltmarsh at the right time of year it is a different thing to the blanched and packaged product. The texture is firmer, the salt intensity is higher, and the faint marine green flavour is intact.

The picking window is specific. June and July produce the best stems: bright green, firm, and tender enough to eat whole after brief cooking. By August the stems are beginning to toughen; by September they are turning red and the texture becomes unpleasant. Pick early in the season for the best results.

This is *marsh* samphire, a flowering plant unrelated to rock samphire (*Crithmum maritimum*). The two share a name and a coastal habitat but are botanically distinct.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low-growing, highly branched annual, typically 10–35cm. The stems are jointed and succulent, resembling a tiny cactus or a miniature jointed finger. There are no visible leaves — the jointed stem segments are the photosynthetic structure. Colour is bright green in summer, turning yellow-green to red by September. The plant grows in dense stands in the bare mud and salt pans of saltmarshes, often forming continuous mats. Tiny flowers appear in August, embedded in the stem joints. The habitat alone — mid-saltmarsh mud — narrows identification considerably. Nothing else in Britain looks like marsh samphire in its habitat.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic lookalikes grow alongside marsh samphire in saltmarsh habitat. Other *Salicornia* species (*S. pusilla*, *S. ramosissima*, and others) grow in similar habitats and are all edible. For foraging purposes they are interchangeable.

Cord grass (*Spartina* spp.) grows in the same saltmarsh zones and is not edible, but it is a grass — flat-leaved, not jointed or succulent, and easily distinguished.

The main practical concern is access: saltmarsh mud can be very deep and unstable in places. Test ground before committing. Some saltmarshes are managed nature reserves with restrictions on picking — check before you go.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Saltmarshes on sheltered coasts around Britain. Most abundant on the muddy estuaries and sheltered bays of East Anglia, Kent, Essex, and Lincolnshire. Also found on the coasts of South Wales, the Solway Firth, and parts of Scotland. Typically absent from exposed rocky coasts. The plant grows in the mid-saltmarsh zone, in the bare or sparsely vegetated areas that flood with each high tide.

---

**In Season:**
June to early August for the best picking. The plant emerges in late spring and grows quickly through June. Stems are at their best — firm, green, and tender — in June and July. August is marginal; September is too late. Cut or snap the stems rather than pulling the whole plant. Take from dense stands only and leave the majority.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Blanch briefly in boiling unsalted water for 2–3 minutes — it needs no added salt. Serve warm with butter alongside fish or lamb. Tempura: dip stems in a light batter and fry in hot oil for 1–2 minutes. Works extremely well. Pickled samphire: blanch briefly, pack into sterilised jars with white wine vinegar and a little sugar. The saltiness mellows in the jar over a few days. Do not overcook — more than 3–4 minutes in boiling water and the stems collapse. Eat whole: the inner core of young stems slides out from the outer skin in one piece, or eat both together.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Marsh samphire grows on intertidal land, which in England is generally owned by the Crown Estate. Picking for personal use is widely accepted. Several Norfolk and Suffolk saltmarshes are SSSI or National Nature Reserve — check reserve rules before picking. Commercial harvesting requires permission and may require a licence from the Crown Estate.

---

## 84. Rock Samphire

**Species Name:** Rock Samphire
**Latin Name:** Crithmum maritimum
**Type:** Coastal & Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** rock-samphire
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Rock samphire grows on sea cliffs, rocky shores, and coastal walls around southern and western Britain. It has a powerful, unusual flavour — aromatic, resinous, faintly aniseed — that was prized enough to be sold on London's streets in the seventeenth century. It is not for everyone, but used carefully it is one of the most interesting coastal plants to cook with.

---

**Full Description:**
Rock samphire is a different plant entirely from marsh samphire, despite sharing a name. It grows from cracks in sea cliffs, on shingle, and on coastal walls and banks, usually within reach of salt spray. It is native to southern and western coasts of Britain and Ireland and becomes scarce further north.

Shakespeare called it "dreadful trade" in King Lear, describing men hanging from cliffs to collect it. That commercial importance reflects how highly it was valued before the global spice trade made exotic aromatics cheap and available. The flavour is distinctive to the point of being divisive: strongly aromatic, with a herbal, resinous character and a faint aniseed or fennel note. Used in moderation it is excellent.

The leaves and stems are both edible. Young growth in spring and early summer is most tender and least resinous. As the season progresses the flavour intensifies. The seeds, dried, have historically been used as a pickling spice.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A compact, bushy perennial, 15–45cm, with fleshy, bright grey-green leaves divided into narrow, cylindrical leaflets of 1–3cm. The overall leaf shape is compound and feathery, though the individual segments are round in cross-section rather than flat. Flowers are yellow-green, small, in compound umbels of 3–8cm across, appearing June to September. The plant smells strongly of its characteristic aromatic oil when the leaves are crushed — this is the most reliable identification check in the field. It grows directly from cliff rock, shingle, or masonry, usually on south- or west-facing exposures. The fleshy, grey-green divided leaves and the strong smell are together specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Rock samphire belongs to the carrot family (Apiaceae), which contains some of Britain's most dangerous plants. This requires care.

Hemlock water-dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) is the most dangerous plant in Britain's flora and grows in coastal wetlands and stream banks near the sea. It does not grow on dry sea cliffs or shingle — rock samphire's actual habitat — and the two plants look quite different, but the family resemblance in the flowers is real. Hemlock water-dropwort causes rapid and fatal seizures. Never pick any umbellifer from coastal habitat without positive identification of every feature.

Rock samphire's strong, specific smell is its best distinguishing feature. No dangerous British umbellifer smells like rock samphire. Crush the leaf and confirm the aromatic, resinous scent before picking anything.

Do not pick rock samphire from wet, low-lying coastal ground. It is a plant of dry, rocky, elevated coastal positions only.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Sea cliffs, coastal rocks, shingle beaches, and old coastal walls and masonry. Most common on the south and west coasts of England, Wales, and Ireland. Particularly abundant on the chalk cliffs of the south coast, the limestone coasts of Somerset and Pembrokeshire, and the granite of Cornwall. Becomes scarce north of the Wash on the east coast and north of Galloway in Scotland. Always within reach of salt spray; does not grow inland.

---

**In Season:**
Young growth emerges from March. Best picked June to August when growth is active and stems are still relatively tender. The seeds, which develop in August and September, can be dried and used as a pickling spice. Avoid over-picking from single plants — rock samphire grows slowly in difficult conditions and cliff-face plants are particularly vulnerable to repeated picking.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use sparingly: the flavour is strong. Finely chopped raw in a sauce for grilled fish — its natural partner. Pickled: pack young shoots into white wine vinegar with sugar and salt, leave for at least two weeks. The pickling mellows the resinous edge and produces an unusual, excellent condiment. Briefly blanched and served with butter. Added to a court-bouillon for poaching fish or shellfish. Seed heads dried and used as a pickling spice — they retain the aromatic flavour well. Start with small quantities until you have a feel for the intensity.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Rock samphire grows on coastal cliffs and foreshores. The foreshore in England is generally Crown Estate land. Picking for personal use is accepted. Many sea cliffs are SSSI or National Nature Reserve land — check before picking, as some reserves restrict plant collection. Do not pick from inaccessible cliff faces; the risk of injury is not worth it.

---

## 85. Dulse

**Species Name:** Dulse
**Latin Name:** Palmaria palmata
**Type:** Coastal & Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** dulse
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Dulse is a red seaweed found on rocky shores around Britain and Ireland. It has been eaten for centuries on the Atlantic coast, particularly in Ireland, Iceland, and maritime Canada. The flavour is intensely savoury — sometimes described as the closest thing the sea has to bacon — and it dries well, which extends its use considerably.

---

**Full Description:**
Dulse grows attached to rocks and to the holdfasts of larger kelps in the lower intertidal and shallow subtidal zone. On Atlantic-facing coasts with good tidal exposure — Ireland, the Hebrides, Orkney, the west coast of Wales — it is common and often abundant. On sheltered east coast shores it is less frequently found.

It has a long food history on both sides of the North Atlantic. In Ireland it was eaten raw as a field snack, dried for winter use, and sold dried in markets well into the twentieth century. In Iceland it has been eaten continuously for over a thousand years. In parts of Atlantic Canada, particularly Nova Scotia, it is still harvested commercially and sold as a snack food.

The flavour is strong: salty, deeply umami, with a mineral marine quality. Dried and fried briefly in a dry pan, the texture becomes crisp and the flavour develops a smokiness that earns the bacon comparison. Fresh, it is chewier and more intensely marine.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A red to dark purple-red seaweed with a fan-shaped or irregularly lobed frond, 20–50cm long and 2–8cm wide. The frond is flat, membranous but leathery, and smooth on both surfaces. It attaches to its substrate by a small, disc-shaped holdfast. Colour is a reliable guide in the field: fresh dulse is deep red to purple-red, turning dark brown when dried and reverting to red when rehydrated. It is often found growing on the stipes of oar weed (*Laminaria digitata*) and other kelps, as well as on bare rock in the lower shore. The broad, flat, red frond without ribs or midrib distinguishes it from many other red seaweeds. *Dilsea carnosa* looks similar but has a thicker, more fleshy frond and a rounder shape.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic seaweeds grow on British rocky shores. The main concern with all foraged seaweed is environmental: do not pick near sewage outfalls, industrial discharge points, or in areas with water quality advisories. The Food Standards Agency advises checking local water quality before picking any seaweed for consumption.

*Dilsea carnosa*, sometimes called sea dulse, resembles dulse but is thicker and less lobed. It is edible and can be used in the same ways.

Some red seaweeds in the genus *Porphyra* are flat and membranous but thinner and more translucent — these are laver (see entry 87) and are also edible.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Rocky shores in the lower intertidal zone, particularly on exposed and semi-exposed coasts. Most abundant on the Atlantic-facing coasts of Ireland, Scotland, Wales, and Cornwall. Often found growing on the stipes of large brown kelps. Also found in rockpools and on rock surfaces exposed only at the lowest spring tides. Tolerates high wave exposure. Look during the lowest tides of the month, which expose the lower shore where dulse grows most densely.

---

**In Season:**
Present on the shore year-round, but at its best from April to September when growth is active and fronds are full-sized and firm. Winter fronds can be smaller and more tattered. Summer is the easiest time to find and identify it. Pick from clean, open ocean-facing coasts only.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Dried: spread on a rack in a warm, airy spot for 24–48 hours or in a low oven (50°C) until completely crisp. Store in an airtight container for weeks. Fresh: add to soups and stews in the last five minutes of cooking — it rehydrates quickly and softens. Fried: dry-fry dried dulse strips in a hot pan with no oil for 1–2 minutes until crisp; the flavour becomes smoky and intensely savoury. Crumbled over eggs, butter, or potato dishes. Dulse butter: blend dried and crumbled dulse with softened butter and salt — excellent on fish, steak, or bread. Soak briefly in cold water before use if the saltiness is too intense straight from the shore.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Seaweed collecting on the foreshore in England is subject to Crown Estate ownership of the intertidal zone. Personal quantities can be collected without a licence. Commercial collection requires a licence. Some seaweed beds in Scotland are subject to specific harvesting management under the Seaweed Industry Act 2023 — check current guidance if collecting in Scotland.

---

## 86. Sea Lettuce

**Species Name:** Sea Lettuce
**Latin Name:** Ulva lactuca
**Type:** Coastal & Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** sea-lettuce
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Sea lettuce is the bright green, lettuce-leaf seaweed found on rocky shores across Britain. It is one of the easiest seaweeds to identify — nothing else on the shore looks quite like it — and one of the most versatile to cook with. The flavour is clean, salty, and distinctly marine.

---

**Full Description:**
Sea lettuce is among the commonest seaweeds in Britain and grows on rocky shores from Cornwall to Shetland. Its vivid, translucent green is immediately visible among the rocks at low tide, and the broad, irregular fronds genuinely do resemble wet lettuce leaves.

The plant is tolerant of a wide range of conditions and is often most abundant in areas of moderate nutrient enrichment — around seabird colonies, near stream outflows, and on shores with some freshwater input. This means it is widely accessible but also requires some care about where it is collected from.

As an introduction to seaweed cooking it is ideal. The flavour is mild enough to work as a vegetable in its own right, and it takes well to drying, frying, and light cooking. The Japanese equivalent — *Ulva* species are eaten throughout coastal Asia — gives some indication of its culinary range.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A bright to mid-green seaweed with broad, thin, irregularly shaped fronds of 10–50cm. The frond is two cells thick — held up to the light it is translucent. The surface is smooth and slightly crinkled at the margins. The holdfast is small and disc-shaped. Colour is the most immediate identifier: no other common British seaweed is this shade of vivid green combined with the broad, flat, translucent frond shape. Gut weed (*Ulva intestinalis*, formerly *Enteromorpha intestinalis*) is a close relative forming tangled, hollow, tubular strands — green, but clearly tubular rather than flat. Also edible.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic seaweeds occur on British shores. The only concern is collection location: do not pick sea lettuce near sewage outfalls, farm runoff, or in areas under water quality advisories. Sea lettuce concentrates nutrients and can accumulate bacteria in polluted water. The vivid green colour can be artificially intense near nutrient-polluted sites — this is a warning sign, not an attraction.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Rocky shores throughout Britain, from the upper intertidal zone down to the lower shore. Grows on rocks, in rockpools, and on other seaweeds. Also common on sheltered harbours and marina walls. Most abundant in mid-shore zones. Tolerates both fully exposed and sheltered coasts, and some freshwater dilution. Present year-round, most vigorous in spring and summer.

---

**In Season:**
April to September for the best quality. Present throughout the year but can be bleached and tattered by winter storms. Spring and early summer fronds are the freshest, most tender, and best-flavoured. Rinse well in clean seawater or fresh water after picking.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Fresh: rinse, chop, and add raw to salads — the salty, marine flavour works well with strong dressings. Dried: spread on a rack or baking sheet in a low oven (50°C) or warm dry room for 24 hours. Dried sea lettuce crumbles easily and can be stored for months. Use as a seasoning in the same way as nori flakes — on rice, eggs, or fish. Fried: fry dried sea lettuce briefly in hot oil until it crisps up; eat as a snack or crumble over food. Added to soups and broths: it takes only 2–3 minutes to soften in hot liquid. Sea lettuce tempura is excellent: dip fresh fronds in a light, cold batter and deep-fry immediately.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Foreshore seaweed collecting is subject to Crown Estate rules. Personal quantities do not require a licence. Some intertidal areas are designated nature reserves with restrictions — check local reserve rules. Water quality should be verified before picking from any new site; the Environment Agency's Bathing Water Quality data is a useful starting point for coastal areas.

---

## 87. Laver

**Species Name:** Laver
**Latin Name:** Porphyra umbilicalis / Porphyra linearis
**Type:** Coastal & Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** laver
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Laver is the seaweed behind laverbread, the traditional Welsh preparation that has been a part of south Wales cooking for centuries. Thin, dark purple-green fronds grow on exposed rocks on the middle shore. The flavour is intensely marine and mineral. It requires long cooking to make the most of it.

---

**Full Description:**
Laver is the British equivalent of nori, the Japanese seaweed used to wrap sushi. They are close relatives — both in the genus *Porphyra* — and laver can be used dried as a nori substitute, though British laver fronds are thinner and more fragile than the cultivated Japanese product.

The great British use of laver is laverbread (*bara lawr* in Welsh), which is neither bread nor cake but a thick, olive-black purée made by boiling laver for several hours until it collapses into a smooth, intensely savoury paste. Mixed with oatmeal and fried as cakes, or served on toast with bacon, it is one of the few traditional British seaweed preparations that has never really gone away.

Laver grows on exposed rocky shores throughout Britain, but the tradition of eating it is strongest in south Wales, where it was historically collected from the Gower, Pembrokeshire, and the Bristol Channel coasts.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A thin, membranous seaweed with irregular, rounded or strap-shaped fronds of 5–20cm, occasionally larger. Colour varies from olive-green through purple-brown to almost black, depending on exposure and age. The frond is one cell thick — extremely thin and fragile when wet. It attaches to rock by a small holdfast at the centre or margin of the frond. Grows on exposed mid-shore rocks, often where the shore is scoured by wave action. The extreme thinness of the frond — thinner than sea lettuce — combined with the dark reddish-purple colour distinguishes it in the field. Multiple *Porphyra* species occur in Britain and are not easily separated without microscopy; all are edible.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic seaweeds occur on British shores. Other thin, dark seaweeds could cause momentary confusion: *Dilsea carnosa* is thicker and more fleshy; *Grateloupia turuturu*, an introduced red seaweed, has longer, strap-shaped fronds — also edible. Sea lettuce (*Ulva lactuca*) is green rather than purple and two cells thick.

As with all foraged seaweed, the critical check is water quality at the collection site. Do not collect near sewage outfalls or in water quality advisory areas.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Exposed and semi-exposed rocky shores throughout Britain, from the middle intertidal zone downward. Most abundant on the wave-exposed Atlantic coasts of Wales, southwest England, Ireland, and Scotland. Grows on flat rock surfaces and boulders, often in areas of strong wave scour. Also found on the east coast but less common. Present year-round; most productive in late winter and spring when fronds are largest and most vigorous.

---

**In Season:**
Year-round, but at its best from late autumn through spring. Winter and early spring produce the largest, freshest fronds on exposed coasts. Summer fronds can be bleached and tattered in strong sunlight. Collect from clean, open coasts during low spring tides when the middle and lower shore is exposed.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Laverbread: wash laver thoroughly, then simmer in a covered pan with a small amount of water for 3–5 hours, stirring occasionally, until reduced to a thick, dark purée. Season with lemon juice and salt. Refrigerate for up to a week or freeze. Laverbread cakes: mix the purée with fine oatmeal, shape into patties, and fry in bacon fat or butter until crisp on both sides. Serve with bacon and cockles — the traditional Welsh breakfast. Laver sauce: stir laverbread into a butter sauce for lamb or fish. Dried laver: dry flat on a rack until crisp, then crumble or cut into sheets and use as nori.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Foreshore collecting is subject to Crown Estate rules. Personal quantities do not require a licence. Commercial laver collection in Wales has a long history and some traditional rights exist; check with Natural Resources Wales for current guidance. Water quality checks are essential before collecting from any new site.

---

## 88. Orache

**Species Name:** Orache
**Latin Name:** Atriplex patula / Atriplex hastata / Atriplex littoralis
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** orache
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Orache is a coastal and saltmarsh plant closely related to fat hen, with similar edible qualities. The leaves are mildly salty from their coastal habitat and cook exactly like spinach. Once common in British kitchen gardens, it fell out of favour centuries ago. As a foraged green it is underrated.

---

**Full Description:**
Several *Atriplex* species grow in coastal habitats in Britain and are collectively called orache. The most commonly encountered are common orache (*A. patula*), spear-leaved orache (*A. hastata*), and grass-leaved orache (*A. littoralis*). All are edible, and distinguishing between them is less important than finding and using them.

Orache is closely related to fat hen (*Chenopodium album*) and to cultivated spinach (*Spinacia oleracea*) — all members of the same family. The flavour is mild and slightly saline, and the cooking properties are similar: brief wilting reduces the volume considerably and produces a soft, pleasant green. Young leaves are best; older leaves become tougher and more fibrous.

The plant was once grown as a vegetable in British kitchen gardens. John Evelyn included it in his 1699 salad book *Acetaria*. Its decline as a cultivated plant reflects the rise of other vegetables rather than any failing of its own.

---

**Identification Notes:**
An upright annual, 30–100cm. *A. patula* and *A. hastata* have arrow- to spear-shaped leaves of 2–8cm, often with a slightly mealy or waxy surface that gives them a dull, slightly dusty appearance. *A. littoralis* has much narrower, strap-shaped leaves. All produce small, inconspicuous flowers in dense spikes, green to reddish, from July onwards. The fruiting bracts — two small, flattened triangular structures enclosing each seed — are a key identification feature, though present only later in the season. The plant typically grows in coastal and disturbed ground. The mealy texture of the leaf surface and the arrowhead or spear-shaped leaf are reliable field characters.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Fat hen (*Chenopodium album*) is closely related, also edible, and grows in similar disturbed ground habitats. The leaves are rounder and less arrow-shaped, and the mealy surface is even more pronounced. No practical concern about confusing the two — both are edible.

Orache belongs to the same family as glasswort and sea purslane, none of which are dangerous.

The main concern is location. Coastal roadsides and waste ground can be contaminated with heavy metals, particularly lead and cadmium, from vehicle exhaust and industrial run-off. Pick from clean coastal habitats, not from verges of busy roads or near industrial sites.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Sea shores, saltmarshes, coastal shingle, clifftops, and disturbed coastal ground throughout Britain. *A. littoralis* is specifically coastal; *A. patula* and *A. hastata* also grow inland on waste ground, field margins, and disturbed soils. Most abundant in late summer. Often grows alongside sea purslane, glasswort, and other saltmarsh and coastal plants.

---

**In Season:**
Young growth from June; plants are at their best in July and August when actively growing. Pick young leaves from the top of growing shoots. By September most plants are flowering and setting seed, and leaf quality declines. The young shoots of *A. hastata* in June are particularly good.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use in exactly the same way as spinach. Wilt in a pan with butter for 2–3 minutes until collapsed — the mild saltiness means little or no added salt is needed. Orache and egg: wilt, chop roughly, and stir through scrambled eggs. Added to soups and broths in the last few minutes of cooking. Young leaves can be eaten raw in salads, where the mild brininess adds interest. Mix with sea purslane and samphire for a coastal green dish alongside fish.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Orache growing on the coast is typically on Crown Estate foreshore, public common, or local authority land. Picking for personal use is permitted. Do not pick from nature reserves without checking rules, as some saltmarsh habitats are specially protected. Plants on shingle beaches above the tideline may be on private land.

---

## 89. Sea Kale

**Species Name:** Sea Kale
**Latin Name:** Crambe maritima
**Type:** Coastal & Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sea-kale
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
Sea kale is a large, striking plant native to British shingle beaches. The blanched, forced shoots were a prized vegetable in Georgian and Victorian kitchen gardens. In the wild, the young spring shoots and flower buds are edible, and the flavour is genuinely impressive. The plant is protected and picking must be done with care.

---

**Full Description:**
Sea kale is one of Britain's most distinctive native coastal plants. It grows on exposed shingle and sandy beaches, its blue-grey waxy leaves spreading in large clumps among the stones. In April and May the new shoots push up through the shingle, pale and blanched where they have been shaded by the pebbles, darkening as they reach light.

The plant's culinary history is well-documented. In the late eighteenth century it was taken from coastal shingle and cultivated, then forced in the same way as chicory — grown in the dark to produce etiolated, tender, pale shoots. The forced shoots were a fashionable vegetable in Georgian and Regency cooking, served like asparagus. The fashion declined but the plant persisted, and the wild form remains on British shingle beaches.

Wild sea kale requires more restraint in picking than its cultivated descendant. The plant is native, on some shingle systems it is not abundant, and it is relatively slow to regenerate. Pick only where plants are numerous, take only a small number of shoots per plant, and leave the majority.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large, robust perennial, spreading to 60–90cm across. The leaves are 20–40cm, deeply lobed and wavy-edged, thick and fleshy, with a distinctive blue-grey-green colour from a waxy bloom. The stems are stout and solid. Flowers appear in May to July: dense, rounded heads of small, white, four-petalled flowers with a strong, sweet honey-like scent, 40–50cm tall. The seeds are enclosed in a spherical, corky pod about 1cm across. Growing on coastal shingle or sand, often half-buried in the stones, with no other British plant combining the blue-grey waxy leaves, shingle habitat, and white flower heads.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Sea kale belongs to the cabbage family (Brassicaceae) and has no dangerous lookalikes in its shingle beach habitat. Other large coastal plants — sea holly (*Eryngium maritimum*), sea beet (*Beta vulgaris* subsp. *maritima*) — look nothing like it. The combination of habitat, leaf colour, and growth habit is specific.

Sea holly (*Eryngium maritimum*) grows on the same shingle beaches but has spiny, thistle-like leaves and blue flower heads. Not dangerous; not edible.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Shingle beaches and sandy shores around the coast of Britain, from Cornwall to Scotland. Most sites are on the south and east coasts of England. Also found on the coasts of Wales and Ireland. The largest and most robust colonies are on undisturbed shingle systems such as Chesil Beach in Dorset, the Suffolk coast, and parts of the Norfolk coast. Rare and localised in Scotland.

---

**In Season:**
Young shoots from April to May, when they push up through the shingle and are at their most tender. Flower buds in May to June, before they open fully, are also edible and have a stronger cabbage-like flavour. Once the plant is in full flower the shoots toughen. The picking window is short — three to four weeks in a good spring. The plant is visible year-round by its large blue-grey leaves.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young shoots: steam or boil for 5–8 minutes, serve with butter and a little lemon — the flavour is mild, sweet, and nutty, closer to asparagus than cabbage. Blanch briefly and serve with hollandaise. Flower buds: cook as you would purple sprouting broccoli — steam or stir-fry for 3–4 minutes. The flavour is more pronounced and cabbage-like than the shoots. Avoid boiling for too long: the texture quickly becomes waterlogged. Sea kale is best kept simple — the flavour needs no embellishment.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Sea kale is listed as a Species of Principal Importance in England under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006. It is not a Schedule 8 species (which would make picking illegal), but its conservation status means restraint is essential. On some protected shingle systems, picking may be restricted by site management rules — check with the site manager or reserve authority before picking. Do not uproot the plant or dig the crowns; pick only aerial shoots and buds from plants in dense populations.

---

## 90. Winkle

**Species Name:** Winkle (Common Periwinkle)
**Latin Name:** Littorina littorea
**Type:** Fish & Shellfish
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** winkle
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec, Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr

---

**Short Description:**
Winkles are the small, dark sea snails found on rocky shores around Britain. They have been eaten for centuries — sold from street barrows in Victorian London, still available from seafood stalls at the seaside today. Collecting your own from a clean shore is straightforward. The eating takes patience.

---

**Full Description:**
The common periwinkle is one of the most abundant marine molluscs on British rocky shores. A single productive stretch of mid-shore rock can hold thousands of individuals. They graze on algae, particularly the green and brown films on rock surfaces, and are found from the uppermost splash zone down to the lower shore.

Winkles have been eaten in Britain since at least the Roman period, with shell middens indicating prehistoric consumption. In the nineteenth century they were a common street food in London and coastal towns, sold boiled and eaten with a pin to extract the meat from the shell. That tradition persists in seafood stalls and fishmongers, where they remain cheap and available.

The key requirement for eating any shellfish from the shore is water quality. Winkles filter-feed, and in poor water quality they can accumulate bacteria, viruses (including norovirus), and toxins. Always check that the area is classified for shellfish collection before picking.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A small, thick-shelled marine snail, 1.5–3cm when fully grown. The shell is tightly coiled, broadly conical, with a pointed apex and a smooth, rounded aperture. Colour is dark grey to black, occasionally banded with lighter grey or brown. The operculum — a hard, circular plate — seals the aperture when the animal withdraws. Found on rocky shores from the upper intertidal zone to the lower shore, often in dense aggregations in rockpools and on damp rocks. The small, dark, coiled shell on a rocky British shore is almost certainly a periwinkle. Flat periwinkle (*Littorina obtusata*) has a much flatter shell without a pointed apex, and is smaller; rough periwinkle (*Littorina saxatilis*) is similar in shape but smaller, rough-textured, and found higher up the shore. Both are edible, though considerably smaller.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous molluscs share the common periwinkle's habitat or appearance on British shores. The tightly coiled, dark shell with a pointed spire is specific to this group of snails.

The danger with winkles is not misidentification. It is water quality. Winkles from polluted water can cause norovirus, bacterial food poisoning, and in areas with harmful algal blooms, paralytic shellfish poisoning (PSP) — which causes rapid neurological symptoms including numbness, tingling, and in severe cases respiratory failure. Do not collect from water quality advisory areas, near sewage outfalls, or during active algal bloom warnings. Check the Food Standards Agency shellfish classification map before collecting from any new site.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Rocky shores throughout Britain, from sheltered estuaries to exposed headlands. Most abundant in the mid-shore zone among barnacles and limpets, and in rockpools. Also found on seaweed-covered rocks and in harbour walls and jetties. Absent from sandy and muddy shores where there is no rock surface to graze. Present year-round; most active in cooler months.

---

**In Season:**
Winkles are at their best from September to April, when water temperatures are lower and the shellfish are in better condition. Avoid picking in summer (May to August) as water temperatures rise — the traditional month-with-an-R rule reflects this, and the old guidance holds. Larger winkles tend to be in deeper water or on the lower shore; look below the main barnacle zone.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Rinse live winkles thoroughly in several changes of cold water. Leave in a bucket of clean, cold salted water for 30 minutes to purge. Boil in salted water for 3–4 minutes — no more, or they toughen. Eat with a pin or a winkle pick: insert into the opening, hook the operculum and pull, then extract the meat in one piece. Serve with bread and butter, malt vinegar, and white pepper — the traditional accompaniment. Winkle butter: extract the cooked meats, chop finely, and blend with softened butter, lemon, and parsley. Excellent on toast or tossed through pasta. The cooking liquor, strained, makes a decent shellfish stock for soups.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Collecting winkles from the foreshore for personal use is generally permitted in England, Scotland, and Wales. The foreshore is Crown Estate land; personal collection does not require a licence. Commercial collecting requires a permit. Some estuaries and inshore waters are designated shellfish harvesting areas with specific rules — check the Food Standards Agency shellfish classification register and local Inshore Fisheries and Conservation Authority (IFCA) rules before collecting from any unfamiliar site. Water quality classification is the most important check to make.

---

## 91. Mussel

**Species Name:** Mussel (Common Mussel)
**Latin Name:** Mytilus edulis
**Type:** Fish & Shellfish
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** mussel
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec, Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr

---

**Short Description:**
Mussels are one of the most accessible wild shellfish on British rocky shores, growing in dense beds on rocks, harbour walls, and pier supports around the coast. The flavour of a freshly picked, well-prepared mussel is considerably better than anything sold in a supermarket. Water quality is the critical variable. Check it every time.

---

**Full Description:**
Common mussels are filter feeders. They draw water through their bodies continuously, extracting plankton and organic particles. This means anything present in the surrounding water — bacteria, viruses, heavy metals, toxins from harmful algal blooms — can accumulate in the mussel's tissue. A mussel from a clean, open Atlantic shore is an excellent thing to eat. A mussel from a polluted harbour or near a sewage outfall can cause serious illness.

This is not a reason to avoid picking mussels. It is a reason to choose your site carefully and check water quality before you go. The Food Standards Agency classifies inshore shellfish harvesting waters and publishes this information publicly. Classified shellfish harvesting areas are monitored regularly. Picking from unclassified waters — a harbour wall, a marina, a sheltered urban bay — carries a risk that cannot be assessed without testing.

The mussels themselves, from a good site in season, are outstanding. Steamed open in a pan with white wine and shallots in five minutes. Baked with garlic butter. Added to a soup or chowder. The yield per mussel is modest, but they are free and abundant on productive shores.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A bivalve mollusc, 4–10cm long, with a smooth, elongated, roughly oval shell. The outer surface is dark blue-black to purple-black, often with a faintly ridged texture. The inner surface of the shell is pearlescent blue-white. Mussels attach to rock and hard substrate via byssus threads — a small bunch of tough, brown fibres extending from the narrow end of the shell. They grow in dense clusters, often in several overlapping layers. The combination of the blue-black elongated shell, the byssus threads, and the dense clustered growth habit is specific. Horse mussel (*Modiolus modiolus*) is much larger (up to 20cm), occurs in deeper offshore water, and is rarely found intertidally. Also edible.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic species resemble common mussels in British intertidal habitats.

The dangers with mussels are environmental, not misidentification.

Norovirus: mussels from sewage-contaminated water can carry norovirus, causing severe vomiting and diarrhoea within 12–48 hours of eating. Heat does not reliably destroy norovirus at normal cooking temperatures.

Paralytic shellfish poisoning (PSP): caused by toxins produced by certain species of harmful algae. These toxins accumulate in mussels and are not destroyed by cooking. Symptoms begin within 30 minutes — numbness and tingling of the lips and face, followed by progressive paralysis. Severe cases cause respiratory failure. The UK Food Standards Agency and local Inshore Fisheries and Conservation Authorities issue biotoxin alerts when harmful algal bloom conditions are detected. Check alerts before picking.

Diarrhoeic shellfish poisoning (DSP): from a different group of algal toxins. Causes severe diarrhoea, nausea, and vomiting within hours. Not usually fatal but extremely unpleasant.

Check the FSA shellfish classification map and current biotoxin alerts for your specific location before every picking trip. Do not pick from unclassified waters.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Rocky shores, harbour walls, pier supports, and any hard substrate in the intertidal and shallow subtidal zone around the British coast. Most abundant on exposed and semi-exposed coasts with good tidal flow. Dense mussel beds form on the mid to lower shore where wave action is moderate and tidal flushing is consistent. Also found on rope and net structures in aquaculture areas — do not pick from active fish farm infrastructure. Most productive wild sites are on open, Atlantic-facing coasts away from harbours and estuaries.

---

**In Season:**
September to April when water temperatures are lower and mussels are in better condition. The old rule — only eat shellfish in months with an R — reflects real seasonal biology. In summer, mussels spawn, lose condition, and the risk of harmful algal blooms is higher. The largest, best-conditioned mussels are found on the lower shore and in deeper water. Pick mussels at least 4cm long — smaller ones are immature and the yield is not worth the effort.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Rinse thoroughly and scrub the shells. Pull off the byssus threads (the beard) from each mussel — grip and pull toward the narrow end. Discard any mussels with cracked shells or that stay open when tapped. Steam: place in a wide pan over high heat with white wine, shallots, and a splash of water — they open in 3–4 minutes. Discard any that do not open. Serve with the cooking liquor and bread. Moules marinière is the classic; the variations are endless. Mussels with garlic butter: open by steaming, place on the half shell, top with garlic and parsley butter, grill for 2 minutes. Smoked mussels: steam open, remove from shells, cold-smoke for 20–30 minutes. Mussel chowder: steam open, remove from shells, add to a base of potato, onion, and stock, finish with cream. The cooking liquor should always be strained and used — it is deeply flavoured.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking mussels from the foreshore for personal use is generally permitted. The foreshore is Crown Estate land. Commercial harvesting requires a licence from the relevant Inshore Fisheries and Conservation Authority (IFCA). Some intertidal mussel beds are subject to private Several Orders — historic rights that grant exclusive picking rights to specific operators. These are marked on IFCA maps. Biotoxin and water quality compliance is a legal requirement for commercial operations and a practical necessity for personal picking.

---

## 92. Cockle

**Species Name:** Cockle (Common Cockle)
**Latin Name:** Cerastoderma edule
**Type:** Fish & Shellfish
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** cockle
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec, Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr

---

**Short Description:**
Cockles live in sand and gravel in sheltered bays and estuaries around Britain. They are among the cheapest and most available shellfish from a fishmonger, and picking your own from a good, clean beach is a straightforward afternoon. The same water quality rules that apply to mussels apply here without exception.

---

**Full Description:**
The common cockle is a bivalve shellfish of sandy and muddy intertidal flats, estuaries, and sheltered bays. It does not grip a rock; it burrows shallowly in the upper layer of sediment, usually at depths of 1–5cm. On a productive cockle beach — Gower, Morecambe Bay, the Thames Estuary, parts of Norfolk — the shells are visible at the surface in thousands.

Like all bivalves, cockles filter-feed. The same water quality concerns that apply to mussels apply to cockles: sewage contamination, bacterial load, harmful algal toxins, and heavy metal accumulation are all possible in polluted intertidal waters. Estuaries, where cockles are most abundant, are also the areas most likely to receive river runoff, agricultural drainage, and urban wastewater. Check classification status before picking from any site.

The cockle has a long history in British food culture. It is one of the defining flavours of the British seaside. Sold boiled from stalls with malt vinegar and white pepper, it is one of the simplest and most honest things you can eat. Freshly cooked from your own picking it is better still.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A round, ribbed bivalve, 2–5cm across. The shell is almost circular in outline, strongly ribbed with 22–28 prominent ribs radiating from the beak. Colour is cream to pale grey-brown, often stained darker by sediment. The inside of the shell is white. Cockles sit just below or at the surface of sand or muddy gravel in sheltered intertidal areas. The strongly ribbed, round, cream-coloured shell is unmistakable among British bivalves. Carpet shell clams (*Ruditapes decussatus*) have an oval, cross-hatched shell — quite different. Dog cockle (*Glycymeris glycymeris*) is larger, more oval, and found below the low-tide mark.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic bivalve resembles the common cockle in British intertidal sediments.

The dangers are the same as for mussels. Norovirus from sewage-contaminated water can cause severe illness. Paralytic shellfish poisoning (PSP) from harmful algal bloom toxins is not destroyed by cooking and can cause respiratory failure. Diarrhoeic shellfish poisoning (DSP) causes severe gastrointestinal illness. Check the FSA shellfish classification map and current biotoxin alerts before picking. Cockles from estuaries and sheltered bays — their preferred habitat — are at higher pollution risk than open-coast mussels.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Sandy and muddy intertidal flats in sheltered bays, estuaries, and tidal inlets around Britain. Most abundant in the large, sheltered estuaries of the Thames, Wash, Morecambe Bay, and the Gower coast of south Wales. Also common in sheltered inlets of Scotland and Ireland. Found from the mid-shore to the low-tide mark, usually in the upper 5cm of sediment. Look for the ribbed shells at or just below the surface — when the tide is out, productive beds are obvious from the density of shells lying on the surface.

---

**In Season:**
September to April, following the same seasonal logic as mussels. Avoid summer picking when water temperatures are higher, harmful algal bloom risk is elevated, and the shellfish have spawned and lost condition. The largest cockles are typically deeper in the sediment and further down the shore; smaller cockles dominate the upper shore.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Rinse thoroughly in several changes of cold water. Leave in a bucket of clean salted water for 30–60 minutes to purge sediment. Boil in salted water for 2–3 minutes until the shells open — do not overcook or they toughen. Remove from shells and eat with malt vinegar and white pepper. Cockle chowder: remove from shells, add to a base of potato, leek, and stock, finish with cream and parsley. Cockle omelette: fold shelled, cooked cockles into a simple herb omelette. Laverbread and cockles: a traditional Welsh pairing — serve cockles with laverbread cakes and bacon. Do not try to eat cockles raw; always cook briefly. Discard any shells that stay firmly closed after cooking.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking cockles for personal use from public intertidal areas is generally permitted. Some of Britain's most productive cockle beds — Morecambe Bay, the Gower, the Burry Inlet — are subject to IFCA management orders and may have seasonal restrictions or picking limits. The Burry Inlet cockle fishery is a designated shellfish harvesting area with specific regulations. Check the relevant IFCA for the area before picking. Commercial cockle picking requires a licence. Water quality compliance is essential.

---

## 93. Bladderwrack

**Species Name:** Bladderwrack
**Latin Name:** Fucus vesiculosus
**Type:** Coastal & Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** bladderwrack
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Bladderwrack is the brown seaweed with paired, air-filled bladders that most people squeezed as children on British beaches. It is one of the most common seaweeds in Britain. The young growing tips have a mild, salty, marine flavour and a mucilaginous texture that works well in stocks and broths. The dried form is useful as a seasoning.

---

**Full Description:**
Bladderwrack is one of the most abundant seaweeds on British rocky shores. It dominates the mid-shore zone on sheltered and semi-exposed coasts, forming dense beds that can cover entire rock surfaces. The paired air bladders that give it its name are immediately recognisable.

As a food, bladderwrack is not eaten in the way that dulse or sea lettuce are eaten. The texture of the mature frond is leathery and the flavour is strongly marine and slightly astringent. The young tips, however, are more tender and mild, and have been used in stocks, broths, and as a wrapping for baked fish. Dried and ground, bladderwrack adds a clean, saline, marine note to food without dominating.

The mucilaginous quality of bladderwrack — from its alginic acid content — is a characteristic you either find useful (as a thickener in soups) or unappealing (slippery texture in cooked dishes). The Japanese use of kombu, a related kelp, is a useful reference point for how to think about it.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A brown to olive-brown seaweed with flat, strap-shaped, repeatedly forked fronds of 15–100cm. The frond has a distinct midrib running its full length. The paired, oval air bladders are usually obvious, occurring regularly along the frond on either side of the midrib. Bladders are 0.5–1.5cm, roughly oval, and pop satisfyingly underfoot. The tips of fertile fronds are swollen and warty-looking when ripe. Spiral wrack (*Fucus spiralis*) is very similar but lacks air bladders and has slightly twisted fronds — also edible. Toothed wrack (*Fucus serratus*) lacks bladders and has a distinctly serrated frond margin — also edible. The paired bladders on a midribbed brown frond are specific to bladderwrack.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic seaweeds occur on British shores. Other *Fucus* species are all edible or harmless.

The main concern, as with all foraged seaweed, is collection location. Do not pick near sewage outfalls, industrial discharge, or harbours with high boat traffic (antifouling paints can contaminate seaweed in marina environments). Bladderwrack has a relatively high natural iodine content. Eating large quantities regularly is not recommended — excessive iodine intake can affect thyroid function. Use it as a flavouring and seasoning rather than a bulk vegetable.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Mid-shore rocky coasts throughout Britain, from sheltered estuaries to moderately exposed headlands. Absent from very exposed shores where wave action is too strong. Most abundant on sheltered, rocky coasts and in sea lochs. Often forms dense belts of monoculture on productive mid-shore rock. Present year-round. Young growing tips are most accessible in spring and summer when the plant is actively growing.

---

**In Season:**
Year-round, but young growth in spring and early summer is most tender and least astringent. Avoid picking from shores after heavy storms, when fronds may be stressed and sediment-coated. Rinse well in clean seawater immediately after picking.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Stock: simmer a handful of young tips in cold water for 20–30 minutes to produce a clean, saline stock for soups and risottos. Do not boil hard — this extracts bitter compounds. Fish baked in bladderwrack: wrap a whole fish in wet bladderwrack fronds and bake at 180°C for 25–30 minutes; the steam and marine flavour penetrate the fish as it cooks. Dried seasoning: dry young tips in a low oven (50°C) for several hours until crisp, then grind to a powder and use as a pinch of seasoning on fish, eggs, or potato dishes. Seaweed bath: large quantities of bladderwrack boiled in a bath produce a traditional Scottish preparation said to ease joint pain — outside the scope of cooking, but worth knowing it exists.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Foreshore seaweed collecting for personal use does not require a licence. Commercial collection is subject to Crown Estate rules and may require a permit. In Scotland the Marine (Scotland) Act 2010 and the Seaweed Industry Act 2023 set a framework for sustainable seaweed harvesting — check current guidance for commercial activity.

## 94. Carrageen

**Species Name:** Carrageen (Irish Moss)
**Latin Name:** Chondrus crispus
**Type:** Coastal & Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** carrageen
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Carrageen is a small, fan-shaped red seaweed found on rocky shores around Britain and Ireland. It has been used for centuries in Irish and Scottish coastal communities as a setting agent and a base for milk puddings. The dried seaweed releases a natural carrageenan gel when simmered in liquid, which sets firmly on cooling.

---

**Full Description:**
Carrageen — also called Irish moss — has a culinary history rooted in the Atlantic-facing communities of Ireland, the Hebrides, and western Scotland. The seaweed's ability to gel liquids on cooling made it a practical alternative to animal-derived setting agents in communities with reliable coastal access.

The carrageenan that carrageen releases is now widely used as a commercial food additive, extracted industrially from related species. The home forager's version is simpler: rinse, dry, and simmer in milk or water; strain and allow to set. The result is a delicate, clean-tasting panna cotta-like pudding.

The seaweed itself, fresh, has very little flavour — slightly marine, mild. The dried form has even less. Its value is the gelling property, not the taste. This makes it versatile for both sweet and savoury applications.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A small, tufted red seaweed, 7–15cm, with a flattened, fan-shaped or irregularly branched frond. The branching is dichotomous (forking repeatedly in pairs) with rounded, channel-shaped tips. Colour varies from greenish-yellow (in sun-exposed upper shore pools) through red-brown to deep purple-red in shaded lower shore habitats. The frond is translucent when held to the light, and the surface is smooth. It attaches to rock by a small disc. A similar species, *Mastocarpus stellatus* (false Irish moss), has more deeply channelled fronds with a crisped, rather than smooth, surface — also edible and often grows alongside carrageen. In practice the two are used interchangeably.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic seaweeds occur on British shores. Other small red seaweeds — pepper dulse (*Osmundea pinnatifida*), various *Ceramium* and *Calliblepharis* species — grow in similar positions but are all harmless.

Pepper dulse (*Osmundea pinnatifida*) is the most useful comparison: it has a very strong peppery flavour and distinctly pinnately branched (feather-like) fronds, quite unlike carrageen's fan shape. If you find a strongly flavoured small red seaweed on the lower shore, it is likely pepper dulse, which is also edible and useful as a seasoning.

As with all foraged seaweed, avoid polluted sites.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Rocky shores on the mid to lower shore, particularly in sheltered rockpools and on shaded rock surfaces. Most abundant on Atlantic-facing coasts: Ireland, the Hebrides, Orkney, Cornwall, Wales, and the west of Scotland. Also found on the east coast of Britain but less commonly. Grows just below and within the bladderwrack zone. Most easily collected during spring low tides when the lower shore is exposed.

---

**In Season:**
Present year-round. Best collected in spring and summer when growth is active and fronds are full. After collection, the seaweed is traditionally spread on grass or a clean surface and allowed to bleach and dry in the sun and wind over several days, which removes much of the colour and some of the marine smell. It can also be dried in a low oven. Store dried in an airtight container for months.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Carrageen milk pudding: rinse 7–10g dried carrageen, simmer in 600ml whole milk for 15–20 minutes, stirring occasionally. Strain through a sieve, pressing the seaweed to extract the gel. Add sugar and vanilla, pour into moulds, cool until set. The texture is similar to panna cotta but lighter. Savoury version: simmer in stock instead of milk, set in a mould, and serve turned out with dressing as a savouryjelly. Carrageen as a stock thickener: add a small piece of dried carrageen to a simmering stock to add body without adding flavour. Blancmange: add lemon zest and juice to the milk version for a citrus set pudding. Use 5–7g dried seaweed per 500ml liquid for a soft set; more for firmer results.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Foreshore seaweed collecting for personal use is permitted without a licence. In Ireland, carrageen has been commercially harvested for generations and specific local arrangements exist. In Scotland, the Seaweed Industry Act 2023 provides a framework for licensed commercial harvesting. Personal quantities require no permit in England, Scotland, or Wales.

## 95. Kelp

**Species Name:** Kelp (Oarweed)
**Latin Name:** Laminaria digitata
**Type:** Coastal & Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** kelp
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Kelp is the large, strap-like brown seaweed found on Britain's lower rocky shores and in shallow coastal waters. It is the backbone of the British subtidal ecosystem. As a food, the young stipe and blades are edible and useful — milder than many seaweeds, with a clean, saline depth that makes it an excellent stock base and a versatile culinary seaweed.

---

**Full Description:**
*Laminaria digitata*, oarweed, is the dominant kelp of Britain's lower intertidal and shallow subtidal rocky coasts. It forms dense underwater forests just below the low-tide mark, with fronds reaching 2m or more. At the lowest spring tides on exposed coasts, the upper fringe of the kelp forest is briefly uncovered, and this is the practical picking zone for the shore forager.

Kelp is the marine equivalent of a structural tree. Dozens of other species live on, in, and around kelp fronds — including dulse, which grows directly on the kelp stipe. The holdfasts harbour a community of invertebrates. Picking kelp should be done with this in mind: cut fronds, never pull them. Never remove the holdfast.

As a food, kelp is less immediately striking than dulse or laver. The flavour is milder, the texture more substantial. Its strength is in what it contributes to stocks and long-cooked dishes: a deep, saline, umami quality — the same contribution that kombu makes in Japanese dashi. It dries well and stores for a long time.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large brown seaweed with a smooth, flexible stipe (stem) of 30–100cm, oval in cross-section and darkening toward the base. The blade is 50–200cm long, divided into broad, strap-like lobes (digitately divided, like fingers). The surface is smooth and glossy, olive-brown to dark brown. The holdfast is a branching, root-like structure gripping the rock firmly. Sugar kelp (*Saccharina latissima*) has an undivided, frilly-edged blade — also edible and used in the same ways. Furbelows (*Saccorhiza polyschides*) has a distinctive warty, bulbous holdfast — also edible. The large size, smooth stipe, and finger-like divided blade distinguish *L. digitata* clearly on the lower shore.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic seaweeds occur on British shores. Other large brown kelps — sugar kelp, furbelows, forest kelp (*Laminaria hyperborea*) — are all edible and used in the same ways.

The practical concern is collection conditions. The lower shore, where kelp grows, is covered for most of the tidal cycle. Only the lowest spring tides expose the kelp fringe. These tides coincide with strong tidal flows and surge on exposed coasts. Be aware of the tidal state and do not get cut off. Kelp-covered rocks are extremely slippery — approach with care.

As with all foraged seaweed, avoid polluted sites.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Lower intertidal and shallow subtidal rocky coasts throughout Britain. Most accessible on coasts with large tidal ranges and exposed, clean shores — the coasts of Pembrokeshire, Cornwall, the Scottish mainland, Orkney, and Shetland. Rare on the sheltered shores of the Solent, Thames Estuary, and other low-energy coasts. Most productive picking during the low spring tides of March to September when the lowest tidal levels are exposed.

---

**In Season:**
Present year-round. Young fronds in spring are the most tender and mildest-flavoured. Autumn and winter fronds are larger and tougher. Collect stipe sections and young blade material; avoid old, tattered fronds. The stipe can be pickled or eaten as a vegetable; the blade is better dried or used in stocks.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Dashi-style stock: simmer a 15–20cm piece of dried kelp blade in cold water, bring slowly to just below the boil, then remove the kelp and use the stock as a base for soups and broths. Do not boil hard — it becomes bitter and slimy. Kelp seasoning: dry the blade completely in a low oven (50°C), then grind to a powder. Use as a pinch of seasoning in place of salt. Pickled kelp stipe: peel the outer layer from the stipe, slice thinly, blanch briefly, then pickle in rice vinegar with a little sugar and salt. The texture is firm and slightly crunchy. Braised kelp: cut blade into squares, simmer in a broth of soy, mirin, and stock for 20 minutes until tender. The flavour develops considerably with long cooking.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Foreshore and intertidal seaweed collecting for personal use is permitted without a licence. The Seaweed Industry Act 2023 in Scotland sets a licensing framework for commercial harvesting. The Crown Estate has licensing arrangements for commercial harvesting in England and Wales. Personal-scale picking from exposed rocky shores does not require a permit. Cut fronds rather than uprooting holdfasts — removing holdfasts destroys the substrate that other species depend on.

## 96. Burdock

**Species Name:** Burdock (Lesser Burdock)
**Latin Name:** Arctium minus
**Type:** Roots
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** burdock
**Seasons:** Oct, Nov, Dec, Jan, Feb, Mar

---

**Short Description:**
Burdock is the large-leaved plant of roadsides and woodland edges whose hooked seed heads stick to clothing and dog fur. The root of a first-year plant, dug in autumn or early spring, is a substantial, earthy vegetable with a mild, slightly sweet flavour. Burdock and dandelion root is one of Britain's oldest soft drinks. It is also one of the better root vegetables the countryside produces for free.

---

**Full Description:**
Burdock grows throughout Britain on disturbed ground, roadsides, hedgebanks, and woodland edges. The plant is biennial: in its first year it produces a large rosette of leaves and a long, fleshy taproot. In its second year it sends up a tall flowering stem, produces the familiar sticky bur seed heads, and then dies. The root is only good in the first year — once the plant has put its energy into flowering, the root becomes woody and hollow.

The large, heart-shaped leaves are immediately recognisable even before you know the plant. The burs — covered in tiny, recurved hooks — are the inspiration for Velcro, which George de Mestral developed after observing how burdock burs attached to his clothing.

The root is long and substantial: 30–60cm is not unusual in good soil, and it goes deep. Digging burdock root is real work. The reward is a vegetable that the Japanese call *gobo* and use extensively in their cooking — braised, stir-fried, pickled, added to soups. In Japanese cuisine it is a staple, not an afterthought.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large, robust biennial, 1–1.5m in the second year. First-year plants form a ground-level rosette of very large, heart-shaped to ovate leaves, 30–70cm long, with a pale, woolly underside and a solid, slightly hollow stalk. The upper surface is dull green and smooth. Second-year plants produce an erect, branched stem and rounded purple-flowered bur heads of 1.5–4cm. The burs are densely covered in hooked spines that attach to clothing and fur. The root is brown-skinned, white-fleshed, cylindrical, 1–2cm diameter, and can reach 60cm depth in loose soil. The very large heart-shaped leaves with woolly undersides and solid stalks distinguish first-year burdock from other large-leaved plants.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Foxglove (*Digitalis purpurea*) produces a first-year rosette that could in theory cause confusion, though the leaves are quite different on inspection — wrinkled, softly hairy, and much smaller. Foxglove leaves have no woolly underside and a different texture. Foxglove is toxic; ingestion causes cardiac arrhythmia and can be fatal. Confirm the woolly white underside and large size before digging anything.

Butterbur (*Petasites hybridus*) has similarly large leaves but they are rounder, the underside is distinctly grey-green rather than white-woolly, and the plant grows in wet habitats — riversides and damp meadows — rather than dry disturbed ground. Not dangerous; not the same plant.

Always check for woolly white leaf undersides and confirm a large, solid-stalked rosette on dry disturbed ground before digging.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, hedgebanks, waste ground, and woodland edges throughout Britain. Tolerates a wide range of soils but most productive on deep, well-drained, loamy soils where the taproot can develop fully. Common on chalk and limestone country roadsides. Avoid plants growing on roadside verges with high traffic: lead and other heavy metals accumulate in roots from vehicle exhaust and road runoff. Pick from hedgebanks and field edges away from busy roads.

---

**In Season:**
Dig first-year roots from October through to March, before the plant sends up its flowering stem. The easiest identification window is summer and early autumn, when the first-year rosettes are large and obvious — mark your site then, return to dig in autumn. Roots are best in October and November; they become harder to dig cleanly in frozen winter ground. Spring roots (February to March) are still good before the flowering stem begins to emerge.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Scrub the root thoroughly — do not peel unless the skin is very thick. Slice into matchsticks or rounds. Burdock oxidises quickly when cut; keep in water with a squeeze of lemon until ready to cook. Kinpira gobo: stir-fry matchstick-cut burdock and carrot in sesame oil, add soy sauce, mirin, and a little sugar, cook until tender and slightly caramelised. This Japanese preparation is the most effective way to cook burdock. Braised: slow-cook in stock with onion and thyme for 45–60 minutes until tender — earthy and satisfying. Burdock root tea: slice thinly, roast in a dry pan until lightly browned, simmer in water for 15 minutes. A reasonable herbal tea with a roasted, slightly coffee-like flavour. Dandelion and burdock: the traditional British soft drink uses the roots of both plants; recreating it at home involves a long simmered extraction and fermentation.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Uprooting plants requires landowner permission under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 — this applies to all plants, including burdock, on land you do not own. The right to pick leaves, fruit, and flowers from public land does not extend to digging roots. Always seek landowner permission before digging burdock root. In practice, on land where the plant is abundant and the owner is not present, most foragers use common sense — but the legal position is clear.

## 97. Horseradish

**Species Name:** Horseradish
**Latin Name:** Armoracia rusticana
**Type:** Roots
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** horseradish
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov, Feb, Mar, Apr

---

**Short Description:**
Horseradish grows wild throughout Britain as a naturalised escape from cultivation. The large, dock-like leaves are unmistakable once you know them, and the pungent smell of the freshly grated root is one of the most distinctive in British food. Dig the root in autumn or early spring. Freshly grated horseradish is a different thing entirely from the bottled version.

---

**Full Description:**
Horseradish is not native to Britain. It was introduced as a cultivated root vegetable, probably in the medieval period, and has since escaped widely from gardens and allotments across England and Wales. Once established in suitable ground it is effectively permanent, as even small fragments of root left in the soil will regenerate.

The plant is found on roadsides, rough grassland, hedgebanks, and waste ground, often in the vicinity of old habitation, gardens, and allotments. It is common enough in many areas to be an occasional weed.

The root's identity is never in doubt. Scratch the skin of any suspected root and smell it — the sharp, eye-watering, unmistakable smell of horseradish is one of the most specific olfactory tests in British foraging. Nothing else smells like it.

Freshly grated horseradish is considerably sharper and more aromatic than any commercial preparation. The heat compounds are volatile and dissipate quickly with air exposure and heat, which is why the bottled product is so mild by comparison. Use the freshly grated root quickly.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large, robust perennial, 50–120cm tall when flowering. The leaves are the key feature for identification: large, 30–50cm, broadly lance-shaped to oblong, with a crisped and toothed margin, smooth and bright green on the upper surface. The lower leaves are distinctly long-stalked and have a slightly furrowed midrib. Smaller upper leaves are stalkless and less toothed. Flowers are small, white, four-petalled, in branched clusters, June to August. The root is large, tapering, 20–50cm, pale brown-skinned, white-fleshed, and smells powerfully and unmistakably of horseradish when cut or scratched. The pungent smell of the cut root is the definitive test.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Dock (*Rumex* spp.) species have similar large, elongated leaves on waste ground but the leaves are not crisped at the margins, and cutting the root produces no horseradish smell — the smell test is decisive.

Great water dock (*Rumex hydrolapathum*) grows in wetland habitats rather than disturbed dry ground. Not dangerous.

No dangerous plant is likely to be confused with horseradish once the smell test is applied. Scratch and smell the root before digging anything.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, rough grassland, hedgebanks, and disturbed ground throughout England and Wales. Less common in Scotland. Often found near old gardens, allotments, and former habitation. Tolerates poor, compacted soils but roots are largest and easiest to dig in deep, fertile, well-drained ground. Avoid picking from busy roadside verges: the root accumulates heavy metals from road pollution.

---

**In Season:**
Dig roots in autumn (September to November) or early spring (February to April), before the plant puts energy into leaf growth. Autumn roots are at their most pungent. Spring roots are slightly milder. The leaves are useful as a guide to finding plants; mark the location when the large leaves are visible in summer, then return to dig in autumn.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Horseradish sauce: grate the raw, peeled root finely and mix immediately with creme fraiche or double cream, a little wine vinegar, mustard, and salt. Use quickly. The heat compounds oxidise on exposure to air, which is why commercial horseradish is so mild. Freshly grated root is eye-wateringly sharp — adjust quantity to taste. Serve with roast beef, smoked fish, or beetroot. Horseradish and apple: grate equal quantities of fresh horseradish and apple, dress with lemon juice and a little cream — excellent with smoked mackerel. Horseradish vinegar: grate into white wine vinegar and leave to infuse for a week; strain and use as a condiment. The root freezes well whole and can be grated from frozen, which preserves sharpness.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Horseradish is a naturalised non-native plant with no legal protection. Uprooting requires landowner permission, as with all plants — the right to pick applies to leaves and flowers, not roots. In practice, horseradish grows in locations where permission can often be sought informally. Do not uproot plants on SSSIs or nature reserves without specific permission.

## 98. Reedmace

**Species Name:** Reedmace
**Latin Name:** Typha latifolia
**Type:** Roots
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** reedmace
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
Reedmace is the tall, sausage-headed plant of pond and lake margins, often wrongly called bulrush. Almost every part is edible at some point in its growing season. The young spring shoots, the pollen, the green flower spikes, and the starchy root are all usable. It is one of the most productive wild plants in Britain by edible parts, though none of them are outstanding enough to make it a priority find.

---

**Full Description:**
Reedmace grows in standing and slow-moving water throughout Britain — ponds, lakes, canals, fen edges, and the margins of slow rivers. The familiar brown, cylindrical flower spike (the "sausage" or "cat's tail") makes it one of Britain's most recognisable wetland plants.

The number of edible parts is unusual. In early spring, the young shoots emerging from the base of the plant can be stripped of their outer leaves to reveal a pale, tender core — edible raw or cooked, with a mild, slightly sweet flavour reminiscent of cucumber. In late spring and early summer, the immature green female flower spike can be cooked and eaten like a miniature corn cob. In June, before the spike splits and releases pollen, the bright yellow pollen can be shaken or swept into a bag — it is rich and golden, useful as a flour substitute or colour in bread and pancakes. The starchy rootstock can be dug year-round and the starch extracted in water.

The catch is that this versatility requires knowing the seasonal window for each part, and none of the parts is transformative enough on its own to make reedmace a top-tier foraging target.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A tall, robust aquatic perennial, 1.5–2.5m. Leaves are flat, strap-shaped, grey-green, 1–2cm wide, erect, arising from the base. The distinctive seed head is cylindrical, 10–20cm long and 2–4cm in diameter — dark brown, velvety, and sausage-shaped. The female flower head (the brown sausage) sits below a shorter male flower head (which releases pollen in June). Great reedmace is by far the more common species in Britain. Lesser reedmace (*Typha angustifolia*) has narrower leaves and a noticeable gap between the male and female portions of the flower spike — also edible, less common. The tall, strap-leaved plant with the cylindrical brown sausage in a wetland margin is unmistakable in Britain.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Yellow flag iris (*Iris pseudacorus*) grows in similar wetland habitats and has strap-like leaves. The leaves are sword-shaped and strongly ridged in cross-section, quite unlike the flat, grey-green reedmace leaf. Yellow flag is toxic — all parts cause gastrointestinal irritation and the root sap can cause skin irritation. The absence of the cylindrical brown sausage and the ridged, sword-like leaf cross-section distinguish it clearly.

Sweet flag (*Acorus calamus*) has very similar strap-like leaves but smells strongly and pleasantly of oranges and spice when crushed — quite distinctive. Its flower is a narrow, angled, yellowish spike, not a round brown sausage.

Confirm the cylindrical brown sausage and the flat (not ridged) leaf cross-section before picking any wetland plant for eating.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Standing and slow-moving freshwater throughout Britain: ponds, lakes, canals, fens, and slow river margins. Most abundant in lowland England. Grows in water from the margin to depths of about 60cm. Often forms dense, monoculture stands at the edges of productive lakes and canals. Widespread and common except in upland and acidic water habitats.

---

**In Season:**
Young shoots: April to May, from the base of the plant at water level. Green female flower spike: May to June, before it turns brown. Pollen: June, when the male portion of the spike is yellow and shedding. Brown sausage for use as a torch or fire-starter: autumn, when dry. Root starch: year-round — dig roots in autumn or early spring for the highest starch content.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young shoots: peel back the outer leaves to the pale inner core, eat raw in strips (mild, slightly sweet, good in salads) or briefly stir-fried with butter. Immature flower spike: boil or steam for 5–10 minutes and eat with butter in the same way as corn on the cob. The flavour is mild and slightly starchy. Pollen: collect by shaking the ripe male spike into a bag; the bright yellow powder can replace up to half the flour in pancakes, bread, or flatbreads — it adds colour and a mild, sweet, nutty flavour. Root starch: pound or blend washed rootstock in water, allow the starch to settle, pour off the water, dry the starch — a fine white flour. Use in porridge or bread. This is time-consuming work for a modest yield.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Reedmace growing in standing water on public commons and nature reserves is often protected as part of a managed wetland habitat. Many reedmace stands are on SSSI land or in nature reserves. Check access and conservation status before picking. Uprooting rootstock requires landowner permission. The plant is not legally protected itself but its habitat frequently is.

## 99. Common Mallow

**Species Name:** Common Mallow
**Latin Name:** Malva sylvestris
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** common-mallow
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Common mallow grows on roadsides, waste ground, and coastal banks throughout Britain. The flowers, leaves, and seed pods are all edible, making it one of the most completely usable wild plants of summer. The flowers are the most useful part — a vivid pink-purple with dark veins, they make a striking natural food colouring.

---

**Full Description:**
Common mallow is a large, conspicuous plant of disturbed and waste ground, roadsides, and coastal banks. In summer its pink-purple flowers are immediately visible from a distance, and it often grows in dense patches.

The whole plant has been eaten for centuries. Roman writers recommended it; John Evelyn listed it as a salad plant. In North Africa and the Middle East, the leaves are still widely cooked as a pot herb. In Britain its use declined as other vegetables became available, but as a foraged plant it remains excellent.

The leaves, particularly young ones, have a mild, slightly bland flavour with a slightly mucilaginous texture. They are best cooked rather than eaten raw in quantity. The flowers are more useful: vivid, edible, and capable of turning liquid a striking purple-grey — they react with acid (lemon juice, vinegar) to produce a vivid lilac-pink, which is one of the more genuinely useful natural food colouring effects in British foraging. The unripe seed pods, called "cheeses" for their shape, are edible raw — mild, slightly sweet, and satisfyingly snackable straight from the plant.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A branching, sometimes sprawling perennial or biennial, 30–90cm. Stems are hairy. Leaves are 5–10cm, rounded to kidney-shaped with five to seven shallow, rounded lobes and a toothed margin. All leaves are hairy on both surfaces. Flowers are 2.5–4cm across, with five notched petals of bright pink-purple with deep purple veins, and a central column of stamens. The calyx (outer sepals) is green and star-shaped. Seed pods (the "cheeses") are flat, disc-shaped, and divided into 10–12 segments, 1–1.5cm across, ripening from green to brown. The vivid pink-purple flowers with dark veins and notched petals are specific. Musk mallow (*Malva moschata*) has deeply divided, feathery leaves and paler pink flowers — also edible.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Hollyhock (*Alcea rosea*) is a tall garden plant that can escape onto waste ground. The flowers are similar in structure and colour to mallow. It is also edible — in the same family — and not dangerous.

Marsh mallow (*Althaea officinalis*) is a coastal and wetland plant with softer, more velvety leaves and pale pink flowers — edible and closely related. It has a long history as a source of mucilaginous root extract.

No dangerous plants closely resemble common mallow in its habitat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, disturbed ground, waste land, coastal banks, and the edges of cultivated fields throughout England and Wales. Less common in Scotland. Most abundant on dry, well-drained soils in sunny, south-facing positions. Particularly common near old buildings, along sea walls, and on disturbed coastal ground. Often grows in dense patches once established.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers and young leaves from June through September. The picking season is long and the plant flowers continuously through summer. Flowers are best picked in the morning before they fully open for the day. Young leaves from new growth at the top of the plant are the most tender throughout the season. Seed pods form from August and are good raw when still green and fresh.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Flowers: use in salads as a vivid garnish. Steep in hot water for 10 minutes to make a greyish-purple tisane — add lemon juice and it turns lilac-pink. This colour reaction makes mallow a useful natural food dye. Crystallised flowers: brush with egg white, dust with caster sugar, and dry on a rack. Leaves: wilt briefly in butter, use as a mild pot herb in the same way as spinach. Good as an addition to soups and stews. The mucilaginous texture thickens soups slightly. "Cheeses" (seed pods): eat raw directly from the plant — mild, green, and satisfying as a trail snack. Mallow leaf soup: wilt leaves in olive oil with onion and garlic, add stock, blend, finish with lemon. Simple and good.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Common mallow growing on public land can be picked for personal use under the right to collect plants. It grows on roadsides and waste ground — urban and semi-urban foraging is usually straightforward. Avoid plants from heavily trafficked urban verges and former industrial land, where soil contamination is possible. No legal protection applies to common mallow.

## 100. Yarrow

**Species Name:** Yarrow
**Latin Name:** Achillea millefolium
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** yarrow
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Yarrow grows in grassland, roadsides, and meadows throughout Britain. The feathery, strongly aromatic leaves and flat-topped white flower heads make it easy to identify once known. The flavour is distinctive and assertive — bitter, herbal, slightly peppery — which limits the quantity that works well in food. Use it as a herb, not a vegetable.

---

**Full Description:**
Yarrow is one of the most common grassland plants in Britain. It grows on roadsides, meadows, lawns, and dry banks from Cornwall to Shetland, producing its flat-topped heads of small white flowers from June through to September. Its feathery, finely divided leaves are distinctive and have a strong, characteristic smell — somewhere between camomile and a bitter herbal tea — when crushed.

The plant has an enormous history in European and worldwide traditional use. Its Latin name references Achilles, who supposedly used it to staunch wounds at Troy. This points to its long association with both food and medicine, though as a food plant it functions primarily as a flavouring herb rather than a significant source of nutrition.

Young leaves in spring are the most useful part. They have a slightly less bitter character than mature leaves and can be used as a herb in salads, omelettes, and soft cheeses. The flavour is strong enough that a small quantity makes a clear impression — treat it like a culinary herb with character rather than a mild salad green.

Note on safety: the CSV master list flags yarrow as requiring Latin name verification. *Achillea millefolium* is the correct and widely accepted name for the common British species, though the taxon is complex with several microspecies. For foraging purposes, the common form in lowland Britain is *A. millefolium* in the conventional sense.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A creeping perennial with erect flowering stems to 60cm. Leaves are 5–15cm, alternate, and very finely divided — two to three times pinnately cut into tiny, narrow segments, giving a feathery or ferny appearance. The leaf surface is slightly hairy. Crush a leaf: the smell is immediately distinctive, aromatic and slightly medicinal. Flower heads are flat-topped compound clusters (corymbs) of small individual white (occasionally pink) flowers, 2–5mm across. The outer ray florets have small white petals; the inner disc florets are cream. Stems are angular. The very finely divided, feathery leaf and the flat-topped white flower cluster in grassland are together specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
This is where care is required. Yarrow belongs to the daisy family (Asteraceae), not the carrot family, but the finely divided leaf can — to an inattentive eye — suggest the leaves of some Apiaceae members in the same habitat.

Fool's parsley (*Aethusa cynapium*) has finely divided leaves but it is a member of the carrot family (Apiaceae), with an umbellifer flower structure quite unlike yarrow's flat-topped daisy-family clusters. Fool's parsley smells unpleasant when crushed; yarrow smells distinctively aromatic and pleasant. Fool's parsley can cause gastrointestinal poisoning.

Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) has finely divided leaves and grows on roadsides and waste ground — the same habitat as yarrow. Hemlock is tall (up to 2.5m), has purple-blotched hollow stems, and smells strongly and unpleasantly of mice when crushed. The smell of crushed yarrow is completely different — aromatic and herbal. Hemlock causes respiratory failure and death.

Crush the leaf before picking anything. If the smell is not immediately and distinctly yarrow — herbal, aromatic, slightly medicinal — do not pick it.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Grassland, roadsides, meadows, lawns, heathland edges, and dry banks throughout Britain. One of the most common and widespread plants in the country. Thrives on well-drained soils in full sun. Found at altitude on upland grassland as well as at sea level. Absent from very wet, acidic, or heavily shaded habitats.

---

**In Season:**
Young leaves from April, when spring growth begins. Best picked in April and May before the plant flowers — young leaves are milder and more tender. The plant continues to produce new growth through summer but mature leaves are more bitter. Flowers from June to September. The flowering plant is easiest to identify confidently; if in doubt, wait for flowers before picking the leaves.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as a herb in small quantities. Chopped young leaves in an omelette: add one tablespoon of finely chopped yarrow to two eggs — the bitter, herbal flavour works with the egg in the same way sorrel does. Fresh soft cheese: stir finely chopped yarrow into goat's or sheep's curd with salt and a squeeze of lemon. Yarrow in salad: a few young leaves as a bitter element among milder salad leaves. Yarrow beer: one of the oldest British brewing herbs, used before hops became dominant. The leaves and flowers add bitterness and aroma to home-brewed ale. Yarrow flower tea: steep fresh or dried flower heads in boiling water for 5 minutes — a bitter, aromatic tea.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Yarrow is a common, unprotected plant growing abundantly on public land throughout Britain. Picking for personal use is permitted. It is not a Schedule 8 species. No legal restrictions apply to picking leaves or flowers from roadsides, commons, and public grassland.

---

## 101. Chickweed

**Species Name:** Chickweed
**Latin Name:** Stellaria media
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** chickweed
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Chickweed is one of the most common plants in Britain and one of the most overlooked. It grows year-round on cultivated ground, gardens, and disturbed soil, and the young shoots are a genuinely good salad green — mild, fresh, and slightly sweet. It is available when almost nothing else is, which makes it more valuable than its weedy reputation suggests.

---

**Full Description:**
Chickweed grows everywhere there is disturbed soil and some moisture: gardens, allotments, field margins, roadsides, and path edges. It is one of the first plants to colonise bare ground and one of the last to die back in cold weather. On mild winters it grows continuously. This makes it the most consistently available wild green in Britain — present when nettles are dormant and wild garlic is weeks away.

The flavour is mild and pleasant, with a fresh, slightly sweet quality that works well raw in salads. It is not a strong-flavoured green; its value is accessibility and availability. A handful of chickweed added to a winter salad does exactly what a mild salad leaf should do.

The plant is named for its popularity with garden birds, who eat the seeds enthusiastically. This is a reliable indicator of edibility — if birds eat it in quantity, it is almost certainly harmless — though not a substitute for proper identification.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low-growing, sprawling annual, 5–40cm. Stems are slender, weak, and much-branched, with a single line of fine hairs running along one side of each internode — this alternating line of hairs, which switches sides at each node, is one of the most reliable identification features. Leaves are 5–25mm, oval, smooth, bright green, and opposite, with a pointed tip. The lower leaves have a short stalk; upper leaves are stalkless. Flowers are tiny (5–8mm), white, with five deeply notched petals that look like ten at first glance. The deeply notched petals are distinctive. Fruit is a small, oval capsule splitting into six parts. The combination of the single hair line on the stem, opposite oval leaves, and deeply notched white petals is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Scarlet pimpernel (*Anagallis arvensis*) grows in similar disturbed habitats. The leaves are similar in shape and size but arranged in opposite pairs with no stalk and often with small dark spots on the underside. The flowers are small and orange-red. Scarlet pimpernel causes gastrointestinal irritation. Check for the single hair line on chickweed's stem and the deeply notched white petals — scarlet pimpernel has unnotched petals and very different flower colour.

Mouse-ear chickweeds (*Cerastium* spp.) look very similar and grow in the same habitats. They are hairy all over — not just in a single line — and the leaves are slightly hairy and greyish-green. They are not toxic but significantly less good to eat.

Common mouse-ear (*Cerastium fontanum*) has hairy stems and leaves throughout, unlike chickweed's single hair line. Confirm the alternating hair line before picking in quantity.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Cultivated and disturbed ground throughout Britain: gardens, allotments, field margins, path edges, and waste ground. Most productive on fertile, moist, well-worked soil. Absent from very dry or acidic habitats. Found at all altitudes but most abundant in lowland agricultural and garden settings. Grows year-round in mild conditions.

---

**In Season:**
At its best from October through April, when the young shoots are fresh and the weather is cool. Summer chickweed on dry soil is often straggly and less good. The plant produces fresh growth continuously throughout mild winters and is one of very few wild salad plants available in January and February. Pick the top few centimetres of young shoots — the stems become stringy lower down.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw in salads: the most straightforward use. Young chickweed shoots work as a mild salad base or addition alongside stronger-flavoured leaves. Chickweed pesto: blend with pine nuts, olive oil, Parmesan, and a little garlic — milder than wild garlic pesto but fresh and good. Wilted: briefly stir-fried or wilted in butter for 1–2 minutes as a side vegetable — the volume reduces dramatically. Added to soups in the last minute of cooking. Chickweed sandwiches: a Victorian preparation, and not as eccentric as it sounds — the mild flavour and slight crunch work well with butter and good bread. Wash thoroughly before eating raw; the stems trap soil.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Chickweed on public land can be picked freely for personal use. It is an unprotected common weed with no legal restrictions. On allotments and gardens it is technically on private land — pick only from your own plot or with permission.

## 102. Cleavers

**Species Name:** Cleavers
**Latin Name:** Galium aparine
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** cleavers
**Seasons:** Feb, Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Cleavers is the sticky, scrambling plant of hedgerows and woodland edges that attaches itself to clothing, dogs, and anything else that passes. The young spring shoots, before they develop their hooks, are a useful wild green. They are also easily made into a cold-pressed juice — one of the most genuinely functional wild plants to use in this way.

---

**Full Description:**
Cleavers is one of Britain's most familiar plants without most people knowing its name. It is the plant children throw at each other's jumpers; the one that arrives in bundles on the dog's coat after a hedgerow walk. The tiny hooks covering stem, leaf, and seed all serve the same purpose: hitching a ride on passing animals to disperse seeds.

The young shoots in early spring, before the hooks fully develop, are edible and usable. The window is February to April, when the tips are soft and the sticky quality is minimal. By May the plant is tall, robust, and thoroughly hooked — at this point it is unpleasant to eat and the picking window has closed.

The most practical culinary use is juicing. Cleavers can be blended or pressed and the resulting green juice is mild, fresh, and distinctly drinkable as a spring tonic. The seeds, roasted, make a reasonable caffeine-free coffee substitute. The plant is not a culinary star — the flavour of cooked shoots is mild bordering on bland — but it is abundant, early, and genuinely edible.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A scrambling, weakly climbing annual, reaching 30–120cm when supported by other vegetation. Stems are square in cross-section and covered in tiny, backward-pointing hooks. Leaves are narrow, 1–5cm, lance-shaped, in whorls of six to eight around the stem, with a single midrib and hooks on the margins and underside midrib. Flowers are tiny, white, four-petalled, 1–2mm, in small clusters. Seeds are 2–5mm, round, and densely covered in hooks. The square, hooked stem with whorled leaves is specific. Sweet woodruff (*Galium odoratum*) has similar whorled leaves but is non-sticky, smells sweetly of vanilla when dried, and grows in shaded woodland rather than hedgerows.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plants share cleavers' distinctive square, hooked stem and whorled leaves in British hedgerows.

Hedge bedstraw (*Galium mollugo*) and lady's bedstraw (*Galium verum*) grow in similar habitats and have whorled leaves but are not particularly sticky and produce more prominent flowers. Both are edible and harmless.

Cleavers is one of the easier spring plants to identify: the sticky, hooky, square-stemmed, whorled-leaf plant scrambling through a February hedgerow has no dangerous equivalent in Britain.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, woodland edges, disturbed ground, and cultivated land throughout Britain. Most abundant on fertile, moist soils with some shade. A common and sometimes invasive weed of gardens and allotments. Grows through and over other vegetation, using its hooks to climb. Abundant from sea level to moderate altitudes.

---

**In Season:**
February to April for the young tips. Growth begins in late January in mild areas and the plant develops quickly through spring. By May most plants are too tall and too hooked to pick comfortably. Pick the top 5–10cm of young shoots from plants that have not yet started to climb extensively. The best picking is in March, when growth is active and tips are still soft.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Cold-pressed juice: place a large handful of young cleavers shoots in a blender with cold water. Blend thoroughly, strain through a fine cloth or sieve, and drink the resulting green liquid fresh. The flavour is mild and fresh. Cooking: wilt young shoots briefly in butter for 2 minutes — they collapse to almost nothing and the flavour is very mild. Best used as an addition to a spring green mixture rather than on their own. Cleavers infusion: steep a handful of fresh shoots in cold water overnight and drink as a spring drink — not a tea, as heat is not required. Roasted seeds: collect ripe seeds, roast in a dry pan until dark brown, grind and brew as a coffee substitute — weak but pleasant.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Cleavers is an unprotected common plant. Picking for personal use from public land, hedgerows, and roadsides is freely permitted. No legal restrictions apply.

## 103. Bittercress

**Species Name:** Hairy Bittercress
**Latin Name:** Cardamine hirsuta
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** bittercress
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr

---

**Short Description:**
Hairy bittercress is the tiny weed that appears in garden pots and flower beds from late winter. It is a member of the mustard family and has a genuinely good flavour — sharp, peppery, and clean, like a mild version of watercress. It is small, so picking in quantity takes patience, but it is one of the best raw wild greens available in January and February.

---

**Full Description:**
Hairy bittercress is one of the first edible plants to emerge after winter. It germinates in autumn and overwinters as a small rosette, then bolts quickly in late winter and early spring. Gardeners know it as a nuisance weed that fires its seeds explosively when touched — the ripe seed pods can scatter seeds over a metre.

The plant is easy to overlook because of its size: a typical rosette is 5–15cm across before bolting. But the flavour is disproportionate to the scale. A handful of bittercress leaves has genuine peppery sharpness — more assertive than chickweed, closer in character to watercress. It requires no cooking: the raw leaves are the product.

Wavy bittercress (*Cardamine flexuosa*) is a closely related and equally edible species, slightly larger and more common in shadier, wetter ground. The two are difficult to separate without close examination and are interchangeable as food. The master species list flags bittercress for Latin name verification on this basis — the entry covers *C. hirsuta* as the primary UK garden and disturbed-ground species, but *C. flexuosa* is equally valid and often co-occurring.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A small annual or biennial, 5–30cm when in flower. The basal rosette has pinnately divided leaves — a central stalk with 2–5 pairs of rounded leaflets and a larger terminal leaflet. The leaflets are 5–15mm, rounded to kidney-shaped, and slightly hairy. Stem leaves are similar but smaller. Flowers are tiny, white, four-petalled (classic mustard-family cross shape), 3–4mm across, in a slender raceme at the top of the stem. Seed pods are narrow, upright, 15–25mm long, and sit close against the stem — when ripe they burst open when touched. The small rosette with rounded pinnate leaflets, tiny white cross-shaped flowers, and upright narrow seed pods on bare or cultivated ground is specific. Wavy bittercress (*C. flexuosa*) has a slightly wavy stem (not perfectly straight), more stem leaves, and is more common in damp shade.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plants closely resemble bittercress in its typical garden and disturbed-ground habitat.

Shepherd's purse (*Capsella bursa-pastoris*) grows in the same habitat and has a similar rosette, but the leaves are more deeply lobed and the seed pods are triangular and heart-shaped — immediately distinct from the narrow, pencil-thin pods of bittercress. Shepherd's purse is edible and harmless.

Common whitlowgrass (*Erophila verna*) is tiny, with undivided leaves and deeply notched petals. Not dangerous; not the same.

The pinnate, rounded leaflets and the narrow upright seed pods distinguish bittercress from all realistic alternatives.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Cultivated ground, garden pots, flower beds, paths, and bare disturbed soil throughout Britain. A ubiquitous garden weed from sea level to moderate altitude. Also found on walls, rocky ground, and gravel. Wavy bittercress is more common in damp shade — woodland edges, stream banks, and shaded gardens. Both grow year-round but are most productive and best quality in late winter and early spring.

---

**In Season:**
Best from January to April, when the rosettes are young and before the plant has bolted fully. The peppery flavour is sharpest in cool weather. Once the plant has bolted and the seed pods are forming, the leaves become smaller and tougher. Pick entire rosettes or the top growth of young plants. Plants on allotments and in pots are often abundant — a few square metres of productive ground can yield a useful quantity.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw in salads: the primary use. The peppery flavour stands alongside stronger salad ingredients. Use as a substitute for watercress or rocket in any application — sandwiches, salads, garnishes. Bittercress and egg: scatter raw bittercress over a fried or poached egg; the residual heat wilts it slightly and the pepper note works well with yolk. Bittercress butter: blend finely chopped bittercress with softened butter and salt. Excellent on fish or stirred into mashed potato. Added to a spring herb omelette in the last 30 seconds of cooking. The flavour is lost with extended heat — keep cooking brief or use raw.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Hairy bittercress is a common, unprotected weed. Picking from public land and from your own garden or allotment is unrestricted. No legal protection applies.

---

## 104. Alexanders

**Species Name:** Alexanders
**Latin Name:** Smyrnium olusatrum
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** alexanders
**Seasons:** Feb, Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Alexanders is a large, strongly aromatic coastal plant that was the celery of the Roman world — cultivated and eaten across Britain before true celery became widely available. It disappeared from kitchen gardens centuries ago but thrives on coastal roadsides, clifftops, and banks around southern and eastern Britain. The stems, leaves, flower buds, and roots are all edible. The flavour is powerful, distinctive, and unlike anything in the modern vegetable canon.

---

**Full Description:**
Alexanders was a deliberate introduction by the Romans, who grew it as a kitchen vegetable throughout their empire. After the Romans left, it persisted as a cultivated plant into the medieval period, when it was used as a flavouring herb and pot vegetable. By the eighteenth century it had been superseded by cultivated celery and fell out of use. In the wild, however, it survived — and on coastal roadsides and clifftop banks in the south and east of England, it is still abundant in early spring.

The timing matters. Alexanders is a biennial or short-lived perennial that emerges and flowers early — February to May — and dies back by early summer. This makes it one of the most useful late-winter and early-spring wild plants, available when the foraging calendar is otherwise thin.

Every part of the plant has a use. The young leaf stems are the most accessible — cut and cooked like celery, they have a strong, resinous, celery-myrrh flavour that is assertive and unusual. Blanching in boiling water for a minute or two mellows the flavour considerably. The flower buds, before they open, work well in salads or briefly cooked as a vegetable. The roots of first-year plants can be dug and roasted.

The flavour divides people. It is not subtle. Those who like it tend to find it one of the most interesting and useful wild plants of the year. Those who do not will find the resinous quality overpowering. Start with the blanched stem before committing to a large quantity.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large, robust biennial or short-lived perennial, 50–150cm when in flower. Stems are thick, hollow, ribbed, and bright to yellow-green. Leaves are large and compound — divided into three broad, glossy, oval leaflets with slightly toothed margins, 3–8cm each. The whole plant is a distinctive yellow-green colour and smells powerfully when crushed: resinous, celery-like, slightly medicinal. Flowers are small, yellow-green, in compound umbels of 5–10cm across, from February to May. Seeds are large, ridged, and black when ripe. The shiny, yellow-green compound leaves with broad oval leaflets and the strong celery-myrrh smell are together unmistakable. No other British coastal umbellifer has this combination of yellow-green colouring and smell.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Alexanders is a member of the carrot family (Apiaceae), which contains deadly species. This requires careful attention.

Hemlock water-dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) is the most dangerous plant in Britain and grows in coastal and riparian habitats. It does not grow on the dry, well-drained coastal roadsides and clifftops where Alexanders thrives — it requires wet, marshy ground or stream edges. The leaves are more finely divided than Alexanders' broad oval leaflets, and it does not smell of celery-myrrh. Nonetheless, any umbellifer found growing in wet coastal ground near a stream or ditch should be treated with extreme caution.

Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) grows on disturbed coastal ground and roadsides — closer to Alexanders' habitat. Hemlock has finely divided, bright green, ferny leaves quite unlike Alexanders' broad, glossy leaflets, purple-blotched hollow stems, and smells powerfully of mice when crushed. Alexanders smells of celery-myrrh. The smell test is decisive.

Wild angelica (*Angelica sylvestris*) and ground elder (*Aegopodium podagraria*) are other coastal and hedgerow umbellifers, both edible, but neither matches the yellow-green colour and the strong specific smell of Alexanders.

Crush the leaf and confirm the powerful, distinctive celery-myrrh smell before picking any umbellifer. If the smell is absent or unpleasant, do not pick it.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Coastal roadsides, clifftop banks, hedgebanks, and disturbed coastal ground, usually within a few kilometres of the sea. Most abundant on the coasts of Kent, Essex, Suffolk, Norfolk, and Dorset on the east and south. Also found on the coasts of southwest England, Wales, and Ireland. Absent from Scotland except as a rare garden escape. Almost always on dry, well-drained soils in full or partial sun. Does not grow in wet ground.

---

**In Season:**
February to May, with the peak picking window in March and April when stems are still young and tender. By May the stems are toughening and the plant is coming into full flower. The flower buds just before opening are excellent in March. The stems of second-year plants are thicker and more useful than first-year plants. By June the plant has died back completely and is gone until the following February.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Stems: cut young stems into 5–8cm lengths. Blanch in boiling water for 1–2 minutes to mellow the resinous flavour, then finish by frying in butter with salt. The flavour has been described as celery crossed with myrrh — unusual, strong, and very good once you know what to expect. Flower buds: add raw to salads just before they open — they have a slightly milder flavour than the stems and a good texture. Alexanders soup: chop blanched stems and cook with potato, onion, and stock; blend. The flavour mellows considerably and the result is a distinctive, pale green soup. Roots: dig first-year roots in late winter, scrub, roast at 200°C until tender — flavour is earthy and strong. Seeds: dried and ground, the black seeds are a spice with a warm, celery-myrrh flavour. Use sparingly.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Alexanders is a naturalised non-native plant with no legal protection. Picking leaves, stems, and flowers from coastal public land is permitted. Uprooting requires landowner permission. Many productive Alexanders banks are on coastal footpaths, clifftop public rights of way, or Common Land — all of these allow personal picking.

---

## 105. Jack-by-the-Hedge

**Species Name:** Jack-by-the-Hedge (Garlic Mustard)
**Latin Name:** Alliaria petiolata
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** jack-by-the-hedge
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
Jack-by-the-hedge is one of the most common hedgerow plants in Britain and one of the most useful early-spring greens. The triangular, toothed leaves smell distinctly of garlic when crushed and have a mild garlic-mustard flavour raw. It is one of the few edible plants that genuinely combines two flavours — garlic and mustard — in a single leaf.

---

**Full Description:**
Jack-by-the-hedge grows along hedgerows, woodland edges, and shaded roadsides throughout Britain. It is a plant of the shade — tolerant of deep hedgerow conditions where little else thrives — and appears reliably in the same spots each spring. As a biennial it grows a ground-level rosette in its first year and sends up a flowering stem in its second.

The common name references its hedgerow habitat; the alternative, garlic mustard, describes the flavour more accurately. Crush a leaf and the smell is distinctly garlicky — not as strong as wild garlic, but unmistakable. The taste, raw, is a mild combination of garlic and a faint mustard heat.

The flavour is at its best in March and early April, when the leaves are young and before the plant has flowered. As spring progresses and the stem elongates, the flavour becomes increasingly bitter and the garlicky note recedes. The white flowers in April and May are also edible and milder than the leaves.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A biennial, 20–100cm in flower. First-year plants form a rosette of kidney-shaped, toothed leaves close to the ground. Second-year plants produce an upright stem with alternate, triangular to heart-shaped, sharply toothed leaves of 3–8cm. The leaves have a bright, slightly glossy surface. Flowers are small (6mm), white, four-petalled, in a rounded cluster at the top of the stem, from April to June. Seed pods are long, narrow, and four-angled, 2–6cm, held upright on short stalks. Crush a leaf: it smells clearly and distinctly of garlic. This smell test is definitive. The triangular, toothed leaf with a garlic smell and white four-petalled flowers in a hedgerow is unmistakable.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plants share jack-by-the-hedge's specific combination of triangular toothed leaves with a garlic smell in shaded hedgerow habitats. The garlic smell on crushing is the definitive test — no dangerous hedgerow plant smells like garlic when crushed.

Dead-nettles (*Lamium* spp.) have square stems and opposite toothed leaves in hedgerow habitats but do not smell of garlic and have tubular, lipped flowers rather than four-petalled cross flowers. Not dangerous.

Ground elder (*Aegopodium podagraria*) can grow in similar shaded positions and has compound umbellifer flowers — quite different from jack-by-the-hedge's white cross flowers. Ground elder does not smell of garlic.

Crush and smell the leaf. If there is no garlic smell, it is not jack-by-the-hedge.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, woodland edges, shaded roadsides, and the bases of walls and fences throughout Britain. Most common in lowland England and Wales. Tolerates deep shade under hedgerows where few other plants survive. Often grows alongside nettles and elder. Found from sea level to moderate altitude.

---

**In Season:**
March to May for the best leaves. Young, pre-flowering leaves in March and early April are the mildest and most useful. Once the plant is in full flower in late April and May, the leaves become more bitter. The white flowers are edible and good in salads in April. Seeds can be collected in June and used as a spice — they have a sharp, mustard-like heat.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw in salads: young leaves add a mild garlic-mustard note as a dressing alternative. One or two leaves in a green salad are more useful than a large quantity — the flavour is assertive enough to dominate. Jack-by-the-hedge sauce: blend young leaves with olive oil, lemon, salt, and pine nuts — a simpler, less rich version of wild garlic pesto. Excellent with pasta or fish. Wilted: briefly wilt in butter for a minute — the garlic note softens and the mustard edge remains. Good alongside fish or chicken. Flowers: scatter over salads as a mild, pretty garnish. Seeds: collect ripe seeds, dry, and use ground as a mild mustard substitute.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Jack-by-the-hedge is an unprotected common plant. Picking leaves and flowers from public hedgerows, roadsides, and commons is permitted. No legal restrictions apply.

## 106. Sorrel

**Species Name:** Sorrel (Common Sorrel)
**Latin Name:** Rumex acetosa
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** sorrel
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Sorrel is the wild green with the sharp, lemon-sour flavour that stops you in your tracks the first time you eat it raw in a field. It grows in meadows, grassland, and roadsides throughout Britain and is available from spring through summer. The sour flavour — from oxalic acid — is exactly what makes it useful: it functions as a seasoning as much as a vegetable.

---

**Full Description:**
Sorrel is common in old meadows, rough grassland, and roadside verges throughout Britain. In a good meadow in April, the broad arrow-shaped leaves are present in their thousands, and the flavour of a leaf eaten straight from the ground — sharp, bright, lemony — is one of the most immediate sensory experiences in British foraging.

The sourness comes from oxalic acid, the same compound that gives rhubarb its bite. This is worth knowing because oxalic acid in large quantities can cause problems: it binds to calcium in the gut and can irritate the kidneys if eaten in very large amounts over time. In normal culinary quantities — a handful of leaves a few times a week — this is not a concern. Those with kidney stones or gout should exercise restraint.

The practical value of sorrel is as a flavouring. A handful of leaves in a soup, a sauce, or a fish dish adds an acidity that replaces or reduces lemon. Classic sorrel sauce — the leaves melted in butter until they turn khaki — is one of the most useful preparations for fish. The colour is not appealing but the flavour is exceptional.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial, 30–80cm in flower. Basal leaves are 4–15cm, arrow-shaped with two downward-pointing lobes at the base (the auricles) that clasp the stalk. The leaf texture is smooth and slightly fleshy. Taste is immediately and unmistakably sharp and sour. Upper stem leaves are smaller and stalkless, clasping the stem directly. Flowers appear from May to July: tall, slender spikes of tiny reddish-green flowers. The whole plant turns reddish as it sets seed. The arrow-shaped leaf with downward-pointing basal lobes and immediate sharp sour taste in grassland is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Sheep's sorrel (*Rumex acetosella*) is a smaller, more slender relative of the same habitat — grassland, heaths, and path edges on acid soils. The leaves are narrower, with the basal lobes pointing outward or slightly upward rather than downward. Also sour, also edible, also good in small quantities. The two species are used interchangeably.

Dock (*Rumex obtusifolius*, *Rumex crispus*, and others) has much larger, broader leaves without the arrow-shaped basal lobes and is not sour in flavour — docks are edible but not the same plant and much less interesting.

Lords and ladies (*Arum maculatum*) has arrow-shaped leaves but they are glossy, often spotted, and entirely without sourness when tasted. Eating lords and ladies raw causes burning and blistering of the mouth and throat. Always confirm sourness before eating any arrow-shaped leaf from grassland.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Old meadows, rough grassland, roadside verges, and hedgebanks throughout Britain. Most abundant on neutral to slightly acid soils. Common on old, unimproved permanent pasture. Also found in upland grassland to moderate altitude. Absent from heavily fertilised agricultural land, where it is quickly outcompeted. The reddish flower spikes and the arrow-shaped leaves make stands easy to spot from a distance in May and June.

---

**In Season:**
April to September for the leaves, with the best quality in April, May, and June before the plant sets seed and the leaves become tougher. The plant produces fresh basal growth through summer even after flowering. A second flush of young leaves can occur in autumn after the first growth has died back. Pick young leaves from the base of the plant — larger, older leaves are more fibrous and more intensely sour.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Classic sorrel sauce: melt a large handful of sorrel leaves in butter over low heat, stirring, until the leaves collapse and turn khaki-green. Add cream or crème fraîche, season, and serve with salmon, trout, or eggs. The colour is unfortunate; the flavour is one of the best combinations in British cooking. Sorrel soup: wilt a large quantity of sorrel in butter with onion and potato, add stock, blend — a sharp, pale green soup that works hot or cold. Raw in salads: a few young leaves add bright acidity to any green salad. Sorrel omelette: wilt briefly and fold into a simple omelette in the last minute of cooking. Sorrel butter: blend wilted, cooled sorrel into softened butter and freeze — extends the season.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Sorrel is an unprotected common plant. Picking from public meadows, roadsides, and commons is permitted. Some old meadow sites are SSSI-designated for their plant communities — picking is allowed on public footpaths through these sites but picking from protected meadow habitats at scale is frowned upon and may be restricted. Take from abundant stands only.

---

## 107. Watercress

**Species Name:** Watercress
**Latin Name:** Nasturtium officinale
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** watercress
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
Watercress grows in fast-flowing, clean streams and springs throughout Britain. The peppery, dark green leaves are one of the most flavourful wild greens available, but collecting from the wild carries a specific and serious risk: liver fluke. This risk can be eliminated by cooking but not by washing. Wild watercress should never be eaten raw.

---

**Full Description:**
Watercress is native to Britain and grows naturally in springs, chalk streams, and clean running water throughout the country. The cultivated watercress sold in supermarkets is the same species, grown in controlled conditions from managed sources. Wild watercress from a clean chalk spring is equivalent in quality and flavour.

The risk is liver fluke (*Fasciola hepatica*). Liver fluke is a parasitic flatworm whose larvae (cercariae) attach to aquatic vegetation in areas grazed by sheep or cattle. Watercress is the primary vehicle. The larvae are microscopic, invisible on the leaf, and are not removed by washing. Once ingested, the larvae migrate through the gut wall to the liver, where they cause fasciolasis — an illness ranging from mild to severe, with symptoms including fever, abdominal pain, nausea, and, in chronic cases, significant liver damage.

Cooking destroys the larvae. Steam or blanch watercress briefly and the risk is eliminated. The flavour and most of the texture survive brief cooking. Eating wild watercress raw, regardless of how clean the stream appears, is not recommended.

Streams below agricultural land — even apparently clean, clear streams — can carry fluke. The only watercress that can be eaten raw safely is commercially grown and certified watercress from a controlled source.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A semi-aquatic perennial, sprawling and rooting at the nodes in the water margin. Stems are hollow and float. Leaves are pinnately compound — a central stalk with 3–7 pairs of rounded leaflets and a larger terminal leaflet. Leaflets are 1–3cm, slightly fleshy, dark green, and smooth. Flowers are small, white, four-petalled (classic mustard family), in clusters at the stem tips from May onwards. The plant grows rooted in stream beds and water margins, often forming dense, floating mats. The hollow, floating stem, compound pinnate leaves with rounded leaflets, and aquatic habitat are together specific. Fool's watercress (*Apium nodiflorum*) is the key lookalike (see below).

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Fool's watercress (*Apium nodiflorum*) grows in exactly the same habitat as watercress and closely resembles it. The leaves are pinnately compound with similar-shaped leaflets. The key differences: fool's watercress has leaflets that are more prominently toothed, a more solid stem (not hollow), and it belongs to the carrot family (Apiaceae) — which means it produces umbellifer flowers (small, white, in flat-topped clusters), not the cross-shaped four-petalled flowers of watercress. Fool's watercress is edible in small quantities and not dangerously toxic, but grows alongside hemlock water-dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) — the most toxic plant in Britain. Hemlock water-dropwort can grow right beside watercress in wet ditches and streams and has been eaten by mistake. It causes rapid, fatal convulsions.

Check the flower structure if the plant is in flower. Check for hollow stems. If any doubt exists about whether you have watercress or an umbellifer, do not pick it.

Cook all wild watercress before eating. Liver fluke larvae are present regardless of water quality.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Fast-flowing, clean streams, springs, and chalk watercourses throughout Britain. Most abundant on chalk and limestone streams in southern England — the Test, Itchen, and similar chalk rivers and their tributaries. Also in spring-fed streams in Wales, northern England, and Scotland. Grows rooted in stream beds and at water margins, often in dense mats. Requires clear, moving water with a constant temperature. Absent from stagnant or polluted water.

---

**In Season:**
March to October in most locations. The plant is evergreen but growth is most vigorous and flavour best from spring to early summer. In summer heat the flavour can become more peppery and slightly bitter. Always cook before eating.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Watercress soup: the classic preparation. Wilt a large quantity in butter with onion and potato, add stock, blend, finish with cream. The peppery flavour survives blending and the soup is excellent hot or cold. Watercress sauce: wilt, blend with butter and cream, serve with salmon or trout. Stir-fried with garlic: 1–2 minutes in a hot pan — the leaves wilt quickly and retain most of their character. Added to pasta dishes in the last minute of cooking. Use anywhere spinach is used — watercress has more flavour and a better texture when briefly cooked.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking watercress from public streams and watercourses for personal use is generally permitted on public land. Most productive watercress streams run through private farmland — check access before entering. Watercress in designated chalk stream SSSIs should be picked with restraint. The liver fluke risk is the greater practical concern: always cook wild watercress regardless of the apparent cleanliness of the source.

---

## 108. Fat Hen

**Species Name:** Fat Hen
**Latin Name:** Chenopodium album
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** fat-hen
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Fat hen is a common weed of cultivated and disturbed ground that was widely eaten in Britain before spinach became available. The leaves cook exactly like spinach and have a similar, slightly earthy flavour. It is abundant from June to September on farmland and waste ground and can be picked in large quantities where it occurs.

---

**Full Description:**
Fat hen is an ancient companion of human cultivation. It is found in archaeological sites going back thousands of years across Europe — in middens, in stomach contents, in the seeds stored as food. Before spinach arrived in Britain in the sixteenth century, fat hen was among the principal pot herbs of the British kitchen garden. Its decline is a function of spinach's availability, not of any failing in its own quality.

The plant is extremely common on cultivated land, field edges, gardens, and waste ground throughout Britain. In a good year it grows to a metre and a half and produces enormous quantities of seed. The leaves of young plants and the young growing tips of larger plants are the most useful parts — picked and wilted in butter, they behave identically to spinach.

The mealy, slightly waxy coating on the young leaves — from tiny, blister-like cells called papillae — is characteristic and one of the identification features. This coating washes off and is not a problem in cooking.

---

**Identification Notes:**
An upright annual, 30–150cm. Leaves are variable in shape: lower leaves tend to be rhombic (diamond-shaped) to triangular, 3–8cm, with irregular teeth; upper leaves are narrower and sometimes almost entire. The upper surface of young leaves has a distinctive mealy, slightly white or grey waxy coating, especially on young growth. The stem is ribbed and often streaked with red or pink. Flowers are tiny, in dense, spike-like clusters along the stem branches, from July onwards — green to whitish, without petals. Seeds are small, black, lens-shaped. The mealy coating on young leaves, the ribbed and often red-streaked stem, and the dense spike-like flower clusters on cultivated or disturbed ground are specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Orache (*Atriplex* spp.) is closely related and grows in similar disturbed habitats. It is also edible. The main distinction is the fruiting bracts — orache has two small triangular bracts enclosing each seed that are absent in fat hen. At the vegetative stage the two are similar; both are edible and used in the same ways.

Good King Henry (*Blitum bonus-henricus*) is a related species of old cultivated ground with more triangular, slightly arrow-shaped leaves — also edible and used identically.

No dangerous plants closely resemble fat hen in its typical habitat of disturbed cultivated ground. The mealy leaf coating, the ribbed and often red-streaked stem, and the habitat are together reliable.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Cultivated land, field margins, gardens, allotments, waste ground, and disturbed soil throughout Britain. Extremely common on fertile, nitrogen-rich soils — it is an indicator of soil enrichment. Most abundant on arable field margins and around manure heaps and compost sites. Found from sea level to moderate altitude throughout England and Wales; less common in Scotland.

---

**In Season:**
June to September when the plant is growing actively. Young plants in June and the growing tips of larger plants through summer are the most useful. The leaves of plants that have begun to flower are still edible but tougher and stronger-flavoured. Pick only from sites without pesticide or herbicide use — fat hen is common on arable margins where spraying occurs. Allotments and organic farm edges are the most reliable clean sources.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Wilt in butter for 2–3 minutes and season with salt and nutmeg — the result is indistinguishable from wilted spinach. Fat hen and egg: wilt and use as a filling for an omelette or stir through scrambled eggs. Added to soups and stews in the last five minutes of cooking. Fat hen soup: wilt with onion and potato in stock, blend — a simple, satisfying green soup. The seeds can be dried and ground as a grain — historically used in porridge. Young leaves can be eaten raw in small quantities but the mealy coating makes them slightly unappealing raw in quantity; cooking is better.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Fat hen is an unprotected common weed. Picking from public land, field edges, and waste ground for personal use is permitted. Avoid picking from arable field margins unless you are confident the crop has not been treated with herbicides or pesticides recently.

---

## 109. Good King Henry

**Species Name:** Good King Henry
**Latin Name:** Blitum bonus-henricus
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** good-king-henry
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul

---

**Short Description:**
Good King Henry is a perennial pot herb of old cultivated ground, village greens, and roadsides that was once grown deliberately in kitchen gardens throughout Britain. It produces edible leaves from April into summer and edible flower spikes that can be cooked like asparagus. It has largely disappeared from cultivation but the wild plant persists in the places where it was once grown.

---

**Full Description:**
Good King Henry is not a native British plant. It is a long-established introduction from central and southern Europe, grown in British kitchen gardens from at least the sixteenth century as a spinach and asparagus substitute. It escaped or was discarded from cultivation and now grows on old village greens, roadsides, farm tracks, and waste ground, particularly in the drier east of England.

Finding it requires knowing where to look. The classic habitat is the edge of a village green, an old farmyard, or the rough ground near a historic building — anywhere with a history of human habitation and a slightly enriched, disturbed soil. Once you know the plant, its very specific distribution starts to make sense as a historical map of where people lived and grew food.

The leaves cook like spinach. The young flower spikes, when they emerge in April and May before the flowers open, can be steamed and eaten with butter — the flavour is mild and slightly earthy, the texture more substantial than asparagus but in the same register. This is one of the more genuinely useful culinary things the plant offers.

The master list notes the Latin name as requiring verification. *Blitum bonus-henricus* is the current BSBI-accepted name; it was formerly classified as *Chenopodium bonus-henricus* and many sources still use that name. The plant itself is not in question, only its taxonomic placement.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial, 30–60cm. Leaves are triangular to broadly arrow-shaped, 5–10cm, with the two basal lobes pointing slightly outward. The surface is dull green and slightly mealy when young, less so than fat hen. The leaf margin is slightly wavy but not toothed. Flowers appear from May in dense, spike-like clusters without petals, similar to fat hen but the spikes are less branched. The plant has a slightly mealy texture on young growth. The triangular arrow-shaped leaf with wavy (not toothed) margins, the perennial growth, and the old-habitation habitat distinguish it from fat hen, which is an annual of disturbed arable ground.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Fat hen (*Chenopodium album*) is the most similar species and grows in some of the same disturbed habitats. Fat hen is an annual with more varied, more toothed leaves and a more prominently mealy coating on young growth. Both are edible.

Orache (*Atriplex* spp.) is also similar and edible. The distinction from Good King Henry is the leaf shape (less arrow-shaped in Good King Henry) and the habitat — orache is a coastal and annual disturbed-ground plant, Good King Henry is perennial and associated with historic habitation.

No dangerous plants closely resemble Good King Henry in its specific habitat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Old village greens, roadsides, farm tracks, field corners, and waste ground associated with historic habitation. Most common in the drier parts of eastern and central England. Often found near old farmyards, on the edges of village greens, and along old green lanes. Absent from very acidic or wet ground. A useful indicator of old cultivated or manured ground.

---

**In Season:**
April to July. Young leaves from April — at their best before the plant flowers. The flower spikes in April and May, before the flowers open, are excellent cooked. Leaves become tougher and less interesting through summer. The plant is perennial and will be in the same spot each year.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Leaves: wilt in butter for 2–3 minutes — use in any spinach recipe. The flavour is mild and earthy. Good King Henry and eggs is the natural pairing. Young flower spikes: steam for 4–5 minutes and serve with butter as an asparagus substitute — the flavour is genuinely similar in register if not in intensity. Added to soups and tarts. The perennial nature of the plant means a reliable supply from the same location each year, which makes it worth cultivating deliberately in a kitchen garden if you find it.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Good King Henry is an unprotected plant. Picking from public roadsides, village greens, and common land is permitted. The plant is not native and has no conservation protection. Take only from abundant stands — it can be locally scarce.

## 110. Three-cornered Leek

**Species Name:** Three-cornered Leek
**Latin Name:** Allium triquetrum
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** three-cornered-leek
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Three-cornered leek is an introduced allium from the Mediterranean that has naturalised widely in southwest England, Wales, and Ireland. The drooping white bells on a distinctively triangular stem are unmistakable. Every part smells and tastes of garlic-onion. It is invasive in many areas and picking it actively helps control the spread.

---

**Full Description:**
Three-cornered leek is not native to Britain. It arrived as a garden plant from southern Europe and has naturalised extensively in southwest England, south Wales, Ireland, and the Channel Islands, where it grows on roadsides, hedgebanks, and waste ground in large, dense colonies. In mild coastal areas of Cornwall and Devon it can dominate road verges.

The flavour is similar to wild garlic but slightly less intense — mild, garlicky, with a fresh onion note. All parts are edible: leaves, stems, flowers, and bulbs. The leaves are the most useful — they appear in winter, before wild garlic, making three-cornered leek one of the earliest edible alliums of the year.

Because it is an invasive non-native, picking it generously is ecologically positive. There is no need for restraint on grounds of conservation — quite the opposite. Pulling or cutting from dense colonies does no ecological harm and may slow the spread.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A bulbous perennial, 20–45cm. Leaves are flat and strap-like, 15–30cm, bright green, arising from the base. The stem is the critical identification feature: sharply triangular (three-cornered) in cross-section — run your fingers along it and the three angles are immediately obvious. Flowers are white, bell-shaped, drooping, with a green stripe along the centre of each of the six tepals, 10–15mm long, in a loose umbel of 3–15 flowers, from February to May. The whole plant smells clearly and unmistakably of garlic and onion when any part is crushed. The triangular stem, drooping white bells with a green stripe, and garlic smell are together specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Wild garlic (*Allium ursinum*) grows in similar coastal and hedgerow habitats in spring. The leaves are broader and more oval, the stem is round (not triangular), and the flowers are upright white stars in a rounded head rather than drooping bells. Both are edible and can be used interchangeably in cooking.

Lily of the valley (*Convallaria majalis*) has drooping white bell-shaped flowers and could be mistaken at a glance. It has no garlic smell whatsoever, no triangular stem, and broader, paired oval leaves. Lily of the valley is poisonous — ingestion causes vomiting, cardiac arrhythmia, and can be fatal. The smell test is absolute: if there is no garlic smell on crushing, do not eat it.

Snowdrop (*Galanthus nivalis*) flowers at the same time and has drooping white flowers, but the plant is tiny (10–20cm), the leaves are narrow and grey-green, and there is no garlic smell. Not typically a confusion risk, but the smell test applies.

Crush the stem or leaf and confirm the garlic-onion smell before eating anything from this group.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, hedgebanks, coastal paths, shaded waste ground, and woodland edges in southwest England, south Wales, the Channel Islands, and Ireland. Most abundant in mild maritime areas. Extends up the Atlantic coast and into parts of southeast England around London. Rare or absent from northern England, the Midlands, and Scotland except as a garden escape. Forms dense colonies on suitable roadsides and can cover large areas of hedgebank.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from January in mild areas, February and March in most sites. The plant is at its best before flowering, from January to March. Flowers in February to April — also edible and mild. By May the leaves are yellowing and dying back. One of the earliest edible alliums of the year, often available weeks before wild garlic.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use in any recipe that calls for wild garlic or spring onion. The flavour is slightly milder than wild garlic and slightly stronger than spring onion. Raw: chop the leaves into salads, dressings, and dips. Three-cornered leek pesto: blend with olive oil, pine nuts, Parmesan, and a squeeze of lemon. Wilted: briefly cook in butter for 1–2 minutes as a side or sauce. Whole stems: grill or roast briefly as you would spring onions — the triangular stem holds up well to direct heat. Flowers: scatter over finished dishes as a mild, edible garnish. Bulbs: the small white bulbs can be eaten but are fiddly to extract and the plant regenerates from them — leaving bulbs is better practice.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Three-cornered leek is a non-native invasive species in Britain. It has no legal protection — quite the reverse. Picking and removing it from the wild is encouraged in areas where it has naturalised. It is listed on Schedule 9 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981, which means it is illegal to plant or otherwise cause it to grow in the wild — but there is no restriction on picking or removing it. Picking generously from dense colonies is ecologically sound.

---

## 111. Ground Elder

**Species Name:** Ground Elder
**Latin Name:** Aegopodium podagraria
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** ground-elder
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
Ground elder is one of the most persistent weeds in British gardens. It is also edible, and the young spring leaves have a mild, parsley-celery flavour that works well in soups and as a cooked green. Eating it is one way to approach the problem of having it in your garden. The lookalike risks are serious and require careful attention — this is an umbellifer, and the carrot family contains some of Britain's most dangerous plants.

---

**Full Description:**
Ground elder was introduced to Britain by the Romans as a pot herb and persisted in cultivation into the medieval period, when it was grown in monastery gardens as a vegetable and a remedy for gout — hence its other common name, goutweed. At some point it escaped cultivation and became the tenacious garden weed it is today. A single fragment of root left in the soil will regenerate an entire plant. Digging it out is largely futile.

The young leaves in March and April, before the plant has put up its flowering stem, are the most useful. The flavour is mild — somewhere between parsley and celery, with a slightly earthy quality — and works well as a pot herb in soups, stews, and as a briefly cooked green. Older leaves through May and June are still edible but increasingly strong and slightly bitter.

This is a species where the identification must be absolutely certain before anything is eaten. Ground elder belongs to the carrot family (Apiaceae), and several of its relatives in the same habitat are dangerous. The master list flags it as expert review critical. The lookalikes section must be read in full before picking.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A spreading perennial, 40–100cm in flower. Leaves are divided into three groups of three leaflets (ternately divided) — nine leaflets in total, each 3–8cm, oval to lance-shaped with a toothed margin and a pointed tip. The leaf stalks are hollow and slightly ridged. Crush a leaf: it smells distinctly of parsley or celery — this is important, though not alone sufficient for safe identification. Flowers are small, white, in flat-topped compound umbels of 4–8cm, from June to August. The stem is hollow, ridged, and hairless. Underground the plant spreads via white, creeping rhizomes. The three-times-three leaflet arrangement — nine leaflets in three groups of three — is the most reliable vegetative identification feature. No other common British umbellifer has exactly this leaf structure.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
This section is the most important part of this entry. Read it before picking anything.

Hemlock water-dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) is the most acutely toxic plant in Britain and grows in damp ground — ditches, stream edges, wet woodland — that can occur alongside ground elder in gardens and hedgerows near water. The leaves of hemlock water-dropwort are more finely divided and ferny than ground elder's broad nine-leaflet structure. The roots are distinctive — thick, white, carrot-like, and parsnip-scented. The plant smells unpleasantly sweet rather than of parsley. Eating any part causes rapid, violent convulsions and death. If there is water nearby, check the ground carefully before picking anything.

Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) grows on disturbed ground and hedgebanks in the same habitat as ground elder. The leaves are finely divided and ferny — quite different from ground elder's broad leaflets. The stem has distinctive purple-red blotches on a hollow, hairless green stem, and the crushed plant smells strongly and distinctly of mice. Ground elder has no stem blotches and smells of parsley.

Elder (*Sambucus nigra*) seedlings have pinnate leaves that can resemble ground elder at first glance. Elder leaves are opposite, with a clearly different texture and an unpleasant smell when crushed.

Confirm all of the following before picking: nine leaflets in three groups of three, parsley-celery smell on crushing, hollow ridged hairless stem, no purple blotches, no nearby wet ground with possible hemlock water-dropwort.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Gardens, hedgebanks, roadsides, woodland edges, and shaded waste ground throughout Britain. Most abundant in old gardens, near historic buildings, and along old hedgerows where it was once cultivated. Spreads aggressively via rhizomes and can form large, dense colonies. Common in both urban and rural settings. Most productive picking is from garden patches where the plant is a known, confirmed presence.

---

**In Season:**
March to May for the most useful leaves. Young leaves in March and April are the mildest and best. By June the plant is in flower and the leaves are tougher and stronger. Pick only young growth from the tops of emerging shoots. In a garden where the plant is a confirmed identification, it can be picked through May without concern. In unfamiliar hedgerow settings, only pick when the plant is at the young rosette stage and all features can be confirmed.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Ground elder soup: wilt young leaves in butter with onion and potato, add stock, blend — a mild, pale green soup with a parsley-celery flavour. Ground elder pesto: blend young leaves with olive oil, pine nuts, and Parmesan — more herbal and less pungent than wild garlic pesto. Wilted as a side: cook in butter for 2–3 minutes, season with salt and lemon. Added to risotto in the last minute of cooking. Use in any recipe calling for parsley as a pot herb — the flavour is similar in register. Do not eat raw in large quantities; briefly cooking is better.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Ground elder is an unprotected non-native plant. Picking from gardens (with permission), roadsides, and public land is permitted. No legal restrictions apply to picking leaves and stems.

## 112. Sweet Cicely

**Species Name:** Sweet Cicely
**Latin Name:** Myrrhis odorata
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sweet-cicely
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
Sweet cicely is a large, soft-leaved umbellifer of northern Britain with a strong, sweet anise smell that is its most important identification feature. The leaves, seeds, and roots are all edible. The flavour is distinctly anise-sweet — the leaves have been used as a sugar substitute in cooking, and they reduce the acidity of tart fruits when cooked together.

---

**Full Description:**
Sweet cicely is native to upland Britain — most common in the north of England, Scotland, and Wales — growing along roadsides, hedgebanks, and stream sides in hilly country. In the south it is less common and more likely to be a garden escape. It is one of the first umbellifers to emerge in spring, often in full leaf in March when the rest of the hedgerow is still bare.

The smell is its defining feature: strongly and unmistakably sweet, like anise or liquorice, from the whole plant. This smell is present in every part and is immediately obvious when any part of the plant is crushed. No dangerous British umbellifer smells like this.

The culinary uses follow from the flavour. The leaves reduce the perceived acidity of rhubarb, gooseberries, and other tart fruits when cooked together — adding sweet cicely leaves to a rhubarb compote allows a significant reduction in the sugar required. The unripe green seeds have an intense anise flavour and can be eaten as a trail snack or added to salads. The roots, roasted, are edible and strongly flavoured.

As with all members of the carrot family, correct identification is essential before eating anything.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large, robust perennial, 60–150cm. The leaves are very large, finely divided two to three times (bipinnate to tripinnate) into many small, soft, bright green leaflets, giving an overall soft, ferny appearance. The upper surface of the leaflets often has pale blotches or whitish patches — a useful field character. The stem is hollow, ribbed, slightly hairy, and without purple blotches. Flowers are white, small, in compound umbels of 3–6cm across, from April to June. The seeds are large — 1.5–2.5cm — narrow, ridged, and dark green when unripe, turning dark brown-black when ripe. Crush any part of the plant: the smell is immediately and powerfully sweet anise. The large, soft, finely divided leaves with pale leaf blotches and the overwhelming sweet anise smell on crushing are together unmistakable.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Sweet cicely belongs to the carrot family (Apiaceae), which demands careful identification.

Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) has similar finely divided leaves and grows in similar hedgebank and roadside habitats. Hemlock's stem has distinctive purple-red blotches, the plant smells strongly of mice when crushed, and the leaves are bright, glossy green without pale blotches. Sweet cicely smells of anise; hemlock smells of mice. The smell test is definitive and should be performed every time.

Hemlock water-dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) grows in wetter ground — stream edges and wet ditches — which overlaps with some sweet cicely habitat in northern Britain. The leaves are more finely divided, the smell is unpleasantly sweet rather than clean anise, and the roots are distinctive thick white tubers. Fatal in small quantities.

Cow parsley (*Anthriscus sylvestris*) is the most common spring umbellifer in Britain and grows in the same hedgerow habitat. The leaves are similar in general appearance but less soft, not blotched, and the smell is green and hay-like rather than sweet anise. Cow parsley is edible but not the same plant.

Crush and smell every time. Clean, sweet, anise smell: likely sweet cicely. Anything else: do not eat it.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadside verges, hedgebanks, and stream sides in hilly country, most common in northern England, Scotland, and Wales. Less common in the south except as a garden escape. Grows in partial shade and tolerates damp conditions. Often found at the base of old hedges and along old green lanes in upland areas. The pale-blotched leaves are visible from a distance in good colonies.

---

**In Season:**
March to June, with the best leaves in March and April before the plant is in full flower. The young leaves in early spring are the most tender. Unripe seeds in May and June are excellent. The plant dies back by midsummer in most locations.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
With rhubarb: add four or five large sweet cicely leaves to a rhubarb compote and reduce the sugar by a third — the anise sweetness counteracts the acidity without masking the rhubarb flavour. With gooseberries: the same technique applies. Leaves raw: one or two young leaves finely chopped into a salad add an anise note. Unripe seeds: eat green seeds straight from the plant as a snack — sweet, anise-flavoured, and good. Add whole to a fruit salad. Root: dig in autumn, scrub, roast at 200°C until tender — intensely flavoured, best as a small addition to a root vegetable dish. Dried leaves lose flavour quickly; use fresh.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Sweet cicely is a native plant in northern Britain with no Schedule 8 protection. Picking leaves, seeds, and flowers from public roadsides and commons is permitted. Uprooting requires landowner permission. In gardens where it is grown, it self-seeds freely and can be managed by picking.

## 113. Hogweed

**Species Name:** Hogweed
**Latin Name:** Heracleum sphondylium
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** hogweed
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
Hogweed is a large, common umbellifer of roadsides and hedgebanks throughout Britain. The young shoots and flower buds, picked in spring before the plant opens, are edible and genuinely good — somewhere between celery and asparagus in character. The identification must be absolutely certain, and the distinction from giant hogweed must be clear, before anything is picked.

---

**Full Description:**
Hogweed is one of Britain's most common large plants. It grows on roadsides, field margins, hedgebanks, and rough grassland throughout the country and is in flower for much of the summer. Most people walk past it without noticing it. Foragers who know it well consider the young shoots and flower buds one of the more rewarding spring wild vegetables.

The young stems, peeled and briefly cooked, have a flavour that is hard to categorise: slightly celery, slightly asparagus, mildly sweet, with a herbal depth. Flower buds just before opening, briefly steamed or fried, are one of the best things the spring countryside produces. They have real substance — this is not a foraged curiosity but a genuinely good vegetable in the right hands.

The critical safety issue is the distinction from giant hogweed (*Heracleum mantegazzianum*). Giant hogweed contains furanocoumarins that cause severe phototoxic burns — blistering on contact with sunlight — and is legally controlled in Britain. It is considerably larger than common hogweed and has specific distinguishing features, but both plants are large, white-flowered umbellifers and the distinction must be confirmed before picking.

The master list flags this species as expert review first. No content from this entry goes Live until the expert reviewer has confirmed the identification guidance, the giant hogweed comparison, and the safety language.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A robust biennial or short-lived perennial, 50–200cm. Leaves are very large — up to 1m on large plants — pinnately divided with broad, irregularly lobed leaflets. The leaflets are hairy on both surfaces, with a rough texture. The stem is hollow, ribbed, hairy (not smooth), and green throughout without purple blotching. Stem diameter is typically 1–4cm. Flowers are white, in large flat-topped compound umbels of 10–20cm, from May to September. The whole plant is rough and hairy to the touch.

Giant hogweed (*Heracleum mantegazzianum*) is much larger: stems reach 2–5m, stem diameter is 5–10cm with distinctive purple blotches and coarse white hairs, leaves can reach 1.5m across, and the flower heads reach 50cm or more. Giant hogweed grows in dense, tall stands and is conspicuously enormous — it dwarfs common hogweed entirely. If the plant is taller than you are and the stem has purple blotches, it is giant hogweed. Do not touch it.

Common hogweed: hairy stem, no purple blotches, stems to 2m. Giant hogweed: blotched purple stem, coarse white hairs, stems to 5m. At any moment of uncertainty, do not pick.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Giant hogweed (*Heracleum mantegazzianum*) is the primary danger. Contact with any part of the plant followed by sunlight exposure causes severe phototoxic burns — fluid-filled blisters that can cover large areas of skin, leave permanent scarring, and, if eyes are affected, cause blindness. It is an offence under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 to plant or cause giant hogweed to grow in the wild. Do not touch it under any circumstances. If you are unsure whether you are looking at common or giant hogweed, walk away.

Hemlock water-dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) grows in wet ground near roadsides and hedgebanks in the same habitat. The leaves are more finely divided than hogweed and the plant is smaller. Fatal in small quantities.

Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) grows in the same roadside habitats. Purple-blotched hollow stem, mousy smell, finely divided ferny leaves. Fatal.

Cow parsley (*Anthriscus sylvestris*) is the most common white-flowered umbellifer on spring roadsides. It is much smaller than hogweed (to 1m), with finely divided, bright green leaves and a smooth, hairless, unblotched stem. Edible.

Only pick hogweed in spring, from confirmed plants that are clearly hairy, clearly unblotched, clearly common-hogweed-sized, and clearly not growing in wet ground.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, hedgebanks, field margins, rough grassland, and woodland edges throughout Britain. Extremely common from sea level to moderate altitude. Grows in partial shade and full sun. Most productive picking sites are well-known, confirmed locations — not first-encounter hedgerow finds.

---

**In Season:**
Young shoots from March, flower buds in April and May before they open. By June the plant is in full flower and the stems are too tough and fibrous for eating. Pick young shoots when they are 15–30cm tall, before the leaves have fully unfurled. The flower buds at the top of the stem, still tightly closed in a papery sheath, in April are the best eating.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young shoots: peel the outer skin from the stem with a vegetable peeler, cut into 5cm lengths, blanch for 2 minutes, then finish in butter with salt. The flavour is mild and celery-like. Flower buds: cut the closed bud heads before they open, fry in butter or olive oil for 3–4 minutes until slightly coloured. Season with salt and lemon. The flavour is nutty and slightly sweet — one of the best spring vegetables the British countryside produces. Tempura: dip young stems and bud heads in a light batter and deep-fry briefly. Hogweed stem is a good vehicle for this treatment. Seeds: dried hogweed seeds have a warm, citrus-spice flavour and can be used as a spice in the same way as coriander seed.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Common hogweed is an unprotected native plant. Picking for personal use from public land is permitted. Giant hogweed, the dangerous lookalike, is listed on Schedule 9 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 — it is illegal to plant or cause it to grow in the wild. If you find giant hogweed on your land, report it to your local council; do not attempt to remove it without specialist guidance and full protective clothing.

## 114. Navelwort

**Species Name:** Navelwort (Wall Pennywort)
**Latin Name:** Umbilicus rupestris
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** navelwort
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Navelwort is the round, fleshy-leaved plant that grows from cracks in stone walls and rocky banks throughout western Britain. The leaves are edible year-round, raw or cooked, with a mild, slightly succulent flavour and a satisfying crunch. It is one of the easiest wild plants to identify correctly and one of the few that can be picked in every month of the year.

---

**Full Description:**
Navelwort grows from the mortar of old stone walls, from cracks in rock faces, and from the surface of dry hedgerow banks throughout western Britain. In Cornwall, Devon, Wales, and the west of Ireland, it is one of the most common plants on any old wall. The round, fleshy leaves with their central dimple — the navel that gives the plant its name — are completely distinctive.

The plant is succulent in the botanical sense: it stores water in its thick leaves, which allows it to survive in the dry, exposed conditions of a sun-baked wall or rocky bank. The leaves are edible raw, with a mild, watery, slightly grassy flavour and a firm, satisfying crunch. They are not intensely flavoured enough to carry a dish on their own, but as a raw snack straight from a wall, or as a background texture in a salad, they are genuinely useful.

The year-round availability is a practical advantage. On mild winters in the southwest, navelwort grows continuously and provides fresh leaves when almost nothing else is accessible from the wild.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A fleshy perennial growing directly from rock and wall crevices. Leaves are round to kidney-shaped, 2–7cm across, thick and succulent, with a distinctly scalloped or wavy margin and a central dimple (the navel) on the upper surface where the stalk attaches beneath. The stalk is central, from the underside of the leaf. Colour is bright to mid-green, sometimes with a slightly bluish bloom. Flowering stems appear from May to August: upright spikes of 10–30cm bearing rows of small, tubular, pale greenish-white drooping flowers. The combination of round, fleshy, centrally attached leaves with a central dimple growing from a wall or rock face is specific. Nothing else in Britain looks like it in this habitat.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plants resemble navelwort in its wall and rocky bank habitat.

Marsh pennywort (*Hydrocotyle vulgaris*) has similar round leaves but grows in wet marshy ground — quite a different habitat — and the stem attaches to the centre of the leaf underside similarly. Marsh pennywort is not toxic but is not the same plant and grows nowhere near walls or dry rocky banks.

Navelwort has no dangerous lookalikes in its habitat. The round, fleshy, navel-dimpled leaf on a dry wall or rock face is specific.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Old stone walls, rocky banks, cliff faces, and hedgerow stone bases throughout western Britain. Most common in Cornwall, Devon, Wales, the Lake District, and the west of Ireland. Prefers shaded or semi-shaded aspects and tolerates both acidic and basic rock. Absent from clay and sandy areas without rock outcrops. Rare in eastern England.

---

**In Season:**
Year-round, with the best leaves from autumn through spring when the plant is actively growing and the leaves are most fleshy and tender. Summer leaves on hot, south-facing walls can be slightly tougher and more watery. The plant is evergreen in mild areas.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw: the primary use. Eat straight from the wall — the mild, succulent crunch is refreshing. Add whole small leaves or sliced large ones to salads. The texture is the main contribution. Navelwort and cheese: the mild flavour and crunch work well alongside strong cheeses. Added to a mixed wild green salad alongside watercress, sorrel, and chickweed for texture contrast. The leaves hold up reasonably well to brief cooking — wilt in butter for a minute — but the texture and most of the interest is lost. Raw is better.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Navelwort on public walls, rocky banks, and accessible cliff faces can be picked for personal use. The plant is not protected and is common in its range. Do not pick from private garden walls without permission. On SSSI-designated cliff habitats, restraint is appropriate.

## 115. Crow Garlic

**Species Name:** Crow Garlic
**Latin Name:** Allium vineale
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** crow-garlic
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul

---

**Short Description:**
Crow garlic is a native allium of dry grassland, roadsides, and arable field margins throughout Britain. The slender, hollow leaves smell unmistakably of garlic when crushed. It is smaller and more pungent than wild garlic and significantly more fiddly to use in quantity, but in the right preparation it has a concentrated, sharp garlic flavour that is worth seeking out.

---

**Full Description:**
Crow garlic grows from a small bulb in dry grassland, road verges, and hedgebanks throughout England and Wales. It is considerably less abundant than wild garlic and produces much smaller leaves, making large-scale picking impractical. As a flavouring — a few leaves chopped finely into a dish — it is useful and good.

The plant is variable. Some populations produce mainly leaves; others produce bulbils — small, purple-green, bead-like structures at the top of the stem in place of flowers. These bulbils are also edible, and in populations where they are numerous they are the most useful part of the plant.

The identifying smell — garlic when crushed — is present throughout. As with all alliums, this is the essential test. No garlic smell means do not eat it.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A bulbous perennial, 30–80cm. Leaves are slender, cylindrical, hollow, and somewhat grey-green, 2–4mm in diameter, sheathing the stem at the base. Crush a leaf: the smell is immediately and strongly garlic. The stem is solid (unlike the hollow leaves). The flower head emerges from a pointed bract and contains a mix of small, pinkish-white flowers and green-purple bulbils — or bulbils only, with no flowers, depending on the population. The bulbils are small, pinkish-purple, bead-like, and are produced densely in a rounded head. The narrow, hollow, cylindrical grey-green leaves with a strong garlic smell in dry grassland are specific. Wild garlic leaves are much broader and flat, not cylindrical.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Wild garlic (*Allium ursinum*) leaves are much broader and flat, and grow in damp woodland — quite a different habitat. Both smell of garlic.

Three-cornered leek (*Allium triquetrum*) has flat, strap-like leaves and a distinctly triangular stem — neither feature matches crow garlic's cylindrical hollow leaves.

Spring squill (*Scilla verna*) grows in coastal grassland and has narrow blue-green leaves and blue flowers — no garlic smell. Not dangerous.

The main risk with narrow-leaved alliums is confusion with autumn squill (*Scilla autumnalis*), grape hyacinth (*Muscari* spp.), or bluebell (*Hyacinthoides non-scripta*) before flowering. None of these smell of garlic. The smell test is the definitive check.

Bluebell leaves are flat, strap-like, and glossy, with no garlic smell. Bulbs and leaves are toxic, causing gastrointestinal symptoms. Always crush and confirm the garlic smell before using any narrow-leaved plant from grassland.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Dry grassland, road verges, hedgebanks, and arable field margins throughout England and Wales. Less common in Scotland. Tolerates poor, dry soils and grows in full sun. Often found on chalk and limestone grassland. Bulbil populations tend to occur on drier, more exposed sites.

---

**In Season:**
May to July when the plant is actively growing and the leaves are at their best. The bulbils, when present, are ready from June onwards. The leaves die back by late summer. Earlier in the season — March and April — the leaves are just emerging and are most tender, though the plant is easier to identify later when the distinctive flower or bulbil head is present.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Leaves: use finely chopped as a substitute for chives or garlic chives — the flavour is stronger than chives and more pungent than wild garlic. Add to compound butters, soft cheeses, omelettes, and dressings. Bulbils: use whole as a garnish or pickle lightly in vinegar with salt and sugar. They have a concentrated, raw garlic flavour and a firm texture. Crow garlic vinegar: steep a small bunch of leaves and bulbils in white wine vinegar for a week — a strongly flavoured garlic vinegar for dressings.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Crow garlic is a native plant with no legal protection. Picking leaves and bulbils from public grassland and roadsides is permitted. Uprooting the bulb requires landowner permission under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981.

Herb Robert is the small, pink-flowered geranium of hedgerows, walls, and rocky banks that turns red through the summer. **Full Description:**
Herb Robert is one of the most common plants of shaded hedgerows, walls, and rocky banks in Britain. The delicate, deeply divided leaves, bright pink five-petalled flowers, and the distinctive reddening of the stems and older leaves through summer make it easy to recognise once known.

---

**Habitat:**
Shaded hedgerow bases, walls, rocky banks, woodland edges, and scree throughout Britain. Tolerates deep shade and dry conditions. Common from sea level to moderate altitude. One of the few plants that grows well in the dry, shaded conditions at the base of old stone walls. Present throughout Britain.

---

**In Season:**

The scope is limited. Young leaves in very small quantities in a mixed salad — a few leaves among many other ingredients — add a faint bitter-herbal note. The flowers are edible and make an attractive garnish on summer salads and desserts. Herb Robert tea: steep a small number of leaves in boiling water for 5 minutes — a slightly bitter, aromatic herbal tea. ## 117. Ground Ivy

**Species Name:** Ground Ivy
**Latin Name:** Glechoma hederacea
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** ground-ivy
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
Ground ivy is a low-growing, strongly aromatic plant of hedgebanks and shaded ground throughout Britain. The small, rounded, scallop-edged leaves smell distinctly of mint and herbs when crushed. Before hops became the standard bittering agent in beer, ground ivy was widely used to clarify and flavour ale — the old name "alehoof" records this use. The young leaves are edible and useful in small quantities.

---

**Full Description:**
Ground ivy is not related to common ivy. It is a member of the mint family, with the characteristic square stem and opposite leaves of that group, and the smell confirms the relationship: distinctly aromatic, slightly minty, with a herbal bitterness. The creeping stems spread along the ground and root at nodes, covering shaded banks and hedge bases in spring.

Before hops became dominant in British brewing from the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, ground ivy was one of the primary bittering herbs used in ale. The old practice of using herbs in this way — gruit ales, as they are sometimes called — is seeing a revival in craft brewing, and ground ivy is one of the more accessible ingredients.

The leaves have a flavour that is simultaneously minty, herbal, and bitter. A few young leaves in a salad or a soup add an aromatic, slightly resinous note. Used in quantity, the bitterness dominates. The plant is better understood as a culinary herb used sparingly than as a vegetable used in bulk.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low, creeping perennial, 10–30cm. Stems are square in cross-section and softly hairy. Leaves are 1–3cm, round to kidney-shaped with a scalloped (crenate) margin, opposite, on long stalks. The surface is softly hairy and slightly wrinkled. Crush a leaf: the smell is immediately distinctive — aromatic, minty, and herbal. Flowers are 1.5–2cm, tubular and two-lipped, blue-violet with small purple spots on the lower lip, in whorls of two to four in the leaf axils from March to June. The creeping habit, round scallop-edged leaves on long stalks, square stem, and blue-violet lipped flowers with a strong aromatic smell on crushing are specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Ground ivy is one of the easier plants to identify confidently. The combination of square stem, opposite scallop-edged leaves, blue-violet lipped flowers, and strong aromatic mint-herb smell has no dangerous equivalent in British hedgerow habitats.

Dead-nettles (*Lamium* spp.) share the square stem and opposite leaves but the leaves are more triangular, the smell is absent or very faint, and the flowers are white or pink and larger. Both are edible.

Common ivy (*Hedera helix*) has similarly shaped leaves at a glance but is a woody climber, entirely without square stems or aromatic smell. Common ivy berries are toxic. No confusion is likely at close inspection.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgebanks, woodland edges, gardens, shaded grassland, and the base of walls throughout Britain. Thrives in partial shade on moist, fertile soils. Common in old gardens and along shaded roadsides. Spreads by creeping stems and can cover large areas of ground in suitable conditions.

---

**In Season:**
March to June for the best young leaves. The plant is at its most useful in spring when the leaves are young, tender, and least bitter. By summer the leaves toughen and the bitterness intensifies. The plant is semi-evergreen in mild areas and young growth can be found in mild winter spells.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Ale flavouring: add a handful of young leaves to a home brew in the same way you would add hops — it provides bitterness and a herbal aroma. Ground ivy and cheese: a few young leaves finely chopped and stirred into a soft goat's or sheep's cheese with salt — the aromatic bitterness works well. Young leaves in salad: two or three leaves added to a mixed green salad as a bitter, aromatic element. Ground ivy tea: steep 10–15 fresh leaves in boiling water for 5 minutes — aromatic, herbal, slightly bitter. Ground ivy jelly: make as you would herb jelly — steep leaves in apple juice with lemon and set with pectin. The flavour is unusual and good.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Ground ivy is an unprotected common plant. Picking from public hedgerows, roadsides, and garden edges is freely permitted. No legal restrictions apply.

## 118. Sow Thistle

**Species Name:** Sow Thistle (Smooth Sow Thistle)
**Latin Name:** Sonchus oleraceus
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** sow-thistle
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Sow thistle is the soft, pale-leaved thistle-like plant of gardens, roadsides, and waste ground throughout Britain. Unlike true thistles, the leaves are not fiercely spiny — the teeth are soft enough to eat raw. The flavour is mildly bitter in the manner of radicchio or dandelion, and young leaves are a good salad addition. It has been eaten as a pot herb across Europe for centuries.

---

**Full Description:**
Sow thistles are not true thistles. They belong to a different genus, *Sonchus*, and the leaves, while lobed and toothed in a thistle-like fashion, are soft enough to handle and eat without discomfort. Two species are common in Britain: smooth sow thistle (*S. oleraceus*) and prickly sow thistle (*S. asper*). Both are edible; smooth sow thistle has the softer leaves and is generally preferred.

The plant is an ancient food plant. Roman writers noted that pigs were fed on it — hence "sow" thistle. In the Mediterranean and North Africa it is still commonly eaten as a pot herb. In Britain its food history largely disappeared with the arrival of other vegetables, but as a foraging plant it is underrated.

The flavour is bitter in the manner of chicory or dandelion — more so as the plant ages and grows taller. Young leaves in spring and early summer are the most useful; older leaves through summer are edible but increasingly bitter and better cooked than raw.

---

**Identification Notes:**
An upright annual, 20–100cm. Leaves are 8–30cm, deeply pinnately lobed with soft, irregularly toothed margins — the teeth are soft-tipped rather than sharply spiny. The leaf base clasps the stem with rounded, ear-like lobes (auricles). The stem and leaves exude a white milky sap when cut. Upper leaves are stalkless; lower leaves may have a short stalk. Flowers are yellow, dandelion-like, 2–2.5cm across, in loose clusters at the top of the plant from May onwards. The whole plant is pale to grey-green. Prickly sow thistle (*S. asper*) has more strongly spiny leaf margins and more tightly clasping, rounded auricles — also edible but slightly less good. The soft, pale leaves with rounded clasping auricles, milky sap, and yellow dandelion-like flowers on disturbed ground distinguish smooth sow thistle.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Spear thistle (*Cirsium vulgare*) and other true thistles have much more fiercely spiny leaves and stems and are not soft enough to handle comfortably. Not directly dangerous but not the same plant.

Dandelion (*Taraxacum officinale*) has similar yellow flowers and milky sap but grows as a ground-level rosette without a tall stem. Also edible.

Nipplewort (*Lapsana communis*) has smaller flowers and non-milky sap. Edible.

Groundsel (*Senecio vulgaris*) has similar yellow flowers and grows in the same disturbed-ground habitat. Groundsel is mildly toxic — it contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids that can cause liver damage with repeated consumption. It has small, untidy yellow flowers without ray petals (or with very short ones), and the leaf shape is quite different from sow thistle. Do not eat groundsel.

The milky sap, the soft (not fiercely spiny) lobed leaves with rounded clasping auricles, and the yellow dandelion-like flowers distinguish smooth sow thistle from groundsel reliably.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Gardens, allotments, roadsides, disturbed and cultivated ground throughout Britain. Extremely common in lowland agricultural and urban settings. Found from sea level to moderate altitude. One of the most persistent garden weeds, capable of rapid growth and prolific seeding.

---

**In Season:**
May to September, with the best young leaves from May to July before the plant runs to height. Young leaves from low-growing plants in May are the most tender and least bitter. Pick the youngest leaves from the base or the growing tip of the plant.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw in salads: young leaves with a mild bitter note, similar to radicchio. Wash thoroughly before eating raw — the milky sap can be sticky. Wilted: briefly cook in butter or olive oil for 2–3 minutes — the bitterness mellows considerably. Sow thistle and egg: wilted leaves stirred through scrambled eggs. Added to soups and stews as a pot herb — the bitterness becomes background flavour with long cooking. The milky sap is harmless but slightly sticky; rinse leaves well before using.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Sow thistle is an unprotected common weed. Picking from gardens, allotments, and public land for personal use is unrestricted. No legal protection applies.

---

## 119. Brooklime

**Species Name:** Brooklime
**Latin Name:** Veronica beccabunga
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** brooklime
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Brooklime is a succulent, semi-aquatic plant of stream banks, ditches, and wet ground throughout Britain. The small, oval, fleshy leaves have a mild, slightly bitter, and faintly peppery flavour — similar to watercress but considerably milder. It grows alongside watercress in many locations, which brings the same liver fluke risk. Always cook it before eating.

---

**Full Description:**
Brooklime grows in wet ditches, along stream margins, and in shallow, slow-moving water throughout Britain. It is common and easy to find wherever there is standing or slow-moving clean water in lowland areas. The plant sprawls along the water margin, rooting at nodes, and produces the characteristic bright blue speedwell flowers from May through summer.

The leaves are edible and have a mild, slightly peppery, slightly bitter flavour — good raw in small quantities as part of a salad, better briefly cooked. The flavour is reminiscent of watercress but more restrained.

The important caveat is habitat. Brooklime grows in exactly the same wet conditions as watercress, and the liver fluke (*Fasciola hepatica*) risk applies to any aquatic or semi-aquatic plant growing in water where cattle or sheep graze the surrounding land. The risk cannot be assessed visually. Cook brooklime before eating if there is any possibility of agricultural land use upstream or in the surrounding fields.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A semi-aquatic perennial, sprawling and rooting at the nodes, 20–60cm. Stems are hollow, succulent, and smooth. Leaves are 2–4cm, broadly oval to almost round, fleshy, with a slightly toothed margin, opposite, and short-stalked. The upper surface is smooth and slightly glossy. Flowers are 5–8mm, bright blue (occasionally pink) with two petals on each flower — actually four petals, two fused into one at the top — with two prominent stamens. Flowers appear in pairs from the leaf axils from May to September. The combination of succulent, opposite oval leaves on a hollow stem in wet ground with bright blue speedwell flowers is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Fool's watercress (*Apium nodiflorum*) grows in the same wet ditch and stream-margin habitat and has edible but more subtly flavoured leaves — it is a carrot family plant with umbellifer flowers rather than speedwell blue ones. Not dangerous in itself but grows alongside hemlock water-dropwort in some sites.

Water forget-me-not (*Myosotis scorpioides*) grows in the same habitat and has similarly small blue flowers, but the leaves are narrower and more lance-shaped, the stem is not hollow and succulent, and the flowers are clearly different — five-petalled and arranged in a curved, unfurling spike. Not dangerous.

Water mint (*Mentha aquatica*) grows in wet ground in the same habitat, smells strongly of mint, and has opposite toothed leaves — the mint smell distinguishes it immediately.

Cook all brooklime from open water sources before eating. Liver fluke risk is real regardless of water appearance.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Stream margins, ditches, ponds, fens, and wet ground throughout Britain. Most abundant in lowland areas with slow-moving or standing clean water. Grows in water to 10cm deep and at the water's edge. Common from sea level to moderate altitude. Often grows alongside watercress, water mint, and fool's watercress.

---

**In Season:**
May to September when growth is active and leaves are at their best. The plant is present year-round but winter growth is slow and the leaves are smaller and tougher. Spring and summer leaves are the most useful.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use in the same way as watercress, but with the expectation of a milder flavour. Briefly blanch before use — 1–2 minutes — to eliminate any liver fluke risk. Brooklime and potato soup: blanch, then blend with potato, stock, and cream. Added to spring salads after blanching. Mixed with watercress and sorrel for a spring green sauce for fish. The mild peppery flavour is a supporting note rather than the lead.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Brooklime grows in and alongside watercourses, which are often on private land or managed watercourse land. Pick only from accessible banks on public footpaths or public land. No legal protection applies to the plant itself. Water quality checks are the primary practical concern.

---

## 120. Miner's Lettuce

**Species Name:** Miner's Lettuce
**Latin Name:** Claytonia perfoliata
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** miners-lettuce
**Seasons:** Feb, Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Miner's lettuce is an introduced North American plant that has naturalised on disturbed and sandy ground throughout Britain. The young leaves and the distinctive disc-shaped stem leaves are entirely edible raw, with a mild, fresh flavour and a juicy texture. It is one of the best winter and early spring salad plants available from the wild, and it is easy to identify correctly.

---

**Full Description:**
Miner's lettuce is not native to Britain. It arrived from western North America, where it was eaten by indigenous peoples and by the gold rush miners who gave it its English name. It has naturalised widely on sandy, disturbed, and cultivated ground in England and Wales and is common enough in many areas to be a useful foraging plant.

The plant's most distinctive feature is the disc-shaped or perfoliate leaf that surrounds the flower stem — a round leaf through which the stem appears to pass. This is completely specific and makes correct identification very straightforward. No other common British plant has this feature.

The flavour is mild, fresh, and slightly succulent — similar in quality to a good butterhead lettuce but with more of a watery crunch. It is one of the most genuinely pleasant raw wild salad plants available in winter and early spring, when it grows most vigorously.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A small, fleshy annual, 5–30cm. Two types of leaf are produced. The basal leaves, which appear first, are oval to spoon-shaped, 1–4cm, on long stalks, and are plain green and smooth. The stem leaves are the defining feature: a pair of leaves fused into a single, round to diamond-shaped disc, 1.5–5cm across, through which the flowering stem passes centrally. Flowers are tiny, white or pale pink, five-petalled, 3–5mm, in a cluster at the top of the stem within the disc leaf, from February to May. The entire plant is smooth and slightly fleshy. The perfoliate disc leaf surrounding the flower stem is unique among common British plants and makes identification certain.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plants resemble miner's lettuce in Britain, particularly at the stage when the perfoliate disc leaf is present. The disc leaf is specific to this species.

Pink purslane (*Claytonia sibirica*, sometimes called *Montia sibirica*) is a close relative that also naturalises in Britain, particularly in damp, shaded woodland. It has similar basal leaves but the stem leaves are not fused into a disc — they remain as separate leaves. The flowers are larger and pink-striped. Also edible and used in the same ways.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Sandy, disturbed, and cultivated ground — gardens, allotments, sandy heathland edges, and woodland margins — throughout England and Wales. Most abundant in southern England and East Anglia on sandy soils. Also found in urban parks and on waste ground. Often appears as a self-sown weed in garden beds where the soil has been disturbed. Grows in partial shade and full sun.

---

**In Season:**
February to May, with the best growth in February, March, and April. The plant germinates in autumn and overwinters as a small rosette, then grows rapidly in late winter. By May most plants have set seed and the quality declines. The disc leaves are most useful in March and April. One of the very best wild salad plants for the late winter gap.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw in salads: the primary and best use. Use the disc leaves and young basal leaves whole — the juicy, mild flavour needs no embellishment. Miner's lettuce with lemon and olive oil, or with a light vinaigrette, is one of the simplest and best early spring salads. Combined with chickweed, bittercress, and a few herb Robert flowers for a complete wild winter salad. Briefly wilted: 30 seconds in a hot pan with butter — the texture collapses quickly and the flavour is lost. Raw is strongly preferable. Added to sandwiches and wraps in place of lettuce — the mild flavour and succulent texture work well.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Miner's lettuce is a non-native, unprotected plant with no legal restrictions on picking. It grows commonly as a weed of gardens and cultivated ground. Picking from public land, allotments (with permission), and gardens is unrestricted.

---
