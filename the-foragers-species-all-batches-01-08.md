## 1. Wild Garlic

**Species Name:** Wild Garlic
**Latin Name:** Allium ursinum
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** wild-garlic
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Britain's most distinctive spring green. Wild garlic carpets damp woodland floors from March to May, its broad leaves appearing weeks before the white flowers open. All parts are edible — leaves, flowers, and bulbs — and the smell alone makes it unmistakable.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild garlic is the smell of spring woodland before it is anything else. Walk into the right wood in March and the scent hits you before you see the plant. By April, entire floors of ancient woodland turn green-white with its leaves and flowers. It is one of the few wild plants where abundance and quality align perfectly — when it is good, there is usually a lot of it.

The plant grows from a small white bulb and produces long, lance-shaped leaves with a pointed tip and a clear central vein. The leaves emerge from ground level in late February, reach full size in March and April, and decline after flowering in May. White, star-shaped flowers appear in April and last several weeks. All three parts — leaf, flower, and bulb — are edible at the right time.

Foragers use the leaves in spring cooking the way they would use basil in summer: in quantity, raw or barely cooked, in pestos, butters, soups, and sauces. The flavour is softer than cultivated garlic, without the heat, but unmistakably garlicky.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Leaves are 5–25cm long, broadly lance-shaped, bright to mid green, with a smooth surface and a single prominent midrib. The leaf base narrows into a long petiole. Crush a leaf between your fingers: the smell is immediately and unambiguously garlic. This is the critical test. Stems are triangular in cross-section. Flowers are white, star-shaped, borne in a rounded umbel of 6–20 flowers on a stem 10–30cm tall. The papery bract below the flower head falls away before flowering begins. The bulb is small, white, and onion-like, typically 1–2cm long.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Three plants share wild garlic's early spring woodland habitat and require careful attention before picking.

Lily of the Valley (*Convallaria majalis*) has similar lance-shaped leaves and grows in some of the same woodland habitats. The leaves are broader and more upright, with parallel veins rather than a single midrib, and there is no garlic smell when crushed. Lily of the Valley is poisonous — ingestion causes vomiting, slow heart rate, and cardiac arrhythmia. Deaths have been recorded.

Lords and Ladies (*Arum maculatum*) has arrow-shaped, glossy leaves that can appear alongside wild garlic in early spring. The shape is different on close inspection, but the critical test is smell: no garlic scent. The plant is toxic, causing severe burning of the mouth and throat on contact with the raw sap.

Three-cornered Leek (*Allium triquetrum*) is edible, with a triangular stem and drooping bell-shaped white flowers with a green stripe. The garlic smell is similar. If you find it growing alongside wild garlic, you can eat it — but identify it correctly before picking.

Crush a leaf and smell it every single time. No garlic smell means do not eat it.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Ancient and semi-ancient deciduous woodland, particularly on damp, humus-rich soils. Most abundant in valley woodlands, along riverbanks, and at woodland edges where moisture is retained. Associates strongly with bluebells and wood anemones in ancient woodland. In ideal conditions it forms dense, continuous carpets covering thousands of square metres. Also found in old hedgerow banks and shaded gardens adjoining woodland. Does not thrive in dry, chalky, or heavily shaded conifer woodland.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves emerge in late February to early March in southern England, two to three weeks later in the north and Scotland. Peak picking is March to April, before flowering begins. The leaves remain edible through May but become stronger and tougher as the plant flowers. Flowers are at their best in April and early May. Bulbs are present year-round but digging them requires landowner permission and is restricted by law. The whole season collapses quickly once summer temperatures arrive — by June, most plants have died back entirely.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
The leaves are the main event. Raw, they make a vivid spring pesto with olive oil, pine nuts, and Parmesan — brighter and sharper than basil. Wilted briefly in butter, they make a sauce for pasta, fish, or eggs. Wild garlic butter — leaves blended into softened butter — freezes well and extends the season by months. The flowers work as a garnish and hold up well in salads. Wild garlic soup — leaves blended with potato and stock — is reliable and crowd-pleasing; the flavour mellows considerably with heat. Use the leaves quickly: they wilt within a few hours of picking and do not store well without refrigeration.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking leaves and flowers from public land for personal use is permitted under the right to collect plants. Uprooting the bulb without landowner permission is illegal under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 and the Theft Act. Wild garlic is not a Schedule 8 protected species, but the restriction on uprooting applies to all plants on land you do not own or have explicit permission to forage. Pick leaves only unless you have landowner permission.

## 2. Common Nettle

**Species Name:** Common Nettle
**Latin Name:** Urtica dioica
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** common-nettle
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
The most versatile wild green in Britain, and one of the most underused. Nettles are at their best in March and April, when the young tops are tender and full of flavour. The sting disappears entirely with heat, and what remains is a deep, iron-rich green with more character than spinach.

---

**Full Description:**
Common nettles grow everywhere in Britain — roadsides, field edges, waste ground, riverbanks, gardens. Most people see them as a nuisance. Foragers see a free, nutritious spring green available in quantity from late winter into early summer, with a second flush of young growth in autumn after the plant has been cut back.

The plant needs no introduction to anyone who has walked through a British summer in shorts. The sting is well-known; the food potential less so. Blanching, wilting, or blending removes the sting completely. What remains is a green with a deep, slightly savoury flavour — stronger and more interesting than spinach.

The quality window for picking is narrow. March and early April produce the best nettles: young, tender tops, dark green and full of flavour. As the plant grows taller through May, the leaves toughen and develop an increasingly rank quality. Once the plant has flowered and set seed, the leaves develop cystoliths (calcium carbonate deposits) that make them mildly irritating to eat even when cooked. Take only the top four to six leaves — the top two leaf pairs and growing tip — and only in spring or from freshly cut-back growth.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Nettles are among the easiest plants in Britain to identify. The opposite, heart-shaped, toothed leaves are covered in stinging hairs (trichomes) on both surfaces and on the stem. The stem is square in cross-section. Leaves are 3–15cm long, narrowing to a pointed tip, with deeply serrated margins. The plant reaches 60–200cm by midsummer. Tiny, inconspicuous flowers appear in long, drooping catkins from June onwards. The sting itself is the primary identification feature: if it stings on contact, you almost certainly have a nettle. Confirm with the square stem and heart-shaped toothed leaf. Young plants may sting less strongly — test with bare skin on an inconspicuous part of the plant.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
White dead-nettle (*Lamium album*) and red dead-nettle (*Lamium purpureum*) share the square stem and opposite toothed leaves but do not sting. Both are edible but significantly inferior in flavour. The flowers distinguish them immediately: tubular, white or pink, quite unlike the drooping catkins of common nettle.

Hemp-nettle (*Galeopsis tetrahit*) has a roughly hairy stem that swells at the nodes, and tubular flowers. Not dangerous; not good to eat.

There is no genuinely dangerous lookalike for common nettle. The sting is your primary identification feature — no other British plant stings in quite the same way.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Disturbed, nitrogen-rich ground. Classic habitats include roadsides and verges, field margins, the base of hedgerows, riverbanks, woodland edges, and any soil disturbed by human activity. Particularly dense growth occurs near animal shelters, old farm buildings, and compost heaps. Nettles indicate fertile, nutrient-rich soil — where they thrive in quantity, the ground beneath is usually worth knowing about.

---

**In Season:**
Young tops from late February in sheltered spots. Peak season is March to April — tender, dark green, and at their best. Quality declines from May as the plant grows taller and tougher. Do not pick once the plant has flowered and set seed (typically from June). A second flush of young growth can be found from September to October after the plant has been cut back in summer. Best in early spring, full stop.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Treat young nettles like spinach. Blanch in boiling water for 30 seconds to remove the sting, then drain and squeeze out the water before using — the volume collapses considerably. Nettle soup with potato, onion, and stock is the classic preparation. Nettle pesto works well with good olive oil and Parmesan. Wilted nettles make an excellent pasta filling, a bed for poached eggs, or a side with butter and nutmeg. The blanching water is mineral-rich and can be used as a vegetable stock base. Nettles can be dried for tea but lose most of their flavour in the process.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking nettle tops for personal use from public land, roadsides, and verges is permitted with no restrictions. Nettles are not a protected species. As with any plant, do not pick from private land without permission. Avoid roadsides or field edges that may have been sprayed with herbicide — look for yellowing or chemical damage on surrounding vegetation if in any doubt.

---

## 3. Hawthorn

**Species Name:** Hawthorn
**Latin Name:** Crataegus monogyna
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** hawthorn
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
Hawthorn offers two separate picking windows across the year: young leaves and buds in spring, and berries in autumn. The spring buds — known as "bread and cheese" in old country names — have a fresh, mild, slightly nutty flavour. The autumn berries (haws) make excellent jellies, ketchup, and fruit leather.

---

**Full Description:**
Hawthorn is Britain's most common hedgerow tree and one of its most generous. Most people walk past it without giving it a second thought. Foragers return to it twice a year — once in March and April for the young leaves and buds, and once in September and October for the berries.

The spring picking window is brief. The young leaf buds appear in March, tightly furled and pale green, and are at their best before the leaves fully open. They have a mild, slightly nutty flavour and a pleasant fresh crunch that works well eaten raw straight from the hedge. The phrase "bread and cheese" dates from agricultural workers eating the fresh buds as a field snack. As the leaves open fully through April, the flavour coarsens and the tenderness is lost.

In autumn, the tree produces clusters of deep red berries (haws) from September. They are floury, dry, and mildly tart — not especially rewarding raw — but excellent cooked. The large seeds inside are not edible and must be strained out when making preserves. The berries remain on the tree into winter in many years, becoming sweeter after frost.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A thorny shrub or small tree reaching 1–10m, with grey-brown fissured bark on older specimens. The leaves are the key feature: deeply lobed with 3–7 forward-pointing lobes cut more than halfway to the midrib, 1.5–5cm long, with small teeth on the lobe margins. The white flowers appear in May in dense flat-topped clusters with a strong, almond-like scent. Berries are 8–12mm, deep red, round to oval, each with a small persistent calyx at the tip and a single large hard seed inside. Thorns are straight, sharp, and 1–2cm long. The deeply lobed leaf is the primary identification feature in spring; the red berry with persistent calyx in autumn.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Midland Hawthorn (*Crataegus laevigata*) is very similar and equally edible. It has less deeply lobed leaves and typically two seeds per berry rather than one. The two species hybridise freely in Britain. There is no practical distinction for foraging purposes — treat them identically.

Blackthorn (*Prunus spinosa*) grows in similar hedgerow habitats. The berries (sloes) are blue-black with a powdery bloom rather than red, and the leaves are unlobed, oval, and much smaller. Not dangerous; sloes are edible and covered in their own entry.

There are no dangerous lookalikes for hawthorn in Britain.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, scrubland, woodland edges, chalk downland, and limestone grassland. Hawthorn is the characteristic hedgerow shrub of lowland Britain, planted in enclosure-era field boundaries throughout England, Wales, and southern Scotland. It also colonises open downland and the edges of ancient woodland. Extremely common and productive in almost every county. Urban parks and older gardens frequently contain mature hawthorn specimens with good berry crops.

---

**In Season:**
Spring leaves and buds: March to mid-April. The tighter the bud, the better the flavour — pick before the leaves are fully open. Flowers appear in May and are edible, but picking them reduces the autumn berry crop. Berries ripen from September, peak through October, and may persist into December in mild years. Quality improves after the first frost. In northern England and Scotland, the berry season runs two to three weeks later than southern England.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Spring buds: eat raw in salads or sandwiches. The flavour is mild enough to use in generous quantities. Autumn berries: hawthorn ketchup (berries simmered with vinegar, sugar, and spices then sieved) is excellent with game and cheese; hawthorn jelly (deep red, sets to a clear preserve, works well alongside meat) is a reliable preserve to make in quantity; hawthorn berry leather (dried, blended, set as a dried snack) is a lesser-known option worth trying. The seeds are large relative to the flesh — always strain the pulp. Dried berries can be used to make a mildly medicinal tea.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking leaves, flowers, and berries from public hedgerows for personal use is permitted. Do not cut or damage the tree, and do not remove branches. Hedgerows bordering farmland may be private — if access is unclear, do not pick.

---

## 4. Wood Sorrel

**Species Name:** Wood Sorrel
**Latin Name:** Oxalis acetosella
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** wood-sorrel
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
A delicate, clover-like woodland plant with a clean, sharp lemon flavour. Found across Britain in damp, shaded woodland from early spring. Best used as a garnish or flavouring rather than a bulk green — the oxalic acid that gives it its sourness means it should not be eaten in large quantities.

---

**Full Description:**
Wood sorrel is a plant for cooks and enthusiastic foragers more than a staple green. The leaves are small, delicate, and intensely sour — a clean, bright citrus acidity that makes an excellent garnish for rich dishes, a sharp note in a salad, or a natural souring agent in cooking. Think of it as a flavouring rather than a vegetable.

The plant grows low to the ground in damp, shaded woodland, often carpeting the floor under mature beech or oak in spring. The trifoliate leaves fold down along the midrib at night and in bright sunlight — a characteristic worth knowing, since plants in full sun may look oddly limp before you recognise what they are. The pale white-pink flowers, with distinctive pink veining, appear from April to June.

It requires no preparation and should be eaten raw. Pick individual leaves and use them immediately: they wilt within an hour of picking and do not store. The flavour is at its brightest in April and May, when the new growth is young and the sourness is clean rather than bitter.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Leaves are trifoliate — three heart-shaped leaflets arranged around a central point, each 1–2cm wide, bright green above, slightly paler below, with a distinct notch at the tip of each leaflet. The overall appearance is very similar to clover but the leaflets are heart-shaped with that tip notch, and the taste is immediately and sharply sour. The plant grows 5–10cm tall. Flowers are 1–1.5cm across, white to pale pink with five petals and distinctive pink to purple veining, rising on a slender stem directly from the base of the plant. The plant often grows in carpets where conditions are right.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Clovers (*Trifolium* spp.) are the most likely confusion. The leaf shape is the key distinction: wood sorrel leaflets are heart-shaped with a notch at the tip; clover leaflets are rounder or oblong with a pale chevron marking and no notch. Taste the leaf — wood sorrel is sharply sour; clover has no strong flavour. Clovers are not dangerous.

Introduced *Oxalis* species (*Oxalis corniculata* with yellow flowers, *Oxalis debilis* with pink flowers) grow in gardens and on disturbed ground. All are edible, though not wild wood sorrel. If you find a sorrel-like plant in a garden or on waste ground rather than woodland, it is likely one of these.

Wood sorrel should not be eaten in large quantities. The oxalic acid that gives it its flavour can interfere with calcium absorption and, in genuinely excessive amounts, may cause kidney issues. Use it as a garnish and flavouring, not a bulk green. Anyone with kidney stones, kidney disease, or gout should avoid it.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Damp, shaded ancient and semi-ancient woodland. Most abundant under mature beech, oak, and hazel on moist, humus-rich soils. Also found in shaded hedgerow banks, rocky woodland ground, and the margins of upland grassland with some shade. Tends to form carpets where conditions suit it. Does not thrive on dry, sandy, or chalky soils. Widespread across Britain including Scotland and upland Wales.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves emerge in late February and are at their best through April and May. Flowers appear from April to June. By midsummer the leaves become tougher and more bitter, and the plant partially dies back. A second flush of lighter growth appears in September and October. The best picking is in spring — April and May for quality and abundance.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use raw. The sharp, citrus-like flavour works as a garnish for rich fish dishes, grilled meat, or any dish that benefits from acidity and freshness. Excellent in a spring salad with mild leaves to balance the sourness. Scatter over soup at the last moment. Use to finish a cold butter sauce or a simple vinaigrette. Do not cook: heat destroys both the flavour and the delicate texture. Picks badly and stores for less than an hour even in a cool container. Take only what you need, use it immediately.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking wood sorrel leaves from woodland with public access is permitted for personal use. Do not uproot the plant. In some ancient woodland SSSIs, local picking guidelines may apply — read any notices at the site entrance. Wood sorrel is not listed on Schedule 8 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981.

## 5. Elderflower

**Species Name:** Elderflower
**Latin Name:** Sambucus nigra
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** elderflower
**Seasons:** May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
One of Britain's best-known forageable plants with a short, reliable window in late May and June. The large, fragrant flower heads are used for cordial, champagne, fritters, and desserts. The plant itself is easy to identify when in flower; the risk lies in picking near similar-looking umbellifers in hedgerow and waterside habitats — always check the whole plant.

---

**Full Description:**
Elder flowers for two to three weeks in late May and June, and then the window closes. The smell of elderflower in full bloom — sweet, musky, and faintly tropical — is one of the most distinctive in the British countryside. The same plant produces elderberries in late summer (see the Elderberry entry for the autumn window).

Elder is a large, fast-growing shrub or small tree found in hedgerows, scrubland, woodland edges, and urban wasteland throughout Britain. It colonises disturbed ground quickly and is rarely far away once you start looking. The plant is easy to recognise when in flower: nothing else produces those large, flat-topped white flower heads with that specific scent.

The fresh flowers should be used within 24 hours of picking. They deteriorate rapidly, turning stale and faintly unpleasant, particularly in warm weather. Pick on dry mornings when the scent is strongest and the pollen is fresh. Avoid heads that are fully open and beginning to drop petals — they are past their best.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Elder is a fast-growing shrub to small tree, 3–10m. The bark is pale grey-brown with distinctive corky ridges and furrows. Leaves are pinnate — 5–7 oval, toothed leaflets arranged along a central stalk, each 5–12cm long, with a slightly unpleasant smell when crushed. This crushed-leaf smell is a useful identification check. The flower heads are large, flat-topped umbels of tiny cream-white flowers, 10–24cm across, with a strong, sweet scent. Flowers appear in late May and June. If you cut a stem and look at the cross-section, it reveals white pith inside — this is characteristic of elder.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) and Hemlock Water Dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) are both tall, white-flowered umbellifers that can grow in similar habitats — particularly near water and ditches. Neither looks like elder when you examine the whole plant: elder is a woody shrub with pinnate leaves, while these are herbaceous plants with finely divided, feathery leaves. However, foragers picking flower heads in a rush, near water, can inadvertently grab stems from adjacent plants. Hemlock Water Dropwort is the most poisonous plant in Britain. Ingestion causes rapid convulsions and death. Do not pick elderflower from plants growing directly at the water's edge without identifying every nearby plant.

Hogweed (*Heracleum sphondylium*) and Ground Elder (*Aegopodium podagraria*) are both white-flowered umbellifers that grow in hedgerow and garden habitats. Neither looks like elder on examination of the growth habit and leaves. Always check the whole plant, not just the flower head.

Identify the plant as elder — woody shrub, pinnate leaves, corky bark, pith in the stem — before picking anything. The flower heads alone are not sufficient identification.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, woodland edges, scrubland, roadsides, urban wasteland, and gardens. Elder colonises disturbed, nitrogen-rich ground rapidly and is found throughout lowland Britain. Particularly common along old field boundaries, the edges of parks, and any ground near human habitation or animal activity. Does not thrive in dense, closed-canopy woodland. Very common once in flower — hard to miss.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from late May, peaking in the first two weeks of June across most of England and Wales. The window is two to three weeks. Scotland and northern uplands are typically one to two weeks later. Check individual flower heads before picking: the florets should be fully open and fragrant, with no dropped petals and no sour or over-ripe smell. The same plant's berries ripen in August to October.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Elderflower cordial is the classic use — flower heads steeped overnight with lemon, citric acid, and sugar syrup, then strained and bottled. Elderflower fritters — heads dipped in a light batter and fried briefly — are excellent with icing sugar and lemon. The flavour works well in panna cotta, whipped cream, and simple dessert bases. Elderflower champagne (a lightly fermented version of cordial) needs more time but is worth making. Avoid metal containers and utensils when steeping — the flowers react with iron and the flavour suffers.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking elderflower from public land and roadsides for personal use is permitted. Elder is not a protected species. Do not cut large branches. On private farmland, seek permission first.

---

## 6. Blackberry

**Species Name:** Blackberry
**Latin Name:** Rubus fruticosus agg.
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** blackberry
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
Britain's most picked wild fruit, and with good reason. Blackberries grow throughout the country on brambles along hedgerows, roadsides, and woodland edges, fruiting from late August through October. No other wild fruit is so abundant, so easy to identify, and so useful in the kitchen.

---

**Full Description:**
Blackberries need little introduction. Most people in Britain have picked them at some point — it may well be the most common foraging activity in the country. What fewer people know is that "blackberry" in Britain covers a species aggregate (Rubus fruticosus agg.) of hundreds of microspecies, largely indistinguishable to anyone outside specialist botany. For foraging purposes, treat them as one plant.

The bramble is an arching, thorny shrub that scrambles over hedgerows, colonises woodland edges and roadsides, and invades open ground with remarkable speed. The berries ripen from late July in sheltered, sunny spots, through August and September across most lowland areas, and into October in hedgerows and woodland edges where the fruit gets less direct sun.

Flavour varies considerably. Some bushes produce large, sweet, juicy berries; others are smaller and more tart. Sun exposure, soil type, and rainfall all affect quality. The best blackberries are usually found in open, sunny spots — south-facing hedgerow banks and woodland clearings — rather than deep shade.

---

**Identification Notes:**
The bramble plant is unmistakable. Arching canes up to 3m, covered in sharp, recurved thorns. Leaves are divided into 3–5 oval, toothed leaflets, green above and pale beneath. Five-petalled white or pale pink flowers appear from May to September, producing berries at multiple stages of ripeness on the same plant simultaneously. Ripe berries are glossy, deep black, compound (composed of many small drupelets), and come away cleanly from the plant. Unripe berries are red, then dark red. Taste a berry before picking in quantity: ripe ones are sweet with some acidity; unripe ones are sharply sour.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
There are no dangerous lookalikes for blackberries. Dewberries (*Rubus caesius*) are similar — smaller, bluer, with a waxy bloom and fewer drupelets per berry — and are equally edible. Wild raspberries (*Rubus idaeus*) are red when ripe, hollow when picked, and grow in different habitat (disturbed upland ground and open woodland). Neither poses any risk.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Ubiquitous. Hedgerows, woodland edges, roadsides, field margins, urban wasteland, railway embankments, parks, and gardens. Bramble colonises any disturbed, open ground and is one of the most common plants in Britain. The best picking spots are open, sunny hedgerows where the berries ripen fully, rather than shaded woodland where the fruit stays small and sour.

---

**In Season:**
Berries begin ripening in late July in sunny spots in southern England. Main season is August to October across most of Britain. October berries in hedgerows can be excellent if the autumn has been warm and dry. The old advice not to pick after Michaelmas (29 September) has some basis — later in wet seasons the fruit becomes mouldy faster. After the first hard frost the berries collapse and are not worth picking.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Blackberry jam, crumble, and fool are the classics. The berries also make excellent blackberry vinegar (underrated — adds depth to salad dressings and sauces), blackberry gin, and sloe-and-blackberry hedgerow jelly. Fresh berries work well with cream, in tarts, or as a sauce for game or dark meat. Freeze well: spread on a tray first, then bag them to keep them separate.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking blackberries for personal use from public land is one of the most clearly permitted activities in foraging law. The Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 permits the picking of fruit, flowers, and foliage for personal use. Do not pick on private land without permission. Avoid roadside verges that may have been sprayed with herbicide.

---

## 7. Sloe

**Species Name:** Sloe
**Latin Name:** Prunus spinosa
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** sloe
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
The small, tart berries of the blackthorn shrub, picked in autumn for sloe gin, sloe berry jam, and hedgerow preserves. Extremely astringent raw — the mouth-coating tannins make them unpleasant to eat straight from the bush. Best picked in October after the first frost, when the skins soften and the flavour deepens.

---

**Full Description:**
Sloes are not good to eat raw. That is not the point. The berry's extreme astringency makes it unsuitable for direct eating, but steeped in gin with sugar for six weeks or more, the result is among the best hedgerow drinks available — dark, berry-rich, and drier than anything sold commercially as "sloe gin."

Blackthorn is one of Britain's most common hedgerow shrubs — a spiny, dark-barked thicket plant that flowers in late winter before the leaves appear. The berries are small (8–15mm), blue-black with a distinctive powdery blue-grey bloom, and cling tightly to the thorny branches in clusters.

The traditional advice to wait for the first frost has practical basis: frost softens the skins and reduces the astringency somewhat. If autumn is mild, picking after late October and pricking the skins with a fork or fork-like implement before use achieves a similar result. Freezing the berries overnight before use also works.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A dense, thorny shrub, 1–4m. The thorns are long, sharp, and numerous — working through a blackthorn hedge in picking season is slow and painful. The bark is very dark, almost black in mature stems. Leaves are small, oval, 2–4cm long, with finely toothed margins and a slightly dull surface. The white flowers appear in March and April before or as the leaves emerge — a hedgerow white with blossom on bare branches in early spring is almost always blackthorn. Berries are 8–15mm, round, blue-black, with a prominent powdery bloom. The flesh inside is green and intensely astringent. The stone is large relative to the berry size.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Hawthorn (*Crataegus monogyna*) has red berries and deeply lobed leaves — straightforwardly different once you know both plants. Not dangerous.

Buckthorn (*Rhamnus cathartica*) has black berries on a thorny shrub. The leaves are broad, oval, with a distinct pointed tip and three or four pairs of veins curving forward toward the tip — different from the small, toothed blackthorn leaf. Buckthorn berries are strongly purgative and should not be eaten. Check the leaf before picking any black hedgerow berry.

Cherry Plum (*Prunus cerasifera*) has larger berries (15–25mm), red or yellow when ripe, and less thorny growth. Not dangerous; the fruit is edible.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, scrubland, coastal clifftops, downland, and the edges of ancient woodland. Blackthorn is a colonising shrub that forms dense thickets in open land. Most productive in sunny, open hedgerows where the branches are exposed to full sun through the growing season. South-facing hedgerows typically produce larger, riper berries than shaded north-facing ones. Extremely common throughout lowland England, Wales, and southern Scotland.

---

**In Season:**
Berries ripen from September. Best picking is October and November, after the first frost or cold spell. In mild autumns, pick from late September and either prick the skins or freeze the berries before use. After December the berries dehydrate on the branch and are not worth picking. The picking window overlaps with rosehip and hawthorn berry season — a good hedgerow walk in October often yields all three.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Sloe gin is the primary use — sloes steeped in gin with sugar for a minimum of six weeks, ideally three months. Sloe berry jam, using the cooked and sieved pulp, is underrated and excellent. Sloe vodka follows the same method as sloe gin for a cleaner, more neutral result. Sloes can be used to flavour hedgerow vinegar and are occasionally combined with rosehips and crab apples in a mixed hedge jelly. The gin-soaked leftover berries from sloe gin are good in chocolates.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking berries from public hedgerows for personal use is permitted. Blackthorn is not a protected species. If the hedgerow borders farmland, it is technically private property — do not strip any plant bare regardless of where it grows.

## 8. Chanterelle

**Species Name:** Chanterelle
**Latin Name:** Cantharellus cibarius
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** chanterelle
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
Among the finest wild fungi in Britain. Chanterelles grow in deciduous and mixed woodland from July to November, their egg-yolk orange colour making them conspicuous against the woodland floor. They have a distinctive apricot-like aroma when fresh and a flavour that degrades rapidly after picking — use within 24 hours.

---

**Full Description:**
Chanterelles are among the most prized wild fungi in Britain and Europe, and one of the few with a flavour distinctive enough to stand alone on toast with butter and nothing else. The apricot scent — subtle but present in a genuinely fresh specimen — is unlike any other British woodland fungus.

They grow in association with mature deciduous trees, particularly oak, beech, and birch, in well-established woodland with undisturbed leaf litter. They fruit in the same spots year after year when conditions are right. A productive chanterelle location is worth recording and returning to — the mycelium is perennial.

The flavour deteriorates quickly after picking. Chanterelles gathered on Monday taste noticeably different from those gathered on Wednesday: the apricot character fades, the texture slackens, and the flavour becomes coarser. Pick what you can use within 24 to 48 hours. They do not store well raw.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 3–10cm across, egg-yolk to pale orange-yellow, irregular and convex when young, becoming wavy and funnel-shaped with age. The cap margin is distinctly wavy and inrolled in young specimens. The surface is smooth, matte, and dry. The underside is the critical identification feature: it bears blunt, forking ridges rather than true gills. Run a finger across the underside — you feel ridges, not blade-like gills. The ridges are the same colour as the cap or slightly paler and run partway down the stem. Flesh is pale yellow, firm, and does not change colour when cut. Stem is 3–7cm, solid, the same colour as the cap. Smell is of apricots or dried fruit — faint but present in fresh specimens.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
False Chanterelle (*Hygrophoropsis aurantiaca*) is the most likely confusion and should be studied carefully before picking. It is orange, similarly shaped, and grows in woodland. The differences: false chanterelle has true, blade-like gills — crowded, thin, and more intensely orange than the cap. The cap is more circular with a margin that rolls inward more uniformly. It grows most commonly in coniferous or mixed woodland rather than purely deciduous. The flesh is thin and orange throughout rather than pale yellow. False chanterelle causes gastrointestinal upset and has been implicated in neurological symptoms in some individuals. Do not eat it.

Jack-o'-Lantern (*Omphalotus olearius*) is bright orange with true gills and grows in dense clusters at the base of trees or from buried roots. Very rare in Britain but worth knowing. Causes severe gastrointestinal poisoning.

The forked ridges on the underside — not gills, not separate, blunt and branching — are the key feature of true chanterelle. Check this on every individual specimen.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Mature deciduous woodland with undisturbed leaf litter, particularly under oak, beech, and birch on acidic to neutral soils. Prefers humid conditions and fruits most reliably after warm, wet summer weather followed by a period of stability. Also found in mixed woodland with conifer. Returns to the same locations year after year. Most productive in old, established woodland rather than young plantations. Found throughout Britain but most reliably in the established oak and birch woods of Scotland, Wales, and the west of England.

---

**In Season:**
July to November, peaking September to October across most of Britain. In Scotland and upland areas, the season can begin in late June in good years. Fruiting is triggered by warm, wet weather following drier conditions — the best chanterelle years follow rain in August and September after a reasonable summer.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Best used simply. Chanterelles on toast with butter is the definitive preparation — it shows the flavour without competing with it. They work well in omelettes, pasta, and risotto but should be added late in the cooking to retain texture and flavour. Do not wash: brush off debris with a dry brush or damp cloth. Sauté in butter over medium-high heat — they release significant moisture and must be cooked until the liquid has fully evaporated before seasoning. They can be pickled (good result) or frozen after sautéing. Drying removes most of the flavour and is not worth doing.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland is generally permitted. Some nature reserves and SSSIs prohibit or restrict picking — check local notices at the site. The Forestry Commission permits personal-use picking in most managed forests. Do not pick in quantities that significantly exceed what you can use.

---

## 9. Elderberry

**Species Name:** Elderberry
**Latin Name:** Sambucus nigra
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** elderberry
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
The berries of the elder, ripening from August on the same shrubs that flower in June. Deep purple-black and hanging in heavy clusters, they are among the most versatile autumn fruits for cordials, syrups, wines, and preserves. They must be cooked before eating — raw elderberries cause nausea and vomiting.

---

**Full Description:**
Elderberries and elderflowers come from the same plant — *Sambucus nigra* — and many foragers work it twice in a year: once in late May for the flowers, and again in September for the berries. The plant is extremely common, grows on disturbed and nitrogen-rich ground throughout Britain, and in good years produces heavy crops of dark fruit.

The berries are small — 5–6mm — but grow in large, drooping clusters that can each weigh several hundred grams when ripe. They are deep purple-black at peak ripeness, and the juice stains everything it contacts. Ripe clusters hang heavily and the berries pull away from the stalks cleanly.

Raw elderberries contain sambunigrin, a cyanogenic glycoside that causes nausea and vomiting even in small quantities. The compound breaks down with heat. All elderberry preparations must be cooked. This includes cordial, syrup, jam, and jelly. Do not eat berries raw and do not prepare cold-pressed elderberry juice intended to be drunk unheated.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Identify this as the same elder shrub described in the Elderflower entry. In berry season, confirm by the opposite pinnate leaves (5–7 toothed leaflets, each 5–12cm), the corky fissured bark on older wood, the unpleasant smell of the crushed leaves, and the clusters of deep purple-black berries on red-pink stalks. Ripe clusters are heavy, pendulous, and easily stripped. The berries are 5–6mm, round, with a tiny persistent calyx at the tip. Stalks are red-pink when ripe. The plant is woody and multi-stemmed, reaching 3–10m.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Dwarf Elder (*Sambucus ebulus*) is herbaceous — it dies back entirely in winter, unlike the woody elder shrub — and produces similar-looking dark berries in late summer. It is a shorter plant (rarely exceeding 1.5m), with a strong, fetid smell and larger, coarser leaves. The berries are toxic and cause severe gastrointestinal symptoms. Always confirm that what you are picking is a woody shrub, not a herbaceous plant.

Deadly Nightshade (*Atropa belladonna*) has black berries, but they are much larger (15–20mm), solitary or in pairs rather than in clusters, and the plant is soft-stemmed and non-woody with large, dull leaves. It looks nothing like elder on close examination but merits mention given the berry colour. Deadly nightshade berries are extremely toxic.

Confirm the woody shrub habit, the pinnate leaves, the corky bark, and the red-pink stalks before picking any elderberry.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, woodland edges, scrubland, roadsides, urban parks, and waste ground throughout Britain — the same habitats as elderflower. Most productive in open, sunny positions. Common on disturbed ground near old buildings, along field boundaries, and in urban green spaces.

---

**In Season:**
Berries ripen from mid-August in southern England. Peak season is September across most of Britain. Berries are ready when the whole cluster is deep purple-black, the stalks have turned red-pink, and the clusters hang heavily. Green or red berries are unripe and more strongly toxic. The season ends with the first hard frosts in October or November. Ripe clusters can be stripped rapidly from the stalks using a fork.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Elderberry cordial and elderberry syrup are the classic preparations — berries simmered with sugar and water, strained, and bottled. Both must be heated to temperature. Elderberry wine is a long-established home production. Elderberry jelly with added apple (for pectin) sets well and is deep red-purple. The cooked, reduced juice makes an excellent sauce for game. Elderberry vinegar is worth making. The berries combine well with blackberries and damsons in autumn mixed fruit preserves.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking elderberries from public land for personal use is permitted. Elder is not a protected species. Restrictions on picking in nature reserves apply to all species — check local notices.

---

## 10. Dandelion

**Species Name:** Dandelion
**Latin Name:** Taraxacum officinale agg.
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** dandelion
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
Every part of the dandelion is edible at some point in the year — leaves, flowers, and roots each have distinct uses. Young spring leaves are the most useful: bitter, slightly peppery, and excellent in salads. One of the first plants any forager should learn, and one of the most reliable year-round.

---

**Full Description:**
Dandelion is one of the first plants a forager should learn. It grows on lawns, roadsides, meadows, parks, and waste ground throughout Britain, flowers from March to November, and offers something different at every season: leaves in spring, flowers from late March onwards, and roots in autumn.

The leaves are best in early spring, before the plant flowers. March and April produce the most tender, mildest growth. As the season progresses and the plant matures, the leaves become tougher and more intensely bitter. This bitterness is not a flaw; it is the characteristic that makes dandelion leaves valuable in a composed salad and widely used in European cooking. But it is an acquired taste, and young spring leaves are the better starting point.

The flower heads are sweet and mild, good in fritters, salads, or made into wine. The roots, dug in autumn, can be roasted, dried, and ground as a caffeine-free coffee substitute — earthy, slightly sweet, nothing like instant coffee, but pleasant.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Leaves are 5–25cm long, deeply and irregularly lobed with backward-pointing teeth — the "lion's teeth" of the French name. They grow in a flat rosette directly from the ground. The leaf surface is smooth, mid-green, with a pale, slightly hollow central midrib. All parts of the plant exude a white milky latex when broken — this is consistent and important. The hollow, unbranched flower stem (scape) is 5–40cm tall. The flower head is 2–5cm across, composed entirely of yellow ray florets. The familiar white, spherical seed head follows. The root is stout, with a milky latex when cut.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Cat's-ear (*Hypochaeris radicata*) is commonly mistaken for dandelion. The leaves are similar but slightly hairy rather than smooth. The critical difference is the flower stem: branched in cat's-ear, with small scale-like bracts, rather than the single smooth hollow stem of dandelion. Cat's-ear is edible and not dangerous.

Hawkweeds (*Hieracium* spp.) and hawkbits (*Leontodon* spp.) also have yellow daisy-like flowers and similar leaves. Most have branched or scaly stems and hairier leaves. All are edible.

No member of the dandelion family has a dangerous lookalike in Britain. The definitive diagnostic is the hollow, single, unbranched, smooth stem producing white latex — if you have those, you have dandelion or a very closely related edible species.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Lawns, meadows, roadside verges, waste ground, parks, gardens, field margins, and almost any open ground with reasonable soil. One of the most abundant flowering plants in Britain. Avoid picking from heavily used dog-walking areas, alongside busy roads where contamination from exhaust and run-off is likely, or anywhere that may have been treated with herbicide. Look for clean, vigorous plants on unpolluted, unsprayed ground.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves are available year-round but are best from late February through April, before flowering. Leaves from sheltered spots in February are mild and tender. By summer the leaves are tougher and more strongly bitter — blanching helps. Flowers appear from March and continue until November; pick them when fully open and fresh. Roots are best dug in October and November, when they are at their fattest after summer growth.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young leaves raw in salad, mixed with milder leaves to balance the bitterness. Blanched briefly, they work as a cooked green alongside butter. Older leaves can be blanched to reduce bitterness. Flowers: strip the petals from the calyx (the calyx is bitter) and add to salads, scatter over omelettes, or use in dandelion fritters. Dandelion and smoked bacon salad with a warm dressing — the classic French *pissenlit au lard* — is the best version of this plant. Roots: scrub, chop, roast at 180°C until dark brown, then grind and brew as dandelion coffee. The result is mild, earthy, and naturally sweet.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking dandelion leaves, flowers, and roots from public land for personal use is entirely unrestricted. Dandelion is not a protected species. Digging roots requires that you do not trespass and do not damage other plants. In practice, dandelion is so common and so regenerative that there is no meaningful conservation concern.

---

## 11. Chickweed

**Species Name:** Chickweed
**Latin Name:** Stellaria media
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** chickweed
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
A mild, tender green available almost year-round, chickweed grows as a low mat on cultivated and disturbed ground. It is one of the few edible plants that can be picked in January and February when almost nothing else is available. The flavour is delicate — grassy, slightly sweet — and best used raw.

---

**Full Description:**
Chickweed is easy to overlook. It grows low, often flattened against the soil, and blends into the background of any patch of disturbed ground. Most gardeners know it as a weed; fewer know that it is one of the most widely eaten wild plants in northern Europe and has been used as a salad and pot herb for centuries.

It grows year-round, though it slows in the coldest weather and in summer heat. The most productive picking periods are late winter — from January through March — when it fills the gap before spring greens arrive, and again in autumn when it makes a second flush of growth. In mild winters it barely stops.

The whole above-ground part of the plant can be eaten raw: stems, leaves, and flowers together. The flavour is delicate and grassy with a slight sweetness — closer to mung bean sprouts than to any leaf green. It makes an excellent base for a mild spring salad and works as a garnish or sandwich filler rather than a bulk cooked vegetable.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low, straggling annual, typically 5–40cm, with weak, tangled stems that root at nodes. The leaves are small, 5–25mm, oval with a pointed tip, smooth and bright green. The stem has a single line of fine white hairs running along one side — this single-sided hair line is one of the most reliable identification features of the species. Flowers are tiny, white, with five deeply notched petals so deeply divided they appear as ten. Examine with a hand lens if needed. The plant often grows in dense mats.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Scarlet Pimpernel (*Anagallis arvensis*) grows in similar disturbed habitats and has a superficially similar low growth habit. The leaves are stalkless, oval, and lack the single-sided hair line on the stem. The flowers are orange-red or (less commonly) blue — not white. Mildly toxic: causes gastrointestinal upset. Check the stem hair line and flower colour.

Petty Spurge (*Euphorbia peplus*) and other spurges can grow alongside chickweed. All spurges exude a white milky latex from broken stems — chickweed does not. If any plant in your handful produces white latex when snapped, discard the entire bundle.

Procumbent Pearlwort (*Sagina procumbens*) is superficially similar but much smaller and finer, with thread-like leaves. Technically edible but not worth eating.

The single line of hairs on the stem is your primary identification check. Confirm it on every plant you pick.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Cultivated ground, gardens, allotments, field margins, waste ground, and any regularly disturbed soil. Thrives in rich, moist, nitrogen-rich ground. Common throughout Britain in both urban and rural settings. Often the dominant plant on recently turned soil and vegetable beds. Avoid picking from areas that may have been treated with herbicide, weedkiller, or pesticide — chickweed is a garden weed and is frequently sprayed.

---

**In Season:**
Technically year-round, but quality varies. Best in late winter and early spring — January to April — when growth is young, tender, and mild-flavoured. A second good period in September and October. In summer the plant runs to seed and becomes tougher and less pleasant. In hard frosts the stems collapse but recover quickly when temperatures rise.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use raw. Chickweed is too delicate and mild to withstand cooking — heat turns it to a wet, flavourless mush. Eat the whole plant: stems, leaves, and flowers together. In salads it works well alongside stronger-flavoured leaves that benefit from something neutral to balance them. Use as a garnish on soup, scatter over scrambled eggs, or pack into a sandwich with more flavourful ingredients. Pick just before using — it wilts within an hour at room temperature.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking chickweed from public land for personal use is unrestricted. It is not a protected species and is found in such abundance on disturbed ground that there are no conservation concerns. Do not pick from private gardens or allotments without permission.

---

## 12. Cleavers

**Species Name:** Cleavers
**Latin Name:** Galium aparine
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** cleavers
**Seasons:** Feb, Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
The sticky, scrambling plant that attaches itself to clothing and dogs in equal measure. In early spring, the young shoots are a useful foraged green — mild, slightly grassy, and at their most tender before the plant becomes coarse and adhesive. Best used as a juiced or blended green rather than eaten raw in large quantities.

---

**Full Description:**
Everyone in Britain has met cleavers without necessarily knowing the name. The sticky, velcro-like stems and whorled leaves attach to clothes, hair, and animal fur with satisfying persistence. Most people's relationship with the plant ends there. Foragers have a different use for it in early spring, when the young growing tips are soft, mild, and surprisingly versatile.

The window is short. From February into April, before the plant has grown tall and coarse, the young growing tips are at their most tender. By May the stems become fibrous and the hooks on the leaf surface become rough enough to be unpleasant in quantity, though blending or juicing removes this issue entirely.

The plant's mild green flavour and high water content make it better suited to juicing, blending into smoothies, or making a simple infused water than to eating as a salad leaf. In small quantities as a garnish the young tips are fine raw, but the texture — even young — is not as pleasant as most spring greens.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cleavers grows as a scrambling annual, using its hooked hairs to grip adjacent vegetation and climb. The stems are square in cross-section and covered in tiny recurved hooks. Leaves are narrow, 1–3cm long, in whorls of 6–8 around the stem, also with hooked margins. The whole plant sticks to anything it contacts. Young growing tips are 5–10cm long in early spring, softer and less adhesive than mature stems. Tiny white four-petalled flowers appear from April. The round, double-lobed fruits are also covered in hooks.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Hedge Bedstraw (*Galium mollugo*) and Lady's Bedstraw (*Galium verum*) are close relatives. Hedge bedstraw is similar in habit but less sticky and has broader leaves. Lady's bedstraw has very narrow leaves in whorls and yellow flowers. Both are edible — the distinction matters more to botanists than to foragers.

There are no dangerous lookalikes for cleavers. The combination of sticky, hooked surfaces, square stems, and whorled narrow leaves is distinctive.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, scrubland, woodland edges, roadsides, waste ground, and any vegetation that provides support for the plant to scramble through. Thrives on nitrogen-rich, disturbed ground. Common throughout lowland Britain. Grows prolifically in hedgerow bases in March and April before the surrounding vegetation shades it out.

---

**In Season:**
Young tips from late February through April. Best in March when the tips are 5–10cm long, soft, and mildly flavoured. By May the plant has grown tall, the stems are fibrous, and the hooks are more pronounced. Pick the top 5–10cm of growth only — leave the older, tougher stems behind.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Juice the young tips with apple and cucumber for a mild spring green drink — the blended or juiced form removes any texture issues. Blend into a green smoothie. The young tips can be added to a green soup and blended without straining. In small quantities the fresh tips make a garnish or a green addition to a spring salad. Steep the dried plant in cold water overnight as a mild herbal infusion. Do not cook as a standalone green — the texture does not hold up.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking cleavers for personal use from public land is unrestricted. It is extremely abundant on disturbed ground and roadsides throughout Britain. Not a protected species.

---

## 13. Sorrel

**Species Name:** Sorrel
**Latin Name:** Rumex acetosa
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** sorrel
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A sharp, lemony wild green found in meadows and grassland from April to September. Sorrel is one of the most useful foraged plants for cooking — the acidity brightens sauces, soups, and salads in a way that raw lemon cannot. Use it as a flavouring and a green rather than as a bulk leaf.

---

**Full Description:**
Common sorrel is one of the few wild plants with genuine currency in professional cooking. The sharp, acidic flavour — oxalic acid in its leaves is responsible — makes it valuable as a souring agent in sauces, soups, and dressings. Sorrel sauce alongside poached fish or eggs is a classic preparation that needs nothing more than sorrel, butter, and a little stock.

The plant grows in meadows, roadside grassland, and the rough grass of old pasture. It is widespread and often abundant, particularly in damp, unimproved grassland in Wales, Scotland, and the north and west of England. In the right meadow in May it can be picked in handfuls.

The flavour intensity varies with age and season. Young spring leaves are bright and clean in their acidity. By midsummer the leaves become larger, tougher, and more intensely bitter alongside the sourness. The same portion-size caution as wood sorrel applies: the oxalic acid that gives sorrel its flavour, in large daily quantities over time, can contribute to kidney stone formation. Use it as a flavouring rather than a staple green.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial of grassland, growing 30–100cm when in flower. The leaves are the key feature: arrow-shaped, 5–15cm long, with two pointed backward-projecting lobes at the base (auricles) that clasp or partially wrap around the stem. The leaf surface is smooth and bright green. Taste a leaf: it is immediately and unmistakably sour. The reddish, branched flower spike appears from May to August, bearing tiny brownish-red flowers. The stem is ribbed and hollow. In a mixed grassland, the arrow-shaped leaf with basal lobes and the sour taste is unambiguous.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Sheep's Sorrel (*Rumex acetosella*) is a smaller, thinner-leaved relative found in drier, more acidic grassland and heathland. The basal lobes point outward or forward rather than backward. Equally edible with the same sour flavour. Not dangerous.

Broad-leaved Dock (*Rumex obtusifolius*) and Curled Dock (*Rumex crispus*) grow in the same habitats. The leaves are much larger, without the arrow-shaped basal lobes, and are not sour. Neither is tasty but neither is dangerous.

Lords and Ladies (*Arum maculatum*) produces arrow-shaped leaves in spring in some hedgerow and woodland habitats. The lobes are rounded rather than pointed, the surface is darker and glossier, and the plant has no sour taste — the raw sap causes severe burning of the mouth and throat. Always taste a small fragment before picking in quantity.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Meadows, roadside verges, rough grassland, and old pasture. Prefers damp, moderately fertile, slightly acidic soils. Most abundant in unimproved or semi-improved grassland that has not been intensively fertilised. Common in the north and west of Britain, less so on dry chalk downland. Also found on woodland edges and in hedgerow banks in suitable conditions.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from April through September. Young growth in April and May is the best quality — clean, sharp, and tender. The plant flowers and seeds through summer; older leaves remain edible but become stronger and tougher. A second flush of new growth after cutting in late summer is worth looking for in September. The plant dies back in winter.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Sorrel sauce is the definitive preparation: shred the leaves, wilt briefly in butter, add a little cream or stock, and serve with poached fish or eggs. The leaves melt into a smooth puree when heated, losing their structure but concentrating their acidity. Sorrel soup — wilted with potato, onion, and stock — is reliable and quick. Raw young leaves work well in salads to add a sharp note. Sorrel butter (blended into softened butter) freezes well. Avoid cooking in iron or aluminium pans — the oxalic acid reacts and discolours both the pan and the sorrel.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking sorrel leaves from meadows and public grassland for personal use is permitted. Sorrel is not a protected species. In protected meadows and SSSIs, local guidelines may restrict picking — read any notices. Do not pick from species-rich meadow habitats in quantities that could damage the plant population.

## 14. Watercress

**Species Name:** Watercress
**Latin Name:** Nasturtium officinale
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** watercress
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Wild watercress grows in fast-flowing, clean streams and ditches year-round, with the best picking in spring and autumn. The flavour is peppery and distinctive. It carries a serious risk from liver fluke larvae, which attach to leaves and stems in slow-moving water near livestock. Do not pick from any watercourse where cattle, sheep, or horses have access upstream. Cook before eating unless the water source is verified clean and fast-flowing.

---

**Full Description:**
Watercress is one of Britain's best wild greens in terms of flavour. Peppery, slightly bitter, and far more characterful than the cultivated product sold in shops, wild watercress picked from a good clean stream in April is worth going out for. The same plant, picked from a slow ditch downstream of a cattle field, carries a potentially life-altering health risk.

The liver fluke (*Fasciola hepatica*) is a parasitic flatworm whose larval stage (metacercariae) encysts on aquatic vegetation — including watercress — in watercourses where infected sheep or cattle are present upstream. Ingestion of these larvae causes fasciolosis, a liver disease that can be acute or chronic. Acute infection causes fever, abdominal pain, and liver damage; chronic infection, if untreated, causes bile duct damage and fibrosis. The larvae are destroyed by cooking.

This risk is not theoretical. Cases of fasciolosis from foraged watercress are recorded in Britain. The plant is rated Expert Only not because it is difficult to identify, but because assessing the water source and the upstream livestock situation requires knowledge and judgment that a beginner forager cannot reasonably be expected to have on their first encounter.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A semi-aquatic perennial, growing in or immediately alongside running water. Stems are hollow, trailing, and root readily at nodes. Leaves are pinnate — 3–9 oval leaflets arranged along a central stalk, the terminal leaflet the largest, all bright to dark green and slightly glossy. The flavour on tasting a small leaf is immediately peppery and unmistakable. The small white four-petalled flowers appear in May to October. The plant typically forms dense floating or submerged mats in flowing water.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Fool's Watercress (*Apium nodiflorum*) grows in identical habitats — slow-flowing streams, ditches, and waterside margins — and is the most important lookalike. The leaves are similar: pinnate, with oval toothed leaflets. It is not poisonous but it is not particularly good to eat, and it can grow in water that also carries the liver fluke risk. Distinguish by leaf shape: watercress leaflets are rounded with a smooth margin; fool's watercress leaflets are more pointed with more pronounced teeth. Taste: watercress is immediately peppery; fool's watercress has little flavour.

Hemlock Water Dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) grows in similar wet habitats and is the most poisonous plant in Britain, causing rapid convulsions and death. It has finely divided, feathery leaves — not the simple pinnate leaflets of watercress. Any aquatic or waterside plant with finely divided leaves should not be picked. Do not confuse the two.

Understand the whole waterside plant community before picking in any wetland or stream habitat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Clean, fast-flowing streams, chalk streams, clear spring-fed ditches, and the margins of rivers with good water quality. Does not thrive in stagnant, polluted, or heavily disturbed water. Most productive sites in Britain are chalk stream systems in southern England, limestone streams in the north and west, and spring-fed moorland becks. Grows throughout Britain where suitable water conditions exist.

---

**In Season:**
Year-round, with the best quality in spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November). Summer growth can become tougher and more bitter. In winter the plant slows but remains harvestable in mild conditions. The quality of the plant is entirely dependent on the quality of the water — the same plant in poor water is not worth picking.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Watercress soup (wilted with potato and stock, blended smooth) is the best cooked use. The peppery flavour mellows with heat to something rounder and more complex. Raw, young spring leaves are excellent in salads with orange, walnuts, and a little blue cheese. Use as a bed for poached eggs or grilled fish. The stems are slightly coarser than the leaves but edible. Use within 24 hours — watercress wilts faster than almost any other foraged green.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking wild watercress for personal use from accessible watercourses is permitted. Watercress is not a protected species. Watercourses on private land require landowner permission. Check water quality and upstream land use before picking — this is not just legal advice but safety-critical. Do not pick from any watercourse classified as poor ecological status under the Water Framework Directive.

## 15. Three-cornered Leek

**Species Name:** Three-cornered Leek
**Latin Name:** Allium triquetrum
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** three-cornered-leek
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
An introduced but now common wild garlic relative found across much of southern Britain, particularly in Cornwall, Devon, and areas with mild winters. Edible and flavourful — all parts taste strongly of onion or mild garlic. The distinctive triangular stem makes it one of the easiest alliums to identify. Foraging it actively helps control an invasive species.

---

**Full Description:**
Three-cornered leek is not native to Britain. It arrived from the Mediterranean, probably as a garden escape, and has naturalised extensively across the milder, wetter parts of the country — Cornwall, Devon, the Scilly Isles, the south Wales coast, and parts of Ireland — where it can form dense, self-spreading colonies in hedgerow banks, roadsides, and disturbed ground.

It arrives in January in mild areas and is often the first onion-flavoured green of the year. The bulbs, leaves, and flowers are all edible and all taste strongly of onion with a hint of garlic. The flavour is more pronounced than wild garlic, less delicate, and holds up better in cooking.

The invasive status is worth knowing. The plant spreads rapidly and can outcompete native spring ephemerals. Picking it heavily, including removing bulbs, is entirely acceptable — ecologically it does more good than harm. This is one of the few species where taking more than you need can be justified.

---

**Identification Notes:**
The triangular stem is the primary identification feature and is unmistakable in the hand. The cross-section is a sharply defined equilateral triangle. Leaves are flat, grass-like, 20–40cm long, bright green, keeled on the underside, emerging from the base of the plant. Flowers are drooping, bell-shaped, white with a green central stripe on each petal, hanging in a one-sided umbel of 3–15 flowers from a triangular stem. The smell: crush any part and it is immediately and strongly onion-like. Bulb is small, white, and elongated, growing at the base of a cluster of leaves.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Wild Garlic (*Allium ursinum*) has flat, broader leaves and a round stem — not triangular. Both are edible; the triangular stem is the distinguishing feature.

Snowdrop (*Galanthus nivalis*) flowers in similar habitats at similar times and has narrow, strap-like leaves. Snowdrops do not smell of onion or garlic when crushed; three-cornered leek does. Snowdrops are toxic. Always crush and smell before picking any narrow-leaved, white-flowered plant from a hedgerow bank in early spring.

Three-petalled drooping flowers with green stripes plus triangular stem plus onion smell: there is no dangerous plant in Britain that matches all three.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerow banks, roadsides, cliff-top grassland, gardens, and disturbed ground in mild, wet areas. Most abundant in the far south-west of England, coastal Wales, and parts of Ireland. Also established in parts of London and the south-east. Prefers sheltered, partially shaded conditions and moist soils. Can form very dense colonies where conditions suit it.

---

**In Season:**
From January in mild coastal areas, through to May when the plant sets seed and dies back. Peak picking is February to April when the leaves are young and tender. Flowers appear in March and April and are excellent. The season ends when the leaves yellow and collapse in late May. Picking bulbs is possible from March onwards and is, uniquely, encouraged for this invasive species.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use exactly as you would use wild garlic — or with slightly more restraint, as the flavour is stronger. The leaves work in pesto, wilted in butter, blended into soups, or used raw in salads. The flowers make a good garnish and can be scattered over finished dishes. The bulbs, roasted or used in place of spring onion, have a good savoury depth. The flavour holds up better than wild garlic when cooked — use it where you want more pronounced onion or garlic character.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking three-cornered leek for personal use, including uprooting bulbs, is permitted on public land in areas where the species is established. Its invasive status means that removal, including bulb removal, is not restricted under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 in the way that uprooting native plants is. In practice, digging bulbs from a public roadside in Cornwall is both legal and encouraged. Do not remove plants from private land without permission.

## 16. Rosehip

**Species Name:** Rosehip
**Latin Name:** Rosa canina
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** rosehip
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
The fruits of the dog rose, hanging red-orange in hedgerows from September onwards. High in vitamin C and excellent for syrups, jellies, and cordials. The seeds inside are covered in irritating hairs that must be removed or strained out before eating — never eat rosehips raw and whole.

---

**Full Description:**
Rosehips are one of the autumn hedgerow's most reliable crops. The dog rose (*Rosa canina*) and its close relatives are common throughout Britain, and in most years the hips colour up from September and persist on the hedge into December or later. They are not edible raw and whole — the seeds inside are covered in fine hairs that cause significant irritation to the mouth and digestive tract (the "itching powder" of folklore) — but processed into syrup, jelly, or cordial they are excellent.

The flavour of rosehip syrup is warm, slightly floral, and mildly tart — distinctive enough to work on its own rather than as a simple background sweetness. Commercial rosehip syrup was produced in Britain during the Second World War as a vitamin C source when citrus was unavailable, and the tradition of making it at home has recently revived.

---

**Identification Notes:**
The dog rose is a scrambling, arching shrub of hedgerows and scrubland, with curved thorns on the stems and pinnate leaves of 5–7 oval, toothed leaflets. The five-petalled pale pink or white flowers (3–5cm across) appear from June and produce oval to round red or orange-red hips from September, 1–2cm long with a persistent, pointed calyx at the tip. Other wild rose species (Field Rose, Burnet Rose, Harsh Downy Rose) produce similar hips and all are edible. The persistent calyx at the tip of the hip is a characteristic shared by all roses and not by any dangerous plant.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous lookalikes for rosehips exist in Britain. Red berries of other species do occur in autumn hedgerows — hawthorn, yew, guelder rose — but none has the characteristic hip shape (elongated oval with a persistent star-shaped calyx at the tip).

Yew berries (*Taxus baccata*) are red with a central opening revealing a hard black seed and have no persistent calyx. Yew is extremely poisonous. Any red berry that is soft, round, and has a calyx is a rosehip; any red berry with a cup-like structure around a central seed on a yew tree is yew and should never be eaten.

Do not eat rosehips whole and raw. Always remove or strain out the seeds and their irritating hairs.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, scrubland, woodland edges, downland, and cliff-top grassland throughout Britain. The dog rose is one of the most common shrubs of lowland hedgerows. Particularly productive in sunny, open hedgerows on chalk and limestone soils in southern England, though present and fruiting well across the country including Scotland and Wales.

---

**In Season:**
Hips ripen from September in the south, October further north. The best picking is October and November. Hips soften and sweeten after the first frost and can persist on the plant into December. In good years a single productive stretch of hedgerow can yield kilograms of fruit. Pick when the hips are bright red-orange and give slightly when squeezed — fully hard, deep red hips are not yet ripe.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Rosehip syrup is the classic use — hips simmered with water, mashed, and strained twice through muslin (to remove all seed hairs), then reduced with sugar. Rosehip jelly uses the same process but with added apple for pectin. Rosehip cordial is similar but lighter. The seeds and hairs must be removed in all uses: strain through fine muslin twice. Do not use a food processor to blend hips with seeds intact. Dried whole hips can be used to make a mild herbal tea if the seeds are removed first.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking rosehips from public hedgerows and land for personal use is permitted. Dog rose is not a protected species. As with all hedgerow picking, do not strip a plant bare.

---

## 17. Bilberry

**Species Name:** Bilberry
**Latin Name:** Vaccinium myrtillus
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** bilberry
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A small, intensely flavoured wild berry of moorland, heathland, and upland woodland. Bilberries are smaller and sharper than cultivated blueberries, with a deeper flavour and a juice that stains everything permanently. Found across upland Britain from July to September. Low-growing and often hidden beneath the foliage — get down on your hands and knees to pick properly.

---

**Full Description:**
Bilberry occupies upland Britain in a way that its cultivated cousin, the blueberry, does not. It grows on open moorland, heathland, and acidic upland woodland from Wales and northern England through to the Scottish Highlands, forming low, dense carpets of small, woody stems. In a good year, the picking is excellent.

The flavour is the reason to go out for them. Bilberries are sharper, more complex, and more intensely coloured than cultivated blueberries — the difference is roughly that between a wild strawberry and a supermarket strawberry. The juice is deep purple-red and stains skin, clothing, and teeth with impressive permanence.

Picking is slow. The berries are small (6–10mm) and set singly or in pairs beneath the foliage. A comb-style berry picker speeds the process on open moorland; hand-picking is necessary around rocks and in bracken. A productive morning on good moorland in August can yield several kilograms.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low, deciduous shrub, 15–60cm. The stems are green and distinctly angled (not round in cross-section). Leaves are small, 1–3cm, oval with a pointed tip and finely toothed margins, bright green and slightly shiny. The flowers, appearing from April to June, are small, drooping, pale pink-green urns, 4–6mm long. Berries are 6–10mm, round, dark blue-black with a powdery grey-blue bloom, with a star-shaped depression at the tip (the persistent calyx). The flesh inside is dark red-purple throughout. Taste confirms identification: tart, sweet, intensely flavoured.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Crowberry (*Empetrum nigrum*) grows on similar acidic moorland and produces small black berries on a low, heath-like shrub with narrow, rolled, needle-like leaves. It is edible but practically tasteless. The leaves are completely different from bilberry.

Cowberry (*Vaccinium vitis-idaea*) grows in similar habitats and produces red berries on a low evergreen shrub with thick, glossy, oval leaves with rolled margins and pale dots beneath. Edible and sharply flavoured. Not dangerous.

Bog Whortleberry (*Vaccinium uliginosum*) grows on wet moorland in northern and upland Scotland. The berries are very similar to bilberry but the leaves are oval, grey-green, and without teeth. Edible and very similar in flavour.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for bilberry in British upland habitats.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Acidic moorland, heathland, and upland deciduous or mixed woodland on peaty or leached soils. Strongly associated with upland Britain: the Pennines, the Yorkshire Moors, the Lake District, Wales, and the Scottish Highlands. Also found on lowland heaths in parts of the south of England (New Forest, Surrey heathlands). Absent from chalk and limestone soils. The best picking is on open moorland where the plants receive full sun.

---

**In Season:**
Berries ripen from mid-July in southern upland sites and August in Scotland and higher ground. Peak season is August. By late September most berries have been taken by birds or have shrivelled. In Scotland and the Highlands the season may extend into early October in good years.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Excellent raw, mixed with yoghurt, or scattered over porridge. The flavour is good enough to eat without preparation. Bilberry jam, tart, and pie all work well; the deep juice sets reliably and the flavour is superior to cultivated blueberry in every cooked application. Bilberry and apple crumble is a reliable combination. The berries freeze well — spread on a tray first, then bag to keep them loose. Bilberry cordial requires more fruit than most foragers can realistically pick but is outstanding.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking bilberries for personal use from open moorland, common land, and public upland areas is generally permitted. Many upland moors are private shooting estates — stick to public footpaths and rights of way unless you have clear access to open moorland under the Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000. Some upland moors have specific picking guidelines or restrictions during grouse season.

---

## 18. Sea Buckthorn

**Species Name:** Sea Buckthorn
**Latin Name:** Hippophae rhamnoides
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sea-buckthorn
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
Vivid orange berries on a spiny coastal shrub, ripening from September along the east coast of England and in coastal dune systems across Britain. The flavour is extreme — intensely sour and bitter with a tropical, mango-like undertone when cooked with sugar. Not eaten raw. One of the most visually striking and distinctive wild fruits in Britain.

---

**Full Description:**
Sea buckthorn is hard to miss. A dense, silver-leaved, viciously thorny shrub bearing dense clusters of small, brilliant orange berries, it colonises coastal dunes and clifftops and is increasingly planted in urban landscapes as an ornamental. The native range in Britain is the east coast of England — particularly Lincolnshire, Norfolk, and Yorkshire — but it has been planted and naturalised widely from Cornwall to Scotland.

The berries ripen in September and remain on the plant through October and into winter, bright orange against the silver foliage long after most other fruit has gone. They are not edible raw in any quantity — the sourness is intense, almost caustic — but cooked with sugar they produce a flavour unlike any other British wild fruit: tropical, citrusy, and rich, with a depth that works in both sweet and savoury applications.

Picking is slow and painful. The branches are densely thorned, the berries are small (6–8mm) and clustered tightly to the stem. Wear thick gloves and long sleeves. Cutting whole berry-laden branches and stripping them indoors saves time and skin.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A dense, suckering shrub or small tree reaching 1–6m. The branches are strongly thorned, with pale grey-brown bark. The leaves are narrow, 4–8cm long, grey-green above and silvery below due to fine, scale-like hairs — the silver foliage is distinctive at a distance. Sea buckthorn is dioecious: only female plants bear berries. The berries are 6–8mm, round to oval, brilliant orange-yellow, growing in dense clusters pressed close to the stems. They have a small calyx depression at the tip and a single hard seed inside. The combination of silver foliage, dense thorns, and brilliant orange berries is unmistakable.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No other British coastal shrub produces dense clusters of small orange berries on silver-leaved, thorny stems. Sea buckthorn is one of the most distinctive plants on the British coast. There are no dangerous lookalikes.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Coastal sand dunes, clifftops, shingle ridges, and coastal scrubland. Native to the east coast of England, from the Wash to the Humber and into Yorkshire. Also naturalised widely on the west coast and in Scotland through deliberate planting for dune stabilisation. Increasingly common in urban parks, motorway embankments, and as a landscape plant. Not found inland in wild settings except as an escape.

---

**In Season:**
Berries ripen from late August in southern areas. Peak picking is September and October. They persist on the plant through November and into winter, though quality declines after the first heavy frosts. The berries are at their most concentrated in October — the vitamin C content is extremely high, making them one of the most nutrient-dense wild fruits in Britain, though this is not the primary reason to pick them.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Sea buckthorn curd — berries cooked with butter, sugar, and eggs — is the most refined preparation and worth the effort. Sea buckthorn syrup (berries simmered with water and sugar, strained) makes an outstanding cordial and a sauce base for game and oily fish. The sourness works well against fat. Sea buckthorn and apple jelly, using apple for pectin, is simpler and faster. The juice, extracted by heating and straining without pips, freezes extremely well and retains its flavour over months.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking sea buckthorn berries for personal use from public coastal land and dune systems is permitted. Many east coast dune systems are managed nature reserves where picking guidelines may apply — check local notices. The plant is widely planted in landscaped public spaces where picking is generally unrestricted.

---

## 19. Hazelnut

**Species Name:** Hazelnut
**Latin Name:** Corylus avellana
**Type:** Nuts
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** hazelnut
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
Britain's native nut, ripening in hedgerows and woodland edges from September. Hazelnuts at peak ripeness — freshly fallen, pale brown, with sweet white flesh — are among the best wild foods in the country. The picking window is short and competitive: squirrels, mice, and other foragers are working the same trees.

---

**Full Description:**
Hazel is one of Britain's most common woodland shrubs, found in hedgerows, coppiced woodland, and scrubland throughout the country. In good years it produces an abundant nut crop. In poor years — late frosts, wet springs — the crop can be almost absent. The difference between years is significant enough to be worth checking before making a special journey.

The nuts are ready when they fall from the tree, typically in late September and October. Before this, the kernels inside the shell are not yet fully formed — cutting one open reveals pale, watery flesh rather than the firm, sweet kernel of a ripe nut. Once fallen, they are at their best: the shell is pale brown and the kernel is white, firm, and sweet. Within a few weeks, squirrels, mice, and other animals will have taken the majority of what lies on the ground.

The nuts can also be picked green and immature in August, when the shell is still pale green. Green hazelnuts are softer, milkier, and more perishable than ripe ones but have their own character — popular in parts of southern Europe but underused in Britain.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Hazel is a multi-stemmed shrub or small tree, 2–8m, with smooth grey-brown bark that peels slightly in papery strips on older growth. The leaves are round to oval, 6–12cm, with a short pointed tip, a heart-shaped base, and double-toothed margins. The surface is soft and slightly hairy. The distinctive male catkins appear from January to March, long, yellow, and pendulous. The nuts are 1–2cm, oval, contained in a pale green leafy husk (involucre) that fringes the nut. The nut shell is smooth and pale brown when ripe.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
There are no dangerous lookalikes for hazelnuts in Britain. No other British woodland plant produces a nut encased in a leafy, fringed husk. The only possible confusion is between hazel and Turkish hazel (*Corylus colurna*), planted in some urban areas, which produces nuts in a more elongated, bristly husk. The nuts are edible.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Woodland understory, coppiced woodland, hedgerows, and scrubland throughout Britain. Particularly common in ancient hedgerows and woodland edges on moderately fertile, well-drained soils. One of the most widespread native shrubs in Britain, absent only from the most acid or water-logged ground. Coppiced hazel in managed woodland can produce very consistent nut crops.

---

**In Season:**
Nuts ripen and fall from late September to November, depending on location and the year's growing season. Peak picking is October. Green immature nuts can be picked from August. After November most fallen nuts have been taken by wildlife or have begun to mould. In years following a warm, dry summer the crop is consistently better.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Eat fresh nuts raw, cracked from the shell. The flavour is sweet and milky with none of the stale bitterness of a nut that has sat on a shelf. Roasted briefly in a dry pan, the skin papery skin rubs off and the flavour deepens. Hazelnut butter (roasted, blended until smooth) is straightforward to make and considerably better than the shop-bought version. Ground roasted hazelnuts add flavour and texture to cakes, biscuits, and chocolate puddings. The green immature nuts can be eaten raw or pickled in brine.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking nuts from public woodland and hedgerows for personal use is permitted under standard foraging rights. Nut-bearing hazel trees in private woodland or farmland hedgerows are technically private property — seek permission where the ownership is clear.

---

## 20. Raspberry

**Species Name:** Raspberry
**Latin Name:** Rubus idaeus
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** raspberry
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Wild raspberries grow in open woodland, clearings, and moorland edges from July to September, smaller and sharper than cultivated fruit but with a more concentrated flavour. One of the safest wild fruits to pick — nothing dangerous looks like it.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild raspberry plants are scattered across upland woodland, moorland edges, and disturbed ground throughout Britain, most abundantly in Scotland and northern England where cooler, damper conditions suit them. The fruit is smaller than anything sold in a shop, brighter red, and considerably more flavourful — the sort of thing that reminds you what a raspberry is supposed to taste like.

The plant is easy to identify: the canes, the leaf shape, and the way the berry separates cleanly from the cone-shaped central plug when ripe are all unmistakable. Wild raspberries come away from the cane hollow and round — the distinctive cup shape — leaving the plug behind. This is not a feature any dangerous plant shares.

The berries are fragile and must be used quickly. They do not travel well and do not store raw for more than a day in the fridge. Freeze what you cannot use immediately by spreading on a tray first.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A shrubby perennial with biennial canes: first-year canes (primocanes) are green-grey with a slight white bloom and fine prickles; second-year canes (floricanes) are brown, woodier, and bear the fruit. Leaves are pinnate with 3–7 leaflets, dark green above and white-silvery with fine hairs beneath — this white underside is a reliable field character. Leaflets are oval with toothed margins. The five-petalled white flowers appear on short sideshoots from May to August. The ripe berry is bright red, 1–1.5cm, and separates cleanly from the conical central plug when picked. The hollow cup-shaped berry left after picking is diagnostic.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
There are no dangerous lookalikes for wild raspberry in Britain. Dewberry (*Rubus caesius*) and blackberry (*Rubus fruticosus* agg.) are relatives with similar growth habits; both are edible. Strawberry (*Fragaria vesca*) produces a red fruit on a low, non-thorny plant very different in habit from raspberry. No dangerous plant in Britain produces a red, hollow, cup-shaped berry that separates from a central plug.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Open woodland, woodland clearings, coppiced woodland, moorland edges, roadside banks, and disturbed upland ground. Most abundant in Scotland, northern England, and Wales. Also found in lowland England in coppiced woodland and on south-facing railway embankments. Prefers slightly acidic, moderately fertile soils and will not thrive in dense, closed-canopy woodland. Fruiting is best in full sun or light shade.

---

**In Season:**
Fruit ripens from mid-July in sheltered lowland sites, through August across most of upland Britain, and into September in Scotland and at higher altitudes. The season is relatively short — three to four weeks per location — but different sites at different elevations extend the total window. Birds and other animals take ripe fruit quickly; visit productive sites regularly once the season begins.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Eat fresh, simply. The flavour is good enough to eat as found, and processing often diminishes rather than improves it. Wild raspberry jam sets easily — the fruit is high in pectin. Wild raspberry ripple ice cream (if you have enough berries, which is rarely the case in practice) is outstanding. The berries blend well with other upland autumn fruits — bilberry, blackberry, bramble — in mixed fruit jams or tarts. Do not cook at high temperature for too long — the flavour volatilises and the colour dulls.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking wild raspberries from public woodland and common land for personal use is permitted. Upland moorland and woodland in Scotland and northern England is often private shooting or farming land — stay on marked paths unless access rights apply under the Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000 or the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003.

## 21. Jack-by-the-Hedge

**Species Name:** Jack-by-the-Hedge
**Latin Name:** Alliaria petiolata
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** jack-by-the-hedge
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
A common hedgerow and woodland edge plant with a distinctive garlic-mustard flavour — mild garlic on the nose, sharp mustard on the finish. One of the easiest spring greens to identify and one of the most underused. The young leaves in March and April are the best picking; by summer the plant is coarse and strongly bitter.

---

**Full Description:**
Jack-by-the-Hedge goes by several names — garlic mustard, hedge garlic, sauce alone — and all of them point at what the plant is: a mild, garlicky, mustardy wild green found along hedgerows and woodland edges throughout Britain. It is one of the most common wild plants in the country and almost entirely ignored as food.

The flavour is distinctive. Crush a leaf and the first impression is garlic — mild, green, without heat. Eat it and the finish is mustard, sharp and clean. Neither element is as strong as its cultivated counterpart, and the combination is interesting enough to use deliberately rather than simply as a background green.

The young leaves in March and April are the best. Small, tender, and at their most mild, they work raw in a salad or as a garnish. By May the leaves are larger and the bitterness increases. By June the plant has run to seed — the seed pods are long, thin, and edible, with a stronger flavour than the leaves.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A biennial or short-lived perennial, forming a rosette of kidney-shaped, toothed leaves in the first year and sending up a flowering stem of 40–120cm in the second. The stem leaves are alternate, triangular to heart-shaped with coarsely toothed margins, bright green. The smell when crushed is distinctly of garlic — persistent and unmistakable. The small white four-petalled flowers appear from April in dense, flat-topped clusters at the stem tip. Seed pods (siliques) are long, thin, and four-angled, 2–7cm, ripening from green to brown through summer. The garlic smell on crushing any part of the plant is the primary identification feature.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Few plants combine heart-shaped toothed leaves, garlic smell, and small white four-petalled flowers in a hedgerow setting. The combination is specific.

Ground Elder (*Aegopodium podagraria*) has similar pale green, toothed leaves in hedgerow and garden settings but is a much lower, spreading plant with compound leaves and no garlic smell. It is edible but looks and smells nothing like Jack-by-the-Hedge.

Hedge Mustard (*Sisymbrium officinale*) is related and grows in similar habitats, with small yellow flowers and deeply lobed lower leaves. Edible but significantly less interesting.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for Jack-by-the-Hedge. The garlic smell on crushing is definitive.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerow bases, woodland edges, shaded roadsides, and the margins of gardens. Thrives in partial shade on moist, nutrient-rich soils. One of the most common hedgerow plants in lowland Britain. Often grows in dense stands along sunken lanes and old hedgerow banks. Common throughout England, Wales, and into southern Scotland.

---

**In Season:**
First-year rosettes are edible through winter, but the best picking is March to May when the plant sends up its flowering stems and the stem leaves are fresh and tender. Seed pods are edible from June through August. By July the leaves are coarse and intensely bitter — not worth picking. The flavour is at its mildest in March and April.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young leaves raw in salads, sandwiches, or as a garnish. The flavour works well alongside strong cheeses, smoked fish, and cured meats — the garlic-mustard combination cuts through richness without overpowering. A traditional use was as a sauce alongside salt fish and salted meat, which is where "sauce alone" comes from. The leaves wilt quickly with heat and are best raw or added at the very last moment. The seed pods, raw or pickled, have a more pronounced mustard flavour and are worth trying.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking for personal use from public hedgerows and roadsides is unrestricted. Not a protected species. Extremely common — no conservation concern.

## 22. Navelwort

**Species Name:** Navelwort
**Latin Name:** Umbilicus rupestris
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** navelwort
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
A succulent-leaved plant of old stone walls and rocky banks, found mainly in the west and south-west of Britain. The round, fleshy leaves — with a distinctive dimple in the centre — are edible raw and have a mild, slightly mucilaginous flavour. Year-round, easy to identify, and found in quantity on suitable walls. One for the west-country forager.

---

**Full Description:**
Navelwort, also called pennywort, is a plant of the Atlantic west — Cornwall, Devon, Wales, and the west of Ireland are its strongholds, though it is found on old walls and rocky banks throughout England and Scotland in suitable conditions. It grows on old stone walls, rocky outcrops, slate banks, and cliff faces, rooting into mortar joints and rock crevices with cheerful persistence.

The leaves are the edible part — fleshy, smooth, round, and unmistakable with the small central dimple (the "navel") that gives the plant its name. The texture is crisp and slightly succulent, similar to purslane. The flavour is mild, slightly grassy, and faintly mucilaginous — pleasant without being exceptional.

It is not a plant you go out specifically to find in large quantities. It is a plant you pick a handful of when you pass a suitable wall — useful in a salad, good as a garnish, interesting enough to bring back. The year-round availability makes it a reliable background green when other things are scarce.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial with fleshy, round to kidney-shaped leaves, 2–7cm across, growing on long stalks from the base. The central dimple (the peltate attachment point of the stalk) is distinctive and consistent. The leaf surface is smooth and slightly waxy. The plant grows from crevices in old walls and rock faces, usually with multiple leaves from the same rootstock. The erect flowering spike appears from May to August, 10–40cm tall, bearing small, drooping, pale green-white tubular flowers. The rounded, fleshy, stalked leaf with a central dimple on a wall or rock face is specific — nothing else matches it.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous lookalikes exist for navelwort. No other British plant growing on walls and rocks produces round, stalked, fleshy leaves with a central dimple.

Marsh Pennywort (*Hydrocotyle vulgaris*) grows in wet, marshy habitats and has similarly round, centrally stalked leaves but is a low, creeping plant of wet ground rather than walls. It is edible but should not be confused with aquatic plants in similar habitats — always confirm the dry, rocky habitat for navelwort.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Old stone walls, rocky banks, slate outcrops, cliff faces, and dry stone walls throughout the wetter, milder parts of Britain. Most common in Cornwall, Devon, Wales, the Marches, and the west coast of Scotland. Absent from chalk and limestone areas. Requires old, undisturbed wall or rock surfaces — not found on new brick or concrete.

---

**In Season:**
Year-round, though the leaves are largest and most tender in spring and autumn. In summer the plant puts energy into flowering and the leaves become smaller. In the mildest coastal areas of the south-west it grows year-round without any dormancy period.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Eat raw. The leaves are best picked individually and used fresh in salads, as a garnish, or as a component of a foraged salad alongside more strongly flavoured greens. The slight succulence makes it work well alongside vinaigrette. Not suited to cooking — heat collapses the texture. Not a bulk green; treat it as a garnish and flavour-interest ingredient.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking navelwort leaves from public walls and road-side banks for personal use is permitted. Do not damage or remove plants from protected historic walls or scheduled ancient monuments. Not a protected species.

## 23. Ground Ivy

**Species Name:** Ground Ivy
**Latin Name:** Glechoma hederacea
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** ground-ivy
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
A low-growing, creeping plant of hedgerow banks and woodland edges with a strong, aromatic, slightly bitter flavour. Not a cooking green in the conventional sense — used in small quantities as a flavouring, a tea plant, and historically as a bittering agent in ale before hops became dominant. One of the easiest spring aromatics to find and identify.

---

**Full Description:**
Ground ivy is everywhere in spring hedgerows and woodland edges and almost completely ignored. The creeping stems spread across the ground in dense mats, the small violet flowers appear from March, and the whole plant has a distinctive, aromatic smell when crushed — sharp, minty, slightly resinous — that makes it immediately identifiable.

It is not a vegetable in any straightforward sense. The flavour is too strong and too bitter to eat in salad quantities. It is an aromatic: a flavouring, a tea herb, and a bittering agent. Historically it was used to clarify and flavour ale — "alehoof" is one of its old names — before hops established dominance in the sixteenth century. The tradition of ground ivy ale is worth reviving.

Use it in small quantities. A few leaves in a spring tea blend, as a flavouring in broth, or scattered over a finished dish work well. A full portion as a cooked green does not.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low, creeping perennial with trailing, rooting stems. The leaves are round to kidney-shaped, 1–3cm across, with scalloped margins and a wrinkled, softly hairy surface. The stems are square in cross-section. The small violet-blue flowers are two-lipped and appear in whorls of 2–4 in the leaf axils from March to June. The smell when any part of the plant is crushed is strongly aromatic — minty, slightly bitter, resinous — and is the primary identification aid. The plant forms dense low mats and the stems root readily at nodes.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Ivy (*Hedera helix*) has no relationship to ground ivy beyond a shared common name — it is a woody climber with lobed, glossy leaves. It is not edible.

Common Violet (*Viola riviniana*) has similarly heart-shaped leaves in some growth forms and also produces violet flowers in spring, but the leaves are smooth and unscented, and the flowers are larger with five petals rather than the two-lipped form of ground ivy.

Bugle (*Ajuga reptans*) has square stems, blue flowers in whorls, and grows in similar habitats. The leaves are broader and less scalloped, and the plant is not aromatic. Edible but not particularly useful.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for ground ivy.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerow banks, woodland edges, shaded grassland, roadsides, and gardens throughout lowland Britain. Thrives in partial shade on moist, moderately fertile soil. One of the most common spring ground-cover plants in hedgerows and old gardens. Forms dense, continuous mats where conditions suit it.

---

**In Season:**
Young growth from late February. Best picking is March to May when the leaves are small and the aroma is at its freshest. The plant continues growing and flowering into June but becomes coarser and more bitter. Technically the leaves are available year-round as the plant is semi-evergreen, but summer and autumn growth is not worth picking.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use sparingly as an aromatic flavouring. Two or three leaves in a pot of herbal tea add a sharp, slightly bitter, minty note. Steep a small bunch in hot water for a simple ground ivy tea. Use in broth alongside other herbs where a slightly bitter, aromatic background note is useful. Ground ivy ale — a rough, bittered ale made with ground ivy rather than hops — is a historical curiosity worth attempting. Do not use as a bulk green.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking ground ivy for personal use from public land is unrestricted. Extremely common; no conservation concern. Not a protected species.

## 24. Herb Robert

**Short Description:**
A familiar plant of hedgerows, walls, and rocky woodland with deeply divided leaves and small pink flowers. The whole plant has a strong, distinctive, slightly unpleasant smell when crushed. Edible in small quantities as a garnish or tea herb — not a cooking green. More useful than its reputation suggests once the flavour is understood.

---

**Full Description:**
Herb Robert is one of those plants that most people recognise without knowing the name. The deeply divided, ferny leaves often flush red in autumn, the small bright pink flowers appear from April through October, and the whole plant produces a distinctive smell when handled — sharp, slightly musty, and not obviously appetising. The Latin name is apt: *robertianum* is thought to derive from medieval associations with a holy man, though the plant's more prosaic reputation in folk medicine is as a wound herb and insect repellent.

The taste is not pleasant enough to eat in any volume, and the smell makes bulk preparation unappealing. Think of it as a minor aromatic rather than a green.

The plant has been used historically in European folk medicine, and while the evidence for most such uses is thin, it is entirely safe to eat in the quantities a forager would realistically use.

---

An annual or biennial, 10–50cm, with brittle, often red-flushed stems. The leaves are deeply divided — pinnate to bipinnate — with 3–5 primary lobes, each further divided, giving a distinctly ferny appearance. The surface is slightly hairy. The whole plant often turns red in strong sun or as it ages. Flowers are 5-petalled, 8–14mm across, bright pink with darker veining, appearing from April throughout summer and into October. The fruit is a long-beaked capsule, 15–22mm. The smell when any part is crushed is strong, distinctive, and not garlic-like — useful in distinguishing it from other hedgerow plants.

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
The deeply divided leaves, red-flushed stems, and small bright pink geranium flowers are specific. No dangerous British plant matches this combination.

Common on old walls in gardens and churchyards. Found throughout Britain including upland areas of Scotland.

---

**In Season:**

**Culinary Uses:**
Use in small quantities. Two or three young leaves in a herbal tea blend add a bitter, aromatic note. Scatter a few raw young leaves over a finished salad for visual interest and a mild bitter edge. The plant has been used to make a simple infused vinegar. Do not cook as a standalone green — the smell and bitterness make it unpleasant in volume. A plant for experienced foragers looking to extend their repertoire rather than a staple.

## 25. Crab Apple

**Species Name:** Crab Apple
**Latin Name:** Malus sylvestris
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** crab-apple
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
Britain's native wild apple, producing small, hard, intensely tart fruits in September and October. Too sour to eat raw in any quantity, but excellent for jelly, wine, and as a natural pectin source in other fruit preserves. Distinguishing true wild crab apple from naturalised domestic apple escapes requires attention — both are edible but the true crab is rarer and worth knowing.

---

**Full Description:**
The true wild crab apple (*Malus sylvestris*) is a native tree of ancient hedgerows and old woodland edges, scarcer and more interesting than it appears. Most of what foragers encounter and pick as "crab apple" in Britain is in fact a feral domestic apple (*Malus domestica*) — a garden variety that has seeded itself into the wild through bird dispersal and grows in hedgerows, railway embankments, and waste ground throughout the country. Both are edible, both make good jelly, and distinguishing them requires looking at the leaf and twig rather than the fruit alone.

The fruit of a true wild crab is small — rarely more than 4cm — and is uniformly bitter and astringent. It is not edible raw in any useful quantity. It is, however, extremely high in pectin, which makes it invaluable for setting other low-pectin fruits in jams and jellies. Crab apple jelly is outstanding — tart, clear, and set firm — and it pairs well with game, mature cheese, and cold meat.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A small, thorny tree or large shrub, 2–10m. True wild crab (*M. sylvestris*) has twigs that are often thorn-tipped, hairless leaves at maturity (slightly hairy when young), and small fruits, 2–4cm, yellowish-green to yellow-flushed red, with a persistent calyx. Feral domestic apple (*M. domestica*) tends to have softly hairy leaf undersides, less thorny twigs, and larger, often more colourful fruit. In practice the distinction is difficult in the field — the two species hybridise freely. For foraging, treat all small wild apples as usable crab apples regardless of the precise taxonomy. The pink-flushed five-petalled flowers in April and May, and the small apple-like fruit with a persistent star-shaped calyx in autumn, are sufficient for identification.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous lookalikes for crab apple. No other British tree produces small apple-like fruits with a persistent calyx. Crab apples may be confused with wild cherries or bullaces in poor light, but the apple-like shape, the calyx, and the absence of a stone inside distinguish them on examination.

Avoid swallowing apple seeds in large quantities — like all members of the rose family, apple seeds contain cyanogenic compounds. A few seeds from incidentally swallowed whole fruit pose no realistic risk, but do not deliberately eat large quantities of seeds.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Ancient hedgerows, old woodland edges, scrubland, and field margins. True wild crab is most frequent in old lowland hedgerows and ancient woodland edges in southern and central England. Feral apple is widespread throughout Britain, common on railway embankments, scrubland, and disturbed ground wherever apple seeds have been distributed by birds. Productive trees tend to be in sunny, open positions.

---

**In Season:**
Fruit ripens from late August, peaking through September and October. Ripe crab apples fall readily from the tree when shaken. Quality after the first frosts can be good — the bitterness mellows slightly. Windfalls collected from beneath the tree are often perfectly usable. By November most fruit has been taken by birds or has rotted on the ground.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Crab apple jelly — the classic preparation. The fruit is simmered, mashed, and dripped through a jelly bag overnight, then boiled hard with sugar to set. No added pectin required; the natural pectin content is high enough to set firmly. The result is clear, amber to pink, tart, and excellent alongside game, pork, and mature cheese. Crab apple wine is straightforward and reliable. The fruit can be used as a pectin source in low-pectin jams such as blackberry or elderberry — add a handful of chopped crab apple to the pan. Dried, powdered crab apple adds sourness to spice mixes.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking crab apple fruit from public hedgerows and woodland edges for personal use is permitted. Fruit on private farmland hedgerows is technically the landowner's property — seek permission where ownership is clear.

## 26. Rowan

**Species Name:** Rowan
**Latin Name:** Sorbus aucuparia
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** rowan
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
The bright orange-red berries of the rowan tree, ripening in September in hedgerows, upland woodland, and urban streets throughout Britain. Too bitter and astringent to eat raw, but excellent for jelly, wine, and chutney. A reliable and abundant crop in most years, most easily picked from street trees in towns and cities.

---

**Full Description:**
Rowan is one of Britain's most familiar trees, found from urban streets to highland glens. In September the clusters of bright orange-red berries are conspicuous and abundant, and in most parts of Britain entirely unpicked — which is a straightforward opportunity for the forager willing to carry a bag.

The berries are not pleasant raw. They are bitter, astringent, and slightly acidic in combination — a mouthful is unpleasant enough to discourage further experimentation. Cooking with sugar transforms them: rowan jelly is a classic British preserve, tart and slightly bitter, excellent with game, lamb, and strong cheese. The bitterness is the point.

Street trees in towns and cities often provide the most accessible and most abundant crop. The berries are frequently left entirely untouched in urban settings, and trees alongside paths can yield large quantities with no difficulty. Upland woodland and hedgerow trees are often stripped by birds before October.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A medium-sized tree, 5–15m, with smooth, grey-brown bark and distinctive pinnate leaves: 11–19 oval, toothed leaflets arranged in opposite pairs along a central stalk, each leaflet 3–5cm long. The leaves are slightly hairy beneath when young and turn orange and red in autumn. The creamy white flowers appear in May in large, flat-topped clusters with an unpleasant, slightly almond-like smell. The berries ripen in August and September: 6–9mm, round, scarlet to orange-red, in dense clusters, each with a persistent calyx at the tip. The pinnate leaves and red berries together are unmistakable — no dangerous British tree matches this combination.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Elder (*Sambucus nigra*) has pinnate leaves superficially similar to rowan, but the leaves are much larger (5–12cm leaflets vs 3–5cm), the leaflets are broader and more oval, and the plant is a multi-stemmed shrub rather than a single-trunked tree. Elder berries are purple-black, not orange-red.

Whitebeam (*Sorbus aria*) has undivided, oval, white-backed leaves — not pinnate — and red berries with similar persistent calyces. Edible.

There are no dangerous lookalikes for rowan. No other British tree has pinnate leaves and orange-red berries in flat-topped clusters.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Throughout Britain from sea level to over 1,000m. One of the most altitude-tolerant native trees in the country. Found in upland woodland, moorland edges, hedgerows, rocky hillsides, and urban streets. Common as a street tree and in parks. Most abundant and productive in the north and west of Britain; in Scotland it is one of the most common native trees of open upland ground.

---

**In Season:**
Berries ripen from late August in the south to September in the north. Street trees in towns often ripen earlier than upland specimens. The peak is September. By late October birds have usually taken most of the crop from exposed trees; sheltered urban trees hold their berries longer. Pick at peak ripeness — when the berries are uniformly orange-red and come away from the cluster readily.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Rowan jelly is the primary use. Simmer the berries with water, strain through a jelly bag overnight, then boil the juice hard with sugar. The result sets firmly — the berries are high in pectin — and is deep orange-red, tart, and bitter. Pairs excellently with game, venison, lamb, and mature hard cheese. Rowan and crab apple jelly is a natural combination: the crab apple smooths the bitterness slightly while adding depth. Rowan wine is straightforward and good. Rowan berries in chutney add tartness and bitterness to balance sweetness. Do not eat raw in quantity — the parasorbic acid they contain causes gastrointestinal upset until broken down by cooking.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking berries from public street trees, public woodland, and common land for personal use is permitted. Urban street trees are usually managed by the local authority — picking fruit from them is generally not restricted in practice. Do not strip a tree bare.

## 27. Wild Strawberry

**Species Name:** Wild Strawberry
**Latin Name:** Fragaria vesca
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** wild-strawberry
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
Tiny, intensely flavoured strawberries found in woodland edges, grassland, and sunny banks from June to August. The fruit is rarely more than 1cm across, but the flavour is concentrated to a degree that cultivated strawberries do not approach. One of the great seasonal rewards — best eaten where they are found.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild strawberries are not a foraging staple in the practical sense — you will never pick enough in one session to make jam. They are instead one of the incidental pleasures of summer walks, a thing found in small quantities and eaten on the spot because that is when they taste best.

The fruit is miniature — rarely exceeding 1cm — and ripens unevenly across a patch, requiring selective picking rather than sweeping handfuls. But the flavour is exceptional: concentrated strawberry with a floral sweetness that the large, watery cultivated fruit does not have. Find a productive south-facing bank in late June and eat what you find.

The plant is common throughout Britain in the right habitats — open woodland edges, chalk grassland, hedgerow banks, and rocky ground with good sun exposure. It spreads by runners and can form dense, productive patches where conditions suit it.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low perennial with trifoliate leaves: three oval, toothed leaflets with prominent teeth at the tip, bright green above with silky hairs beneath. The plant spreads by long runners. The white five-petalled flowers are 12–18mm across, appearing from April and continuing into summer. The fruit is a true strawberry in miniature — red, conical, 6–10mm, with small yellow seeds on the surface and a green calyx of pointed sepals at the top. The flavour confirms identification: the sweetness is immediate and unmistakable.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Barren Strawberry (*Potentilla sterilis*) grows in similar habitats — woodland edges and hedgerow banks — and is easily confused before the fruit develops. The leaves are very similar but the leaflets have a notch at the tip rather than a prominent tooth, and the gap between the petals is visible (the petals do not overlap). It produces no proper fruit — only a small, dry, inedible seed cluster. Not dangerous but produces no edible yield.

Mock Strawberry (*Potentilla indica*, also known as *Duchesnea indica*) produces small red strawberry-like fruits with yellow flowers rather than white, found in some gardens and waste ground. Edible but essentially tasteless. Not dangerous.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for wild strawberry.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Sunny woodland edges, open woodland on thin, well-drained soils, chalk and limestone grassland, hedgerow banks, rocky grassland, and cliff-top scrub. Most productive on south-facing slopes with good sun exposure. Common throughout England and Wales, less abundant in Scotland and northern uplands. Thrives in calcareous (chalk and limestone) grassland.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from April. Fruit from late May in the most sheltered spots in the south, June across most of Britain, into August in the north and at altitude. Picking is best in the morning, when the fruit is cool and the flavour at its most pronounced. The season at any one site is short — two to three weeks of peak fruit — but different habitats and aspects extend the total window.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Eat where you find them. Wild strawberries are best raw and fresh, warmed by the sun. A small bowlful with cream is not improved by any further intervention. If you have enough — which is rare — a simple tart with pastry cream and a handful of fruit is the best preparation. Wild strawberry vinegar, made by steeping the fruit in white wine vinegar for a week, captures the flavour in a form that lasts and works well in dressings.

---

**Legal Notes:**

## 28. Wild Cherry

**Species Name:** Wild Cherry
**Latin Name:** Prunus avium
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** wild-cherry
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
Wild cherries ripen in July and August in woodland, hedgerows, and old orchards across Britain. The flavour ranges from sweet to sharply bitter depending on the individual tree. The fruit is small — 1–2cm — and the best trees are worth returning to year after year. Birds will take most of the crop before you get there unless you move quickly.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild cherry, or gean, is a common woodland tree whose fruit is largely left to the birds in Britain — an oversight, because the best trees produce fruit that is genuinely excellent. The cherries are small compared to cultivated varieties, red to deep red when ripe, and the flavour varies considerably between trees: some are sweet and worth eating raw by the handful; others are sharp or moderately bitter, better for cooking.

The tree is easy to identify when in flower, with its spectacular display of white blossom in April, and in fruit by the distinctively cherry-shaped drupes in July. The challenge is timing: the fruit ripens fast, the birds monitor productive trees closely, and a window of a few days at peak ripeness can be the difference between an excellent crop and bare branches.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A medium to large tree, 10–25m, with distinctive reddish-brown bark marked with horizontal lenticels (raised breathing pores that appear as pale brown lines running around the trunk). Older bark becomes grey-brown and plates away. Leaves are alternate, oval to oblong, 6–15cm, with a long pointed tip and finely toothed margins, with one or two red glands where the leaf stalk meets the blade. The white flowers are 2–3cm across, in clusters of 2–6, appearing in April with or just before the leaves. Cherries are 1–2cm, red to dark red, round, with a single hard stone inside. The stone and the drupe shape are diagnostic — no other British tree produces a similarly sized red stone fruit.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Bird Cherry (*Prunus padus*) is a smaller tree of upland and northern woodland, with creamy white flowers in long drooping racemes (rather than the clusters of wild cherry) and small, black, very bitter cherries. Edible but not pleasant raw.

Cherry Plum (*Prunus cerasifera*) produces larger fruit, red or yellow, on a less tall tree often found in hedgerows. Edible.

Bitter Cherry (*Prunus cerasus*) is similar to wild cherry but often shrubby and with more strongly bitter fruit. The cherries of any tree that prove extremely bitter should be cooked with sugar rather than eaten raw.

Cherry laurel (*Prunus laurocerasus*), a common garden hedge plant, produces black cherry-like fruits that are mildly toxic. It is a dense evergreen shrub, not a tall tree, with thick, glossy, elongated leaves quite unlike wild cherry. Do not confuse.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Deciduous woodland, particularly ancient woodland on well-drained, moderately fertile soils. Also found in hedgerows, along woodland rides, and in old orchards and parkland. Common throughout England, Wales, and into Scotland. Less frequent on acid, waterlogged, or chalk soils.

---

**In Season:**
Cherries ripen from late June in the warmest, most sheltered spots in southern England. Main season across most of Britain is July. In Scotland and at altitude, August. The fruit ripens fast once it starts — check productive trees every few days during the season rather than waiting for an assumed peak.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Eat ripe sweet fruit raw. For cooking, wild cherry jam requires a little more sugar and patience than cultivated varieties — the stone-to-flesh ratio is less favourable. Cherry brandy, following the same method as sloe gin, is excellent. The fruit combines well with summer berries in a mixed fruit compote. Sour trees are better cooked with sugar than eaten raw — stew the stoned fruit with a little water and sugar as a sauce for game or dark meat.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking wild cherries from public woodland for personal use is permitted. Fruit-bearing trees in private woodland are private property — seek permission where ownership is clear. Do not pick from trees on nature reserves without checking local guidelines.

---

## 29. Dewberry

**Species Name:** Dewberry
**Latin Name:** Rubus caesius
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** dewberry
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A bramble relative producing small, blue-black berries with a distinctive waxy grey-white bloom in August and September. Less abundant than blackberry, and found in more specific habitats — chalk grassland, coastal dunes, and scrubby hedgerows — but the flavour is excellent and the fruit is rarely picked by anyone else.

---

**Full Description:**
Dewberry is consistently overlooked, which works in the forager's favour. It occupies some of the same habitats as blackberry — hedgerows and scrubby ground — but is more specifically associated with calcareous grassland, coastal dunes, and open scrub on dry, well-drained soils. Where blackberry is picked heavily in September, dewberry on a chalk hillside or a coastal dune system is usually untouched.

The fruit is smaller and more loosely druped than blackberry — typically 3–10 large, rounded drupelets per berry rather than the tight cluster of smaller drupelets on a blackberry. The waxy, blue-grey bloom that coats each drupelet is the field character that distinguishes it at a glance. The flavour is good — slightly less sweet than blackberry, perhaps more aromatic.

The plant is lower-growing and less aggressively thorny than blackberry. The stems have a blue-grey waxy bloom similar to the fruit. It is a more delicate plant in every sense.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low, scrambling, weakly prickled bramble, 30–80cm. The stems are slender, round in cross-section, and covered with a bluish-grey waxy bloom. Leaves are trifoliate with 3 leaflets, somewhat similar to blackberry but with leaflets that are less deeply toothed. Flowers are white, 5-petalled, slightly larger relative to the plant than blackberry. The fruit is 1–2cm, composed of 3–10 large drupelets, each coated with a prominent waxy grey-white bloom — this bloom is the most reliable field character. The whole fruit appears grey-blue rather than black, even when ripe. The flesh is juicy, pink-red, and without a central plug.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Blackberry (*Rubus fruticosus* agg.) is more vigorous, more thorny, and produces larger clusters of small, tightly packed, glossy black drupelets without a waxy bloom. Both are edible. The waxy bloom and the loose, large drupelets of dewberry are diagnostic.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for dewberry or any closely related *Rubus* species in Britain.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Chalk and limestone grassland, coastal dunes and dune scrub, dry scrubby hedgerows, and open calcareous woodland edges. More specific in its habitat than blackberry — look for it on chalk downland in southern England (North and South Downs, Chilterns, Cotswolds), limestone grassland in the north, and coastal dune systems throughout Britain. Less common on acid or heavy clay soils.

---

**In Season:**
August to September. The season overlaps with and is slightly earlier than blackberry, which can help narrow the identification in the field. In most years the crop is gone by late September.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use exactly as you would use blackberries: raw, in jam, crumbles, or tarts. The flavour is slightly more aromatic and the higher juice content makes dewberry jam slightly looser-set than blackberry, which can be corrected by adding crab apple or a little lemon juice for pectin. The fruit is fragile and does not travel as well as blackberry — use the same day if possible.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking dewberries from chalk downland, coastal dune systems, and public land for personal use is permitted. Some chalk downland SSSIs may have specific picking guidelines — read notices at the site. Not a protected species.

---

## 30. Barberry

**Species Name:** Barberry
**Latin Name:** Berberis vulgaris
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** barberry
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
A spiny, yellow-flowered hedgerow shrub producing small, elongated red berries in autumn. Extremely tart — the high acidity makes the berries unpleasant raw but outstanding in jams, jellies, shrubs, and as a souring agent in cooking. Now rare in some regions due to eradication as an intermediate host for wheat rust — look for it on chalk and limestone soils in southern England.

---

**Full Description:**
Barberry has an interesting history in Britain. It was systematically eradicated from agricultural areas through much of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, as it serves as an intermediate host for black stem rust (*Puccinia graminis*), a serious fungal disease of wheat and other cereals. The result is that it is now absent from many of the lowland agricultural landscapes where it was once common. It persists on chalk downland, limestone scrub, and in some older hedgerows where eradication was incomplete, and is occasionally grown as an ornamental in gardens and parks.

The berries are extremely tart — the highest acidity of any common British wild fruit. Raw, they are bracingly sour rather than pleasant. In cooking with sugar, particularly in preserves and fruit cheeses, that acidity is an asset: barberry jelly is vivid red-orange, sets firmly, and is sharp enough to cut through richness in a way that most preserves cannot.

The plant is worth knowing and worth picking where it is found. The crop is usually small, and competition from other foragers is essentially zero.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A deciduous shrub, 1–3m, with distinctive three-parted or five-parted spines at the nodes — this is the primary identification feature. The bark is grey-brown. Leaves are 2–5cm, oval with spiny-toothed margins, growing in clusters from the nodes between the spines. Yellow flowers appear in April and May in drooping clusters. The berries are elongated, 8–10mm, bright red, with a persistent calyx at the base and usually containing 1–2 hard seeds. The combination of three-parted spines at the nodes, oval toothed leaves in clusters, and elongated red berries is specific to *Berberis vulgaris* and its relatives.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Guelder Rose (*Viburnum opulus*) produces red berries in autumn but is a multi-stemmed shrub with no spines, large lobed leaves, and berries growing in flat-topped clusters. The berries are toxic when raw.

Holly (*Ilex aquifolium*) has red berries and spiny leaves but no spines at the nodes, and the berries are round rather than elongated. Holly berries are toxic.

Rowan (*Sorbus aucuparia*) has red berries and pinnate leaves, both quite unlike barberry.

The three-parted spines at the nodes are diagnostic for barberry. No dangerous British plant has this feature.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Chalk and limestone scrub, old hedgerows on calcareous soils, and limestone woodland edges. Most frequent in southern England on the North and South Downs, the Chilterns, and in the limestone areas of Yorkshire and the north. Increasingly found in urban landscapes as an ornamental planting. Absent from most intensively farmed lowland areas due to historical eradication.

---

**In Season:**
Berries ripen in September and October. They persist on the shrub into November in some years. Pick when uniformly bright red. Green berries are unripe and even more intensely sour.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Barberry jelly — the classic preparation and the best use of the fruit. High natural pectin, sets firm, brilliant red-orange, and sharp enough to pair with fatty or rich dishes. Barberry shrub (a drinking vinegar — berries steeped in vinegar with sugar) is excellent. The berries are used extensively in Persian cooking as a souring agent in rice dishes, particularly with poultry. In this context they work dried rather than fresh. Dried barberries can be prepared from fresh fruit spread on a tray and dried slowly in a low oven.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking barberry berries from public land for personal use is permitted. On chalk downland SSSIs, local guidelines may apply. The species is uncommon enough in some areas that it is worth not stripping any individual plant entirely.

---

**SEO Description:** Barberry berries ripen in September on chalk scrub in southern England. Intensely tart — excellent for jelly and Persian cooking.

## 31. Bullace

**Species Name:** Bullace
**Latin Name:** Prunus domestica subsp. insititia
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** bullace
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
A wild or semi-wild plum found in old hedgerows across England, producing small, round, dark blue-black or yellow-green fruits in September and October. Less well known than sloe, with a rounder, more plum-like flavour and considerably less astringency. Excellent for jam, wine, and fruit cheese.

---

**Full Description:**
Bullace sits somewhere between sloe and domestic plum — related to both, with more flesh than a sloe and more character than a supermarket plum. It is a plant of old hedgerows and orchards, often a relic of earlier cultivation rather than truly wild, and it is found throughout lowland England and into Wales wherever old field boundaries and untouched hedgerow margins survive.

The fruits vary considerably in colour and size between individual trees: some produce dark blue-black fruits with a powdery bloom similar to sloe, larger and rounder; others produce greenish-yellow or reddish-purple fruits. All have in common a flesh that is less astringent than sloe — some trees produce fruit that is nearly pleasant raw, though most are sharp enough to benefit from cooking and sugar.

It is rarely picked and poorly understood, which leaves productive bullace hedges available to anyone who takes the time to learn the plant. A good bullace tree in October can yield kilograms of fruit.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A shrubby tree or large shrub, 2–6m, similar to blackthorn but generally less densely thorny. The bark is darker than blackthorn. Leaves are 4–8cm, oval to elliptical, softly hairy beneath, with finely toothed margins — larger and softer than the small, dull blackthorn leaf. The white flowers appear in April, in pairs or small clusters, slightly later than blackthorn. The fruit is 1.5–3cm, round, not elongated — this roundness distinguishes it from sloe at a glance. The colour ranges from dark blue-black with bloom to yellow-green or reddish-purple. The flesh is yellow-green, juicy, with a large stone.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Sloe (*Prunus spinosa*) is smaller-fruited (8–15mm vs 15–30mm for bullace), more densely thorny, flowers earlier on bare branches in March, and has a much smaller, more astringent fruit. Both edible; the size difference is the clearest field distinction.

Wild Plum (*Prunus domestica*) is a larger, less thorny tree with bigger fruit and less astringency. Also edible. The distinction between bullace and wild plum is one that taxonomists argue about — for foraging, treat any plum-like hedgerow fruit as usable.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for bullace.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Old hedgerows, woodland edges, and the remnants of traditional orchards in lowland England and Wales. Often a relic of older mixed hedgerow planting or a survivor from abandoned orchards and smallholdings. Most frequently encountered in the southern half of England but present further north in old farming landscapes.

---

**In Season:**
September and October. The fruit ripens later than most summer stone fruit and is best picked after the first cold nights of autumn when the astringency has reduced. In mild years some trees produce fruit into November.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Bullace jam is the most common preparation — stone the fruit first (the stone-to-flesh ratio is more favourable than sloe), cook with sugar and a little water. Bullace wine is straightforward and reliable. Bullace cheese (a firm fruit paste made by cooking the strained pulp with sugar until very thick) is excellent alongside strong cheeses and cold game. The fruit can replace damsons in any recipe and is a direct substitute for sloe in gin, though the result is softer and more plum-like.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking bullace from public hedgerows and woodland edges for personal use is permitted. Fruit from hedgerows bordering private farmland is technically private property — seek permission where ownership is clear.

---

## 32. Juniper

**Species Name:** Juniper
**Latin Name:** Juniperus communis
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** juniper
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
Britain's native juniper produces the small, dark blue-black berries used to flavour gin. The plant is scarce and heavily protected in many of its remaining habitats — picking from wild stands requires care, restraint, and knowledge of where picking is permitted. Only pick ripe, dark berries; unripe green berries are toxic in quantity.

---

**Full Description:**
Juniper is one of only three native conifers in Britain, and one of the most threatened. Its populations have declined sharply over the last century due to overgrazing, lack of regeneration, and changes in land management. The remaining wild stands — on chalk downland in southern England, limestone pavement in the north, and upland moorland in Scotland — are often protected and in some cases fragile enough that significant picking would cause genuine damage.

This is not a plant to forage casually. Pick a small quantity from a large, clearly productive stand on accessible land, take only ripe berries, and never strip a plant. In many of its habitats, particularly on SSSIs and nature reserves, picking may be restricted or prohibited.

The berries themselves take two years to ripen, which means ripe dark berries and unripe green berries coexist on the same plant at the same time. Only the dark, ripe berries should be picked. Unripe green juniper berries contain higher concentrations of volatile oils that cause gastrointestinal irritation and, in genuinely large quantities, kidney damage.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A variable evergreen shrub or small tree, prostrate to upright, 30cm to 10m depending on habitat and form. The leaves are needle-like, 1–2cm, sharp-pointed, arranged in whorls of three. Each needle has a single broad white band (stomatal band) on the upper surface — this single white band distinguishes it from other conifers with needle-like leaves. The bark is reddish-brown and fibrous. The "berries" are technically fleshy cones — 5–9mm, round, ripening from green in the first year to dark blue-black with a powdery grey-blue bloom in the second year. Both ripe and unripe berries are present simultaneously. Crush a ripe berry: the smell is intensely resinous and aromatic — the smell of gin.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Yew (*Taxus baccata*) also has needle-like leaves and grows in some of the same habitats, particularly chalk downland and limestone woodland. Yew needles are flat, softer, and arranged in two ranks along the stem rather than in whorls of three, and the plant produces red fleshy berries with a visible central seed — not round, dark, fleshy cones. Yew is extremely toxic — all parts except the red flesh of the berry are poisonous. Do not confuse.

Cypress species (*Cupressus*, *Chamaecyparis*) are common in gardens and occasionally naturalised. They have scale-like rather than needle-like leaves and different fruit structure.

Confirm the whorled needles, the single white band on each needle, and the round dark berry with a waxy bloom before picking.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Chalk downland and chalk scrub in southern England (North Downs, Box Hill); limestone pavement and rocky limestone grassland in northern England (Yorkshire Dales, Cumbria); open moorland and pine woodland in Scotland. Also found on coastal clifftops in some areas. A highly specific habitat specialist — it does not grow on neutral or acid grassland without underlying chalk or limestone. Increasingly scarce and locally protected.

---

**In Season:**
Ripe dark berries from September through November. The berries are ripe when dark blue-black with a clear grey-blue waxy bloom. Pick selectively — only dark berries, leaving the green unripe ones. A small quantity goes a long way in cooking; you need very few berries relative to their flavour contribution.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Juniper berries are a spice, not a bulk fruit. Crush them before use to release the volatile oils. Use sparingly: 3–6 berries per portion of meat or game is typically sufficient. Classic uses are as a seasoning for venison, wild boar, and duck; in a dry rub for cured meat; in gin (obviously); and in sauerkraut and fermented cabbage dishes from central European tradition. Dried berries keep well in an airtight jar for a year.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Juniper is not listed on Schedule 8 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981, so picking berries for personal use is not prohibited by species protection law. However, many juniper populations are on SSSIs and nature reserves where picking is restricted by site-specific bylaws. Always check notices at the site. Take a very small quantity only — a handful of berries is sufficient for most purposes and has no meaningful impact on a healthy stand.

---

## 33. Wild Gooseberry

**Species Name:** Wild Gooseberry
**Latin Name:** Ribes uva-crispa
**Type:** Berries
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** wild-gooseberry
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
A spiny shrub of hedgerows, rocky woodland, and scrub, producing small green or occasionally reddish berries from June to August. Native or naturalised throughout Britain. The berries are sharply tart and seedy but the flavour is excellent when cooked — the wild version has considerably more character than cultivated gooseberries.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild gooseberry is less easy to find in quantity than its cultivated relative, and the berries are smaller and seedier, but the flavour is worth seeking out. It grows in hedgerows, rocky scrub, and the edges of ancient woodland throughout Britain, often unnoticed because the plant is not particularly striking outside the brief fruiting window.

The berries, picked in June and July while still firm and green, are ideal for gooseberry fool and tarts. Riper, softer berries in August have a sweeter flavour but less acidity. The sharp, floral gooseberry character is stronger in the wild plant than in any cultivated variety — useful in cooking where that sourness is the point.

Finding a productive wild gooseberry in a quiet hedgerow feels like a genuine discovery, which is part of the appeal. The small size of most wild populations means picking in quantity is rarely possible; take what the plant offers without stripping it.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low, dense, spiny shrub, 60–150cm, with three-parted spines at the nodes and smaller spines on the internodes. The leaves are 2–5cm, rounded to kidney-shaped with 3–5 rounded lobes and toothed margins, slightly hairy. Flowers are small, drooping, greenish-pink, appearing in March and April. The berries are 1–2cm, oval to round, green (sometimes reddish or yellowish when ripe), with distinct longitudinal veins visible through the skin and fine hairs or bristles on the surface. The spiny shrub, lobed leaves, and distinctly veined, hairy berries are the key identification features.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Cultivated gooseberry (*Ribes uva-crispa* var. *sativum*) is the same species, just a garden escape — equally edible and often indistinguishable.

Redcurrant (*Ribes rubrum*) and Blackcurrant (*Ribes nigrum*) are close relatives that grow in similar habitats. Both produce berries in hanging clusters rather than singly or in pairs; the berries are smooth, not hairy; and the leaves have a distinctive strong scent when crushed (particularly blackcurrant). Both are edible.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for wild gooseberry in Britain.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, rocky scrub, limestone woodland edges, and the bases of old walls. Occurs throughout Britain and is considered native in some areas, particularly in the north of England and Scotland on limestone. Elsewhere it is likely a garden escape or relic of cultivation. Most productive in partial shade on well-drained, moderately fertile soil.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers in March and April. Berries from June. Firm, green, tart berries in June and July are ideal for cooking. Softer, sweeter ripe berries in August. The season is short — most plants have dropped or lost their berries to birds by September.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use exactly as cultivated gooseberries but expect more seeds and smaller fruit. Gooseberry fool — stewed with sugar, cooled, folded through whipped cream — is the simplest and best use. Gooseberry jam sets firmly (high pectin) and keeps well. The tart green berries in June are an excellent match for elderflower — the classic combination of elderflower cordial alongside fresh gooseberries, or gooseberry and elderflower jam, works with wild fruit of both.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking wild gooseberries for personal use from public hedgerows and woodland for personal use is permitted. Not a protected species.

---

**SEO Title:** Wild Gooseberry — Foraging Guide UK | The Foragers

**SEO Description:** Wild gooseberries ripen in June and July in hedgerows across Britain. Sharper and more flavourful than cultivated. UK foraging guide.

## 34. Dog Rose

**Species Name:** Dog Rose
**Latin Name:** Rosa canina
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** dog-rose
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul

---

**Short Description:**
The most common wild rose in Britain, flowering in June and July in hedgerows, scrubland, and woodland edges. The pale pink or white flowers are edible and mildly fragrant, used in syrups, vinegars, and as a garnish. The same plant produces rosehips in autumn — covered separately. Pick flowers in the morning when fully open and the fragrance is at its strongest.

---

**Full Description:**
Dog rose is the hedgerow rose of English summers — the scrambling, arching shrub with pale pink five-petalled flowers that appears in June along field boundaries and country lanes. It flowers for three to four weeks, the blooms lasting individually for only a day or two before dropping their petals.

The flowers are less intensely flavoured than cultivated rose varieties, but they are freely available and have a genuine delicate fragrance that works in cold preparations. Rose petal syrup, rose petal vinegar, and crystallised rose petals are the most useful applications. The flowers must be used on the day of picking — they deteriorate within hours.

The white base of each petal, where it attached to the calyx, is mildly bitter and should be removed before use. Use the petals only, not the stamens or calyx.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A scrambling, arching shrub to 3m, with curved thorns. Leaves are pinnate with 5–7 oval, toothed leaflets. The flowers are 4–6cm across, with five petals, pale pink to white, often paler towards the centre, with a cluster of yellow stamens. The petals are slightly notched at the tip. A strong, classic rose fragrance — present but not as concentrated as cultivated roses. The flowers appear in June and July, singly or in small clusters. Later in the season the developing hips are visible below the petals — green, elongated ovals, which ripen red by September.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Field Rose (*Rosa arvensis*) is similar — a trailing, smaller-flowered rose with white flowers and a single style fused into a column (rather than the separate styles of dog rose). Equally edible.

Burnet Rose (*Rosa pimpinellifolia*) grows on coastal dunes and limestone grassland with small white flowers and distinctive dark purple-black hips. Equally edible.

Sweet Briar (*Rosa rubiginosa*) has strongly scented leaves (apple-like) and smaller flowers. Equally edible.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for British wild roses.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, scrubland, woodland edges, downland, and roadsides throughout Britain. One of the most common flowering shrubs of the lowland British countryside. Most abundant in sunny, open hedgerows on well-drained soils.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from late May in the south, June across most of Britain, into July. The peak is brief — three to four weeks. Pick in the morning when the flowers are fully open and the scent is strongest. Avoid flowers that are already losing petals or showing browning at the edges.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Rose petal syrup — petals steeped in hot sugar syrup, strained, and bottled — is the most useful preparation and has a genuine, delicate flavour. Rose petal vinegar follows the same principle. Crystallised petals, coated with egg white and fine sugar and dried, make a long-lasting garnish. Scatter fresh petals over desserts or fold into whipped cream. The white base of each petal should be removed — cut or tear it away — as it is bitter. Do not heat the petals for long: the delicate flavour volatilises quickly.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking dog rose flowers from public hedgerows for personal use is permitted. Picking flowers reduces the rosehip crop on the same plant later in the season — pick with this in mind and leave most flowers unpicked on any individual plant.

---

## 35. Giant Puffball

**Species Name:** Giant Puffball
**Latin Name:** Calvatia gigantea
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** giant-puffball
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
One of Britain's most spectacular fungi — a pure white, spherical fruiting body that can reach the size of a football or larger. Edible only when the flesh inside is pure, uniform white throughout. Once any yellowing or browning begins inside, the fungus is past eating. One dangerous lookalike at the button stage requires a specific identification check.

---

**Full Description:**
Giant puffball is hard to miss. A white sphere sitting in a field, meadow, or woodland edge at the right time of year draws attention from some distance. In a good year on good ground — old pasture, parkland, or the edge of deciduous woodland — the same site can produce multiple specimens.

The edibility window is specific. Slice the puffball in half from top to bottom: the flesh inside must be pure, uniform white throughout, with no colour variation, no developing gills or cap structure, and no dark patches. White throughout: edible. Any other colour: not edible.

The reason for the internal check is the dangerous lookalike: the button-stage egg of the Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*) and the Destroying Angel (*Amanita virosa*) is small, white, and spherical, and can superficially resemble a very young puffball at the soil surface. Cut it open and the developing cap and gill structure are immediately visible inside as a distinct shape — not the uniform white flesh of a puffball. This check is non-negotiable.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Fruiting body is spherical to slightly flattened, 10–60cm across, exceptionally larger. Surface is smooth to slightly scurfy when young, pure white, becoming cream-yellow then brown as it ages and the spore mass develops. The base has a narrow sterile attachment point where it connects to mycelium in the soil — not a stem. Cut the fungus in half vertically: the flesh must be uniformly pure white throughout, with no internal structure, no colour gradient, no gills, cap outline, or stipe development visible. The outer skin peels away cleanly from the white flesh on a fresh, edible specimen.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*) and Destroying Angel (*Amanita virosa*) button stage: young Amanita species emerge as spherical white "eggs" at or just below soil level, covered in a white universal veil, which can superficially resemble a small puffball. Cut the specimen in half: if you see the outline of a cap, gills, and stipe developing inside — as a distinct structure within the egg — you have an Amanita. Do not eat it. Death Cap causes liver and kidney failure and is frequently fatal. Destroying Angel is similarly lethal.

Common Puffball (*Lycoperdon perlatum*) is much smaller (3–6cm), has a distinct stem-like base, and often has small spines or warts on the surface. Edible when flesh is white throughout.

Earthball (*Scleroderma citrinum*) is hard, yellowish-brown, warty, and has dark purple-black flesh inside from an early stage. Toxic.

Cut every specimen in half before cooking. White throughout: proceed. Anything else: discard.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Old pasture, parkland, and the margins of deciduous woodland. Most productive on old, unimproved grassland that has not been ploughed or heavily fertilised. Tends to return to the same sites in good years. Also found on roadside verges and garden lawns in some areas. Typically fruits after warm, wet summer weather followed by a period of stable conditions in late August through October.

---

**In Season:**
August to October. Individual specimens have a short edibility window — perhaps a week at most from appearance to over-ripeness. The flesh yellows quickly and once any discolouration begins the fungus is not worth eating. Check any site you know to be productive every few days during the season.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
The best preparation is the simplest: slice the puffball into 2cm steaks and fry in butter over medium-high heat until golden on both sides. The texture is firm and the flavour mild but genuinely good — creamy, slightly nutty. It absorbs flavours from the pan well, so good butter and a little seasoning is all it needs. Very large specimens can be sliced into multiple steaks — a single large puffball can feed several people. It does not dry or preserve well; eat fresh within 24 hours of picking.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public land is generally permitted. Some nature reserves and SSSIs restrict picking. Do not pick in quantities that exceed immediate use — the fungus has a role in the ecosystem and takes large amounts of mycelium to produce.

## 36. Hedgehog Mushroom

**Species Name:** Hedgehog Mushroom
**Latin Name:** Hydnum repandum
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** hedgehog-mushroom
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
One of the safest edible fungi in Britain to identify. The underside bears dense pale spines rather than gills — a feature shared by no dangerous British species. The flavour is mild, nutty, and slightly peppery, and holds up well to cooking. Found in deciduous and mixed woodland from August to November.

---

**Full Description:**
Hedgehog mushroom is a forager's friend precisely because it is so straightforward. The spiny underside is not shared by any dangerous British fungus, which removes the most anxiety-inducing step of fungi identification. Once you know the spines, you know the hedgehog mushroom.

The flavour is mild and versatile — not as assertive as chanterelle or cep, but reliably good. The slight pepperiness mellows with cooking. It works well in dishes where a subtle, nutty mushroom flavour is wanted without the dominant earthiness of some other woodland fungi.

It is also a generous producer. In good years it appears in quantity in suitable woodland and can be found when other species have finished. The season extends into November, giving it value as a late-autumn find.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 4–17cm across, irregular, convex to wavy-edged, cream to pale tan-orange, smooth and dry. The surface is matte. The margin is typically wavy and irregular. The key identification feature is the underside: dense, crowded, pale cream spines (technically "teeth"), 3–6mm long, running slightly down the stem. Run a finger across the underside and you feel the spines clearly. The spines are the definitive character — nothing dangerous in Britain has them. The stem is 2–7cm, thick, solid, the same colour as the cap or slightly paler. The flesh is firm, white, and does not change colour when cut. Smell is mild and pleasant.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Terracotta Hedgehog (*Hydnum rufescens*) is a closely related species with a smaller cap (2–7cm) and more reddish-orange colour. Equally edible and often found alongside *H. repandum*. Treat identically.

No dangerous British fungi have spiny undersides. The hedgehog mushroom's spines are the single most reliable safety feature in British fungi foraging.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Deciduous and mixed woodland, particularly under beech, oak, and mixed broadleaf on well-drained soils. Forms mycorrhizal associations with trees and fruits in established woodland. Often found in the same locations year after year. Prefers undisturbed leaf litter. Found throughout Britain, most reliably in established deciduous woodland in England and Wales.

---

**In Season:**
August to November, with peak fruiting from September to October. One of the later-finishing edible species and often findable in November when most others have finished. Fruiting is most reliable after warm, wet conditions in late summer and autumn.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Sauté in butter until the moisture has cooked out — they release less water than many woodland fungi. The slight pepperiness mellows to a gentle warmth in the finished dish. Excellent in omelettes, on toast, in pasta, or as a component in a mixed mushroom dish. Pairs particularly well with cream and thyme. Can be dried successfully — the flavour concentrates and the texture, once rehydrated, is better than many dried fungi. Slice thinly before drying.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland is generally permitted. Check notices at nature reserves and SSSIs. Take what you can use and leave the rest.

## 37. Oyster Mushroom

**Species Name:** Oyster Mushroom
**Latin Name:** Pleurotus ostreatus
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** oyster-mushroom
**Seasons:** Oct, Nov, Dec, Jan, Feb, Mar

---

**Short Description:**
A shelf fungus growing in tiered clusters on the trunks and stumps of deciduous trees, most commonly beech and oak, from October through to early spring. The fan-shaped, grey-blue caps and white gills that run down a short, off-centre stem are distinctive. One of the most reliably identifiable edible fungi in Britain, and among the best in flavour.

---

**Full Description:**
Oyster mushroom is a woodland find with genuine culinary value. The flavour is mild, sweet, and delicate — less earthy than most woodland fungi, closer to the cultivated version sold in supermarkets (which are the same species) but fresher and more fragrant when picked young.

It grows as a saprophyte on dead or dying deciduous wood — beech stumps are the classic substrate, though it also colonises oak, ash, and other broadleaf trees. The tiered clusters of overlapping caps are conspicuous and the habitat narrows identification considerably. Finding oyster mushrooms is largely a matter of learning to scan tree trunks and stumps rather than the woodland floor.

Young, fresh caps are the best eating. The flesh is firm and white; older specimens become tough and the flavour dulls. Pick the caps before the margins have curled upward or begun to dry.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Caps are fan-shaped to semicircular, 5–20cm across, growing in overlapping clusters from wood. The surface is smooth, grey-blue to grey-brown in young specimens, paler in older ones. The margin is smooth and inrolled when young. The gills are white, crowded, and run down the stem — decurrent. The stem is short, 1–3cm, thick, off-centre or lateral (the cap attaches towards one side rather than centrally), white. Flesh is firm and white. Smell is mild and pleasant. The growth habit — fan-shaped caps in tiered clusters on dead wood — combined with the white decurrent gills and off-centre stem is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Angel Wings (*Pleurocybella porrigens*) is white, fan-shaped, and grows on conifer wood rather than deciduous. It has been implicated in fatalities in Japan, though its toxicity in Britain is not established. Grow on conifers only, much smaller (2–8cm), pure white with no blue-grey tone. Do not eat from conifer wood.

Crepidotus species are small, white to brownish, bracket-forming fungi on wood with brown spores — not white. None is a match in size or colour.

Velvet Roll-Rim (*Tapinella atrotomentosa*) is brown-capped, with brown-tinted gills and a velvety stem growing from conifer wood. Not closely similar.

Confirm deciduous substrate, grey-blue cap, and white decurrent gills on every specimen.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Dead and dying deciduous trees and stumps, particularly beech, oak, and ash. Found throughout Britain in deciduous woodland, parks, and anywhere mature broadleaf trees occur. Most productive in autumn and winter — the fruiting season is longer than almost any other British edible fungus and extends into early spring in mild years.

---

**In Season:**
October to March. The season is specifically a cold-weather one — oyster mushrooms fruit most reliably from October through December and can continue into February and March. They can appear in mild spells at almost any time of year but the main flush is autumn and winter. A winter woodland walk that finds nothing else on the floor should check tree trunks and stumps for oysters.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Sauté in butter over medium-high heat until the edges are golden and the moisture has evaporated. The caps wilt considerably in the pan — start with more than you think you need. Oyster mushrooms work well in stir-fries, pasta, on toast, and as a component of any mixed mushroom dish. They pair well with garlic, thyme, and cream. The stems can be tough — trim them and add to stock instead. Oyster mushrooms dry acceptably, though the fresh flavour is better.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland is generally permitted. Check notices at nature reserves. Take young, fresh specimens and leave older, deteriorating ones — they continue to release spores and contribute to woodland ecology.

---

## 38. Chicken of the Woods

**Species Name:** Chicken of the Woods
**Latin Name:** Laetiporus sulphureus
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** chicken-of-the-woods
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
A spectacular bracket fungus with vivid yellow and orange tiered shelves, growing on the trunks of living and dead deciduous trees. The young flesh is edible and has a texture resembling chicken — dense, firm, and fibrous — but causes reactions in some people, particularly when growing on certain tree species. Only eat young, fresh growth from the outer edges, and not from yew, robinia, or conifers.

---

**Full Description:**
Chicken of the Woods is one of the most visually striking fungi in Britain. A fresh fruiting body — vivid orange and sulphur yellow, sometimes spanning half a metre across — is visible from considerable distance and unmistakable. The common name is apt: the texture of young flesh is genuinely close to chicken, firm and slightly fibrous, which makes it one of the more versatile edible fungi in the kitchen.

The complications are specific and worth knowing clearly. First: this fungus causes gastrointestinal or allergic reactions in a proportion of people, even when fresh and well-cooked. The reaction rate is higher when the fungus is growing on certain tree species — yew, robinia (false acacia), and conifers are associated with a higher incidence of adverse effects. Specimens on oak, beech, and sweet chestnut are generally considered safer. Second: old, dry, or deteriorating specimens are more likely to cause reactions. Only eat young, fresh, soft growth from the outer edges of the bracket — the flesh should be pale, moist, and tender, not tough, dry, or crumbly. Third: try a small amount the first time you eat it, regardless of tree species or freshness.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Fruiting body is a series of overlapping, fan-shaped brackets, 10–60cm across per cluster, growing in a tiered mass from the trunk or base of a tree. The upper surface of each bracket is vivid orange to orange-red when fresh, fading to pale yellow-cream with age. The underside and margin are bright sulphur yellow — this two-tone colouring is the key visual feature. The pore surface on the underside is finely porous, not gill-like. The flesh is pale cream-yellow, firm, and moist in young growth. The smell is mild and pleasant. There is nothing in Britain that looks like this.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous British fungus resembles fresh Chicken of the Woods. The vivid orange and sulphur yellow colouring combined with the bracket habit on deciduous trees is entirely specific.

Giant Polypore (*Meripilus giganteus*) is large, brown-capped with a white pore surface that stains black when bruised, and grows at the base of trees in compound clusters from buried roots. Edible but poor quality. Not similar in colour.

The risk with this species is not misidentification but host tree and freshness. Check both.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Living and dead deciduous trees, particularly oak, sweet chestnut, cherry, willow, and beech. The bracket grows from wounds, old pruning cuts, or the base of the trunk. Most commonly found on mature oak and sweet chestnut in English woodland and parkland. Specimens on yew and robinia (false acacia) should not be eaten. Found throughout Britain but most productive in the established woodland and parkland of England.

---

**In Season:**
May to October. The season is long but the edibility window for any individual fruiting body is shorter — young, fresh growth in its first week or two is the target. The outer, growing edge of the bracket is the best eating. Return to productive trees annually — the mycelium fruits from the same host repeatedly.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Slice the young, tender outer growth into pieces and sauté in butter or oil over medium-high heat. The texture holds up well and does not collapse like many fungi. Works well in place of chicken in stir-fries, curries, and pasta sauces where texture is important. Breadcrumbed and fried, the texture resembles a chicken escalope. Season generously — the flavour is mild. Do not eat old, dry, or crumbly growth.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland is generally permitted. Check notices at nature reserves and SSSIs.

---

## 39. Beefsteak Fungus

**Species Name:** Beefsteak Fungus
**Latin Name:** Fistulina hepatica
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** beefsteak-fungus
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
A striking bracket fungus growing at the base of old oak and sweet chestnut trees, with a surface and flesh that genuinely resembles raw beef liver. Edible when young and fresh — the flesh is rich, slightly sour, and complex. One of the more unusual edible fungi in Britain, and straightforward to identify once you know what you are looking at.

---

**Full Description:**
Beefsteak fungus is one of those species that rewards the forager's willingness to look at something strange and decide it is food. The resemblance to a thick piece of raw liver is not metaphorical — the surface is reddish-brown and glistening, the cross-section reveals marbled cream-and-red flesh, and a cut surface exudes a reddish liquid. It looks like meat.

It grows specifically on old, mature oak and sweet chestnut — most often at the base of ancient or veteran trees, and consistently on the same tree year after year where conditions are stable. The brown, slightly bitter staining it produces in the timber of host trees gives "brown oak" its characteristic colour, prized in furniture making.

Young specimens in August and September are the best eating. The flesh becomes tougher and more acidic as the fungus ages, and the texture of old specimens deteriorates to something unpleasant.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Fruiting body is a thick, tongue-like to fan-shaped bracket, 10–30cm across, 2–6cm thick. The upper surface is reddish-brown, slightly sticky, and rough-textured — remarkably liver-like. The margin is paler, rounded, and slightly pinkish when young. The underside has a pore surface of closely packed individual tubes, cream to pale pink, that bruise to reddish-brown when pressed and can be peeled away in layers. Flesh is 2–4cm thick, cream to pink, marbled with reddish streaks. A cut surface exudes a red-tinged watery liquid. The smell is mild and slightly acidic. The combination of liver-like surface, marbled flesh, and individual tube structure on the underside growing on old oak or sweet chestnut is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous British fungus resembles beefsteak fungus closely. The liver-like surface, marbled flesh, and specific host tree combination narrow identification effectively.

Chicken of the Woods (*Laetiporus sulphureus*) is bright orange and sulphur yellow — nothing like the reddish-brown of beefsteak fungus.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
The base and lower trunk of old, mature oak and sweet chestnut trees. Most frequently found on ancient or veteran oak in old parkland, wood pasture, and ancient woodland. Less common than some bracket fungi but reliable on the right trees. Found throughout Britain wherever old oak and sweet chestnut occur; most abundant in the ancient wood-pasture landscapes of southern England.

---

**In Season:**
August to October. Young, fresh brackets in August and September are the best quality. By October the flesh has often toughened and the acidity has increased. Check any productive tree from late July onwards.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Slice young flesh thinly and eat raw in a salad with strong, sharp dressing — the slightly sour, rich flavour works well against acidity. Sautéed in butter, the flesh toughens and the flavour becomes more intense and bitter — use in small quantities alongside other ingredients rather than as the main component. Marinating in olive oil and grilling briefly works better than long cooking. The flavour is strong enough to divide opinion; the raw salad preparation is the most reliable way to appreciate it.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland and parkland is generally permitted. Ancient and veteran trees in many historic parklands are protected — take the fungus from the base without damaging the tree, and do not remove the whole bracket where it is still actively growing.

## 40. Velvet Shank

**Species Name:** Velvet Shank
**Latin Name:** Flammulina velutipes
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** velvet-shank
**Seasons:** Nov, Dec, Jan, Feb, Mar

---

**Short Description:**
A winter woodland fungus growing in clusters on dead and dying deciduous wood from November through to March. The orange-brown, slimy cap and distinctive dark velvety stem are the key identification features. Edible and worthwhile, but confused by several lookalikes — the velvety stem base must be confirmed on every specimen.

---

**Full Description:**
Velvet shank occupies a season almost nothing else does. It fruits in the coldest months — November through February, often appearing after frost — on dead elm, oak, beech, and other deciduous wood. In a mild December woodland it can be abundant while almost every other edible fungus has finished.

The cultivated version is the *enoki* mushroom of Japanese cooking — a white, elongated form grown in the dark. The wild version looks quite different: orange-brown, clustered, and with the dark velvety base that gives it its name.

The lookalike concern with velvet shank is serious enough to rate this Expert Only. Several toxic species grow in the same habitat and season on dead wood, most notably Funeral Bell (*Galerina marginata*), which can appear alongside velvet shank on the same substrate. The velvety dark stem base is the critical feature of velvet shank and must be confirmed on every individual specimen.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Caps are 2–8cm, convex becoming flattened with age, honey-orange to orange-brown, smooth and slimy when wet. The centre is typically deeper orange-brown than the paler margin. Gills are cream to pale yellow, broad, and widely spaced. The stem is the definitive identification feature: 2–7cm, tough and fibrous, distinctly two-toned — the upper portion is orange-yellow matching the cap, and the lower portion is covered in dense dark brown to black velvety hairs, particularly towards the base. This dark, velvety lower stem is the feature that confirms velvet shank and distinguishes it from dangerous lookalikes. The fungus grows in clusters from dead wood.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Funeral Bell (*Galerina marginata*) is the critical lookalike and one of the most dangerous fungi in Britain. It contains the same amatoxins as Death Cap and causes liver and kidney failure. It grows on dead wood in small clusters or groups, has a similar orange-brown cap (2–5cm), and appears in the same season. The differences: Funeral Bell has a ring (skirt) on the stem; a brown spore print (not cream-white); a slender, uniformly brown stem without velvety dark hairs at the base; and typically grows singly or in smaller groups than velvet shank. Always check the stem base for dark velvety hairs on every specimen. Always take a spore print if in doubt — white for velvet shank, rusty brown for Funeral Bell.

Honey Fungus (*Armillaria* spp.) grows in clusters on dead and living wood with a ring on the stem and a more scaly cap surface. Several species are edible (with caution) but the ring and scaly cap are quite unlike velvet shank.

Confirm the velvety dark lower stem on every single specimen. Do not assume all members of a cluster are the same species.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Dead and dying deciduous trees, stumps, and fallen wood, particularly elm, oak, beech, and ash. Found in woodland, hedgerows, and urban parks wherever mature dead deciduous wood is present. Throughout Britain. The winter fruiting season is the most reliable identification aid — very few fungi appear in December and January, which narrows the field considerably.

---

**In Season:**
November to March. The season is specifically cold-weather: velvet shank fruits after the first frosts and continues through winter. It can appear in the same week as the first hard frosts and may produce multiple flushes through the winter months.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Remove the tough, velvety stems entirely — they are fibrous and unpleasant to eat. Use the caps only. Sauté in butter until the moisture has evaporated; the caps release significant water. The flavour is mild and pleasant with a slight sweetness. Works well in soups, noodle dishes, and alongside other winter vegetables. The cultivated enoki form has a different texture and flavour from the wild version — treat the wild caps as you would any mild woodland mushroom.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland is generally permitted. Check notices at nature reserves and SSSIs.

---

## 41. Cep

**Species Name:** Cep
**Latin Name:** Boletus edulis
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** cep
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
The most prized edible fungus in Britain — known also as penny bun, porcini, or king bolete. The brown, bun-shaped cap, pale spongy pore surface, and distinctive net-like pattern on the upper stem are the key identification features. Excellent raw, dried, or cooked. Several lookalikes require attention, one of which is toxic.

---

**Full Description:**
Cep is the benchmark edible fungus in European cooking. In Britain it grows in deciduous and mixed woodland from July to November, most reliably under beech and oak, and produces one of the deepest, most complex flavours of any foraged ingredient. A fresh cep sliced raw over a plate of food — the Italian approach — is as good as anything the woodland offers.

The name "porcini" on restaurant menus and in shops almost always refers to this species or its closely related relatives (*Boletus pinophilus*, *Boletus aereus*, *Boletus reticulatus*) — all edible, all similar, all covered in practice by this entry.

The cap of a young, firm cep is dense and heavy. The pore surface beneath should be white in young specimens, becoming yellow-green as it ages. The flesh does not change colour when cut — this is important and distinguishes cep from several toxic lookalikes. The spongy pore layer can be separated from the cap and is worth keeping; it softens faster than the cap but has good flavour.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 5–30cm, convex, bun-shaped, smooth, and brown — from pale tan to deep chocolate-brown. The surface is matte and slightly tacky when moist. The underside has a spongy layer of densely packed tubes, the pore surface appearing white in young specimens, becoming cream-yellow then greenish-yellow with age. This pore surface, not gills, is diagnostic of boletes. The stem is 5–20cm, stout and club-shaped, pale brown to cream, with a prominent fine network of pale ridges — the reticulation — on at least the upper portion. This net pattern on the stem is one of the clearest identification features. Flesh is white throughout, firm, and does not change colour when cut or broken. The smell is strong, pleasant, and distinctly fungal.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Satan's Bolete (*Rubroboletus satanas*) has a pale grey-white cap, a red pore surface, and a stem that is red at the base. The red pores are the immediate warning sign — cep never has red or orange pores. Causes severe gastrointestinal poisoning.

Bitter Bolete (*Tylopilus felleus*) has a very similar appearance to cep — brown cap, pale pore surface, net pattern on stem — but the pores become distinctly pink with age, and the flesh is intensely bitter. Taste a small raw piece: bitterness is immediate and unmistakable. Technically not poisonous but inedible and ruins any dish it contaminates.

Bay Bolete (*Imleria badia*) has a darker brown cap, pores that turn blue when cut, and a stem without a clear net pattern. Edible and good.

The rules for all bolete picking: reject any specimen with red, orange, or pink pores; reject any that stains blue immediately when cut; taste a small piece raw and reject if bitter. White flesh, white-to-yellow pores, no colour change, no bitterness: proceed.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Deciduous and mixed woodland, forming mycorrhizal associations with oak, beech, birch, and pine. Most productive in established ancient woodland and old parkland with mature trees. Also found in mixed woodland with some conifer component. Returns to the same sites year after year in favourable conditions. Most reliably found in the south and east of England and in Scotland under pine; less common in the wet west.

---

**In Season:**
July to November. The main flush follows warm, wet weather after a dry period in late summer and autumn. In good years the season has two peaks: a summer flush in July to August under certain tree associations, and the main autumn flush from September. Scotland and upland areas are typically later. After the first hard frosts the season ends abruptly.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Eat fresh and young. Slice raw over risotto, pasta, or carpaccio — the flavour raw is exceptional. Sauté in butter with garlic and thyme; the moisture must cook out fully before the flavour develops properly. Dry excess ceps by slicing thinly and air-drying or using a low oven — dried cep has an even more concentrated flavour than fresh and is worth making in quantity. Rehydrate in warm water; use the soaking water as stock. Cep pasta, cep risotto, and cep on toast are all classics worth knowing.

---

**Legal Notes:**

Take what you can use and leave small, undeveloped buttons to mature.

## 42. Field Mushroom

**Species Name:** Field Mushroom
**Latin Name:** Agaricus campestris
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** field-mushroom
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
The classic wild mushroom of open grassland, appearing in rings and arcs in traditional pasture from August to October. White cap, pink-then-brown gills, and a mild mushroom smell. Excellent eating. The Yellow Stainer lookalike is a serious risk and requires a specific test on every specimen — cut the base of the stem and watch for yellow staining.

---

**Full Description:**
Field mushroom is the one most people imagine when they think of picking wild mushrooms. It grows in old permanent pasture, sports fields, golf course roughs, and the rough grass of traditional meadows — open, grazed land rather than woodland. In a good year, a productive field in September can hold hundreds of specimens.

The flavour of a fresh field mushroom — picked that morning, fried in butter — is substantially better than anything sold in a supermarket. The cultivated button mushroom is a domesticated form of the same genus, and the relationship between the two is something like the relationship between a wild and a cultivated strawberry.

The Yellow Stainer (*Agaricus xanthodermus*) is the reason this is rated Expert Only. It grows in the same habitats, looks nearly identical to field mushroom, and causes gastrointestinal illness in a significant proportion of people who eat it. The test is straightforward and must be done on every specimen.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 5–15cm, convex then flattening, white to pale grey-brown, smooth or slightly scaly. The gills are not attached to the stem — they are free. This is critical: in young mushrooms the gills are pink, deepening through brown to dark chocolate-brown as spores mature. This pink-to-brown gill progression is a key positive identifier. The stem is 3–8cm, solid, white, with a thin ring (skirt) on the upper portion. The ring is fragile and may be absent on older specimens. Flesh is white and firm. The smell is distinctly of fresh mushroom — clean, pleasant, mild.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Yellow Stainer (*Agaricus xanthodermus*) grows in the same habitats and at the same time. The cap and overall structure are nearly identical. The test: cut the very base of the stem and watch the cut surface for 30 seconds. Yellow Stainer turns bright, chrome yellow immediately at the base. Field mushroom either does not change colour or shows only a very slight, slow yellowing. Additionally, crushed Yellow Stainer flesh has an unpleasant, inky or phenolic smell — not the clean mushroom smell of field mushroom. Do this test on every specimen. Yellow Stainer causes vomiting, sweating, and gastrointestinal distress.

Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*) has white gills (not pink), a volva at the base of the stem (a cup-like structure at or just below soil level), and typically a greenish-yellow cap. The free gills and no volva are positive identifiers for field mushroom — always pull the whole stem from the ground and check the base for a volva before eating any white-capped grassland mushroom.

Destroying Angel (*Amanita virosa*) is entirely white with free white gills and a volva. Lethal. Check the base.

The sequence: pink-to-brown gills, no volva, cut base does not turn yellow, clean mushroom smell. All four must be confirmed.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Old permanent pasture, traditional meadows, golf course rough, sports fields, and any long-established, unfertilised grassland. Does not grow in woodland. The key habitat indicator is old, unimproved grassland that has not been ploughed or heavily fertilised in living memory. Forms fairy rings that can persist and expand over decades. Absent from intensively managed or recently reseeded grass.

---

**In Season:**
August to October, peaking in September. Fruiting is triggered by rain following dry conditions on warm soil. The same field can produce several flushes through the season. After the first hard frosts the season ends. Morning is the best time to pick — the caps are still firm and the gills are at their most vivid pink on young specimens.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Fry in butter, ideally in a cast iron pan at medium-high heat. Do not wash — brush off debris with a dry brush or damp cloth. The gills of older, open-capped specimens discolour other ingredients black when cooked alongside them; scrape the gills out if cooking in a pale sauce or with eggs. Field mushroom on toast with butter is the definitive preparation. Soups and risottos work well. The flat, open-capped specimens of mature mushrooms are excellent stuffed and grilled.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking field mushrooms from public grassland and common land for personal use is permitted. Private farmland and golf courses require permission. Do not pick from land that may have been treated with fungicide.

## 43. Parasol Mushroom

**Species Name:** Parasol Mushroom
**Latin Name:** Macrolepiota procera
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** parasol-mushroom
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, elegant fungus of woodland edges, rough grassland, and hedgerow margins, with a distinctive snakeskin-patterned stem and a large, scaly, parasol-shaped cap. One of the best edible fungi in Britain when fresh and correctly identified. The Shaggy Parasol lookalike causes reactions in some people; the deadly Dapperling species require careful attention to stem and ring characters.

---

**Full Description:**
Parasol mushroom is among the finest edible fungi in Britain — large, flavourful, and with a texture that holds up to frying in a way that few other species match. A fresh, large cap, breadcrumbed and fried in oil, is one of the better things a forager can put on a plate in September.

The species is not rare, but it requires the right habitat — the open grassland and woodland edge conditions it prefers are less common than deep woodland. It appears from late August, often in the same spots year after year, and the tall, distinctive fruiting body is visible from a distance.

The identification requires care at two points. First: distinguish it from Shaggy Parasol (*Chlorophyllum rhacodes*), which causes gastrointestinal reactions in some people. Second: be aware of deadly Dapperling species (*Lepiota* spp.) which are smaller but share some structural features. Height, cap size, and stem characters are the key distinguishing factors.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A tall, stately fungus: stem 15–35cm, cap 10–30cm across. The cap is initially egg-shaped then opens to a flat parasol shape with a persistent central boss (a raised umbo at the centre). The cap surface is pale cream-brown with large, flat, brown scales that darken towards the centre. The stem is long, slender, hollow, and covered with a distinctive snakeskin pattern of brown markings on a pale background — this snakeskin stem is one of the most reliable identification features. The ring is large, double-layered, and can be moved up and down the stem freely — this moveable ring is another key character. Gills are free, cream-white, crowded, and do not change colour. The flesh is white and does not redden or change colour when cut.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Shaggy Parasol (*Chlorophyllum rhacodes*) is shorter and stockier, with a greener-tinged cap surface, a stem that lacks the snakeskin pattern (it is smooth or fibrous brown), a ring that does not slide freely, and flesh that turns orange-red to red when cut. This reddening flesh is the clearest distinction. Causes vomiting and gastrointestinal upset in a proportion of people.

Deadly Dapperlings (*Lepiota brunneoincarnata*, *L. helveola*, and others) are much smaller — cap 2–5cm, never approaching the 10–30cm of a mature parasol. They contain amatoxins and are lethal. The size difference is dramatic: a Lepiota at 3cm cannot be confused with a mature Macrolepiota at 25cm. The risk comes from picking immature or button-stage specimens before the cap has opened fully — do not pick any small, young, button-stage lepiota-type fungi. Only pick fully opened, large specimens.

The snakeskin stem, moveable ring, and non-reddening white flesh are the three positive identifiers. Confirm all three.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Woodland edges, rough grassland, hedgerow margins, and open parkland with old, established grass. Most productive on well-drained, sandy or chalky soils in the south of England and East Anglia. Less common in the wet west and upland areas. Often appears in scattered groups or loose rings in the same spots year after year.

---

**In Season:**
August to October, peaking September. The large, distinctive caps are visible from some distance across open grassland. Young, firm, still partly closed specimens are the best eating — the open, flat caps of older specimens are edible but collect debris and deteriorate faster.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
The cap alone is used — remove the stem, which is too tough and fibrous to eat. Wipe clean, do not wash. The classic preparation is breadcrumbed: dip in beaten egg, coat in breadcrumbs, and fry in oil or butter in a hot pan until golden on both sides. The texture is extraordinary — firm, meaty, and satisfying. Also excellent grilled over high heat, or sliced and sautéed in butter. Too good to put in a soup.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public grassland and woodland edges is permitted. Check notices at nature reserves.

## 44. Shaggy Ink Cap

**Species Name:** Shaggy Ink Cap
**Latin Name:** Coprinus comatus
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** shaggy-ink-cap
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, cylindrical, shaggy-capped fungus found on roadsides, disturbed ground, and grassland from September to November. One of the easiest edible fungi to identify — nothing else looks like it. The caps must be picked young, before the gills begin to autodigest into black ink. Deteriorates within hours of picking.

---

**Full Description:**
Shaggy ink cap is one of the few edible fungi genuinely suited to beginners — with the clear caveat that it must be identified and used at the right stage. The tall, white, shaggy cylindrical cap is unmistakable in the field. No dangerous British fungus looks like it.

The autodigestion process — the mechanism by which the fungus liquefies its gills to release spores — begins from the base of the gills upward once the cap begins to open. When the lower margin of the cap starts to turn black and ink-like, that process has begun and the mushroom should not be eaten. Pick only caps where the gills are still white to pale pink, the cap is tightly cylindrical or just beginning to open, and the lower margin is still white. Use within two to three hours of picking.

The short usability window is the challenge. These mushrooms are found, identified, and cooked on the same day. They cannot be transported, stored, or delayed.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 5–15cm tall, cylindrical to narrowly oval, covered in large white shaggy scales — the "shaggy" of the name. The scales have brownish tips, particularly at the centre of the cap. Gills are initially white, then pink, then self-digesting to black from the base upward. The stem is 10–25cm, hollow, white, with a small moveable ring. The whole fruiting body is white and clean-looking when young. The autodigestion ink is black and unmistakable. The shaggy, cylindrical white cap on roadsides or grassland is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Common Ink Cap (*Coprinopsis atramentaria*) is shorter, less shaggy, and brownish-grey rather than white and scaly. Edible alone, but causes unpleasant reactions (flushing, palpitations, vomiting) if consumed within several days of drinking alcohol — the compound coprine blocks alcohol metabolism. Do not eat if you have drunk or plan to drink alcohol.

Magpie Inkcap (*Coprinopsis picacea*) is larger, with a black-and-white pattern on the cap surface. Mildly toxic. The all-white, heavily shaggy scales of *Coprinus comatus* distinguish it.

No dangerous lookalikes closely resemble Shaggy Ink Cap at the correct picking stage.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Disturbed ground, roadsides, path edges, grassland, lawns, and any recently disturbed soil. Often appears in urban settings — parks, verges, and gardens. Found throughout Britain and commonly encountered in towns and cities. Does not require woodland.

---

**In Season:**
September to November, occasionally appearing in spring. Fruiting is triggered by rain on disturbed ground. Appears quickly — a cluster can appear overnight and be past eating within 48 hours. Check any productive site regularly during the season.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use immediately. The window between picking and cooking is two to three hours at most. Brush off any debris, trim the base, and sauté in butter over medium heat. The caps release considerable moisture and reduce dramatically in size. The flavour is mild, sweet, and delicate — not complex, but genuinely good. Works best in a simple omelette or on toast where the flavour is not overwhelmed. Does not dry, freeze, or store in any useful form. Cook what you pick, that day.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking shaggy ink caps from public grassland, roadsides, and parks for personal use is unrestricted. Found on disturbed ground in urban settings as much as countryside — one of the few edible fungi commonly found within cities.

## 45. Horn of Plenty

**Species Name:** Horn of Plenty
**Latin Name:** Craterellus cornucopioides
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** horn-of-plenty
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
A dark, funnel-shaped fungus of deciduous woodland that is easy to overlook despite its quality. The deep grey-black colour and funnel shape are specific, but its camouflage against the woodland floor means careful searching is needed. One of the best-flavoured woodland fungi in Britain — earthy, complex, and excellent dried.

---

**Full Description:**
Horn of plenty's common name is generous — the fruitings are rarely abundant enough to fill a horn. It grows scattered through beech and oak woodland in small groups, the dark, funnel-shaped fruiting bodies blending almost perfectly with decomposing leaves. Finding them requires deliberate, low searching rather than casual observation.

The flavour makes the search worthwhile. Fresh, horn of plenty has an intense, earthy, complex flavour — more assertive than chanterelle, with a depth that deepens further on drying. Dried horn of plenty is one of the most flavourful dried fungi available, and a small amount goes a long way.

The identification is reliable once you know the species, but requires some field experience to find and correctly attribute in variable light conditions.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Fruiting body is 3–12cm tall, funnel or trumpet-shaped with wavy, ragged edges. The interior surface — the inside of the funnel — is rough-textured, dark grey-black to dark brown. The outer surface (the exterior of the trumpet) is smooth to slightly wrinkled, grey to grey-blue, noticeably paler than the interior. There are no gills, spines, or pores on the outer surface — it is smooth and spore-bearing directly. The flesh is thin, fragile, grey-brown. The smell is mild and pleasant. The dark, trumpet-shaped form without any gill or pore surface, in deciduous woodland, is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Trumpet Chanterelle or Winter Chanterelle (*Craterellus tubaeformis*) is closely related — smaller, more yellow-brown, with visible forked ridges on the outer surface. Equally edible.

Earthtongue fungi (*Geoglossum* and related genera) are dark, tongue or club-shaped, and grow from soil or turf. They are not hollow or funnel-shaped. Not dangerous but not edible.

The dark hollow funnel shape with no gill or pore surface distinguishes horn of plenty from any dangerous species.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Deciduous woodland on moist, well-drained calcareous or neutral soils, particularly under beech on chalk and limestone. Also found under oak in suitable conditions. Most reliably found in established beech woodland in southern England (the Chilterns, North and South Downs, the Cotswolds) and in mixed woodland in Wales. Forms loose associations with tree roots but fruiting can appear spread through a wood rather than in neat clusters.

---

**In Season:**
August to November. The main flush follows warm, wet conditions in late summer and autumn. The dark colour makes individual specimens easy to miss — get low to the ground and look along the woodland floor rather than down onto it.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Sauté in butter — the thin flesh cooks quickly and the flavour concentrates rapidly. Excellent in pasta, risotto, and omelettes. The flavour is assertive enough to stand alone on toast. Drying is strongly recommended: spread on a rack in a low oven until bone-dry, then store in an airtight jar. Dried horn of plenty lasts well and the flavour is outstanding — use as you would a truffle, in small quantities to add depth to sauces and soups.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland is generally permitted. Check notices at nature reserves and managed estates.

## 46. Wood Blewit

**Species Name:** Wood Blewit
**Latin Name:** Lepista nuda
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** wood-blewit
**Seasons:** Oct, Nov, Dec, Jan

---

**Short Description:**
A late-season edible fungus with unmistakable lilac-violet colouring throughout the cap, gills, and stem. Found in woodland and hedgerows from October into January. Must be cooked — causes gastrointestinal upset when eaten raw. Confused by several lilac-coloured toxic species, which requires careful attention to habitat, smell, and spore print.

---

**Full Description:**
Wood blewit is one of the few edible fungi that fruits well into winter — a genuinely useful find in November and December when almost everything else has finished. The violet and lilac colouring is striking and unusual among British fungi, making it visually distinctive once you know the species.

The caveat is specific: wood blewit must be cooked. Raw specimens cause nausea and vomiting in most people. This is not a danger from misidentification but an inherent property of the species — the toxins break down with heat and a well-cooked blewit is safe and good to eat.

The colour fades with age, which complicates identification of older specimens. Rely on young to mid-aged specimens where the violet colouring is still clear.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 6–15cm, convex to wavy and flat with age, initially deep violet-lilac, fading to brownish-lilac then buff-brown in older specimens. The cap margin remains inrolled for some time. Gills are crowded, lilac to pale violet, fading to buff-pink with age. The stem is 4–8cm, stout, pale violet-lilac with a fibrous texture, often with a bulbous base. Flesh is lilac-tinged throughout, firm, and does not change colour when cut. The smell is strong, sweet, and distinctive — fruity and perfumed, sometimes compared to frozen orange juice concentrate. This strong sweet smell is one of the most reliable identification features alongside the violet colouring. Spore print: pale pink to lilac-pink.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Violet Webcap (*Cortinarius violaceus*) is deep violet-purple throughout, growing in deciduous and conifer woodland. It has rusty brown gills (from the rusty brown spore print of all webcaps), a cobweb-like partial veil (cortina) on young specimens leaving fibrous remains on the stem, and a very different smell. Many webcap species are extremely toxic — the Deadly Webcap (*Cortinarius rubellarius*) and related species cause irreversible kidney failure. Spore print rust-brown: do not eat. Always take a spore print when picking violet-coloured fungi.

Amethyst Deceiver (*Laccaria amethystina*) is much smaller (cap 1–5cm), brighter purple, with widely spaced, thick, deeply coloured gills and a spore print that is white. Not toxic but much smaller — unlikely to be gathered in useful quantity or confused with a mature blewit.

Lilac Fibrecap (*Inocybe lilacina*) is small and toxic — cap 2–5cm, lilac-brown, with a pointed umbo, and a brown spore print. Much smaller than wood blewit.

The sequence: strong sweet fruity smell, violet colouring throughout, pale pink spore print. Any rust-brown spore print means do not eat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Deciduous woodland, particularly under oak, beech, and ash on well-drained soils. Also found in hedgerows, compost heaps, and gardens where there is decaying woody material. The garden and hedgerow habitat is worth knowing — wood blewit is one of the few edible fungi that appears reliably in domestic gardens. Found throughout Britain including Scotland.

---

**In Season:**
October to January. The cold-season fruiting is one of its most useful characteristics. Flushes after the first frosts and continues through mild winter spells. Often available when chanterelle, cep, and field mushroom have all finished.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Cook thoroughly — do not eat raw. Sauté in butter over medium heat until fully cooked through; the lilac colour fades to buff-brown on cooking. The flavour is sweet, mild, and slightly perfumed. Works well in creamy sauces, pasta, and risotto. Pairs with thyme and cream. The strong sweet smell softens to something more subtle in the cooked dish. Does not dry as well as cep or horn of plenty but can be frozen after cooking.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland and accessible hedgerows is permitted. Garden blewits on private property are the landowner's by default — ask before picking from gardens you do not own.

## 47. Winter Chanterelle

**Species Name:** Winter Chanterelle
**Latin Name:** Craterellus tubaeformis
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** winter-chanterelle
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
A smaller, slimmer relative of the chanterelle, found in mossy woodland from September through to December. The yellow-brown cap with a central depression, hollow yellow stem, and forked ridges rather than true gills on the underside are the key identification features. Excellent flavour and a useful late-season find. Also known as trumpet chanterelle or yellowfoot.

---

**Full Description:**
Winter chanterelle fills the gap after the main chanterelle season ends. It fruits from September and continues reliably through November and into December — later than most other quality edible fungi — in mossy, damp woodland that suits it.

The flavour is good but different from true chanterelle: less of the apricot character, more earthy and autumnal, with a slight bitterness that is not unpleasant in cooked dishes. The hollow stem gives it a lighter, more delicate texture than the solid chanterelle. In terms of structure and identification, it is close enough to true chanterelle that experience with one makes the other straightforward, but it is a separate species with a distinct season and habitat preference.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 2–8cm across, convex then depressed at the centre, often funnel-like in mature specimens, with a wavy, irregular margin. The surface is yellow-brown to grey-brown, often darker at the centre. The stem is 3–8cm, cylindrical, hollow, yellow to pale orange-yellow — the hollow stem visible when the specimen is cut is one of the most useful identification checks. The underside has forked, running ridges (not gills) similar to chanterelle, but more irregular and yellowish. Flesh is thin, pale yellow, fragile. Grows in groups to dense clusters in moss, often with multiple fruit bodies from the same mycelium.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
True Chanterelle (*Cantharellus cibarius*) is larger, more robust, more uniformly yellow, with a solid stem, and a distinct apricot smell. The hollow stem of winter chanterelle is the clearest distinction from chanterelle.

False Chanterelle (*Hygrophoropsis aurantiaca*) is orange, with true blade-like gills rather than forked ridges, and typically grows in conifer woodland. See the chanterelle entry for a full description. Causes gastrointestinal upset.

Funeral Bell (*Galerina marginata*) grows on dead wood rather than from soil or moss directly, has a ring on the stem, true gills, and a rusty-brown spore print. The habitat difference — moss and soil versus wood — is the primary field distinction.

Confirm the hollow yellow stem and forked ridges on every specimen.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Mossy, damp deciduous and mixed woodland, particularly under beech, birch, and pine on acidic or neutral soils. Often found in the same woodland as true chanterelle but in the moister, more mossy areas. The dense moss carpet that characterises productive sites is the habitat clue. Widespread in Britain, particularly in the wetter woodland of Scotland, Wales, and the west of England.

---

**In Season:**
September to December. The late season is the main appeal — this is one of the few quality edible fungi still producing in November and December. Fruiting is triggered by cool, wet conditions and continues through the first frosts.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Sauté in butter — the hollow stems and thin flesh cook quickly. The moisture evaporates fast and the flavour concentrates. Works well in pasta, omelettes, and mixed woodland mushroom dishes. The slight bitterness pairs well with cream and a little sherry or white wine. Dries well given the thin flesh — a low oven for a few hours produces a good dried product with concentrated flavour.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland is generally permitted. Check notices at nature reserves and managed estates.

## 48. St George's Mushroom

**Species Name:** St George's Mushroom
**Latin Name:** Calocybe gambosa
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** st-georges-mushroom
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
A spring mushroom of chalk grassland, downland, and old meadows, traditionally appearing around St George's Day (23 April). Robust white cap, crowded white gills, and a strong, distinctive floury smell. Excellent eating. A small number of toxic lookalikes in grassland habitats require careful attention to smell, gills, and habitat.

---

**Full Description:**
St George's mushroom is the reward for knowing where to look in April and May, when almost no other edible fungi are available in Britain. It grows in the same spots year after year on old, established grassland, often in rings or arcs, and a productive site in a good year can provide excellent picking when the countryside is otherwise mushroom-free.

The strong, floury smell is the single most useful identification feature and distinguishes it from all dangerous lookalikes in its habitat. Learn this smell — it is unmistakable, persistent, and present from the moment you find the specimen.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 5–15cm, robust and convex, white to cream, sometimes with a faint buff or ochre tint, smooth and slightly undulating at the margin. Gills are white, crowded, and narrow, running slightly down the stem. The stem is 3–8cm, stout, solid, white, with a slightly swollen base. Flesh is firm, white, and does not change colour when cut. The smell is strong, distinctive, and immediately floury — like fresh damp flour or raw pastry. This smell is the primary identification feature and is present in both fresh and older specimens. Spore print: white.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Deadly Fibrecap (*Inocybe erubescens*, syn. *I. patouillardii*) is the most serious lookalike. It grows on chalk and limestone grassland in spring at the same time and in similar habitats. The cap is white when young, becoming reddish-brick with age, and the flesh reddens distinctly when cut or bruised. The smell is spermatic or unpleasant, not floury. The gills are white then buff-pink, not crowded white. It contains muscarine and causes severe poisoning. If the flesh reddens when cut, stop.

Fool's Funnel (*Clitocybe rivulosa* and *C. dealbata*) are small, white, funnel-shaped fungi of grassland. They contain muscarine. Much smaller than St George's — cap 2–5cm — with decurrent gills and a faint, unpleasant smell.

The floury smell, white crowded non-decurrent gills, and white non-reddening flesh confirm St George's mushroom. Cut every specimen and check the flesh.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Old chalk and limestone grassland, downland, old meadows, and traditional pasture. Most productive on south-facing slopes on chalk in southern England. Also found on limestone grassland in the north. Requires established, undisturbed, unfertilised grassland — will not fruit on improved or recently ploughed land. The same rings and arcs return to the same sites for decades.

---

**In Season:**
April to June. The traditional date is around St George's Day (23 April), though the actual timing varies with altitude, aspect, and the year's weather. In warm years it appears in early April; in cold springs it may not appear until May. The season is relatively brief — four to six weeks in most years.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
The strong floury flavour mellows on cooking to something rich, round, and complex. Sauté in butter until golden — the moisture cooks out and the flavour concentrates well. Excellent in pasta and risotto. The flavour is robust enough to pair with strong ingredients like anchovies, capers, or aged cheese. The spring season makes it particularly valuable — there is nothing else comparable in April and May.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public downland and grassland is generally permitted. Many productive chalk downland sites are SSSIs and nature reserves — check local notices, and tread carefully on species-rich turf.

## 49. Morel

**Species Name:** Morel
**Latin Name:** Morchella esculenta agg.
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** morel
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Britain's most prized spring fungus — a honeycomb-capped, hollow-stemmed species found in woodland edges, old orchards, and disturbed ground from March to May. The honeycomb cap is unlike any other edible British fungus. Must be cooked before eating. The False Morel is dangerous and has caused fatalities in Europe.

---

**Full Description:**
Morel is the spring equivalent of the cep — a fungus of genuine quality that appears in a brief window when almost nothing else is available, in specific habitats, and rewards those who know where and when to look.

The honeycomb structure of the cap — a network of ridges and pits covering an irregularly shaped, hollow head — is entirely distinctive among British edible fungi. Nothing edible looks like it; nothing safe looks like it; and one thing dangerous does look superficially like it, which requires clear attention.

In Britain, morels are found in old orchards and cultivated ground, woodland edges on calcareous soils, the sides of old paths and tracks, and sometimes on ground disturbed by fire. The habitat association with old, established orchards and riverside woodland is useful for locating them. They are never common — a good find in a productive year feels like a genuine discovery.

Morels must be cooked. Raw morels contain hydrazine compounds that cause illness. Thorough cooking breaks these down completely.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Fruiting body is 5–15cm tall, consisting of a distinct honeycomb-like cap (the pileus) and a hollow, ribbed, cream-white stem. The cap is irregularly ovoid, covered with a network of deep, angular pits separated by ridges — the honeycomb structure. The colour is brown to tan-brown throughout the cap, paler cream on the ridge tops. The cap and stem are entirely hollow — cut the specimen in half and both sections are hollow all the way through. This hollow structure is both an identification feature and a practical one: wash the inside of the cap thoroughly, as debris and insects accumulate in the pits. The stem is pale cream-white, ribbed, and hollow.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
False Morel (*Gyromitra esculenta*) has a deeply convoluted, saddle-like or brain-like cap — not a true honeycomb with regular pits and ridges. The cap is brown and folded rather than pitted. The stem is not fully hollow — it has internal chambers separated by partitions, visible when cut. False morel contains gyromitrin, which breaks down to monomethylhydrazine (a rocket fuel component) in the body. It has caused fatalities in Europe, particularly in Scandinavia. The difference between the regular, deeply pitted honeycomb of a true morel and the irregular, wrinkled, lobed cap of a false morel is clear on careful examination. Cut every specimen: fully hollow throughout means morel; chambered or solid means false morel.

Elfin Saddle (*Helvella* spp.) species have saddle-like or lobed grey-brown caps, not honeycomb-pitted. Some are mildly toxic.

The fully hollow, regular honeycomb cap is the definitive feature of true morel.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Woodland edges on calcareous soils, old orchards and abandoned gardens, riverside woodland, burnt ground and post-fire sites, and disturbed calcareous ground. The association with old apple orchards is well established in Britain — south-facing orchard edges on chalk or limestone are the most productive sites. Also found along old tracks and paths through woodland. Not common anywhere in Britain but present in most counties.

---

**In Season:**
March to May. The season is brief — typically four to six weeks — and timing varies with the year's weather. Warm, dry springs bring morels early; cold, wet springs delay them. In southern England the season begins in March; in Scotland and upland areas it may not start until late April.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Cook thoroughly without exception. Sauté in butter after halving and washing the hollow cap carefully to remove debris. The flavour is rich, earthy, and complex — one of the finest of any edible fungi. Excellent in cream sauces, with eggs, and alongside poultry. Morels stuffed with a little soft herb butter and baked briefly are outstanding. Dry excess morels by threading on string or placing in a low oven — dried morel is exceptional and keeps well.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking morels for personal use from public woodland and grassland is permitted. Old orchards on private land require landowner permission. Given how scarce morels are in Britain, take only what you will use and leave smaller specimens to mature and sporulate.

## 50. Cauliflower Fungus

**Species Name:** Cauliflower Fungus
**Latin Name:** Sparassis crispa
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** cauliflower-fungus
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
A large, distinctive fungus growing at the base of pine and other conifers from August to November, with a mass of flattened, wavy, cream-coloured lobes resembling a cauliflower or sea coral. One of the easiest edible fungi to identify — nothing dangerous looks like it. Excellent eating once cleaned, which requires patience.

---

**Full Description:**
Cauliflower fungus is among the most reliably identifiable edible fungi in Britain. The large, pale, coral-like mass of flattened, curling lobes at the base of a conifer trunk has no dangerous equivalent. Once you have seen it, you will not confuse it with anything else.

It fruits at the base of living or recently felled conifers — most commonly Scots pine, but also larch and spruce — returning to the same tree year after year until the host dies. In good years a single fruiting body can weigh several kilograms.

The challenge is cleaning rather than identification. The lobes trap debris, soil, needles, and insects with impressive efficiency. Separating the lobes and washing thoroughly in cold water takes time. The effort is worthwhile: the flavour is mild, nutty, and sweet, with a satisfying texture that holds up well to cooking.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Fruiting body is a large, rounded mass of flattened, wavy, ribbon-like lobes branching from a central base, 15–50cm across, cream-white to pale yellow, sometimes with brownish tips in older specimens. The lobes are thin, crisped, and overlapping. The base is a thick, rooting stalk connected to the host tree's roots. The whole structure resembles a sea coral or a cream-coloured cauliflower. Grows specifically at the base of conifers. Spore print: white. Flesh is white, firm, and does not change colour when cut.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous British fungus resembles cauliflower fungus. The coral-like mass of pale, wavy lobes at the base of a conifer is entirely specific.

Ramaria species (coral fungi) are much smaller — 5–15cm — with round, upright, repeatedly branching coral-like structures, yellow or orange. Some Ramaria species cause gastrointestinal upset. They do not grow at the base of conifers in the same way and are much smaller. Cauliflower fungus is much larger and distinctly flattened-lobed rather than round-branched.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
At the base of living and recently dead conifers, particularly Scots pine, larch, and spruce. Found in conifer woodland and mixed woodland with a significant conifer component throughout Britain. Most common in Scottish Caledonian pine forest and in pine plantations throughout England. Returns to the same tree or tree stump year after year.

---

**In Season:**
August to November. Peak fruiting from September to October. Check any productive conifer from late August onwards. A large fruiting body may take several days to reach peak size and is best picked when the lobes are pale cream and the tips have not yet browned.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Clean thoroughly before cooking — separate the lobes and wash in several changes of cold water to remove debris. Dry gently before cooking. Sauté in butter in a hot pan; the lobes release moisture and benefit from medium-high heat to develop some colour. The nutty, mild flavour works well in pasta, risotto, and stir-fries. The texture holds up better than most fungi — it does not collapse. Dries reasonably well, though the texture of fresh specimens is considerably better.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland and Forestry England managed forests is generally permitted. Take what you can use — a single large fruiting body is usually more than sufficient.

---

## 51. Dryad's Saddle

**Species Name:** Dryad's Saddle
**Latin Name:** Cerioporus squamosus
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** dryads-saddle
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A large, scaly bracket fungus growing from dead and dying deciduous trees from May onwards. The pale, cream-brown cap with dark brown scales and the distinctive watermelon rind smell of young specimens are the key identification features. Only young, tender growth is worth eating — older brackets become tough and unpalatable quickly.

---

**Full Description:**
Dryad's Saddle is one of the most commonly encountered bracket fungi in lowland Britain. It grows on elm, ash, beech, and sycamore — dead standing trees, old stumps, and wounded living trees — and can produce very large brackets, sometimes exceeding half a metre across. The size is deceptive: only the youngest, outermost growth is worth eating.

The species was formerly known as *Polyporus squamosus*, and the change to *Cerioporus squamosus* reflects recent molecular reclassification. Either name may appear in field guides depending on the edition.

The smell of a fresh, young specimen is the most pleasant surprise about this fungus: distinctly of watermelon rind — faintly sweet, faintly cucumberish. It fades as the bracket ages. An old, tough bracket has no smell to speak of and is not worth picking.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Bracket is fan-shaped to kidney-shaped, 10–60cm across, growing from a lateral or off-centre stem attached to the wood. The upper surface is pale cream to tan, covered with concentric zones of flat, dark brown scales on a paler background — the scale pattern is distinctive. The underside has a white to cream pore surface with angular pores visible to the naked eye, becoming more elongated towards the stem. The stem is 2–5cm, stout, black at the base where it attaches to the wood. Flesh is white, firm in young specimens, becoming tough and corky with age. The watermelon rind smell on young specimens is specific and pleasant.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous British bracket fungi closely resemble Dryad's Saddle. The scale pattern on the upper surface and the large, angular pores on the underside are specific.

Giant Polypore (*Meripilus giganteus*) is a large bracket growing at the base of trees in compound clusters; the pore surface stains black when bruised. Edible but poor quality and a different growth habit.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Dead and dying deciduous trees, stumps, and standing deadwood. Particularly associated with elm, ash, beech, willow, and sycamore. Found throughout Britain in deciduous woodland, parks, hedgerows, and urban green spaces. One of the most widely distributed bracket fungi in lowland Britain. The spring and early summer season — May to June — is when the youngest growth appears and quality is at its best.

---

**In Season:**
May to September. The youngest brackets appear in May and June and are the best quality — pale, tender, and aromatic. By July and August most brackets have hardened. New growth at the very outer margin of large brackets can occasionally be found later in the season. Check any productive tree from late April in mild years.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use only the youngest, most tender outer growth — the margin of the bracket where the flesh is still soft and white. Slice thinly and sauté in butter or oil; older, tougher growth will not soften regardless of cooking time. The mild, slightly sweet flavour pairs well with garlic, lemon, and soft herbs. Works well in a stir-fry where the thin slices can be cooked quickly at high heat. The texture is firmer than most woodland fungi and holds up to longer cooking than, say, oyster mushroom.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking fungi for personal use from public woodland, parks, and accessible hedgerow trees is generally permitted. Do not damage or strip bark from living trees to access brackets.

## 52. Jelly Ear

**Species Name:** Jelly Ear
**Latin Name:** Auricularia auricula-judae
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** jelly-ear
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
A gelatinous, ear-shaped fungus found year-round on dead and dying elder, and occasionally other deciduous trees. The rubbery, translucent, brown fruiting bodies are unmistakable once seen. Edible and widely used in East Asian cooking under the name wood ear or cloud ear mushroom. Mild flavour; the texture is the point.

---

**Full Description:**
Jelly Ear is one of the most reliably findable edible fungi in Britain. It grows on elder — almost exclusively on elder — year-round, and a dead elder branch in a damp hedgerow will often carry a crop in every month of the year. The connection to elder is so consistent that finding jelly ear is largely a matter of finding dead elder wood.

The fungus is the same species sold dried in Chinese supermarkets as wood ear or black fungus — a standard ingredient in hot and sour soup and many other East Asian dishes. The flavour is mild to the point of neutrality; the value is entirely in the texture: gelatinous, slightly crunchy, and satisfying in a way that no other British fungus quite matches.

Fresh jelly ears shrivel to a fraction of their size on drying but rehydrate well. Dried specimens found on the branch during dry weather are still usable — rehydrate in cold water for twenty minutes.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Fruiting body is 3–10cm, irregularly ear-shaped to cup-shaped, gelatinous and translucent when fresh, rubbery when handled. The outer surface is brown to reddish-brown, with a slightly velvety texture from fine hairs. The inner surface (the concave side) is pale greyish-brown and smooth. The whole fungus is gelatinous and wobbles when moved — this texture is unmistakable. Grows directly from bark, without a stem. Almost always on elder. The ear shape, gelatinous texture, and elder substrate are collectively specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Tripe Fungus (*Auricularia mesenterica*) is a related species growing on elm and other deciduous trees, with a more bracket-like, zoned upper surface and a grey, wrinkled underside. Edible but less good than jelly ear.

Witches' Butter (*Tremella mesenterica*) is bright yellow and gelatinous, growing on dead wood. Edible. Unmistakably yellow — not similar to jelly ear in colour.

No dangerous British fungus closely resembles fresh jelly ear. The ear shape, brown translucent gelatinous texture, and elder substrate are specific.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Dead and dying elder, almost exclusively. Also occasionally on other deciduous trees including elm and beech in some areas. The elder connection is so reliable that any dead elder branch in a damp hedgerow or woodland edge is worth checking. Found throughout Britain including urban areas wherever elder grows. Year-round availability makes it the most accessible edible fungus in Britain by season.

---

**In Season:**
Year-round. Fruiting is most abundant in damp conditions — autumn and winter produce the largest crops. Summer drought causes the fresh fungus to shrivel on the branch but does not destroy it; rain brings it back to full size within hours. Check dead elder in any season.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Rinse thoroughly and remove any tough attachment points. Use in stir-fries, hot and sour soup, noodle dishes, and any preparation where a slightly crunchy, gelatinous texture is an asset. The fungus absorbs flavour from surrounding ingredients well. In British cooking, it works in a mushroom soup where its texture contrasts with softer fungi. It can be pickled — the gelatinous texture holds up well in an acidic pickling liquor. Do not cook for long at high heat — the texture breaks down.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking jelly ear for personal use from accessible dead wood on public land is unrestricted. Found on dead elder in urban as well as rural settings. Not a protected species.

## 53. Scarlet Elf Cup

**Species Name:** Scarlet Elf Cup
**Latin Name:** Sarcoscypha austriaca
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** scarlet-elf-cup
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar

---

**Short Description:**
One of the most visually striking fungi in Britain — a vivid scarlet cup growing on dead twigs and sticks in damp woodland in January and February. Edible, though the flavour is mild. Found when almost nothing else is available. The brilliant red colour in a winter woodland is unmistakable.

---

**Full Description:**
Scarlet elf cup arrives in the coldest months — January and February — when the woodland floor is bare and the only colour comes from mosses and the occasional bracket. A cluster of brilliant red cups on a mossy stick in a stream valley is genuinely startling.

Two species share this appearance and name in Britain: *Sarcoscypha austriaca* and *S. coccinea*. Recent work has established that *S. austriaca* is the more common of the two in Britain, though the two are indistinguishable without microscopy. For foraging purposes they are identical — both edible, both mild-flavoured, both found in the same habitats and season.

The flavour is mild and not exceptional — this is a fungus of visual pleasure and seasonal scarcity rather than culinary greatness. A small number in a winter salad or scattered over a finished plate justify the finding more than any attempt at a serious cooked dish.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Fruiting body is a cup or shallow bowl, 2–6cm across, brilliant scarlet on the inner (concave) surface, pale pink-white and slightly hairy on the outer (convex) surface. The cup sits on a short, pale stalk attached directly to dead wood — twigs and thin sticks, often partially buried in leaf litter and moss. The flesh is thin and brittle when fresh. Grows in small clusters of 2–10 cups from the same piece of wood. The brilliant scarlet inner surface is unmistakable in the field — no other British winter fungus produces this colour.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Ruby Elf Cup (*Sarcoscypha jurana*) is very similar, also edible. Indistinguishable without microscopy.

No dangerous British fungus resembles scarlet elf cup. The vivid red cup shape in winter woodland on dead sticks is specific.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Damp deciduous woodland, particularly in stream valleys and on valley sides where moisture is retained through winter. Most commonly found on dead hazel, elm, and willow sticks partially buried in moss and leaf litter. The substrate — thin, dead, partly buried sticks in very damp conditions — is the habitat to search. Widespread but never abundant; found throughout Britain including Scotland, most reliably in the wetter west.

---

**In Season:**
January to March. The peak is February. By late March the season is over and the cups have usually sporulated and deteriorated. The short winter season is the defining characteristic — find it when almost nothing else is available and appreciate it for that.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as a visual garnish and minor flavour element rather than a bulk ingredient. A few cups placed raw on a finished dish bring colour and texture. The flavour is mild and slightly mushroomy — not complex. They can be briefly sautéed in butter but the texture softens and the brilliant colour fades. Best used raw for visual effect. Scatter over a winter salad, a pale soup, or a white risotto for colour.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking for personal use from public woodland is unrestricted. Found in small enough quantities in any one spot that conservation impact is minimal — take what you can use and leave the rest.

## 54. Bay Bolete

**Species Name:** Bay Bolete
**Latin Name:** Imleria badia
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** bay-bolete
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
A common and reliable edible bolete of deciduous and conifer woodland, with a dark chocolate-brown cap, cream-yellow pores that turn blue-green when bruised, and a pleasant, mild flavour. One of the most frequently encountered edible boletes in Britain. The blue staining reaction is key to identification — and to understanding the broader bolete safety rules.

---

**Full Description:**
Bay Bolete is the everyday edible bolete of British woodland — not as prized as cep but common, reliable, and good enough to make picking worthwhile when cep is scarce or absent. In many woodlands it is more abundant than cep and fruits over a longer season.

The blue staining on the pore surface when cut or bruised is characteristic and useful to know — it is this reaction, combined with the dark brown cap and mild flavour, that confirms identification. Understanding which boletes stain blue and which do not, and what else to check alongside the staining, is the core of safe bolete foraging.

The species was recently reclassified from *Boletus badius* to *Imleria badia* — some older field guides use the former name.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 4–15cm, convex, dark chocolate-brown to chestnut-brown, slightly greasy when moist, matte when dry. The pore surface is cream-white in young specimens, becoming cream-yellow then turning distinctly blue-green when bruised or cut — this reaction happens within seconds. The stem is 5–12cm, cylindrical to slightly club-shaped, pale brown with faint vertical streaking, no net pattern (unlike cep). Flesh is pale yellow-white, turning blue when cut — particularly just below the pore layer. The blue staining is prompt and unmistakable. Smell is mild and pleasant.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Cep (*Boletus edulis*) does not stain blue. The net pattern on the cep stem is absent in bay bolete. Both edible.

Devil's Bolete (*Rubroboletus satanas*) has a pale grey cap and red pores — the red pores are an immediate disqualifier. Bay bolete never has red or orange pores.

The key rules for any blue-staining bolete: reject if the pores are red or orange; reject if the staining is extremely rapid and vivid scarlet-red rather than blue-green (Scarletina Bolete — edible but know what you have). White to yellow pores that stain blue-green, dark brown cap, mild smell: bay bolete.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Deciduous and conifer woodland on acidic to neutral soils. Found under oak, beech, birch, and pine. One of the most common edible boletes in Britain, found throughout England, Wales, Scotland, and Ireland. Often abundant in birch and oak woodland on acidic soils where cep is absent. Returns to the same sites year after year.

---

**In Season:**
July to November, with peak fruiting in September and October. More consistently abundant than cep across the season. A good year for bay bolete is a good autumn to forage even if cep fruitings are modest.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use exactly as cep — the flavour is good if milder. Sauté in butter, add to pasta and risotto, or dry for later use. The blue staining disappears on cooking and is no cause for concern. Dried bay bolete has a more concentrated, earthier flavour than fresh. A reliable stand-in for cep in any recipe.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking for personal use from public woodland is generally permitted. Follow the same guidelines as for cep — take what you can use and leave small specimens.

---

## 55. Slippery Jack

**Species Name:** Slippery Jack
**Latin Name:** Suillus luteus
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** slippery-jack
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
A bolete of Scots pine woodland and plantations, with a slimy brown cap, yellow pores, and a distinctive ring on the stem. Edible once the slimy skin and spongy pore layer are removed, but not exceptional. Found specifically and only under Scots pine — the habitat is a reliable identification aid.

---

**Full Description:**
Slippery Jack is a pine woodland specialist — it grows under Scots pine and occasionally other two-needled pines, and is absent from all other habitats. This strict habitat association is one of the most useful identification features. If there is no Scots pine, it is not Slippery Jack.

The slimy cap skin is its most distinctive feature in the field — in wet weather the cap is viscous enough to slip from the fingers, giving the common name. This sliminess is also the reason to remove the cap skin before cooking: left on, it can cause gastrointestinal upset in some people and contributes an unpleasant texture to the cooked dish.

The flavour after preparation is decent but not exceptional — mild and slightly sweet. It is worth picking in quantity when the pine woodland is producing well, particularly for drying.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 5–12cm, convex, dark chestnut-brown, intensely slimy and shiny when moist, drying to a duller brown. The pore surface is yellow, fine-pored, and does not stain blue when bruised. The stem is 4–8cm, pale yellow to white, with a distinct ring — a membranous skirt that often has a purplish-brown lower edge. Above the ring the stem is pale and dotted; below it is darker and more fibrous. Flesh is pale yellow and does not change colour when cut. The Scots pine habitat, slimy brown cap, yellow pores, and ring on the stem together are specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Larch Bolete (*Suillus grevillei*) grows under larch exclusively, has a bright golden-yellow to orange cap (also slimy), yellow pores, and a ring. Edible. Habitat distinguishes the two — Scots pine versus larch.

Weeping Bolete (*Suillus granulatus*) grows under pine without a ring, with a similarly slimy brown cap. Edible but causes gastrointestinal upset in some people if prepared without removing the skin.

The ring on the stem is the feature that separates Slippery Jack from most other slimy pine boletes.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Strictly under Scots pine — in Caledonian pine forest in Scotland, pine plantations throughout Britain, and wherever Scots pine grows in parks and gardens. Absent from all other habitats. The most abundant edible bolete of Scottish pine woodland. Found wherever Scots pine is established — which in lowland England and Wales means largely in plantations.

---

**In Season:**
September to November. Peak in October under Scottish pine. Fruiting follows the same wet-after-dry trigger as other boletes. Check the ground under Scots pine from late September.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Peel the slimy cap skin before cooking — it pulls away easily when fresh. Remove the spongy pore layer as well, as it becomes unpleasantly gelatinous when cooked. What remains is the cap flesh: mild, slightly sweet, worth using. Sauté in butter or dry for later use. Dried Slippery Jack has better flavour than fresh and the preparation step is done before drying — peel the skin, remove the pore layer, slice thinly, and dry.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking for personal use from public woodland, including Forestry England managed pine forests, is generally permitted. Check notices at specific sites.

## 56. Saffron Milk Cap

**Species Name:** Saffron Milk Cap
**Latin Name:** Lactarius deliciosus
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** saffron-milk-cap
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
A distinctive milk cap of conifer woodland, with an orange-zoned cap, orange gills, and a bright orange-red latex that bleeds from any cut surface. Highly regarded in continental Europe — less commonly picked in Britain. The latex colour is the key identification feature. Several related milk caps require careful distinction.

---

**Full Description:**
Saffron Milk Cap is one of the most prized edible fungi in Spain and France, where it is sold commercially and eaten simply — fried in oil or grilled. In Britain it is less well known and less commonly picked, partly because its conifer woodland habitat is less familiar to most British foragers than deciduous woodland, and partly because the milk cap genus contains several toxic relatives that require attention.

The orange latex — the milk that bleeds from any cut or broken surface — is the most important identification feature. It is brilliant orange-red in fresh specimens and stains the fingers, knife, and cut surface. This colour is specific to *L. deliciosus* and its close relatives among the edible milk caps.

The species is most productive in Scotland, northern England, and parts of Wales under Scots pine and larch, though it occurs throughout Britain wherever suitable conifer woodland is established.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 5–15cm, convex then funnel-shaped, orange to orange-brown with concentric paler and darker zones — zoning is often visible. The surface is smooth, slightly sticky when moist, and often develops blue-green staining in older specimens. Gills are crowded, orange, running slightly down the stem, and stain green with age or bruising. The stem is 3–8cm, stout, the same orange colour as the cap, often with slightly paler, pitted spots. The latex: cut or break the cap and a bright orange-red latex bleeds from the cut surface immediately. This latex does not change colour on exposure to air in fresh specimens — it remains orange. In older specimens it may be reduced. The green staining on older gills and cap is characteristic of the species complex.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
False Saffron Milk Cap (*Lactarius deterrimus*) is closely related, growing under spruce rather than pine. The latex turns wine-red on exposure to air (not remaining orange) and the flavour is more bitter. Edible but inferior. Habitat and latex colour change distinguish it.

Woolly Milk Cap (*Lactarius torminosus*) has a pink-orange cap with a distinctly woolly, shaggy margin and white latex that does not change colour. Causes severe gastrointestinal cramps. The hairy cap margin and white latex are the disqualifiers.

Ugly Milk Cap (*Lactarius necator*) is dark olive-brown with white latex. Not similar in colour.

The orange latex that remains orange on exposure to air, combined with the orange-zoned cap under conifers, is the confirmation sequence.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Under conifers — primarily Scots pine and larch, occasionally spruce — in conifer woodland and mixed woodland with significant conifer component. Found throughout Britain wherever suitable conifers are established. Most productive in Scottish pine woodland and in pine plantations in northern England and Wales. Forms a mycorrhizal association with the host tree and returns to the same sites in favourable years.

---

**In Season:**
August to November. Peak fruiting September to October under Scottish pine. Fruiting follows warm, wet conditions in late summer and autumn.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
The traditional continental preparation is simplest: fry in olive oil in a hot pan until golden. The flavour is mild and slightly fruity — the orange colour fades on cooking and the flesh turns green-grey, which looks alarming but is entirely normal. Grilling over high heat is excellent. Use in pasta, risotto, or alongside game. The mild, slightly resinous flavour pairs well with strong herbs and pork. The green discolouration on cooking is a feature of the species, not a sign of spoilage.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking for personal use from public conifer woodland is generally permitted. Check Forestry England and private estate notices.

## 57. Amethyst Deceiver

**Species Name:** Amethyst Deceiver
**Latin Name:** Laccaria amethystina
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** amethyst-deceiver
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
A small, vivid purple-violet fungus of deciduous woodland, with widely spaced, deeply coloured gills and a thin fibrous stem. Edible, though small and not substantial enough to pick in quantity. The brilliant colour is striking and makes it memorable — but the violet woodland habitat also contains genuinely dangerous Cortinarius species requiring careful distinction.

---

**Full Description:**
Amethyst Deceiver earns its name from the way it changes with age and drying conditions. Young, fresh specimens in damp conditions are brilliant purple-violet throughout — cap, gills, and stem. In drier conditions or as they age, they fade to a dull pinkish-brown that barely resembles the fresh colour. A mushroom that looks vivid purple in the morning can look like a different species entirely by afternoon.

The edibility is real but limited. The caps are small — rarely more than 5cm — and the flesh is thin. Picking enough to make a meaningful dish requires finding a dense, productive patch. The colour makes them attractive as a garnish and as a visual element in a mixed mushroom dish.

The rating of Expert Only reflects the violet colouring and woodland habitat shared with several Cortinarius species, some of which are lethal.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 1–5cm, convex then flat to slightly depressed, vivid purple-violet when fresh and moist, fading to pinkish-buff when dry. The surface is slightly streaked and may crack in dry conditions. Gills are widely spaced, thick, and deeply purple-violet — more widely spaced than most small woodland fungi. The stem is 4–10cm, slender, fibrous, purple-violet. Flesh is thin and pale purple-lilac. Spore print: white. The wide spacing of the vivid purple gills is the most distinctive feature in fresh specimens.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Violet Webcap (*Cortinarius violaceus*) is deep violet-purple throughout, larger (cap 5–12cm), with a cobweb-like partial veil (cortina) leaving rusty fibres on the stem of young specimens, rusty-brown gills from the spore print, and grows in both deciduous and conifer woodland. Many Cortinarius species are seriously toxic. Spore print: rust-brown. Any violet-coloured fungus with a rusty-brown spore print is a webcap and should not be eaten.

Lilac Bonnet (*Mycena pura*) is small with lilac-pink colouring and crowded gills, grows in similar woodland habitat. Mildly toxic. The crowded gills distinguish it from the widely spaced gills of amethyst deceiver.

Always take a spore print when picking violet-coloured woodland fungi. White: amethyst deceiver. Rust-brown: webcap, do not eat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Deciduous woodland with well-developed leaf litter, particularly under beech, oak, and birch on acidic to neutral soils. Also found in mixed woodland. Widespread throughout Britain in established woodland. Often grows in scattered groups through the leaf litter layer.

---

**In Season:**
August to November. Fruiting peaks in September and October. Individual specimens are short-lived but the mycelium produces successive flushes through the season.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as a garnish and visual element. Sauté briefly in butter — the vivid colour fades significantly on cooking but does not disappear entirely. The flavour is mild and unremarkable. Best used in small amounts alongside more strongly flavoured fungi in a mixed mushroom dish, where the colour adds interest. Not worth picking as a standalone ingredient unless you find a very large, productive patch.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking for personal use from public woodland is permitted. Not a protected species.

---

## 58. Larch Bolete

**Species Name:** Larch Bolete
**Latin Name:** Suillus grevillei
**Type:** Fungi
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** larch-bolete
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
A slimy, golden-yellow to orange bolete found exclusively under larch from August to November. Edible once the slimy cap skin is removed. The strict larch habitat association makes identification straightforward — if there is no larch, it is not this species. Related to Slippery Jack but brighter and more orange in colour.

---

**Full Description:**
Larch Bolete is as habitat-specific as British fungi get. It grows under larch, and only under larch. This single fact simplifies identification enormously — a slimy, orange-yellow bolete under a larch tree is almost certainly this species.

The flavour after preparation is mild and acceptable. Like Slippery Jack, the slimy cap skin must be removed before cooking, both for texture and to avoid gastrointestinal irritation in those susceptible to it. The pore layer can also be removed, though it is less essential than with Slippery Jack.

In Britain, larch is most common in planted forestry — particularly in upland areas of Scotland, Wales, and northern England — and in parkland and estate planting. Larch bolete follows the tree wherever it is planted.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Cap is 4–10cm, convex, golden-yellow to bright orange-yellow, intensely slimy and shiny when moist. The pore surface is bright yellow, fine-pored, and does not stain blue when bruised. The stem is 4–8cm, yellow, with a distinct white to pale yellowish ring that may have a darker lower margin. Below the ring the stem often has glandular dots. Flesh is pale yellow and does not change colour when cut. The combination of bright orange-yellow slimy cap, yellow pores, ring on stem, and larch substrate is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Slippery Jack (*Suillus luteus*) is darker brown and grows under Scots pine, not larch. The colour difference — dark chestnut versus bright orange-yellow — is clear.

Weeping Bolete (*Suillus granulatus*) grows under pine without a ring. Under larch, the ring on the stem is diagnostic for larch bolete.

No dangerous British species closely resembles larch bolete under larch trees.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Exclusively under larch (*Larix decidua* and related species). Found in larch plantations, mixed woodland with larch, and parkland larch throughout Britain. Most abundant in upland forestry plantations in Scotland, Wales, and northern England. Wherever mature larch is established, larch bolete is likely present in fruiting years.

---

**In Season:**
August to November. Fruiting follows the same autumn pattern as other boletes. Check larch woodland from late August in good years.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Peel the slimy cap skin before use — it pulls away cleanly on fresh specimens. The pore layer can be left on or removed according to preference. Sauté in butter, or slice and dry for later use. The flavour is mild, slightly sweet, and similar to Slippery Jack. Best used in mixed mushroom dishes or soups where subtlety is appropriate. Drying concentrates the flavour usefully.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking for personal use from public and Forestry England managed larch woodland is generally permitted. Check notices at specific sites.

---

## 59. Common Mallow

**Species Name:** Common Mallow
**Latin Name:** Malva sylvestris
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** common-mallow
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A common wayside plant with striking pink-purple flowers and rounded, lobed leaves, found on roadsides, waste ground, and hedgerows throughout Britain from June to September. Leaves, flowers, and immature seed pods are all edible. One of the most versatile edible wild plants on disturbed urban ground — widely overlooked.

---

**Full Description:**
Common mallow is one of those plants that becomes more interesting the more you look at it. The flowers are attractive — deep pink-purple with darker veining — and the leaves, flowers, and immature seeds are all edible at different stages. It is a plant of roadsides and urban waste ground rather than pristine countryside, which makes it widely accessible.

The leaves have a mild, slightly mucilaginous quality when cooked — they thicken soups and stews in the same way as okra, to which mallow is related. This property has led to mallow being used in Middle Eastern and North African cuisines for centuries, particularly in cooked green dishes where the thickening is welcome.

The flowers are the most immediately appealing part. The five-petalled pink-purple flowers make a good garnish and can be used to make a blue-violet tea that turns pink when lemon is added — the pH-sensitive anthocyanins make it a natural litmus indicator as much as a drink.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial, 45–120cm, with a sprawling to erect habit. Leaves are 5–10cm across, rounded with 5–7 shallow, rounded lobes and a slightly wrinkled surface. The whole plant is softly hairy. Flowers are 3–5cm across, five-petalled, pink-purple with darker purple veining, appearing in clusters from the leaf axils from June to October. The immature seed pods ("cheeses") are round, flat, segmented, and green, borne at the leaf axils. Ripe seeds in the segments have a mild, pleasant flavour. The rounded lobed leaf and distinctive pink-purple five-petalled flower are specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Marsh Mallow (*Althaea officinalis*) is a related species of coastal and estuarine marshes, taller and more grey-woolly, with paler pink flowers. Equally edible.

Hollyhock (*Alcea rosea*) is a garden plant, much taller, with large flowers. Edible. The scale difference from common mallow is clear.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for common mallow.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, waste ground, hedgerow bases, disturbed soil, and the edges of urban green spaces throughout lowland Britain. Most common in southern and central England. Also found on coastal cliffs and dry grassland. A plant of human-disturbed ground — often more common in towns and on verges than in open countryside.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from June to October. Young leaves are best in spring before the plant flowers, but remain edible through summer. Immature seed pods (cheeses) from July. The plant is at its most productive June to September.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young leaves in salads or cooked as a pot herb — the slight mucilaginous quality thickens soups and stews. Use as you would spinach but expect the thickening effect. Flowers as garnish, in salads, or to make mallow tea — steep several flowers in hot water for a few minutes for a pale blue-violet infusion. Immature seed pods raw as a snack — mild, pleasant, slightly green-flavoured. In Middle Eastern cooking, dried mallow leaves (*khobeiza*) are used in cooked greens dishes that make the most of the thickening property.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking from public roadsides and waste ground for personal use is unrestricted. Not a protected species. Extremely common on disturbed urban ground.

## 60. Yarrow

**Species Name:** Yarrow
**Latin Name:** Achillea millefolium
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Expert Only
**Slug:** yarrow
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A widespread grassland and roadside plant with finely divided, feathery leaves and flat-topped clusters of small white flowers from June to September. Edible and aromatic — used as a herb in small quantities rather than a bulk green. Expert Only due to its strong resemblance to several dangerous umbellifers when young and not yet in flower.

---

**Full Description:**
Yarrow is a plant with a long history of culinary and medicinal use in Britain — the young leaves have a sharp, slightly bitter, aromatic flavour that works in small quantities as a herb. The dried flowers make a pleasant tea. It is common in grassland and on roadsides throughout Britain and not difficult to find.

The Expert Only rating is not about yarrow's own toxicity — it is a safe, mild herb. The rating is about the identification risk before the plant is in flower. The finely divided, feathery young leaves of yarrow look superficially similar to those of hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) and other dangerous umbellifers at the same early growth stage. An experienced forager confirms identification on multiple features simultaneously; a beginner may not. Once yarrow is in flower the identification is easy; before flowering it requires more care.

Use it as a culinary herb — three to four leaves — not as a salad green in volume.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial, 30–80cm, spreading by rhizomes and forming patches. Leaves are 5–15cm, alternate, deeply and repeatedly divided into many narrow, feathery segments — the specific epithet *millefolium* (thousand leaves) describes this accurately. The leaf surface is slightly hairy and grey-green. The whole plant has a strong, distinctive aromatic smell when crushed — sharp, slightly medicinal. This smell is the most important identification feature. Flowers are white (occasionally pale pink), tiny, arranged in flat-topped clusters (corymbs) 5–12cm across from June to October. Each individual flower head has 5 ray florets. The flat-topped white flower cluster in grassland, combined with the feathery grey-green leaves and strong aromatic smell, is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) has similarly divided, feathery leaves when young and also produces white flowers in flat-topped umbels. Hemlock is deadly — all parts are toxic, causing progressive paralysis and respiratory failure. Distinguishing features: hemlock grows taller (1–2.5m), typically in damp, disturbed ground near water; the stem has distinctive purple-red blotching; the leaves smell unpleasant — foetid, mousy — when crushed, not aromatic; the flowers are true umbels with compound branching, not flat-topped corymbs. Crush the leaf and smell it: yarrow is immediately aromatic and pleasant; hemlock smells unpleasant and mousy. Never rely on leaf shape alone in this plant family.

Fool's Parsley (*Aethusa cynapium*) has similar divided leaves, an unpleasant smell, and white umbel flowers. Toxic. Grows in disturbed ground and gardens. Smaller than yarrow at the same growth stage.

Wild Carrot (*Daucus carota*) has finely divided leaves, white umbel flowers, and is edible. The smell is carrot-like when crushed. Not dangerous.

Crush the leaf before picking anything with finely divided leaves. Aromatic and pleasant: yarrow. Unpleasant, foetid, or mousy: do not eat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Grassland, roadsides, field margins, meadows, cliff-top grassland, and any open, well-drained ground throughout Britain. One of the most common grassland plants in the country. Found from sea level to upland moorland edges. Grows in both improved and unimproved grassland.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers June to September. Young leaves from April. The leaves are most aromatic in spring before flowering, when growth is young and oils are at their highest concentration. Dried flowers for tea in July and August.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as a herb rather than a vegetable. Two or three young leaves chopped finely add a sharp, slightly bitter, aromatic note to salads, omelettes, and soft cheeses. The flavour is assertive — similar to a combination of tarragon and mild wormwood. Steep a few flower heads in hot water for a mild herbal tea. Use in the same way as you would use tarragon or lovage: sparingly, to add complexity to a dish. Do not cook large quantities.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking yarrow from public grassland and roadsides for personal use is unrestricted. Not a protected species. Extremely common.

---

## 61. Silverweed

**Species Name:** Silverweed
**Latin Name:** Potentilla anserina
**Type:** Roots
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** silverweed
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A low-growing plant of roadsides, damp grassland, and coastal ground with distinctive silver, pinnate leaves and yellow flowers. The starchy roots were a significant food source in Scotland and Ireland before the potato arrived. Edible boiled, roasted, or ground into flour — mild, slightly sweet, with a texture between parsnip and water chestnut.

---

**Full Description:**
Silverweed is one of those plants that rewards historical curiosity as much as culinary interest. Before the widespread adoption of potato in the eighteenth century, silverweed roots were a significant carbohydrate source across upland Scotland and Ireland — dug in autumn and spring, boiled or ground into a coarse flour, and eaten as a staple alongside dairy and fish. The specific epithet *anserina* refers to geese, which graze the plant heavily, but the roots were a human food long before geese claimed them.

The plant is common and easily found on the right ground — roadsides and path edges, coastal dunes, grazed grassland, and disturbed damp soil. The silver underside of the leaves makes it identifiable at a distance. The roots are small — pencil-thick at best on most plants — and digging them requires some effort. In sandy or loose coastal soil the roots come up more easily than in heavy clay.

The flavour is mild and starchy, slightly sweet, somewhere between a small parsnip and a water chestnut. Not spectacular, but genuinely interesting as a foraged root vegetable with a deep history.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A prostrate perennial spreading by long, reddish runners. Leaves are pinnate, 10–40cm, with pairs of toothed leaflets of alternating sizes along the stalk. The upper surface is green; the underside is densely covered in silvery-white silky hairs — the silver colouration is visible from above in good light and is the plant's most distinctive field character. The yellow flowers are 1.5–2cm across, five-petalled, appearing from May to August on separate stalks from the runners. The roots are thin, white to pale brown, and pencil-thick on productive plants in good soil. The silver leaf underside combined with the prostrate running habit and yellow five-petalled flowers is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Tormentil (*Potentilla erecta*) grows in similar grassy habitats and has four-petalled yellow flowers rather than five, and unwinged, non-silvery leaves. Not dangerous.

Creeping Cinquefoil (*Potentilla reptans*) has five-petalled yellow flowers and spreading runners but the leaves are palmate (five leaflets from a central point) rather than pinnate. Not dangerous.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for silverweed. The silver leaf underside is the definitive field character.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, path edges, damp grassland, coastal dunes, machair, and any disturbed, moderately fertile ground. Particularly common on damp compacted soils along tracks and field margins. Very common in coastal Scotland and Ireland on machair and dune grassland, where the sandy soil makes root digging practical. Found throughout Britain.

---

**In Season:**
Roots are present year-round but are best dug in spring (April to May) before the plant flowers, and in autumn (September to October) after flowering. The spring roots are the sweetest; autumn roots are larger. The above-ground plant is most identifiable from May to August when the silver leaves and yellow flowers are visible. Mark productive patches in summer, return to dig in spring or autumn.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Boil the roots whole until tender — about 15 minutes — then eat as you would baby potatoes or small parsnips. The mild, slightly sweet flavour pairs well with butter and salt. Roasting in a hot oven develops a little sweetness and colour. The roots can be dried and ground into a coarse flour used to thicken soups and make simple flatbreads. On coastal machair, where the soil is sandy and the roots grow larger and cleaner, the preparation effort is more rewarding than on heavy inland clay.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Digging roots requires landowner permission on private land. On public land and common ground, personal-use digging of silverweed roots is generally permitted, though the restriction on uprooting applies technically — check local access arrangements. The plant is not protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981.

---

## 62. Wood Violet

**Species Name:** Wood Violet
**Latin Name:** Viola riviniana
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** wood-violet
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
The most common native violet in Britain, flowering in hedgerows, woodland edges, and grassland from March to May. The flowers and leaves are both edible — the flowers as a garnish and crystallised sweet, the leaves as a mild salad addition. No dangerous lookalikes. One of the earliest spring flowers worth picking.

---

**Full Description:**
Wood violet appears in hedgerow banks and woodland edges in March, often alongside primrose and wood anemone, one of the first colours of spring after the long monochrome of winter. The purple-violet flowers are small but striking, and the plant is common enough to find without significant effort across most of Britain.

Both the flowers and leaves are edible. The flowers have a mild, faintly sweet, faintly floral flavour — they are not strongly flavoured enough to drive a dish, but they are pleasant raw and excellent crystallised. The leaves are slightly more substantial but still mild, with a gentle herby note and a faint mucilaginous quality that makes them useful as a subtle salad addition.

This is a plant for those who enjoy the pleasure of eating something seasonal, small, and specific rather than a foraging staple in any practical sense.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low perennial, 5–15cm. Leaves are heart-shaped to kidney-shaped, 2–6cm, with rounded, scalloped margins and a slightly hairy surface. They emerge on long stalks from a compact rosette at the base of the plant. Flowers are 15–25mm across, five-petalled, violet to blue-purple with a paler centre and fine darker veining, on separate stalks 5–15cm tall. The lower petal has a short spur projecting backwards — visible from the side. Stem leaves are similar to basal leaves but smaller. The heart-shaped leaves, violet five-petalled flowers with backward spur, and woodland edge or hedgerow habitat are together specific to the violet family.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Sweet Violet (*Viola odorata*) is very similar but strongly scented — wood violet has little to no scent. Both equally edible.

Common Dog-violet (*Viola riviniana*) is the same species — "wood violet" and "common dog-violet" are both used for this species.

Bugle (*Ajuga reptans*) has violet-blue flowers but they are tubular and two-lipped, quite unlike the open five-petalled violet flower.

No dangerous British plant closely resembles violet. The characteristic five-petalled flower with backward spur is specific to the genus.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerow banks, woodland edges, ancient woodland ground flora, grassland, and scrubby ground throughout Britain. Very common — one of the most widespread native plants in the country. Most abundant in the dappled shade of hedgerow banks and open woodland edges on moderately fertile, well-drained soils.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from March to May, occasionally producing cleistogamous (self-fertilising, petal-less) flowers in summer that are not worth picking. The spring flowering period is the picking window — the flowers are best in April at peak bloom. Leaves available from March through to summer but most tender in spring.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Flowers as garnish on salads, desserts, and soft cheese. Crystallised flowers — coated in egg white and fine caster sugar then dried — make a long-lasting sweet decoration. A small handful of flowers in a spring salad alongside mild leaves adds colour and a faint floral note. Young leaves in small quantities in salads — the mucilaginous quality gives body to a dressing. Violet flower syrup (flowers steeped in hot sugar syrup) is pale blue-violet and pleasant in drinks. Do not use in volume — this is a garnish and flavouring ingredient.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking violet flowers and leaves for personal use from public land is permitted. In ancient woodland SSSIs, local picking guidelines may apply. The plant is not protected under Schedule 8 but ancient woodland ground flora should be treated with care.

---

## 63. Sweet Violet

**Species Name:** Sweet Violet
**Latin Name:** Viola odorata
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sweet-violet
**Seasons:** Feb, Mar, Apr

---

**Short Description:**
The strongly scented wild violet of hedgerow banks and woodland edges, flowering from February in sheltered spots. The deep purple or white flowers have a distinctive, intense fragrance unlike any other British wild flower. Edible flowers used for crystallising, syrups, and garnishes. One of the earliest edible flowers of the year.

---

**Full Description:**
Sweet violet is the scented violet of cottage gardens and old hedgerow banks — the one that actually smells of violets, a perfume so specific and so distinctive that encountering it in a February hedgerow is startling. It flowers earlier than wood violet and is smaller and neater, with deeper purple flowers and leaves that are rounder and more clearly heart-shaped.

The fragrance is the point. The flowers are used in perfumery, confectionery, and cooking specifically for that scent — an intense, slightly powdery floral note that is genuinely concentrated enough to come through in crystallised sweets and syrups. It is brief: there is a phenomenon known as "violet anosmia" where the ionone compounds in violet scent temporarily numb the smell receptors to themselves, making the scent seem to disappear after a few minutes. It comes back.

The flowers are best in February and March before the plant produces cleistogamous seed-setting flowers in summer.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low perennial, 5–15cm, spreading by runners. Leaves are heart-shaped, 2–5cm, broader than they are long, with rounded scalloped margins and a softly hairy surface. The petiole is hairy. Flowers are 12–20mm, five-petalled, typically deep violet-purple (less commonly white or pale pink), with a strong, sweet fragrance. The spur behind the lower petal is slightly curved. The key distinction from wood violet is scent: sweet violet is unmistakably and strongly fragrant; wood violet has little to no scent. Both edible.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Wood Violet (*Viola riviniana*) is very similar visually. The scent test distinguishes them — sweet violet is strongly fragrant, wood violet is not. Both edible; the distinction matters for culinary use, where the fragrance is the value of sweet violet specifically.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for sweet violet.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerow banks, woodland edges, scrubby grassland, and old gardens. More restricted in range than wood violet — most common in southern England on chalky or well-drained soils. Less common in upland areas and Scotland. Often found in old gardens where it has naturalised from cultivation. Tolerates partial shade but flowers most prolifically in a sheltered, sunny hedgerow bank.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from late January in the mildest, most sheltered spots in the south; February and March across most of England. The season is short — four to six weeks — before the plant sets seed. Pick flowers in the morning when the scent is strongest and the petals are fully open.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Crystallised violets — the classic preparation. Coat individual flowers in egg white beaten to a light foam, dust with fine caster sugar, and dry on baking parchment in the lowest possible oven (or simply air dry in a warm room over 24 hours). The result keeps for weeks and retains the colour and faint fragrance. Violet syrup (flowers steeped in hot sugar syrup overnight) is pale blue-violet and fragrant — use in drinks, desserts, and pastry. Fresh flowers as garnish on cold desserts. A handful of petals (remove the green calyx) in a spring salad.

---

**Legal Notes:**

## 64. Meadowsweet

**Species Name:** Meadowsweet
**Latin Name:** Filipendula ulmaria
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** meadowsweet
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, frothy-flowered plant of damp meadows, riverbanks, and marshes, with a powerful, sweet, almond-like fragrance. The flowers are used for cordials, syrups, and infusions. The same plant contains salicylates — the chemical precursor to aspirin — so people sensitive to aspirin should avoid it. One of the most fragrant of all British wild flowers.

---

**Full Description:**
Meadowsweet's fragrance is one of the most evocative in the British countryside — heavy, sweet, slightly almond-like, with a depth that carries on warm summer air from some distance. It dominated damp meadows and riverbanks in June and July, and the flowers were historically used to scent homes, strew on floors, and flavour mead (one theory for its name).

The flowers are the useful part. They infuse readily into liquids, transferring their fragrance reliably into syrups, cordials, and creams. The flavour is unmistakable — sweet, floral, with a faint almond or marzipan note from the methyl salicylate compounds present throughout the plant.

These same salicylate compounds are chemically related to aspirin. Anyone who is sensitive to aspirin, takes blood-thinning medication, or has Reye's syndrome should avoid meadowsweet preparations. For everyone else it is entirely safe in reasonable culinary quantities.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial of damp ground, 60–120cm. Leaves are pinnate with pairs of large, toothed leaflets alternating with smaller ones along the stalk — the alternating large-small pattern is characteristic. The upper surface is dark green and hairless; the underside is white and woolly. Flowers are tiny, cream-white, five-petalled, borne in large, dense, frothy clusters at the stem tips. The whole plant has a strong, distinctive fragrance when the flowers are open or when any part is crushed. Stems are reddish-tinged and angular. Flowering from June to August.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) grows in some of the same damp, tall-herb habitats and has white umbel flowers. The differences are clear on examination: hemlock has finely divided, ferny leaves (not pinnate with large leaflets), purple blotches on the stem, and smells foetid when crushed — not of almond and sweetness. Never pick from tall, white-flowered damp vegetation without checking every plant feature.

Elder (*Sambucus nigra*) also has large, flat-topped cream-white flower clusters but is a woody shrub, not a herbaceous plant, with distinctive pinnate leaves and corky bark.

The strong sweet-almond fragrance, the pinnate leaves with alternating large and small leaflets, and the frothy cream flower clusters on a tall herbaceous plant in damp habitats are specific to meadowsweet.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Damp meadows, riverbanks, marshes, fens, and the margins of streams and ditches. Requires moist to wet conditions and does not thrive on dry soils. Found throughout Britain including Scotland, most abundantly in the north and west where damp ground is more prevalent. Often grows in dense, tall-herb communities alongside purple loosestrife, great willowherb, and common reed.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from June to August. Peak fragrance and flavour is in the first two weeks of flowering when the buds are just opening — fresh, open flowers have the strongest scent. As the flowers age and begin to go over, the fragrance turns slightly musty. Pick clusters where most florets are still in bud or just opening. Use within a few hours — the fragrance fades quickly once picked.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Meadowsweet cordial — flower heads steeped in hot sugar syrup with lemon overnight, then strained — is excellent. The flavour is distinctly sweet and almond-like, different from elderflower and worth making in its own right. Meadowsweet cream: steep a handful of flowers in warm double cream for 30 minutes, strain, and use in panna cotta or whipped cream for desserts. A few flower heads added to a gooseberry or strawberry jam while cooking add a faint floral note that works well with the fruit. Do not boil the flowers — heat above 80°C reduces the flavour. Cold or warm infusion only.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking meadowsweet flowers from public damp grassland and riverbanks for personal use is permitted. In protected wetland SSSIs and nature reserves, local picking guidelines may restrict it — check notices. Not a protected species.

## 65. Red Clover

**Species Name:** Red Clover
**Latin Name:** Trifolium pratense
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** red-clover
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
One of the most recognisable wildflowers in Britain, found in meadows, roadsides, and grassland from May to September. The pink-purple flower heads and three-part leaves marked with a pale chevron are unmistakable. Flowers and young leaves are edible — the flowers make a pleasant tea and work as a garnish. Genuinely a Beginner species: nothing dangerous looks like it.

---

**Full Description:**
Red clover is one of the few genuinely Beginner-rated flowers in British foraging. Almost everyone recognises it. The pink-purple globe-shaped flower head and trefoil leaf with its pale V-shaped marking are familiar from childhood. It grows in abundance on road verges, meadows, and rough grassland throughout Britain from late spring into autumn.

The flowers are the most useful part. They have a sweet, mildly honey-like flavour and make a good herbal tea — steep a few fresh or dried heads in hot water for a light, pleasant infusion. Fresh flowers can be added to salads and scattered over food as a garnish. The immature young leaves, before they toughen in summer, are edible raw in small quantities, though slightly bitter.

It is not an exciting culinary ingredient, but it is reliable, abundant, widely available, and exactly the kind of species a beginner forager can learn with complete confidence.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial of grassland and roadsides, 20–60cm. Leaves are trifoliate — three oval leaflets, each with a pale, whitish V-shaped or chevron marking on the upper surface. This chevron marking is consistent and reliable. Stems are hairy. Flower heads are globe-shaped, 2–4cm across, composed of many small, tubular, pink-purple florets, on hairy stalks. Flowers appear from May to September. The combination of trefoil leaves with pale V marking and globe-shaped pink-purple flower heads is unmistakable.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
White Clover (*Trifolium repens*) is a low-growing relative with white flower heads. Equally edible.

Zigzag Clover (*Trifolium medium*) is similar to red clover with slightly narrower leaflets and a less pronounced chevron. Equally edible.

Hare's-foot Clover (*Trifolium arvense*) has small, pale pink, fluffy flower heads. Edible.

No dangerous plant in Britain resembles red clover. The globe-shaped pink-purple flower head and three-part leaf with chevron are collectively specific.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Meadows, roadside verges, rough grassland, field margins, clover leys, and any open grassy ground throughout Britain. One of the most common and widespread flowering plants in the country. Particularly abundant in unimproved or semi-improved grassland in spring and early summer.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from May to September, with the first main flush in May and June and a second flush in August and September on plants that have been cut or grazed. Pick flowers when fully open and fresh.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Clover flower tea: steep 4–6 fresh or dried flower heads in hot water for 5 minutes. The result is mild, slightly sweet, and honey-like. Fresh flowers scattered over salads or used as a garnish. Dried flowers keep their flavour well and can be stored for winter tea. Young leaves in small quantities in spring salads — the slight bitterness works alongside other mild leaves. The flowers can be dipped in batter and lightly fried as fritters — sweet and pleasant.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking red clover from public grassland and roadsides for personal use is entirely unrestricted. Extremely common; no conservation concern.

---

## 66. Evening Primrose

**Species Name:** Evening Primrose
**Latin Name:** Oenothera biennis
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** evening-primrose
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, striking plant of sand dunes, roadsides, and disturbed ground with large, pale yellow flowers that open in the evening. Introduced from North America but naturalised widely across Britain. Roots, young leaves, and flowers are all edible. The roots of first-year plants are the most useful — starchy, slightly peppery, cooked as a root vegetable.

---

**Full Description:**
Evening primrose is not native to Britain. It arrived from North America in the seventeenth century and has naturalised on coastal dunes, sandy roadsides, railway embankments, and disturbed ground throughout southern and central England. It is now a familiar sight in these habitats without being native to them.

The plant is a biennial — first-year plants form a low rosette of leaves, second-year plants send up a tall flowering spike in June. Both are edible at different stages: the first-year root is the main culinary target, best dug in autumn before the plant sends up its flowering stem; the young leaves of first-year plants are edible in spring; and the large yellow flowers of the second-year plant can be used as a garnish.

The flowers open in the evening — hence the name — and attract moths for pollination. They close the following morning. They are large (3–5cm across), pale lemon-yellow, and individually handsome.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A biennial. First-year plants form a low, flat rosette of lance-shaped leaves, 10–30cm, with a reddish midrib. Second-year plants produce an erect stem, 50–150cm, with alternate lance-shaped leaves, terminating in a long spike of large flowers. Flowers are 4–6cm across, four-petalled, pale yellow, with a cross-shaped stigma. The flower buds are reddish. The stem and leaves are softly hairy. The large, four-petalled pale yellow flowers on a tall, hairy stem are distinctive. The reddish midrib on the first-year rosette leaves and the reddish flower buds are useful secondary features.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Large-flowered Evening Primrose (*Oenothera glazioviana*) and other introduced Oenothera species are present in Britain. All are edible. The genus is identifiable by the four-petalled pale yellow flowers and cross-shaped stigma.

Common Mullein (*Verbascum thapsus*) grows in similar habitats — sandy, disturbed ground — and also produces a tall flowering spike. The flowers are five-petalled and yellow but much smaller (2cm), and the leaves are enormously thick and woolly-grey. Not dangerous; easily distinguished.

No dangerous British plant closely resembles evening primrose when in flower.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Sand dunes, sandy roadsides, railway embankments, waste ground, and coastal disturbed ground throughout southern and central Britain. Most common in East Anglia, the south-east, and coastal areas. Less common in the north and Scotland. Requires well-drained, light soils and open, sunny conditions.

---

**In Season:**
First-year roots: dig in autumn (September to November) before the ground freezes and the plant directs energy into its flowering stem. Young rosette leaves: March to May of the first year. Flowers: June to September of the second year, opening in the evening and closing by morning.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
First-year roots: scrub, peel, and boil or roast — the flavour is starchy and mildly peppery, slightly mucilagenous when boiled, better roasted. Use as you would a small parsnip. Young leaves: add in small quantities to salads or wilt briefly as a mild pot herb. Flowers: use as a garnish on salads and cold plates — they are large enough to be striking and the pale yellow colour is good. The flowers can be briefly sautéed in butter as a side dish; the petals collapse quickly.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Digging first-year roots requires care on public land — the restriction on uprooting plants without landowner permission applies, though evening primrose is introduced and not native. On private land, seek permission. Not a protected species.

---

## 67. Lime Blossom

**Species Name:** Lime Blossom
**Latin Name:** Tilia cordata
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** lime-blossom
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul

---

**Short Description:**
The small, fragrant flowers of the small-leaved lime — one of Britain's native linden trees — collected in June and July for tea and infusions. The flowers have a mild, sweet, slightly honeyed fragrance and make one of the most pleasant wild herbal teas available. Found in ancient woodland and widely planted in urban streets.

---

**Full Description:**
Lime blossom tea is a well-established European tradition, particularly in France where *tilleul* — lime flower infusion — is a standard herbal drink. The British equivalent is right there in parks, avenue plantings, and ancient woodland throughout the country, largely unpicked.

Both small-leaved lime (*Tilia cordata*) and large-leaved lime (*Tilia platyphyllos*) produce usable flowers, as does the hybrid common lime (*Tilia x europaea*), which is the most commonly planted street tree in Britain. All three are edible. *Tilia cordata* is the native species; the hybrid and large-leaved lime are also native or naturalised.

The flowers, collected with their pale green wing-like bracts, are the picking target. Pick them when the flowers are just fully open and still fresh — they deteriorate quickly. Dry in a single layer in a warm place and store in an airtight jar for winter use.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A medium to large deciduous tree, 10–30m. Leaves are heart-shaped, 3–9cm (small-leaved lime) to 6–15cm (large-leaved lime), with a pointed tip, asymmetric base, and finely toothed margins. The underside of small-leaved lime leaves has tufts of brown hairs in the vein axils — useful for species identification, though less critical for foraging. Flowers are small, 5–8mm, creamy-white to pale yellow, strongly fragrant, hanging in clusters of 4–12 from a distinctive strap-like pale green bract 4–10cm long. The bract remains attached to the fruit stalk after the flowers fall. Street trees in Britain are predominantly the hybrid *Tilia x europaea*, recognisable by its large, slightly lumpy trunks and prolific sprouts at the base.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous lookalikes exist for lime blossom. The distinctive pale green wing-like bract attached to the flower cluster is unique to Tilia and found on no other British tree. The sweet fragrance confirms identification.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Ancient woodland in England and Wales (small-leaved lime is a significant component of ancient woodland on suitable soils), avenue plantings in parks and gardens, and urban street trees (hybrid lime). The street tree planting makes lime blossom surprisingly accessible in towns and cities throughout Britain. Country-house avenues of lime are particularly productive. The flowers are at height on street trees — pick from lower branches or collect freshly fallen flowers from the ground on still days.

---

**In Season:**
Small-leaved lime flowers in late June to July. Large-leaved lime flowers a week or two earlier. The hybrid common lime flowers in between. The season is brief — two to three weeks per tree. The flowers smell strongly and sweetly when open; the fragrance fades as they age. Pick when the flowers are open and fragrant, not yet browning at the edges.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Lime blossom tea: steep 3–4 fresh flower clusters (with bracts) in hot water for 5–7 minutes. The result is pale gold, mild, sweet, and slightly honeyed. One of the best wild herbal teas available in Britain. Dried flowers keep the flavour well — dry in a single layer in a warm, dry room or in the lowest possible oven. Use dried flowers through autumn and winter. Fresh flowers can be used in infusions for panna cotta, custard, and ice cream — the flavour infuses well into cream and milk.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking lime blossom from publicly accessible trees in parks and on streets for personal use is generally unrestricted. Do not pick from private gardens without permission. Street trees are managed by local authorities — collecting a small personal quantity from the flowers is not restricted in practice.

---

## 69. Sweet Woodruff

**Species Name:** Sweet Woodruff
**Latin Name:** Galium odoratum
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sweet-woodruff
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
A low, carpet-forming plant of shaded woodland with whorled leaves and small white flowers in spring. The dried plant has a powerful, sweet vanilla-like fragrance from coumarin — quite different from the fresh plant, which is nearly odourless. Used to flavour drinks, particularly the German Maibowle (May wine). Not for use in large quantities.

---

**Full Description:**
Sweet woodruff is a plant whose interest lies almost entirely in what happens when it dries. The fresh plant has almost no smell — a faint green, grassy quality at most. Dried, it develops a powerful, sweet, vanilla-like fragrance from coumarin compounds that form as the plant wilts and dries. This is the same compound that gives new-mown hay its characteristic smell.

The dried plant has been used to flavour wine and drinks since medieval times. The German tradition of Maibowle — white wine infused with fresh woodruff, sparkling water, and sometimes strawberries — is the best-known culinary use. The fresh plant in late April and May is the one to use for this purpose: it has just enough coumarin at the point of picking to infuse the wine without over-medicating it.

Coumarin in large quantities has anticoagulant properties — it is related to warfarin. This does not mean woodruff is dangerous in normal culinary use, but it does mean it should be used in the quantities a flavouring requires, not eaten in volume as a vegetable.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low, spreading perennial, 15–40cm, forming carpets in shaded woodland. Leaves are lance-shaped, 2–4cm, in neat whorls of 6–8 around the square stem. The leaf margins and midrib have tiny forward-pointing hooks visible under magnification. Flowers are small, white, four-petalled, borne in loose clusters above the leaf whorls from April to June. The square stem, the neat whorls of lance-shaped leaves, and the white four-petalled flowers in shaded woodland are together specific. Fresh plant: faint smell. Dried plant: strong vanilla-like coumarin fragrance.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Cleavers (*Galium aparine*) is a close relative with whorled leaves and a square, hooked stem. It is a scrambling annual of hedgerows and disturbed ground rather than a low perennial of shaded woodland, and the leaves are narrower and more hooked. Edible.

Lady's Bedstraw (*Galium verum*) has narrow leaves in whorls and yellow flowers. Grows in open grassland. Edible.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for sweet woodruff.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Shaded deciduous woodland, particularly on calcareous (chalk and limestone) soils, forming dense carpets under beech and ash. Also found in moist, shaded hedgerows and on damp, shaded limestone rock faces. Most abundant in the south and east of England on chalk and limestone woodland. Also found in Wales and into Scotland in suitable conditions.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from April to June. The fresh plant for Maibowle is best picked in late April and May — just before or at the point of flowering. For drying, pick at peak flowering and dry in small bunches in a warm, dry place.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Maibowle (May wine): steep a small bundle of fresh woodruff (about 30g) in a bottle of dry white wine in the fridge for 30 minutes — no longer, or the flavour becomes medicinal. Remove the woodruff, add sparkling water and sliced strawberries. A traditional German spring drink. The dried plant can be used in pot pourri and to scent linen, and dried woodruff sachets in wine is the principle behind the traditional use. Do not eat in large quantities as a vegetable or salad ingredient.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking sweet woodruff from public woodland for personal use is permitted. On chalk woodland SSSIs, local guidelines may apply. Not a protected species.

The young leaves are better than the older ones, which become tougher and more strongly flavoured. The flowers are mild and pleasant raw.

It is not a foraging staple but it is an early, reliable, and easily identified edible wild plant of a specific habitat. Finding it is a sign that the damp meadow or streamside grassland you are walking through is unimproved and ecologically interesting.

Bistort is a plant with deep roots in northern English food culture. Dock pudding — made from bistort leaves, oatmeal, nettles, and onion, fried in butter — is a traditional dish of the Lake District and Pennines, still made and competed over at the World Dock Pudding Championship in Cumbria. The name "dock pudding" is a misnomer: it is bistort, not dock, that goes in.

The plant grows in damp, traditionally managed meadows in the north and west of England and in upland Wales and Scotland, and is an indicator of long-undisturbed grassland. The young leaves in May and June are the picking target — tender, mild, and slightly mucilaginous when cooked, with a flavour somewhere between spinach and sorrel without the pronounced sourness of the latter.

It is less common in the south of England and largely absent from the drier east. The Lake District and the Pennine Dales are its stronghold.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial of damp grassland, 30–80cm. Basal leaves are large, 10–20cm, oval to triangular with a heart-shaped or slightly arrow-shaped base, smooth, mid-green. The leaf stalk is winged — a flange of green tissue running along it. Stem leaves are smaller and clasp the stem with arrow-shaped bases. The flower spike is dense, cylindrical, 3–6cm, pale pink to white, appearing from May to July on a long, erect stem. The knobbly, twisted rhizome is characteristic but underground. The combination of large oval leaves with winged stalks, and the dense pale pink cylindrical flower spike in damp northern meadow, is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Common Dock (*Rumex obtusifolius*) has similar large, oval leaves in the same habitats. The leaves lack the winged leaf stalk of bistort, the flower spike is a loose, branched structure rather than a dense cylindrical spike, and the plant has no distinctive smell. Edible but significantly less good.

Lords and Ladies (*Arum maculatum*) produces large, arrow-shaped leaves in spring in some damp habitats. The leaves are glossier and more clearly arrow-shaped, without the winged leaf stalk, and any raw contact with the sap causes burning of the mouth. Smell and habitat differ — bistort is a meadow plant; lords and ladies prefers shaded woodland and hedgerow banks.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Damp meadows, hay meadows, streamside grassland, and the margins of ditches and ponds, particularly in northern England, the Lake District, Pennine Dales, upland Wales, and parts of Scotland. An indicator plant of traditionally managed, long-undisturbed damp grassland. Absent from most of lowland England south of the Midlands.

---

**In Season:**
Young leaves in May and June are the picking target — tender and mild before the plant flowers fully. The plant flowers May to July; once in full flower the leaves become tougher and less good. Pick the youngest, smallest leaves from the base of the plant.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Dock pudding: blanch bistort leaves with young nettle tops, chop, mix with cooked oatmeal and finely chopped spring onion, season, and fry in butter as small patties. The traditional Lake District version. The leaves can also be wilted in butter and used as a simple cooked green alongside lamb or beef — the mild, slightly mucilaginous character works well with strong-flavoured meat. Not good raw in any significant quantity.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking bistort leaves from public meadows and accessible damp grassland for personal use is permitted. Many productive bistort sites are in SSSIs and nature reserves — read notices and tread carefully on species-rich meadow turf. Not a protected species.

## 72. Scurvygrass

**Species Name:** Scurvygrass
**Latin Name:** Cochlearia officinalis
**Type:** Coastal
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** scurvygrass
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
A small, white-flowered coastal plant of salt marshes, sea cliffs, and coastal roadsides, with fleshy, kidney-shaped leaves and an intense, wasabi-like heat. Used historically to prevent scurvy on long sea voyages. A true flavour specialist's ingredient — the hot, pungent leaves work in small quantities as a seasoning and garnish.

---

**Full Description:**
Scurvygrass earned its name from its documented use by sailors and arctic explorers to prevent scurvy — the Vitamin C content is genuinely high, and the plant was gathered and eaten on coasts wherever it was found during long voyages. The historical significance is interesting; the culinary application is more immediate.

The flavour is the surprise. A small leaf of scurvygrass has a sharp, hot, wasabi-like punch that is quite unlike any other British coastal plant. It is not a salad green in any conventional sense — eating a bowlful would be unpleasant. It is a seasoning, used in the way you might use wasabi or English mustard: in small quantities to add heat and punch to a finished dish.

It grows abundantly on coastal clifftops, in salt marshes, and increasingly along major roads inland where salt spray from gritting has created suitable conditions on road verges. The inland road verge populations are substantial and often accessible where coastal sites are not.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A biennial or short-lived perennial, 10–40cm on coastal sites, often prostrate in exposed positions. Leaves are fleshy, bright green, kidney-shaped to heart-shaped, 1–4cm, with a slightly wavy margin. The texture is succulent and crisp. Stem leaves clasp the stem with rounded, ear-like lobes. Flowers are small, white, four-petalled (cross-shaped), borne in dense clusters from March to June. The fleshy, kidney-shaped leaves with clasping stem leaves and white cross-shaped flowers in a coastal or salty roadside habitat are specific. Taste a small leaf: the hot, pungent flavour is immediate and unmistakable.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Danish Scurvygrass (*Cochlearia danica*) is smaller and earlier-flowering, found in similar coastal and road verge habitats. Equally edible and equally pungent.

Common Whitlowgrass (*Erophila verna*) is a tiny, white-flowered annual of dry, open ground — much smaller than scurvygrass with non-fleshy leaves. Edible but not pungent. Not dangerous.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for scurvygrass in its coastal or road verge habitats.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Coastal salt marshes, sea cliffs, rocky shores, and coastal cliff-top grassland. Also now commonly found on the central reservations and verges of major A-roads and motorways in England, where winter gritting creates salty soil conditions that suit it. The inland road verge habitat has expanded significantly since the 1980s — a useful finding if coastal access is limited.

---

**In Season:**
Young leaves from February on sheltered coastal sites. Main season March to June before the plant sets seed. The leaves are most pungent and tender in spring. Road verge populations flower slightly later than coastal populations in the same region.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as a seasoning rather than a bulk green. A few leaves, finely chopped, on grilled fish or shellfish provide a sharp, hot note that pairs well with the sea. Mix into a butter with lemon for a finishing butter on white fish. Add to a seaweed or coastal salad in small quantities. The flavour is too intense for more than a garnish-level quantity in most dishes. Works well alongside other foraged coastal ingredients — laver, sea purslane, rock samphire.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking scurvygrass from public coastal land for personal use is permitted. On road verges, the land is typically highway authority property — picking personal quantities of plants from verges is not restricted in practice. Not a protected species.

## 73. Sea Beet

**Species Name:** Sea Beet
**Latin Name:** Beta vulgaris subsp. maritima
**Type:** Coastal
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sea-beet
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
The wild ancestor of beetroot, spinach beet, and sugar beet, growing on shingle beaches, sea walls, and coastal disturbed ground throughout Britain. The leaves are thick, glossy, and dark green with a mild, earthy flavour very close to cultivated spinach. One of the most useful coastal vegetables — productive, easy to identify, and good to eat.

---

**Full Description:**
Sea beet is the plant that all cultivated beets come from — beetroot, chard, perpetual spinach, and sugar beet are all domesticated varieties of *Beta vulgaris*, and the wild coastal plant is their ancestor. Knowing this makes the flavour less surprising: sea beet leaves taste like a slightly stronger version of spinach beet, with a mild, earthy sweetness and a satisfying, meaty texture when cooked.

It is one of the most consistently productive coastal vegetables available to the forager. The leaves are present for much of the year on sheltered coasts, and a single plant can yield several handfuls of leaves over a long season. The shingle beach and sea wall habitat is accessible on most stretches of the British coast.

The leaves should be picked young and medium-sized — the largest, oldest leaves become tougher and more strongly flavoured. Cook as you would spinach or chard.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial, sprawling to erect, 30–120cm. Leaves are thick, leathery, and glossy — this fleshy texture is distinctive and separates sea beet from most other coastal greens. Dark green above, slightly paler below, with a prominent pale midrib. The leaves are broadly triangular to rhomboid, 5–15cm, with a slightly wavy margin. Stems are reddish-tinged. The flowering spike is tall and branched, bearing small, green, inconspicuous flowers from July. The thick, glossy, dark green leaf with a pale midrib and reddish stem on shingle or coastal disturbed ground is specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Orache (*Atriplex* spp.) grows in similar coastal habitats and has similarly shaped leaves, but they are grey-green and mealy-textured rather than dark glossy green. Edible.

Sea Purslane (*Atriplex portulacoides*) has smaller, grey-green, oval leaves and a more sprawling habit. Edible.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for sea beet on the British coast. The thick, glossy, dark green leaf is specific.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Shingle beaches, sea walls, coastal cliffs, saltmarsh edges, and any disturbed coastal ground above the tide line. Found on most stretches of the British coast including Scotland. Most productive on south and east-facing shingle banks and the bases of sea walls where the plants grow large and productive.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves available for most of the year on sheltered coasts. Best from June to October when growth is most vigorous. The plant dies back partially in hard winters but recovers quickly. Young leaves in spring are the most tender; summer and autumn leaves are larger and more robust.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Cook exactly as spinach or chard — wilted in butter, blanched and squeezed dry, or added to soups and pasta. The flavour is stronger than cultivated spinach and holds up well to cooking without becoming bland. Excellent as a bed for grilled fish or shellfish fresh from the same stretch of coast. The thick leaves take slightly longer to wilt than spinach. The stems of young plants are tender enough to eat; older stems become fibrous and are better discarded. Works well in a coastal frittata with sea herbs and eggs.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking sea beet leaves from public coastal land for personal use is permitted. Not a protected species. Do not strip individual plants bare — the perennial root needs foliage to survive and regenerate.

## 74. Sea Aster

**Species Name:** Sea Aster
**Latin Name:** Tripolium pannonicum
**Type:** Coastal
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sea-aster
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A salt marsh plant with fleshy, narrow leaves and lilac daisy flowers in late summer. The young leaves and stems are edible — salty, slightly bitter, and succulent. Found in salt marshes and tidal mudflats around the British coast. A genuine coastal vegetable with good flavour, underused and little known outside specialist foraging circles.

---

**Full Description:**
Sea aster grows in the upper zones of salt marshes, in tidal creeks, and on the edges of coastal mudflats, often in company with sea purslane, sea lavender, and glasswort. The plant is subjected to regular tidal inundation, and the fleshy leaves reflect this: they are full of water, slightly salty, and have a pleasant, mild, slightly bitter flavour that works well in coastal cooking.

The flowers are small, lilac daisy-like, and appear from July to September — they are edible as a garnish but the leaves and young stems are the more useful parts. The plant is widely eaten in France, where it appears on restaurant menus in coastal Brittany and Normandy as a salted, foraged vegetable alongside fish.

In Britain it is almost entirely unpicked, which is an oversight. A productive salt marsh in August yields tender young shoots that, blanched briefly to reduce the salt content and wilted in butter, are excellent with grilled fish or shellfish.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial, 15–60cm, of salt marsh and tidal zones. Leaves are fleshy, narrow, 3–10cm, with a smooth surface and a clearly fleshy, succulent texture. The lower leaves have a slight stalk; upper leaves clasp the stem. Stems are fleshy and often reddish. The flower heads are 1–2cm across, with pale lilac to purple ray florets surrounding a yellow disc, appearing in dense clusters at the stem tips from July to September. The fleshy narrow leaves, salt marsh habitat, and lilac daisy flowers together are specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Sea Lavender (*Limonium vulgare*) grows in the same salt marsh habitat with small purple flowers, but the flowers are tubular and borne in flat-topped sprays quite unlike the daisy flower heads of sea aster. The leaves are broader and less fleshy. Edible in small quantities.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for sea aster in salt marsh habitats.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Salt marshes, tidal creek edges, mudflats, and brackish coastal ground throughout Britain. Most abundant on the east and south coasts of England where extensive salt marshes develop in sheltered estuaries. Also found on the west coast of England and Wales and parts of Scotland. The mid to upper salt marsh zone is the most productive habitat.

---

**In Season:**
Young shoots and leaves from June. Best picking July to September when the plant is in active growth. The young growth at the tips of stems is the most tender. Leaves become tougher and more fibrous after flowering. The plant dies back in winter.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Blanch young leaves and stems briefly in boiling water to reduce the salt content, then drain and dress with butter or olive oil. The flavour is mild and slightly bitter with a pleasant saline edge that makes salt largely unnecessary. Serve alongside grilled sea bass, plaice, or shellfish for a fully coastal plate. Works in a salt marsh vegetable medley alongside samphire and sea purslane. The flowers make a good garnish on seafood dishes.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking sea aster from public salt marsh for personal use is permitted. Many productive salt marshes are nature reserves or SSSIs — check notices and stick to accessible edges. Do not pick in nature reserves without checking guidelines.

## 75. Sea Rocket

**Species Name:** Sea Rocket
**Latin Name:** Cakile maritima
**Type:** Coastal
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sea-rocket
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A sprawling, fleshy annual of sandy beaches and coastal dunes with small lilac-pink flowers and deeply lobed, succulent leaves. The leaves have a strong, peppery, rocket-like flavour — genuinely useful as a coastal seasoning. Grows in the strandline zone of sandy beaches throughout Britain and is one of the more flavourful coastal greens.

---

**Full Description:**
Sea rocket grows on the strandline — the zone of seaweed and debris deposited at the high tide mark — on sandy beaches, coastal dunes, and shingle banks throughout Britain. It is an annual that germinates in spring, grows rapidly through summer, and dies after seeding in autumn.

The name is accurate. The flavour is rocket — sharp, peppery, and slightly mustardy — but stronger and more saline than the cultivated version, with the fleshy, almost succulent texture typical of plants adapted to coastal salt conditions. It is one of the most directly useful coastal greens in terms of flavour: strong enough to work as a seasoning in its own right, interesting enough to justify picking in small quantities whenever you encounter it.

The leaves are best when young and picked from the growing tips. Older, larger leaves become tougher and more intensely flavoured to the point of bitterness.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A sprawling annual, 15–45cm, with fleshy, succulent stems. Leaves are 3–8cm, deeply pinnately lobed with rounded segments — the lobing gives them a feathery, irregular outline. The texture is fleshy and slightly crisp. Flowers are four-petalled (cross-shaped), 6–12mm, pale lilac to white-pink, appearing from June in loose clusters. Seed pods are distinctive: two-jointed, the upper joint detaching at maturity to float and disperse on water. The fleshy, deeply lobed leaves on a sprawling plant in the strandline zone of a sandy beach, with lilac-pink four-petalled flowers, are together specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Sea Sandwort (*Honckenya peploides*) is a low, creeping plant of sandy coastal ground with small white flowers and oval, fleshy, untoothed leaves. Edible but much milder and less interesting.

Sea Kale (*Crambe maritima*) has large, glaucous blue-grey, wavy-edged leaves and grows on shingle — a quite different appearance. Edible and good.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for sea rocket in its strandline beach habitat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Sandy beaches, shingle and sandy strandlines, and the fore-dune zone of coastal dune systems. Found on most sandy and shingle beaches around Britain, though less common on exposed north and west-facing coasts. Most productive on the sheltered sandy beaches of the south and east coasts of England. The strandline zone — the band of seaweed and debris at the high tide mark — is the primary habitat.

---

**In Season:**
June to September. The plant appears in June as it germinates and grows, reaching full size by July and August. Young growing tips are the best picking throughout the season. The plant flowers and seeds from June onwards; after seeding the leaves become progressively tougher. The season ends when autumn storms and cold destroy the plant.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as a peppery coastal green, raw or barely cooked. Young leaves and growing tips raw in a coastal salad, alongside other shore-gathered ingredients. Scatter over grilled or raw fish — the sharp, peppery heat cuts through rich oily fish particularly well. Wilted briefly in butter as a side dish, the flavour mellows to something less aggressive but still characterful. The flowers are milder than the leaves and make a good garnish.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking sea rocket from public beaches and coastal land for personal use is unrestricted. Not a protected species. Found on most accessible sandy beaches.

## 76. Wood Avens

**Species Name:** Wood Avens
**Latin Name:** Geum urbanum
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** wood-avens
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
A common hedgerow and woodland edge plant whose roots smell and taste distinctly of cloves. Found throughout Britain in shaded, moderately fertile conditions from spring to autumn. The clove-scented roots are the main culinary interest — used dried as a spice and to flavour drinks and preserves. Also known as herb bennet.

---

**Full Description:**
Wood avens is not an impressive-looking plant. The small yellow flowers and hook-tipped seeds — the ones that catch on clothing — make it familiar without being memorable. What is memorable is what happens when you dig up and snap the root: a clear, sweet, clove-like fragrance, surprisingly strong and entirely specific.

The roots contain eugenol — the same compound that gives cloves their flavour — and have been used as a clove substitute in cooking and medicine in Britain since at least the medieval period. The dried root is genuinely useful as a mild spice and as a flavouring for drinks and preserves in the same way as you might use a stick of cinnamon or a few dried cloves.

The leaves are edible but mild and unremarkable — the root is the reason to know this plant.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial, 20–60cm. Basal leaves are pinnate with a large, rounded terminal leaflet and smaller lateral ones; stem leaves are simpler, three-lobed. All leaves have toothed margins and slightly hairy surfaces. The yellow flowers are five-petalled, 8–15mm across, held upright on long stalks, appearing from May to August. The fruit is a rounded cluster of hooked achenes that attach readily to fabric and fur. The root: dig a small section and snap it — the clove scent is immediate and strong. This smell is the defining identification feature.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Water Avens (*Geum rivale*) grows in damp, shaded habitats and has drooping, bell-shaped, pink-orange flowers — quite different from the upright yellow flowers of wood avens. The roots of water avens also have a mild clove scent. Edible.

Cinquefoils (*Potentilla* spp.) have similar yellow five-petalled flowers but their leaves are palmate or have more uniform leaflets, and the roots do not smell of cloves.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for wood avens. The clove-scented root is definitive.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Shaded hedgerow banks, woodland edges, the bases of walls, and gardens throughout Britain. Thrives in partial shade on moderately fertile, well-drained soils. One of the most common plants of hedgerow bases and urban woodland edges. Found throughout Britain including urban areas.

---

**In Season:**
Roots can be dug year-round but are strongest in flavour in spring (before flowering) and autumn (after the plant has died back). The above-ground plant is identifiable from May to August when leaves and flowers are present. Dig roots only from sites where the plant is abundant — it is common but the root takes several years to grow to useful size.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Dry the roots: wash, slice thinly, and dry in a low oven or in a warm, dry place. Dried, they retain the clove flavour for months. Use as you would cloves or cinnamon — in mulled drinks, steeped in warm milk for a mild clove custard, or used to flavour a simple syrup. A few pieces of dried root in sloe gin or hedgerow wine add a spiced depth. The fresh root steeped briefly in warm ale is a historical drink called "ale and herb bennet." The dried root in a small jar makes a pleasant storecupboard spice from a hedgerow weed.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Digging wood avens roots requires landowner permission on private land. From public hedgerow banks and accessible ground, personal-use digging of abundant, established plants is generally practised without restriction. Not a protected species.

## 77. Hawthorn Blossom

**Species Name:** Hawthorn Blossom
**Latin Name:** Crataegus monogyna
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** hawthorn-blossom
**Seasons:** Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
The white flowers of the hawthorn, appearing in April and May and covering the hedgerows in a brief, spectacular display. Edible flowers with a mild, slightly almond-like, faintly sweet flavour. Used in cordials, infusions, and as a garnish. Picking the flowers reduces the autumn berry crop on the same plant — pick lightly from any individual tree.

---

**Full Description:**
Hawthorn blossom fills lowland Britain's hedgerows in May with a mass of white flowers and a scent that is simultaneously sweet and slightly unpleasant — the almond-like fragrance mixed with a note variously described as "fishy" or "earthy," caused by trimethylamine, the same compound released in decaying fish. This is not necessarily a drawback — the smell is characteristic and the taste of the flowers is considerably more pleasant than the smell suggests.

The flowers are edible raw, scattered over salads or used as a garnish, and can be made into a simple cordial or infused in honey. They have a mild, slightly sweet, faintly almond-like flavour that is pleasant without being strongly characterful.

This is the same plant as the Hawthorn entry (berries and spring leaves). Picking the flowers reduces the berry crop later in the season — be selective about how much you take from any single tree. The blossom entry exists separately because the picking window, culinary application, and seasonal context are completely different.

---

**Identification Notes:**
For full plant identification see the Hawthorn entry. In flower, hawthorn is unmistakable in a British hedgerow context: dense clusters of small, five-petalled white flowers (each 8–15mm across) with pink-tinged anthers, covering the branches before or as the deeply lobed leaves fully open. The almond-fishy fragrance is characteristic. Flowers in April and May. No other common hedgerow shrub flowers white in this way at this time of year in this density. ---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**

No dangerous lookalikes exist for hawthorn blossom.

---

**Habitat:**
See Hawthorn entry. Ubiquitous in hedgerows, scrubland, and woodland edges throughout Britain.

---

April to May. The timing varies by two to three weeks between southern England (earlier) and Scotland (later). The blossom season for any individual tree is brief — one to two weeks. A cold snap can delay or shorten the season. Pick flowers when fully open and fresh, before they begin to brown.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Fresh flowers scattered over salads — the mild flavour and visual effect. Hawthorn blossom cordial: steep a large quantity of flowers in hot sugar syrup with lemon, leave overnight, strain. The result is pale, delicately flavoured, and floral. Infuse in honey for a light blossom honey. A few flower clusters in a simple spring salad dressing. The flowers can be crystallised in the same way as violet flowers for decoration. Avoid the leaves on the same cluster when using flowers raw — the young leaves are edible but have a slightly different character.

---

**Legal Notes:**

## 78. Primrose

**Species Name:** Primrose
**Latin Name:** Primula vulgaris
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** primrose
**Seasons:** Feb, Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
One of Britain's most iconic spring wildflowers, appearing in hedgerow banks and woodland edges from February. The pale yellow flowers and young leaves are edible — the flowers mildly sweet, the leaves slightly more bitter. Best used as a garnish and in small quantities. Primroses are declining in some areas — pick sparingly and never strip a plant.

---

**Full Description:**
The primrose is as closely associated with early British spring as any plant — the pale yellow flowers appearing in February in sheltered hedgerow banks, often before most other colour has returned to the countryside. It is a plant that almost everyone in Britain recognises and many feel attached to.

It is also edible, which fewer people know. The flowers have a mild, sweet, faintly floral flavour and work well in crystallised form, as a salad garnish, and in simple spring preparations. The leaves are more substantial but also more bitter — small quantities work in salad; larger amounts are less pleasant.

Primroses are not abundant everywhere. The species has declined in some regions due to agricultural improvement, loss of hedgerow habitat, and in some areas picking pressure. Pick only a very small number of flowers from any plant in any visit — the plant needs to seed and regenerate, and the visual pleasure of a primrose bank in March is one worth protecting.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low perennial, 8–20cm. Leaves are basal, oval to oblong, 5–25cm, with a wrinkled, crinkled surface and irregularly toothed margins, narrowing to a winged stalk. The whole leaf is softly hairy beneath. Flowers are 2–3cm across, five-petalled, pale yellow with a deeper yellow centre, each on a separate stalk rising from the base. The flowers have a faint, sweet fragrance. The wrinkled leaf narrowing to a winged stalk, the pale yellow five-petalled flower on a separate stalk from the base, and the hedgerow bank or woodland edge habitat in early spring are together specific.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Cowslip (*Primula veris*) has similar leaves but flowers in a cluster on a single upright stem rather than separately from the base. Flowers are deeper yellow, smaller, and nodding. Equally edible.

Oxlip (*Primula elatior*) is similar to cowslip with flowers in a one-sided cluster. Edible. Found mainly in ancient woodland in East Anglia.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for primrose.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerow banks, woodland edges, ancient woodland ground flora, grassland, and sea cliffs throughout Britain. Most abundant in the south and west of England and Wales. Requires undisturbed, moderately fertile, well-drained conditions and is an indicator of old, established habitats. Often found alongside violets, wood anemone, and wild garlic in ancient woodland edges.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from late January in the most sheltered spots in the south-west; February and March across most of southern England; March and April further north. The season is four to six weeks at any one site. Pick only the flowers, not the whole plant, and take just one or two from each plant you visit.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Crystallised primroses are the traditional confection — coat individual flowers in lightly beaten egg white, dust with caster sugar, and dry in the lowest oven or at room temperature. They keep for several weeks. Fresh flowers scattered over a spring salad or a soft, mild cheese. Primrose petal syrup (flowers steeped in hot sugar syrup, strained) is pale yellow and mildly floral. A few flowers floated on a cold drink. Do not cook the flowers — heat destroys both colour and flavour. The leaves in very small quantities in salad; they are better as a garnish than a bulk ingredient.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Primrose is not on Schedule 8 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 and picking flowers for personal use from public land is legal. However, picking from Sites of Special Scientific Interest may be restricted by local bylaws, and local authority bylaws in some areas restrict picking from public land. Pick with restraint wherever you find them — never strip a plant, and in areas where primroses are clearly scarce, do not pick at all.

## 79. Sweet Chestnut

**Species Name:** Sweet Chestnut
**Latin Name:** Castanea sativa
**Type:** Nuts
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sweet-chestnut
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct, Nov

---

**Short Description:**
The large, starchy nuts of the sweet chestnut tree, falling from October in parks, woodland, and former chestnut coppice throughout Britain. Rich, floury, and versatile — roasted, boiled, or dried and ground into flour. Introduced by the Romans but naturalised and productive throughout southern Britain. Requires distinguishing from the unrelated and toxic horse chestnut.

---

**Full Description:**
Sweet chestnut is the most calorie-dense and versatile wild nut available in Britain, and in good years a productive tree can yield kilograms of large nuts for the effort of picking them off the ground. The nuts fall from late September and the season continues through October and into November.

The tree was introduced to Britain by the Romans and has naturalised across southern and central England, where it was widely planted for timber, coppice, and food. Ancient chestnut coppices in Kent, Sussex, and parts of the Midlands still produce regular crops. Urban parks and large gardens throughout southern Britain often contain mature, productive sweet chestnut trees.

The flavour is rich, floury, and sweet — the roasted chestnut of street vendors is the archetype. Unlike other nuts, chestnuts are low in fat and high in starch, making them more similar nutritionally to a root vegetable than to a walnut or hazelnut. They cannot be eaten raw in large quantities — the tannins in raw chestnuts cause digestive discomfort — but brief cooking neutralises this.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large deciduous tree, 20–35m. The bark of mature trees is distinctively spirally furrowed — the furrows run diagonally around the trunk in a characteristic twist. Leaves are large, 14–28cm, lance-oblong with prominent, forward-pointing serrations along the margins. The nuts are enclosed in a very spiny green husk (cupule) covered in long, sharp, densely packed spines — quite unlike the smooth, rounded case of a horse chestnut. The nuts themselves are 2–4cm, deep chestnut-brown, flattened on one side, with a pointed tip and a pale basal scar. Each husk typically contains 2–3 nuts. The spiny husk is the primary identification feature — handle with gloves.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Horse Chestnut (*Aesculus hippocastanum*) is the critical distinction. The conkers (seeds) of horse chestnut are toxic and inedible. Horse chestnut has a smooth or lightly spined green husk (far fewer, blunter spines than sweet chestnut), large, palmate compound leaves (five to seven leaflets radiating from a central point — completely different from the single, long, serrated sweet chestnut leaf), and the conker is rounder and shinier with a large pale patch. The leaf is the simplest distinguishing feature: horse chestnut leaves are compound and palmate; sweet chestnut leaves are simple and lance-oblong with toothed margins.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Parks, woodland, former coppice, and hedgerow trees throughout southern and central Britain. Most productive in ancient sweet chestnut coppice in Kent, Sussex, and the Weald. Also found in parkland of country houses and urban parks throughout England. Less common north of the Midlands and rare in Scotland.

---

**In Season:**
Nuts fall from late September, with the main crop in October. In good years the ground beneath a productive tree is carpeted with fallen nuts in October. After November most fallen nuts have been taken by squirrels, jays, and other animals or have begun to rot. Pick up fallen nuts rather than trying to open the husks directly — use a stick to roll the spiny husks and release the nuts without spines in the fingers.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Roasting: score a cross in the flat side, roast at 200°C for 15–20 minutes until the shell splits and the flesh is tender. Peel while hot. Boiling: cut the shell, boil for 20–25 minutes, peel. The boiled flesh can be pureed for soups and desserts. Chestnut flour: dry peeled chestnuts in a low oven until completely dry, then grind. The resulting flour is naturally sweet and gluten-free, good for simple flat breads and cakes. Chestnuts roasted with butter, thyme, and bacon is a reliable and excellent preparation. Store fresh nuts in the fridge — they mould quickly at room temperature.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Picking sweet chestnuts from public parks and woodland for personal use is permitted. Nuts in private woodland and parkland are technically private property. Large-scale commercial picking from public forests requires a licence from Forestry England.

## 80. Walnut

**Species Name:** Walnut
**Latin Name:** Juglans regia
**Type:** Nuts
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** walnut
**Seasons:** Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
A large, introduced tree found in old parkland, country estates, and some hedgerows throughout Britain, producing its familiar nut in September and October. The green unripe nuts in July can be pickled whole; ripe fallen nuts in September and October are collected from beneath the tree. The staining properties of walnut husks are considerable — wear gloves.

---

**Full Description:**
Walnut is not native to Britain. It was introduced from central Asia via the Roman world, planted widely in country estates, walled gardens, and old orchards, and is now found in parkland and large gardens throughout England and Wales. Occasional hedgerow trees exist as relics of older planting.

The nut itself needs no introduction. Ripe walnuts in October, collected fresh from beneath a productive tree, have a flavour and texture that the dried supermarket product rarely matches — the kernel is white, fresh, and mild, with none of the slight bitterness that develops with drying.

The green, unripe nut — picked in late June and early July before the shell inside has hardened — can be pickled whole in spiced vinegar to make pickled walnuts, a traditional British preserve. The timing of picking for pickling is critical: push a skewer through the centre of the nut; if you meet hard resistance from a developing shell, the picking window has passed.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large deciduous tree, 15–25m, with a broad, domed crown. The bark is smooth, pale grey on young trees, becoming deeply fissured and dark grey on older specimens. Leaves are pinnate — 7–9 oval leaflets arranged along a central stalk, 40–60cm total length. The leaflets are 6–12cm, with entire margins (not toothed), slightly aromatic when crushed. The nut is enclosed in a thick, fleshy green husk that turns black as it rots and releases the familiar corrugated shell inside. The husks stain skin and clothing a deep, lasting brown-black from juglone — this staining is a reliable field character. The large pinnate leaves, the corrugated shell, and the black-staining fleshy husk are together unmistakable.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Ash (*Fraxinus excelsior*) has similar pinnate leaves but the leaflets are toothed, the tree has distinctive paddle-shaped ash keys rather than husked nuts, and the bark is quite different. Not dangerous; the similarity is superficial.

Elder (*Sambucus nigra*) has smaller pinnate leaves with a distinctly unpleasant smell when crushed. No fruit resemblance to walnut.

No dangerous lookalikes exist for walnut. The corrugated shell inside the fleshy, black-staining husk is completely specific.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Old parkland, country estates, walled gardens, and occasional hedgerows throughout England and Wales. Rare in Scotland. Most productive on well-drained, moderately fertile soils in a sheltered, sunny position. Urban parks in southern England occasionally contain mature productive trees. The nut crop varies considerably year to year with spring frost timing.

---

**In Season:**
Green nuts for pickling: late June to early July, before the shell hardens — test with a skewer. Ripe nuts: September to October after they fall naturally. Pick up fallen nuts daily from beneath productive trees — squirrels and jays take them rapidly. The black-stained husks on the ground are a reliable indicator that nuts are present.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Fresh ripe nuts: crack the shell, remove the kernel, eat immediately. The white, fresh kernel is considerably better than dried. Pickled walnuts: pierce each green nut all over with a skewer, brine for a week, drain, leave to dry and blacken in the open air for a few days, then cover in hot spiced vinegar. Mature in the jar for at least one month before eating. Walnut oil: press or blend fresh kernels for a deeply flavoured oil. Walnut cake, walnut bread, and walnut sauce for pasta all follow logically from a productive tree. Wear gloves throughout — juglone staining is stubborn and persistent.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Fallen walnuts beneath trees on public land (parks, commons) can generally be collected for personal use. Walnuts on private estate and parkland trees are private property. Do not pick from trees without checking access and ownership. The high value of walnut crops makes private ownership more likely to be enforced than for most other wild nuts.
