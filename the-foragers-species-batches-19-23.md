## 181. Agrimony

**Species Name:** Agrimony
**Latin Name:** Agrimonia eupatoria
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** agrimony
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, slender hedgerow herb with spikes of small yellow flowers and pleasantly aromatic leaves. Agrimony has been used as a culinary herb and tea plant for centuries. The young leaves have a faint apricot-and-spice scent and make a mild, pleasant tisane.

---

**Full Description:**
Agrimony grows in grassland, along hedgerows, and at the edges of footpaths and lanes throughout Britain. It is not a plant most people stop to look at. The stems are tall and straight, reaching up to 80cm, and the yellow flower spikes open progressively from the bottom up through summer. Up close it is well worth knowing.

The leaves are pinnate, arranged in a distinctive alternating pattern of large and small leaflets, softly hairy and strongly aromatic when rubbed. The scent is warm and slightly sweet, with a faint similarity to apricot or dried fruit. Young leaves picked before flowering make a reasonable tea on their own. The dried plant has a longer history in herb teas and countryside kitchens than its current obscurity might suggest.

Agrimony is not a significant food plant and will never anchor a meal. What it offers is a gentle, warming flavour that earns a place in wild herb blends, as a tisane ingredient, and as a seasoning in slow-cooked dishes where subtlety matters.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Stems are upright, 30–80cm tall, covered in soft white hairs. Leaves are pinnate with five to nine pairs of large leaflets and smaller leaflets between them, giving the leaf an interrupted appearance. Leaflets are oval, toothed, softly hairy on the underside, and 2–6cm long. The whole plant is aromatic when touched. Flowers are small, five-petalled, bright yellow, 5–8mm across, arranged in a long slender spike. The fruit that follows is a small hooked bur, 5–8mm long, that clings readily to clothing and animal fur. This bur is the surest confirmation in late summer: the hooks are stiff and distinctive.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Agrimony is a reasonably distinct plant in its habitat and season. The main sources of confusion are other hedgerow herbs rather than anything dangerous.

Hemp Agrimony (*Eupatorium cannabinum*) shares the name and sometimes the habitat but looks quite different. It has opposite leaves, clusters of dull pink-mauve flowers, and no pinnate leaf structure. Not edible, though not significantly toxic in casual contact. The name is the only real point of confusion.

Cinquefoils (*Potentilla* species) have pinnate or palmate leaves and yellow flowers but are low-growing, rarely more than 30cm, and lack the alternating large-small leaflet arrangement. All are harmless.

Agrimony is not toxic. No part of the plant causes harm in normal culinary use. Pregnant women are sometimes advised to avoid large medicinal quantities, but this does not apply to culinary use.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Dry grassland, roadside verges, hedgerow bases, footpath edges, and chalk or limestone downland. Prefers well-drained, often calcareous soils. Fairly widespread throughout England and Wales, less common in Scotland. Particularly abundant on the southern chalk and limestone belts. Not found in waterlogged ground or dense shade.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from April onwards, though small and not worth picking until May. Best leaf quality before the plant flowers, June to July. Flowers open from June to August. The burred fruits appear from August and persist into September. For tea and culinary use, pick the young leafy tops in June before the flower spike fully develops, or dry the whole plant in late summer for winter use.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Dried agrimony leaves make a mild tisane on their own or blended with other hedgerow herbs. The flavour is gentle and warm, with a slight apricot note. Combine with meadowsweet, lemon balm, or lime flowers for a more complex infusion. Young leaves can be used fresh in a herb oil or tucked under the skin of a roasting bird alongside thyme. The flavour does not survive high heat well, so use it raw or at low temperatures. Dry the leaves quickly and store in a sealed jar out of direct light.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Agrimony is not a protected species. Picking leaves and flowers from public land for personal use is permitted. Do not uproot plants without landowner permission.

---

## 182. Beech Leaf

**Species Name:** Beech Leaf
**Latin Name:** Fagus sylvatica
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** beech-leaf
**Seasons:** Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Beech produces its best food in spring, when the leaves unfurl in a brief window of translucent green softness. The young leaves are tender, mild, and slightly nutty, good in salads and sandwiches and the basis of an excellent leaf gin liqueur.

---

**Full Description:**
Beech is one of Britain's most recognisable trees, forming cathedral-like canopies on chalk and limestone soils across southern England and Wales. For most of the year it offers nothing to the forager. For two to three weeks in spring, that changes entirely.

The new leaves emerge in late April in a flush of bright lime-green that is among the most striking sights in the British countryside. For a short window before they harden, they are edible and worth eating: tender, slightly sticky with a waxy surface, mild and nutty with a faint astringency. That window closes fast. By mid-May the leaves have toughened and taken on the leathery quality they hold for the rest of the year. Miss the window and you wait another twelve months.

The main culinary use is beech leaf noyau, a traditional British leaf gin liqueur made by macerating young beech leaves in gin with sugar. It is one of the better things you can make from a single spring afternoon's picking. The leaves are also worth eating raw, in salads or sandwiches, for those two or three weeks when they are at their softest.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Beech is a large deciduous tree reaching up to 40m, with smooth grey bark and a wide spreading crown. Young leaves are 4–9cm long, oval, with a pointed tip and a wavy margin, covered in fine silky hairs when fresh that disappear as the leaf matures. In spring the leaves are a distinctive translucent lime-green; by summer they are mid-green and opaque. The tree flowers in April and May alongside leaf emergence: inconspicuous yellow-green catkins on male trees, small female flowers in pairs. Beechnuts in their spiky case appear in autumn. The grey bark and the distinctive narrow pointed dormant buds (10–20mm, red-brown, sharply pointed) make identification straightforward for most of the year.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plants resemble beech at the leaf-picking stage. The combination of smooth grey bark, wavy-margined silky young leaves, and the distinctive buds is sufficient for a confident identification on any established tree.

Hornbeam (*Carpinus betulus*) has similar leaves but a fluted, muscular-looking grey bark rather than smooth, and the leaves are more sharply and doubly toothed. Hornbeam leaves are edible but rarely eaten.

Young beech leaves are not toxic. There are no safety concerns associated with eating them in culinary quantities.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Beech grows best on free-draining chalk, limestone, and sandy soils in southern and central England. It forms pure beech woodland on the North and South Downs, the Chilterns, and the Cotswolds, and is widely planted elsewhere as a parkland and estate tree throughout Britain. Less common on heavy clay or waterlogged soils. Often forms a continuous canopy that shades out most understorey vegetation.

---

**In Season:**
The picking window is narrow: late April to mid-May, depending on location and elevation. Southern England goes first. Northern and upland woods can be two to three weeks behind. Pick when the leaves are still silky and slightly translucent, before they take on their waxy summer texture. Once the leaf is flat and smooth and the silky hairs have gone, it has passed. Check the trees from the last week of April and pick within a day or two of the leaves reaching the right stage.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Eat young leaves in salads or sandwiches while fresh — they have a mild, slightly nutty flavour with a pleasant freshness. For beech leaf noyau, pack a clean jar with freshly picked young leaves and cover with gin. Leave for two to three weeks, then strain and add a sugar syrup (one part sugar to one part water, dissolved over heat). The result is a pale golden-green liqueur with a gentle, slightly floral and nutty flavour. Serve chilled or over ice. It does not keep indefinitely once the sugar is added but lasts well for several months in the fridge.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Beech is not a protected species. Picking leaves for personal use from public woodland or public rights of way is permitted. Do not pick from trees on private land without permission.

---

## 183. Black Mustard

**Species Name:** Black Mustard
**Latin Name:** Brassica nigra
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** black-mustard
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, vigorous annual of riverbanks and cliffs with bright yellow flowers and pungent seeds. The leaves have a strong mustardy bite and can be used young in salads or cooked as greens. The seeds, ground or cracked, make the base of a decent homemade mustard.

---

**Full Description:**
Black mustard grows tall and fast on rich disturbed soils, riverbanks, and sea cliffs around Britain's coasts and river systems. By midsummer it can reach 1.8m on a good patch, a wall of yellow flowers visible from a distance. It is one of several mustard-family plants to have gone feral in the British countryside, and it has done so with considerable enthusiasm.

All parts of the plant are edible at different stages, but the character changes with age. Young spring leaves, 5–10cm long, have a clean, peppery mustard heat that works well in small quantities in salads or sandwiches. As the plant grows and flowers, the leaves become coarser and the heat intensifies — usable as a cooked green, but in smaller quantities. The flowers are edible and less pungent than the leaves. The seeds are the real prize: small, round, dark brown to black, with the strongest flavour of the whole plant, ideal for pickling, grinding into mustard, or using in spiced vinegars.

This is a plant to come back to at different stages of the year rather than a single-season find.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Lower leaves are large (up to 20cm), stalked, irregularly lobed with a large terminal lobe and smaller side lobes, slightly hairy, and dark green. Upper leaves are much smaller, unstalked, narrow and unlobed, with a glossy surface. Flowers are small, four-petalled, bright yellow, 8–12mm across, held in terminal clusters that elongate into a long spike as the season progresses. Flower buds cluster tight at the tip above the open flowers. Pods are 10–20mm long, four-angled, held tightly against the stem, each producing four to ten small round seeds. The whole plant has a pungent mustard smell when any part is crushed. Stem is branched from low down and becomes woody at the base.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Black mustard belongs to the brassica family, which contains several similar yellow-flowered plants. None are toxic.

Charlock (*Sinapis arvensis*) is the most common yellow-flowered field weed and is edible. Its pods spread away from the stem on short stalks, rather than lying flat against it as in black mustard. The leaves are less lobed and rougher in texture.

Hedge Mustard (*Sisymbrium officinale*) is a smaller, more wiry plant with tiny yellow flowers and narrow pods held tightly against the stem. Edible but with a stronger and less pleasant flavour.

Wild Radish (*Raphanus raphanistrum*) has larger flowers (15–20mm), often pale yellow, with distinct purple veining. Its pods are fleshy and constricted between seeds rather than straight. Edible.

Garlic Mustard (*Alliaria petiolata*) is a spring-only plant with white flowers and kidney-shaped lower leaves that smell of garlic when crushed. Edible.

No member of the brassica family present in Britain is toxic to humans. Flavour is the main variable, not safety.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Riverbanks, sea cliffs, shingle beaches, coastal paths, waste ground, and disturbed soils near human settlement. Particularly abundant along major river systems and on southern coastal cliffs. Prefers fertile, well-drained soils in full sun. Found throughout England and Wales, less common in Scotland.

---

**In Season:**
Young leaves from April into May, when the plant is still low-growing and the leaves are at their mildest. Flowers from May to August — pick the buds or freshly opened flowers for the most versatile use. Seeds ripen from July into September. The seeds shatter and fall readily when ripe, so check regularly once the pods begin to turn brown and yellow.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young spring leaves in salads, sandwiches, or lightly wilted as a side. Use sparingly — the heat is real. Flower buds can be added to salads or pickled briefly in white wine vinegar. For seed mustard: toast the seeds lightly in a dry pan, then grind coarsely with salt, vinegar, and a little honey. The flavour is hot and direct, quite different from commercial mustard. Seeds can also be used whole in pickles, spiced oils, and Indian-influenced dishes. Dried seeds keep well and retain their heat for months.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Black mustard is not a protected species. Picking leaves, flowers, and seeds for personal use from public land is permitted. On riverbanks, be aware of bank ownership — the public right of way may run above the bank, not on it. Avoid picking from land that shows signs of herbicide use.

---

## 184. Bramble Shoot

**Species Name:** Bramble Shoot
**Latin Name:** Rubus fruticosus
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** bramble-shoot
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
The blackberry plant offers more than fruit in autumn. In early spring, the young green shoots pushing up from the hedgerow base are tender, mildly flavoured, and can be eaten raw or cooked like asparagus. Most foragers walk straight past them. They should not.

---

**Full Description:**
Bramble is so common in Britain that it barely registers as a plant worth knowing in spring. By September the blackberries are on everyone's radar. But from late February through April, the new shoots emerging from the woody hedgerow base are a different kind of find, tender and edible and almost entirely overlooked.

The shoots grow quickly. The window for picking is measured in days, not weeks. At 5–15cm, before the stems have hardened and the prickles become stiff, the tips and young side shoots can be snapped off cleanly and eaten raw or lightly cooked. The flavour is mild, green, and faintly sweet — closer to green bean than to blackberry. With the young leaves stripped away and the shoot quickly blanched, they make a passable spring vegetable. Eaten raw, straight from the hedgerow, they are worth stopping for.

This is a classic early-spring gap-filler: easy to find, easy to identify with complete certainty, and available when most other edible greens are still weeks away.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Bramble shoots emerge from the base of established hedgerow plants and from the root system. The young stems are green, often tinged red, with developing prickles that are still soft and easy to handle at the picking stage. Leaves in pairs, with a third terminal leaflet, developing as the shoot extends. The edges are toothed and the surface slightly hairy. The characteristic arching bramble habit begins to develop from about 20cm onwards. By the time prickles have hardened and the plant starts arching outward, the picking window has closed. Bramble is so familiar from its autumn fruit that identification presents no meaningful challenge.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plants closely resemble young bramble shoots. The prickles, even in their early soft stage, and the developing three-leaflet arrangement are sufficient identifiers for a plant most British adults will have been picking blackberries from since childhood.

Raspberry (*Rubus idaeus*) produces similar edible young shoots, also edible. Stems are more upright, prickles finer, and the leaf underside has a distinctly silvery-white colouring. Worth picking for the same reasons.

Both are edible. No toxic confusion is possible with any established British plant.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Ubiquitous. Hedgerows, woodland edges, scrub, waste ground, railway embankments, field margins. Bramble colonises any disturbed ground with reasonable light. The most productive shooting patches are where established hedgerows meet undisturbed ground — the root systems are well-established and produce vigorous new growth in early spring.

---

**In Season:**
The picking window for tender shoots is short: late February to April, depending on location and weather. Mild springs produce vigorous early growth. The shoot is at its best at 5–15cm, before the stems have hardened and the prickles have stiffened. Check hedgerow bases from mid-February in sheltered southern spots. Once the shoot exceeds 20cm and begins to arch, the texture becomes too coarse.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Snap the shoot at 5–15cm, strip away any developing leaves, and rinse well. Eat raw in a spring salad with a sharp dressing, or blanch briefly in boiling water for 60 seconds and serve with butter and salt. The flavour is mild and green; the texture, blanched, is somewhere between asparagus and green bean. They can be added to a spring stir-fry or used alongside other early greens in an omelette. They do not store well — pick and use the same day.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Bramble is not protected. Picking shoots and leaves from public land for personal use is permitted. As with all foraging, avoid land that shows signs of pesticide or herbicide use.

---

## 185. Broom

**Species Name:** Broom
**Latin Name:** Cytisus scoparius
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** broom
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun

---

**⚠️ Safety-flagged species. Expert review required before this entry goes Live. See note on toxic parts below.**

---

**Short Description:**
A familiar shrub of heathland, roadsides, and light woodland, covered in vivid yellow pea-like flowers in spring. The flower buds can be pickled like capers and the flowers added in small quantities to salads. The rest of the plant, including the seeds and pods, is toxic and must not be eaten.

---

**Full Description:**
Broom is one of the most conspicuous shrubs in the British countryside in late spring. The vivid yellow flowers, appearing in April and May on plants covering heathland, cliff edges, railway cuttings, and open woodland, are unmistakeable at a distance. It is also a plant to approach with caution.

The flowers are the only edible part. Used in small quantities, pickled buds have a pleasant, slightly peppery flavour similar to capers, and fresh flowers add colour and a mild savour to spring salads. This is a culinary use from an older British kitchen tradition, well-attested but now rarely practised.

The rest of the plant is another matter entirely. The stems, leaves, pods, and seeds all contain the alkaloids sparteine and cytisine. Cytisine is toxic and causes nausea, vomiting, and in significant doses can affect heart rhythm. The seeds are the most concentrated source of toxicity. The distinction between edible flowers and toxic everything-else is not subtle, but it is absolute.

Broom is not a beginner species. Pick flowers only, in modest quantities, and be certain you have not confused the plant with laburnum.

---

**Identification Notes:**
An upright shrub, 1–2m tall, with distinctly green, slender, five-angled stems that give the plant its broom-like appearance even in winter. Leaves are small (5–15mm), usually trifoliate or reduced to a single leaflet, and appear on young growth only — mature stems are almost leafless. Flowers are 20–25mm, bright golden-yellow, with the typical pea-family structure of a broad upper petal and a narrowed keel. They appear singly or in pairs along the stem, not in hanging clusters. Pods are flat, 3–4cm long, turning black when ripe, with a covering of fine silky hairs at the margins. The pod splits explosively when ripe, scattering seeds with an audible crack on hot days.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
The critical lookalike for Broom is Laburnum (*Laburnum anagyroides*), one of Britain's most toxic trees. Both have yellow pea-like flowers. The distinction is clear but must be checked every time.

Laburnum is a tree or large shrub, often multi-stemmed and heavily branching. Its flowers hang in long pendulous chains (15–30cm racemes). Broom is a smaller shrub with green broom-like stems, and its flowers appear singly or in pairs directly along those stems, never in hanging chains. Laburnum has rounded grey-brown bark; Broom has distinctly green angled stems. If yellow pea-like flowers hang from a tree in long drooping clusters: do not pick them. That is laburnum, and all parts are deadly toxic. Deaths, mostly in children who have eaten the seeds, are recorded.

Gorse (*Ulex europaeus*) has yellow pea-like flowers and similar habitat but is densely spiny throughout. The flowers are edible and not dangerous to confuse with Broom, but the two plants are easy to distinguish by the spines alone.

Western Gorse (*Ulex gallii*) and Dwarf Gorse (*Ulex minor*) are similar to Common Gorse with the same spiny habit. All have edible flowers.

Broom stems, pods, leaves, and seeds are toxic. Flowers only, in modest culinary quantities. Never eat the seeds.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Heathland, open woodland edges, roadside verges, railway cuttings, cliff tops, sand dunes, and disturbed acid soils. Common on light, well-drained, acidic to neutral soils throughout Britain. Often found alongside heather and gorse. Thrives in full sun; absent from heavy clay and waterlogged ground.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers open from April and are at their best through May and into June. Pick flower buds before they fully open for pickling — the tight bud stage gives the best texture. Open flowers can be picked for immediate use in salads. The plants can be in flower for six weeks or more depending on location and weather.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Flower bud capers: pick tight buds before they open, pack into a sterilised jar, and cover with hot white wine vinegar seasoned with salt and a little sugar. Leave for at least two weeks before using. The flavour is pleasantly pungent and slightly floral, a reasonable substitute for salted capers in sauces and salads. Fresh flowers can be scattered over a spring salad in small quantities. Do not cook them in any preparation that includes the stems or seeds. Do not eat large quantities of the flowers — the toxins are present throughout the plant, though at much lower levels in the flowers than in the seeds.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Broom is not a protected species. Picking flowers for personal use from public land is permitted. Do not uproot or cut the shrub without landowner permission.

---

## 186. Wild Celery

**Species Name:** Wild Celery
**Latin Name:** Apium graveolens
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Expert
**Slug:** wild-celery
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**⚠️ Safety-flagged species. Expert review required before this entry goes Live. This species has a deadly lookalike that grows in similar habitat. Difficulty rating is Expert. Read the Lookalikes section carefully.**

---

**Short Description:**
The wild ancestor of cultivated celery, found on coastal grassland, saltmarshes, and brackish ditches. The leaves and stems have a concentrated celery flavour and are genuinely useful in the kitchen. The risk from confusion with Hemlock Water Dropwort is serious and real.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild celery is the plant that cultivated celery comes from. The family resemblance is clear in the smell and the flavour — both unmistakeably celery — but the wild plant is smaller, tougher, and far more intense. Where cultivated celery is mild enough to eat by the stalk, wild celery is a seasoning.

It grows in a narrow band of habitat: coastal and estuarine ground, the edges of saltmarshes, brackish ditches, sea walls, and damp coastal grassland. This restricted range is actually a significant safety feature: the further you are from tidal or coastal water, the less likely you are to be looking at wild celery.

The flavour makes it worth knowing. Finely chopped leaves added to stocks, soups, and stews give the kind of celery depth you cannot get from the cultivated plant. The stems can be used similarly. A small amount goes a long way.

This is not a beginner species, and it is not a species to attempt an identification of casually. The lookalike section below is required reading before picking anything that smells of celery near coastal water.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A biennial or short-lived perennial, 30–60cm tall. Stems are hollow, grooved, and solid green, without any purple blotching. Leaves are bright, glossy green, pinnate to bipinnate, with wedge-shaped, irregularly toothed leaflets. The smell when any part is crushed is immediately, strongly, and unmistakeably of celery. This smell is the critical feature. Flowers are small, white, in compound umbels 3–6cm across, appearing from June to August. The plant grows in clumps, often at the base of sea walls, in rough grassland above the tideline, or along the edges of brackish ditches.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Wild celery has one critical lookalike: Hemlock Water Dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*). This plant causes more deaths from plant poisoning in Britain than any other species. It grows in or beside water and on wet ground, has a strong celery-like smell, and is superficially similar in appearance.

The distinctions are real and testable, but they must all be checked, not just one.

Hemlock Water Dropwort roots are white, fleshy, and clustered in bundles like small parsnips. Wild celery has a single, slender taproot. If you are digging anywhere near wet ground and find white fleshy root clusters: stop. Do not eat them. Do not handle them. The roots are the most toxic part of the plant.

Hemlock Water Dropwort grows in and immediately beside water — rivers, streams, ditches, and marshy ground. Wild celery grows on coastal and brackish ground, typically saltmarsh edges, sea walls, and rough ground above the tideline, not beside freshwater.

Cut the stem of a suspect plant. Hemlock Water Dropwort exudes a pale yellowish sap from a cut stem. Wild celery does not.

If you find a celery-smelling plant beside a freshwater stream or river: do not pick it. It is almost certainly Hemlock Water Dropwort.

Wild celery also shares its family (Apiaceae) with Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*). Hemlock smells musty and unpleasant, quite different from the clean celery scent of *Apium graveolens*. Hemlock stems have distinctive purple-red blotching. Wild celery stems are plain green.

Do not pick wild celery unless you have confirmed the habitat (coastal/brackish, not freshwater), the smell (clean celery), the root form (single taproot), and the stem (plain green, no blotching, no yellow sap).

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Coastal and estuarine habitats: saltmarsh edges, brackish ditches, sea walls, rough coastal grassland above the tideline, and occasionally shingle with brackish influence. Found on all British coasts but most common in southern and eastern England. Absent from freshwater habitats. If a plant that smells of celery is growing beside a freshwater river, ditch, or stream, treat it as Hemlock Water Dropwort until proven otherwise.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from late April onwards. Peak season May to July, when the plant is in active growth. Flowers June to August. Pick leaves before flowering for the best flavour. The plant is biennial and does not produce usable growth until its second year.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use the leaves and young stems as a seasoning. Finely chop and add to fish stock, soups, stews, potato dishes, or wherever celery flavour is needed. The intensity is much greater than cultivated celery — use a third of what you would use of the cultivated plant and adjust from there. The leaves can be dried and crumbled into spice mixes. Not suitable for eating raw in large quantities; the flavour is too strong and the plant has mild stimulant properties in excess.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Wild celery is not a protected species, but it grows in coastal habitats that may be within SSSIs, National Nature Reserves, or local nature reserves. Check before picking in any designated conservation area. Do not uproot plants.

---

## 187. Common Vetch

**Species Name:** Common Vetch
**Latin Name:** Vicia sativa
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** common-vetch
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul

---

**Short Description:**
A sprawling, climbing annual of hedgerows, rough grassland, and arable margins, with red-purple flowers and paired leaflets ending in a curling tendril. The young shoots and leaves are edible raw or cooked. The seeds need cooking and should not be eaten raw in large quantities.

---

**Full Description:**
Common vetch is one of those plants that most people walk past without registering. It climbs through hedgerow grasses and scrambles up fences in the way vetches do, its tendrils hooking onto any available support. By June it is in flower, small red-purple blooms sitting in pairs at the leaf axils from April through to autumn.

The young growing tips, the leaves, and the flowers are all edible. The tender shoot tips have a fresh, mild, pea-like flavour and can be eaten raw in salads or lightly cooked as a spring green. In this they are similar to garden pea shoots, to which they are closely related. The flavour rewards picking tender tips rather than older, tougher growth.

The seeds require a note of caution. Uncooked seeds contain compounds that can cause problems if eaten in significant quantities, though the risk is low from a handful of seeds. Cooking removes this concern. The seeds, boiled, have an earthy, leguminous flavour. The key instruction is straightforward: cook the seeds before eating them.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A climbing annual, reaching 20–80cm with support, or scrambling low across the ground in the open. Leaves are pinnate, with four to eight pairs of oblong or linear leaflets (10–20mm long) and a branched tendril at the tip. A small, triangular stipule with a dark nectar-producing spot sits at the base of each leaf stalk. Flowers are 10–18mm, in one or two pairs at the leaf axils, red-purple to occasionally pale pink or bicoloured. Pods are 25–70mm long, hairy, turning brown-black when ripe, containing four to twelve seeds. The whole plant is roughly hairy.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Vetch identification requires attention as several species look similar, but none are dangerously toxic.

Tufted Vetch (*Vicia cracca*) is more vigorous, with longer racemes of 10–40 blue-violet flowers, and leaflets narrower and more numerous (8–12 pairs). The flowers are in one-sided clusters on a distinct stalk, unlike the paired axillary flowers of common vetch. Edible — same uses.

Bush Vetch (*Vicia sepium*) has dull purple flowers in clusters of two to six and slightly broader leaflets. Edible — same uses.

Hairy Tare (*Vicia hirsuta*) is smaller, with tiny (3–4mm) pale lilac flowers in clusters of one to nine, and very narrow leaflets. Technically edible but too small to bother with.

Narrow-leaved Everlasting Pea (*Lathyrus sylvestris*) and Meadow Vetchling (*Lathyrus pratensis*) are both edible pea-family plants with similar tendriled growth. Neither are problematic.

Bitter Vetch (*Lathyrus linifolius*) has edible tubers and is not confused easily in flower.

No dangerously toxic plants in Britain closely resemble the vetches in their climbing habit, pinnate leaves with tendrils, and pea-family flowers.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerow bases, rough grassland, arable field margins, road verges, and disturbed ground. Prefers well-drained soils and full sun. Common throughout Britain and Ireland on calcareous and neutral grassland. Often found scrambling through tall grasses and nettles in summer.

---

**In Season:**
Growing tips from April onwards, while the plant is still producing tender new growth. Best in April and May before the plant coarsens. Flowers from May to September — pick for salads when fresh. Seeds ripen from July and are ready when the pod begins to turn from green to brown but before it splits.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Pick growing tips 5–10cm long for salads or lightly cooked. The flavour is mild and pea-like. Add to a spring leaf salad with wood sorrel, young hawthorn leaves, and a sharp dressing. Blanch briefly if cooking. Flowers work as a salad garnish. Seeds: cook them. Boil for 20 minutes and use in the same way as small dried peas or lentils — in soups, with grains, or mashed with oil and seasoning. They have more flavour than they look.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Common vetch is not protected. Picking for personal use from public land, roadside verges, and footpaths is permitted. Avoid picking from field margins where the land has been recently sprayed.

## 188. Corn Mint

**Species Name:** Corn Mint
**Latin Name:** Mentha arvensis
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** corn-mint
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
The native British mint, found on damp arable margins and disturbed ground. Corn mint has a strong, clean mint flavour and can be used in the kitchen exactly as you would use cultivated mint. Less known than spearmint or water mint but no less useful.

---

**Full Description:**
Corn mint is Britain's native mint species, found on arable land, damp meadows, and disturbed ground throughout the country. It is less celebrated than water mint or spearmint but shares the essential character of the genus: that fresh, sharp mint smell that is impossible to mistake once you know it.

The flavour is strong and clean, with a pronounced menthol note that is closer to peppermint than to spearmint. It is a useful kitchen herb in its own right, and in fields and farm margins where it grows as a weed it appears in quantity that makes picking worthwhile.

Mint identification is straightforward once you grasp the family logic: square stem, opposite leaves, aromatic smell. Within the mints, the smell itself is the primary identifier. Corn mint has a strong, direct menthol quality. The lilac flowers appear in whorls around the stem rather than in a terminal spike, which distinguishes it from spearmint in flower. The leaves are stalked, rounded, and noticeably wrinkled.

As with all mints, the plants hybridise freely. Identifying the precise species matters less in culinary terms than confirming you have a mint at all.

---

**Identification Notes:**
An upright perennial, 10–40cm tall, dying back in winter. Stem is square in cross-section, with spreading soft hairs. Leaves are 2–4cm long, broadly oval, stalked, with a rounded base and toothed margin, softly hairy on both surfaces, and strongly aromatic when crushed. The smell is strongly minty, with a pronounced menthol quality. Flowers are small, lilac to pale pink, in whorls at the leaf axils from June to October, not in a terminal spike. This arrangement of flowers in whorls along the stem, with a leafy tip above them, distinguishes corn mint from spearmint and peppermint, which produce a terminal flower spike. The whole plant smells strongly of mint when brushed.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
All British mints are edible. There is no toxic lookalike among the mint family's major species.

Water Mint (*Mentha aquatica*) is the most common British mint, growing in wetter habitats — streamsides, marsh edges, and pond margins. Its flowers form a terminal rounded head rather than axillary whorls, and the smell is slightly coarser. Edible and commonly used.

Spearmint (*Mentha spicata*) has smooth leaves, pointed at the tip, and a terminal flower spike. The smell is sweeter, less menthol-forward. Edible, widely used.

Pennyroyal (*Mentha pulegium*) is a smaller, creeping mint of damp ground, with smaller rounder leaves and a distinctly strong pennyroyal-menthol scent. It is edible in small culinary quantities but contains pulegone, which causes liver damage in large doses and is an abortifacient. Do not use in pregnancy and do not consume in large amounts. It is much smaller and more sprawling than corn mint, typically 5–20cm.

Marjoram (*Origanum vulgare*) and other labiates can occasionally be confused with mints before flowering but have a quite different smell. The square stem and mint smell together are sufficient to confirm genus.

The single reliable safety note: confirm the mint smell. Any square-stemmed aromatic plant that smells of mint is a mint. If it smells of anything other than mint, do not assume it is one.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Arable field margins, disturbed farmland, damp grassland, streamsides, and woodland rides on heavier soils. Common throughout Britain, particularly on clay soils in the Midlands and northern England. Often found as an arable weed in and around root vegetable fields. Also found on damp roadsides and the margins of wet hollows.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from June once the plant is growing actively. Best before and during early flowering, June to August. The plant continues to grow and produce leaves through September and October but the flavour becomes more variable in autumn. Pick in the morning before the heat of the day drives off the volatile oils.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use exactly as you would spearmint or any cultivated mint. In sauces, salads, tabbouleh, and cold drinks. Mint sauce for lamb: finely chop the fresh leaves, combine with sugar and vinegar, and leave for 30 minutes before serving. Mint oil: pack a jar with leaves and cover with a neutral oil, leave for a week, then strain. Dried mint: pick sprigs, tie in small bundles, and hang in a warm dry place for a week. Corn mint dries well and holds its flavour. The menthol note makes it particularly good in cold drinks and desserts.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Corn mint is not protected. Picking for personal use from footpaths, verges, and public land is permitted. Be aware that arable field margins may have been treated with herbicides — check for signs of chemical damage before picking.

---

## 189. Corn Salad

**Species Name:** Corn Salad
**Latin Name:** Valerianella locusta
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** corn-salad
**Seasons:** Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Also known as lamb's lettuce. A low-growing winter and spring annual of arable land, old walls, and rocky ground. The small, rounded leaves are mild, tender, and good in salads from November through to April, when most other green leaves are still weeks away.

---

**Full Description:**
Corn salad is the wild form of lamb's lettuce, the salad leaf now sold in every supermarket. The cultivated version is bred for larger, more uniform leaves. The wild plant is smaller in every dimension and more scattered in habit, growing as a weed of arable fields, old walls, and bare sandy ground, but it has exactly the same mild, tender, slightly nutty quality that made it worth domesticating.

It is a plant of the lean months. Corn salad grows through winter and spring on ground that is otherwise bare, and it is ready to pick when almost nothing else is. The small rosettes of rounded leaves are at their best from October through to flowering in April. The flavour is mild, clean, and slightly sweet, with enough substance to carry a light dressing.

It is undemanding to find and gather, produces nothing that could be confused with anything harmful, and fills the winter salad gap that few other wild plants address.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low-growing annual, forming a small rosette 5–15cm across. Leaves are 1–4cm long, spoon-shaped to oblong, entire or very slightly toothed, pale to mid-green, slightly fleshy, and smooth. The leaf base clasps the stem in the upper leaves as the plant develops. Flowers are tiny (1–2mm), pale lilac to whitish, in small dense clusters at the tips of forked stems, appearing from March to May. The plant has a distinctive forked branching pattern, each branch ending in a small head of flowers. The whole plant is very small and inconspicuous until it is in flower.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Corn salad has no dangerous lookalikes in Britain. Its small size, rounded leaves, and winter-spring habit on disturbed ground make it unlikely to be confused with anything toxic.

Field Forget-me-not (*Myosotis arvensis*) grows in similar habitats and is similar in scale but has hairy, narrower leaves and blue flowers. Not toxic, not edible.

Common Whitlowgrass (*Erophila verna*) grows in similar rocky and bare ground, is similar in size, and is harmless. Its leaves are narrower and more pointed.

Hairy Bittercress (*Cardamine hirsuta*) is another small winter annual of similar habitats, edible and with a pleasant peppery bite. Its leaves are pinnate rather than simple, which distinguishes it from corn salad.

No part of corn salad causes harm.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Arable field margins, bare sandy or chalky ground, old walls, disturbed soil, and garden beds. Prefers well-drained, often calcareous soils in open sunny positions. Found throughout England and Wales, less common in Scotland. Often grows in quantity on the disturbed banks of old walls and in the ballast of disused tracks and paths.

---

**In Season:**
The rosettes are present and pickable from October through to April. At their best November to March, when the leaves are young and before the plant sends up its flowering stem. Once in flower (from March to May), the leaves become smaller and the plant puts its energy into setting seed. A second generation germinates in late summer and early autumn. Pick from October onwards for the best quality.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use exactly as you would lamb's lettuce from a supermarket. Rinse the rosettes and dry well. Dress lightly — a good olive oil, a few drops of lemon juice, sea salt, and black pepper is enough. Good with sliced raw beetroot, toasted walnuts, and a sharp blue cheese. The flavour is mild enough to work as a base rather than a feature; the texture is the point. Wild corn salad is smaller than the cultivated version and takes a little more washing; otherwise the preparation is identical.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Corn salad is not protected. Picking from public land, footpaths, and verges for personal use is permitted. On arable land it grows as a weed of field margins, where herbicide use is common — avoid picking where surrounding vegetation shows signs of chemical damage.

## 190. Dittander

**Species Name:** Dittander
**Latin Name:** Lepidium latifolium
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** dittander
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, robust plant of coastal ground and brackish ditches with a fierce peppery heat, used as a horseradish substitute before cultivated horseradish arrived in British kitchens. The leaves are edible young, though the flavour is strong and not to everyone's taste.

---

**Full Description:**
Dittander is one of the more interesting overlooked plants on the British coast. It grows on shingle, sea walls, and the margins of brackish ditches with a vigour that belies its culinary obscurity, and it has a flavour history that goes back to medieval English cooking, when it served as one of the primary sources of peppery heat in northern European kitchens before black pepper was widely available.

The plant is a brassica-family perennial, and the heat is the thing. The large, glaucous leaves have a hot, sharp, peppery quality that is reminiscent of horseradish, though with more of a radish character. Young spring leaves are the most palatable: still relatively mild, with a clean bite that works in small quantities as a condiment leaf. As the plant grows and the leaves harden, the heat intensifies and the texture becomes coarser.

It is not a species that appears in most foraging guides. Finding it on a coastal walk is a small pleasure, and knowing what to do with it is a slightly larger one.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A tall, robust perennial, 50–120cm when in flower. Lower leaves are large (10–30cm), oval to elliptical, slightly wavy-edged, glaucous blue-green, on long stalks. Upper leaves are smaller, narrower, and unstalked, clasping the stem. The whole plant is smooth and hairless with a grey-green, slightly waxy appearance. Taste a small fragment: the heat is immediate and distinctive, like a sharp radish. Flowers are tiny, four-petalled, white, in large much-branched clusters on the upper stem, appearing from May to July. The flower head is distinctive in its density and the way it branches repeatedly into small clusters. The plant spreads by creeping roots and often forms patches.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Dittander has no dangerous lookalikes in its coastal habitat. The combination of habitat (coastal/brackish), size, grey-green waxy leaves, and fierce peppery taste makes it identifiable with care.

Garden Horseradish (*Armoracia rusticana*) grows as an escape on rough ground, roadsides, and waste land and has a similar taste. Horseradish leaves are larger (40–50cm), darker green, and more strongly ribbed and wavy. It does not generally grow on coastal shingle or brackish ground. Edible and well-known.

Field Penny-cress (*Thlaspi arvense*) is a smaller annual of arable land with similar white four-petalled flowers but much smaller, with notched round flattened seed pods. Not relevant in coastal habitats.

Sea Rocket (*Cakile maritima*) grows on similar shingle and coastal habitats and is edible with a mild peppery bite. Its leaves are more deeply lobed and fleshy; the flowers are pink or pale lilac. Different enough to distinguish on inspection.

None of the plants likely to be encountered in dittander's coastal habitat are toxic.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Coastal shingle, sea walls, brackish ditches, saltmarsh margins, and estuarine rough ground. Occasionally found on disturbed ground inland near rivers but primarily coastal. Most common in eastern and southern England. A relatively scarce plant in some areas — pick lightly and leave the patch intact.

---

**In Season:**
Young leaves from March into May, before the plant flowers. This is the best window for culinary use. Once in full growth, the leaves coarsen and the heat intensifies beyond what most people find pleasant raw. Flowers May to July — edible as a fiery garnish. After flowering, the leaves are too tough for most uses but the roots can be dug (with landowner permission) and used exactly as horseradish.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young leaves: use finely shredded in small quantities as a condiment, in the way you might use fresh horseradish. Good with cold beef, smoked fish, and in dressings where heat is wanted. Do not use large quantities raw — the heat is real and can irritate the mouth and stomach. A small leaf rolled into a pinch and eaten with oily fish is about right. The leaves can be very briefly blanched to moderate the heat and used as a cooked green with butter and lemon. The root, in spring before flowering, can be grated fine and used as a direct horseradish substitute in sauces and condiments.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Dittander is not a nationally protected species, but many coastal habitats where it grows are SSSIs or within managed nature reserves. Check before picking in designated conservation areas. Pick leaves only; do not uproot on public land or in nature reserves. On some coastal sites managed for wildlife, even leaf picking may be restricted.

---

## 191. Gorse Flower

**Species Name:** Gorse Flower
**Latin Name:** Ulex europaeus
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** gorse-flower
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Gorse flowers almost year-round, with the heaviest display from January to May. The flowers have a faint coconut-vanilla scent and a mild sweetness. They can be used in salads, infused into syrups and wines, and are one of the earliest edible flowers available in the British winter.

---

**Full Description:**
The old saying that gorse flowering marks the end of kissing season only works because gorse is in flower almost all year round. The main display runs from January into May, when the south-facing slopes of heathland turn bright yellow. A second, lesser flush comes in autumn. In mild winters and in sheltered coastal spots, gorse never entirely stops.

The flowers are the edible part. They have a faint, warm scent that sits somewhere between coconut and vanilla, and a mild, sweet flavour that works in small quantities as a garnish or infused into something more substantial. Gorse flower wine and gorse flower syrup are old preparations with genuine results. The flowers can also be scattered raw over salads or incorporated into spring baking where a floral note is wanted.

The spines are the only practical challenge. The plant is impenetrably spiny and picking requires patience, gloves, and a cup or bag. Once the flowers are separated from the plant, they present no difficulties.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large, dense, evergreen shrub, 1–3m tall, armed throughout with long (10–25mm) rigid, finely grooved spines. The leaves are reduced to small linear scales on mature growth, making the plant appear leafless. Flowers are typical pea-family structure, 15–20mm, bright golden-yellow, with a distinct coconut-vanilla scent on warm sunny days. Found in heathland, cliff tops, commons, road verges, and rough ground throughout Britain. The combination of impenetrable spiny habit and brilliant yellow pea-flowers makes gorse impossible to confuse with any toxic plant.

Western Gorse (*Ulex gallii*) and Dwarf Gorse (*Ulex minor*) flower later, mainly in late summer and autumn, and are smaller and less vigorous. Both have edible flowers.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Broom (*Cytisus scoparius*) has yellow pea-like flowers and overlaps in habitat and season. Broom has green, unspined, angled stems with small leaves — it does not have the impenetrable spiny habit of gorse. Broom flowers are edible in small quantities; the rest of the plant is toxic. The two plants are straightforward to distinguish: if it has spines, it is gorse.

Laburnum (*Laburnum anagyroides*) has hanging clusters of yellow pea-flowers but is a tree with smooth rounded bark and no spines. All parts of laburnum are toxic. The plant looks nothing like gorse. Any yellow pea-flower on a spiny heathland shrub is gorse.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Heathland, cliff tops, road verges, commons, and rough acid grassland. Tolerates thin, poor, acidic soils and heavy exposure. Common throughout Britain and Ireland. Often dominant on southern heathland in winter, when it provides the main colour in an otherwise dormant landscape.

---

**In Season:**
Main flowering period January to May. A second flush in late September and October. In mild coastal areas, flowers may be present in some quantity year-round. Pick in dry weather when the flowers are fully open and the scent is strongest. Check each flower as you pick — ants and small insects use the nectaries.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Gorse flower syrup: steep a generous handful of flowers in 300ml of water just off the boil for one hour, strain well, and dissolve 200g sugar into the warm liquid. Good over ice cream, in sparkling water, or drizzled over a sharp citrus tart. Gorse flower wine is a traditional British country wine, made by fermenting a large quantity of flowers with sugar, yeast, and citric acid. It is golden, slightly sweet, and floral — easy to make in spring. Flowers as salad garnish: scatter a small handful over a spring salad. Raw flowers are slightly bitter; the coconut note is more pronounced in infusions.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Gorse is not protected. Picking flowers for personal use from heathland, commons, and verges is permitted. Many heathland sites are SSSIs — picking flowers in small quantities for personal use is generally fine, but cutting or uprooting the shrub is not. Check local bylaws on managed heaths and nature reserves.

---

## 192. Greater Stitchwort

**Species Name:** Greater Stitchwort
**Latin Name:** Stellaria holostea
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** greater-stitchwort
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
One of spring's most familiar hedgerow flowers, producing masses of white star-like blooms from April through June. The young leaves and flowers are edible raw and add a mild, pleasant flavour and visual appeal to spring salads. Easy to identify, common, and pleasant to eat.

---

**Full Description:**
Greater stitchwort is a fixture of April hedgerows throughout Britain. The flowers are bright white, deeply notched, and appear in such numbers on the weak straggling stems that they seem almost to light up the hedgerow base from the inside. Most people know it by sight even if they do not know the name.

The young leaves and the flowers are both edible raw. The leaves are narrow, grass-like, and slightly rough to the touch, with a mild, fresh flavour. The flowers are mild and sweet, with a pleasant but not assertive flavour. Neither are major food plants, but both are genuine contributions to a spring salad bowl. They are found in abundance at exactly the time when variety in wild salad materials is most welcome, and they are completely unmistakeable.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A straggling perennial, 15–60cm, often sprawling through grasses and hedgerow plants. Stem is square in cross-section, rough and hairy on the angles. ---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plant resembles greater stitchwort in its hedgerow setting and flowering season.

Lesser Stitchwort (*Stellaria graminea*) has smaller flowers (5–12mm), narrower leaves, and prefers more acid soils. Edible.

Chickweed (*Stellaria media*) is a much smaller plant of disturbed ground, edible and well-known, with smaller flowers and a distinctive single line of hairs down one side of the stem.

Bladder Campion (*Silene vulgaris*) and White Campion (*Silene latifolia*) have white notched flowers but petals in two distinct lobes rather than ten separate-looking segments, and a swollen bladder-like calyx. Not toxic, not worth eating.

Herb Robert (*Geranium robertianum*) has five-petalled pink flowers and is not confused in flower.

No plant resembling greater stitchwort in the April hedgerow causes harm.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, woodland edges, grassy banks, and roadside verges on a range of soil types. Common throughout Britain and Ireland. Particularly abundant in Devon and Cornwall hedgebanks and throughout the English Midlands. Less common on very acid or very wet soils. Often grows with bluebells, red campion, and wood anemone in the hedgerow understorey.

---

**In Season:**
Young leaves from early April. Flowers from April into June. Pick flowers for salads when freshly opened; they wilt quickly once picked and are best used the same day. The leaves are at their most tender before flowering — pick the fresh growing tips rather than older, tougher growth.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Add fresh flowers and young leaves to spring salads for colour and a mild, pleasant flavour. The flowers work particularly well with bitter leaves — dandelion, sorrel, or hawthorn tip — where their sweetness provides contrast. Use quickly: they do not keep once picked. Add at the last minute to avoid wilting. The flowers can also be used to decorate a spring cake or dessert, pressed briefly between sheets of absorbent paper to remove moisture.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Greater stitchwort is not protected. Picking for personal use from public footpaths and verges is permitted. Do not pick from nature reserves or SSSIs without checking local guidance.

## 193. Gutweed

**Species Name:** Gutweed
**Latin Name:** Ulva intestinalis
**Type:** Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** gutweed
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A common bright green seaweed of rocky shores, rock pools, and harbour walls. The tubular fronds are edible raw or cooked, with a fresh, salty, sea-vegetable flavour. Widely available and easy to identify, it makes a good introduction to coastal foraging.

---

**Full Description:**
Gutweed is familiar to anyone who has walked on a rocky British shore. The inflated, bright green tubes, often matted in tangled masses across rocks, harbour walls, and around the base of sea defences, are a feature of sheltered intertidal zones from spring to autumn.

The name is descriptive. The fronds are hollow tubes, 5–30cm long, resembling the intestines of a small animal when you look closely. They are bright green and translucent in good light. The flavour is clean, salty, and marine, with an appealing freshness when eaten raw. Cooked, gutweed crisps up in a hot pan and develops a satisfying umami depth.

It is closely related to sea lettuce (*Ulva lactuca*), which is flatter and broader, and the two are edible in identical ways. The main practical difference is texture: gutweed's tubular structure makes it slightly crunchier. Both make a good introduction to shore foraging, being easy to find, easy to identify, and genuinely good to eat.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Bright to mid green, sometimes yellowish-green in bright sun or when exposed to air. Fronds are unbranched hollow tubes, 5–30cm long, 2–10mm in diameter, with a blunt or rounded tip. Attached to rocks by a small holdfast at the base. The tubular structure is diagnostic — squeeze the frond gently between thumb and finger and the hollow interior is evident. Found in the upper and mid intertidal zone on sheltered rocky shores, harbour walls, rock pools, and estuarine rocks.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Filamentous green algae (*Cladophora* and related species) grow in similar habitats and are bright green. They are thread-like or hair-like rather than tubular, and typically form woolly or cotton-wool mats. Not edible; not toxic. The tubular structure of gutweed distinguishes it clearly from these on inspection.

Sea lettuce (*Ulva lactuca*) is flat and leaf-like, not tubular. Edible — same uses.

No toxic seaweed in Britain closely resembles gutweed. No British seaweed is significantly toxic when eaten in normal culinary quantities.

Pick only from clean, well-flushed water. Avoid collecting near sewage outfalls, industrial discharge points, marinas with engine fuel in the water, or heavily trafficked ports. The smell of the water is the first check: it should smell clean and marine, not chemical or sewage-like.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Mid to upper intertidal zone on sheltered rocky shores, rock pools, harbour walls, sea walls, and estuarine rocks. Often abundant in areas with some freshwater run-off. Tolerates reduced salinity, which is why it thrives in harbours and at river mouths. Present from spring to autumn; growth peaks in summer. Absent from very exposed wave-beaten coasts.

---

**In Season:**
April to October, with the most vigorous growth from May to August. Pick young, bright green fronds on a dropping tide. Avoid fronds that are yellowing or slimy, which indicates deterioration from sun or air exposure. The best picking is from fronds submerged or recently uncovered on a falling tide, not those that have been baking in the sun for hours.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw: rinse well in fresh water, dry, and eat in a simple seaweed salad with rice wine vinegar, sesame oil, and a few flakes of dried chilli. The texture is crisp and pleasant when fresh. Pan-fried: heat a dry pan until very hot, add a small handful of rinsed and dried gutweed, and toss for 30 to 60 seconds until it crisps and darkens slightly. Eat immediately — it loses its crispness within minutes. A good crispy garnish for fish, rice, or noodles. Dried gutweed can be crumbled over food as a salty, umami-rich seasoning. Blanch first if using in soups or stir-fries.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Seaweed picking for personal use is generally permitted from the foreshore (the area between high and low water marks), which is Crown Estate land in most of England and Wales. Some beaches and marine nature reserves may have specific restrictions. Commercial harvesting requires a licence from the Crown Estate. Picking for personal use in small quantities is not affected by this.

## 194. Hawthorn Leaf

**Species Name:** Hawthorn Leaf
**Latin Name:** Crataegus monogyna
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** hawthorn-leaf
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Hawthorn produces its earliest leaves in March, weeks ahead of most hedgerow plants. The young leaves are edible raw, with a mild, nutty flavour and a satisfying crunch. Known historically as "bread and cheese", they were a standard hedgerow snack for farmworkers for centuries.

---

**Full Description:**
Most foragers know hawthorn for its flowers in May and its berries in September. Fewer think of the leaves, which emerge earlier than almost any other hedgerow species and which have been eaten by people walking hedgerows for as long as hedgerows have existed.

"Bread and cheese" is the old country name for young hawthorn leaves and buds, eaten as a snack by farm labourers walking the fields in early spring. The name describes the texture well: the leaves have a mild, slightly nutty flavour and a firmness that makes them more substantial than most salad leaves. Eaten fresh from the hedge they are immediately appealing.

The window is short. By May the leaves have hardened and developed a more astringent quality, and the focus shifts to the flowers. In March and April, those fresh young leaves, green and soft, emerging from the naked grey thorns, are one of the first real signs of the season.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Hawthorn leaves are 1–4cm long, deeply and irregularly lobed into three to seven lobes, the upper lobes more pronounced. In early spring they emerge folded and bright lime-green, with a smooth surface and a faint shine. As they expand they become mid-green and slightly waxy. The lobing pattern is distinctive: no other common hedgerow shrub produces leaves with quite this shape at this early stage. Young leaves also emerge alongside the developing flower buds in April. The thorns, grey bark, and overall hedgerow habit confirm the identification.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plant closely resembles hawthorn at the leaf-picking stage. The lobed leaf shape, the thorny shrub habit, and the grey-barked appearance together make hawthorn one of the most reliably identifiable hedgerow plants in Britain.

Midland Hawthorn (*Crataegus laevigata*) has less deeply lobed leaves and two seeds per berry rather than one. Edible in identical ways.

Blackthorn (*Prunus spinosa*) produces thorny grey-barked stems but its leaves are oval and unlobed, emerging later than hawthorn. The leaves are edible though rarely eaten.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, scrub, woodland edges, and thorny thickets throughout Britain and Ireland. Among the most common hedgerow trees in the country. Found from sea level to 500m on a wide range of soils.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves emerge from early March in sheltered spots in southern England, a week or two later in the north. The picking window is March into April, while the leaves are still soft and the flavour mild. By May the leaves are fully expanded, tougher, and less pleasant raw. The flowers that follow in May take over as the main picking target.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Eat young leaves raw, directly from the hedge, as the tradition demands. Add to spring salads with wood sorrel, lesser celandine petals, or cleavers tips for a hedgerow mix. The leaves add substance and a mild nutty flavour that contrasts well with sharper greens. Lightly blanched, they can be added to soups or used as a cooked green, though the flavour is less interesting than the raw leaf. They do not store or travel well — eat them fresh.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Hawthorn is not protected. Picking leaves for personal use from public hedgerows and footpaths is permitted. Do not cut or uproot the shrub.

## 195. Heather

**Species Name:** Heather
**Latin Name:** Calluna vulgaris
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** heather
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Heather covers vast areas of British upland and heathland, flowering from July to September in dense masses of pink-purple. The flowers are used to make heather ale, heather honey, and floral infusions. They have a mild, slightly floral and honey-like flavour.

---

**Full Description:**
Heather is one of the defining plants of British upland landscapes. From Dartmoor to the Scottish Highlands, the late-summer flowering of heather is among the most extensive seasonal colour changes in any British landscape. In August, on good heather moorland, you can walk for hours with nothing but purple around you.

The flowers are edible and have a flavour to match their visual drama: mild, faintly honey-like, and pleasantly floral without being sweet. Heather ale is a genuinely ancient preparation in Britain, probably the original ale before hops became the standard flavouring, and heather honey from bees working the moors is among the finest British honeys.

In the kitchen, the flowers work best in infusions: heather-infused cream, heather flower cordial, or added to a honey-based drink or dressing. The scale of the plant's abundance on the British uplands means that picking a modest quantity has no meaningful impact.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low, densely branched, evergreen shrub, 20–80cm tall, with woody stems and tiny, overlapping, scale-like leaves (1–3mm) arranged in four rows along the stem. Flowers are 3–4mm, bell-shaped, pale to deep pink-purple, with protruding stamens, arranged in racemes on the upper stems. The whole plant turns deep purple-pink in a good flowering year. Flowering August to October. Found on acid heathland, moorland, bogs, and open woodland on acid soils.

Bell heather (*Erica cinerea*) flowers earlier (July to August) and has slightly larger, more strongly coloured bell-shaped flowers and thread-like rather than scale-like leaves. Edible in the same ways.

Cross-leaved heath (*Erica tetralix*) flowers from June to September with pinkish bell-flowers in a tight cluster at the tip. Grows on wetter boggy ground. Edible.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic plant closely resembles heather in its habitat and flowering period.

Rosemary (*Rosmarinus officinalis*), a garden escape, has narrow aromatic leaves and blue flowers. Not confused with heather.

The heathers and heaths (*Calluna* and *Erica*) are all edible. No dangerous confusion exists with any established British plant.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Acid heathland, moorland, blanket bog, and open oak or birch woodland on acid soils. Dominant over vast areas of upland Britain — the Scottish Highlands, Welsh uplands, Dartmoor, Exmoor, the North York Moors, and the Pennines. Also found on lowland heathland in Dorset, Surrey, and the New Forest. Absent from chalk, limestone, and fertile lowland farmland.

---

**In Season:**
July to September, with peak flowering in August. Bell heather begins slightly earlier, in July. Pick flowers when they are fully open and bright in colour. Fresh flowers work best; dried flowers are good for infusions.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Heather flower infusion: steep a generous handful of flowers in hot water for 10 minutes and strain. Sweetened with heather honey, it makes a pale pink, mildly floral drink. Heather cream: warm double cream, steep with a large handful of flowers for 30 minutes, strain, and chill. Use to make panna cotta, ice cream, or a pouring cream for shortbread. For heather ale, the traditional preparation involves mashing a modest quantity of dried flowers with grain, water, and yeast. The recipe is more involved but the results are distinctive.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Heather is not protected. Picking flowers for personal use from open moorland and heathland is generally permitted. Many heather moors are managed for grouse and have restricted access during the shooting season (August to December). Avoid picking in nature reserves without checking local guidance.

## 196. Himalayan Balsam

**Species Name:** Himalayan Balsam
**Latin Name:** Impatiens glandulifera
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** himalayan-balsam
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
An invasive annual of riverbanks and damp ground, reaching up to 2m by late summer. The seeds are edible and have a walnut-like flavour. The flowers are sweet and can be eaten raw. Foraging this species is actively encouraged, as removal helps control a damaging invasive plant.

---

**Full Description:**
Himalayan balsam arrived in Britain as a garden plant in the 1830s and has spent the time since colonising every riverbank, drainage ditch, and wet margin it can reach. It grows fast, tall, and in such density that it outcompetes native plants for light and space, then dies back in autumn to leave bare soil vulnerable to erosion. It is one of Britain's most problematic invasive species, and picking and eating it is, from an ecological perspective, the right thing to do.

The flowers are large, hooded, pink-purple to white, and mildly sweet. The seeds are the more interesting food: small, dark, and packed inside the explosive pod, they have a nutty flavour and a pleasant crunch. Children quickly discover that the ripe pods explode on touch, and this fact has made the plant well-known long before most people thought of eating it.

Pick freely. Pick enthusiastically. A species you can eat without any conservation concern is a small pleasure.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A tall, hairless annual, reaching 1–2m. Stems are hollow, reddish-green, with swollen nodes, and snap cleanly. Leaves are opposite or in whorls of three, lance-shaped, 5–15cm long, with reddish mid-veins and regularly toothed margins. Small reddish glands are present at the base of each leaf. Flowers are 25–40mm, hood-shaped, pink-purple (occasionally white), in clusters on short stalks from the leaf axils, from July to October. The seed pods are club-shaped, 15–30mm, green ripening to pink, which explode violently when ripe and touched, scattering seeds several metres. The explosive pod is the plant's most distinctive feature.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No British plant closely resembles Himalayan balsam in its full-grown, flowering state. The explosive seed pods, hollow red-tinged stems, and large hooded flowers are diagnostic.

Touch-me-not Balsam (*Impatiens noli-tangere*), a very rare native, grows in a few damp woodland sites and is smaller, with yellow flowers. Edible.

Orange Balsam (*Impatiens capensis*) is a naturalised species in some areas with orange-spotted flowers. Edible.

All Impatiens species in Britain are edible. No toxic confusion exists.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Riverbanks, streamsides, drainage ditches, damp roadside verges, canal margins, and wet woodland edges. Grows in dense stands, often covering hectares of riverbank. Common throughout Britain, particularly along English and Welsh river systems. Less common in Scotland but spreading. In late summer, large stands are hard to miss.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from July into September. Seeds ripen from August and the pods explode from late August through to the first frosts. The flowers are edible throughout summer. For seeds: pick pods that are swollen and beginning to colour pink but before they have triggered — press gently between finger and thumb to feel if they are ready to pop. Strip the seeds from the opened pod directly into a bag.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Seeds: roast lightly in a dry pan for two to three minutes until fragrant. The walnut-like flavour develops with heat. Use in baking, scattered over salads, or ground into a paste with oil and salt. They are small but plentiful. Flowers: eat fresh as a sweet garnish, add to salads, or use to decorate a summer dessert. Young shoots in early summer can be blanched and eaten as a vegetable, though the plant's availability in summer means there are usually better options around.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Himalayan balsam is classified as a Schedule 9 species under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. It is illegal to plant or cause it to spread in the wild. Picking and eating it is legal and ecologically beneficial. Pulling it up and composting it is also encouraged — the shallow roots pull cleanly in wet soil. Do not compost flowering stems or seed pods in a way that allows seeds to spread.

---

## 197. Hoary Cress

**Species Name:** Hoary Cress
**Latin Name:** Lepidium draba
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** hoary-cress
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
A persistent, vigorous perennial of roadsides, coastal ground, and disturbed soil, covered in dense white flowers in spring. The young leaves have a mild peppery flavour and can be used in salads or cooked as greens. Not spectacular, but readily available and edible.

---

**Full Description:**
Hoary cress is one of those plants that country walkers notice without necessarily knowing. By May, large patches on roadsides, railway embankments, and waste ground turn white with a dense flat-topped flower head, the whole plant smothered in small four-petalled blooms. The effect is striking. The plant itself is less exciting than it looks in flower.

The leaves are the useful part, and mainly in spring before the plant flowers. They are grey-green, slightly hairy, and broadly oval, with a mild peppery flavour that sits somewhere between watercress and radish. Young leaves in March and April are pleasant in a salad or lightly cooked with butter. The flower buds, like many brassicas, can be steamed or stir-fried as a sprouting broccoli substitute. Once the plant is in full flower, the leaves coarsen.

Hoary cress is an introduced species, probably arriving in Britain from eastern Europe in the early 19th century. It has established itself thoroughly on disturbed coastal and transport corridor soils. There is no conservation concern about picking it.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A robust, grey-green perennial, 30–80cm tall when in flower. Leaves are 3–8cm long, oval to elliptical, with a clasping base that wraps around the stem, softly hairy, grey-green, and slightly waxy. Lower leaves are stalked; upper leaves are unstalked and clasping. Flowers are small (3–4mm), white, four-petalled, in dense flat-topped clusters that elongate as the season progresses. The seed pod is distinctive: heart-shaped, notched at the tip, 3–4mm, borne on spreading stalks. The whole plant has a faint peppery smell when crushed. Stems are stiff, erect, and branched in the upper half.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic plants closely resemble hoary cress in its spring roadside setting.

Field Penny-cress (*Thlaspi arvense*) is an annual of arable ground with a flattened, broadly winged seed pod. Edible.

Smith's Pepperwort (*Lepidium heterophyllum*) is similar and edible.

Dittander (*Lepidium latifolium*) is larger, more coastal, and has a much fiercer heat. Related and edible.

Shepherd's Purse (*Capsella bursa-pastoris*) is smaller, with heart-shaped pods and a rosette of deeply lobed basal leaves. Edible.

All the pepperworts and their relatives in Britain are edible. No toxic confusion exists with hoary cress in its typical roadside habitat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, road verges, railway embankments, coastal wasteland, and disturbed soil throughout England. Particularly common near ports and on coastal ground in southern and eastern England. A classic plant of the roadside ecology that has spread along transport corridors. Less common in Wales and Scotland.

---

**In Season:**
Young leaves from March while the plant is low-growing and the leaves are at their mildest. Flower buds in April, before the main flowering period. In full flower from April to June. Once in full flower, the leaves coarsen and become more strongly flavoured than most people find pleasant raw. The plant can produce a second flush of young growth after cutting back.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young leaves in spring salads, adding a mild peppery note alongside blander greens. Use sparingly — the flavour is pleasant but assertive. Flower buds: blanch for 60 seconds and eat with butter and salt, or add to a stir-fry. The flavour is cleaner and more pleasant than the leaves at this stage. Leaves can be cooked briefly as a peppery side vegetable with fish or eggs. Do not use in large quantities raw — the heat intensifies with volume.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Hoary cress is not a protected species in Britain. Picking for personal use from roadsides, verges, and public land is permitted. It is listed as a noxious weed in some other countries where it is invasive; in Britain it is established but unprotected.

## 198. Japanese Knotweed

**Species Name:** Japanese Knotweed
**Latin Name:** Reynoutria japonica
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** japanese-knotweed
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Britain's most notorious invasive plant is also an edible one. In early spring, the young shoots are tender, tart, and genuinely good to eat, with a flavour often compared to rhubarb. Pick from established patches, never transport the plant, and eat freely.

---

**Full Description:**
Japanese knotweed has a reputation in Britain entirely built on destruction: foundations undermined, mortgages refused, riverbanks destabilised. All of this is real. It is also, in spring, one of the more interesting seasonal foods available for free in Britain, and picking it is an entirely defensible act.

The young shoots emerge from the ground in late March and early April, red-tinged and asparagus-like, pushing through the previous year's dried hollow stems with considerable speed. At 15–30cm, before they have fully expanded their leaves, they are at their best. The flavour is tart and sharp, like rhubarb, with more complexity and a faint astringency. The texture, raw, is crisp and juicy. Cooked, they soften and the tartness mellows. They make a good crumble, a sharp compote, or a sauce for duck or pork.

Pick freely. Eat what you pick. Do not take any plant material away from the site except to eat it — even composting cut material near running water carries a risk of spreading new colonies.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Young shoots in spring: 10–40cm, asparagus-like, red to green, with small folded leaves emerging from bamboo-like segmented nodes. Stems are hollow and will snap cleanly. The growing tip unfurls rapidly into the full leaf shape. Mature plants: stout, hollow, bamboo-like stems, 1–3m tall, with a zig-zag pattern between nodes (10–20cm apart). Leaves are large (10–15cm), broadly shield-shaped (truncated at the base, not tapering), mid-green. Autumn flowers are small and creamy-white in loose spikes, appearing in August and September. Dried hollow stems persist through winter. The colony habit, bamboo-like appearance, and rapid spring growth are unmistakeable to anyone who has encountered the plant before.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
In its mature summer form, Japanese knotweed is unmistakeable. The spring shoots require more care.

Giant knotweed (*Reynoutria sachalinensis*) and the hybrid between the two (*Reynoutria × bohemica*) are also invasive, also edible, and essentially indistinguishable for foraging purposes.

Asparagus shoots, in the unlikely event of encountering wild or cultivated asparagus emerging nearby, are rounder in cross-section and do not have the characteristic bamboo-like nodes or the hollow stem.

Japanese knotweed is not toxic. The main risk from the plant is regulatory — transport of living material carries legal penalties, not from eating it.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Riverbanks, roadsides, railway embankments, wasteland, and disturbed ground throughout Britain. Dense colonies often cover large areas. Common in towns and cities as well as the countryside. The plant spreads from fragments as small as 1–2cm of rhizome.

---

**In Season:**
Spring shoots from late March to late April, depending on location. The window is short — the shoots grow quickly and within a week or two of emerging they have hardened and the leaves expanded fully. Pick at 15–30cm, before the leaves have fully opened. Once the stem is hollow and the leaves are fully expanded, it is past the best eating stage. Check established patches from mid-March in sheltered areas.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw shoots: crisp and sharp, good in a salad or sliced thinly with salt and lemon. Japanese knotweed crumble: slice shoots into 2–3cm pieces, toss with sugar, and bake under a standard crumble topping. The tartness cuts through the sweet topping well. Compote: cook shoots with sugar and a little water until collapsed, about 10 minutes. The resulting compote is tart, pink, and pairs well with yogurt, oatmeal, or as a sauce for duck or pork. The flavour is closest to rhubarb, though with a slightly more complex, slightly mineral edge.

---

**Legal Notes:**
It is a criminal offence under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 to cause Japanese knotweed to grow in the wild. This means: pick the shoots to eat, but do not transport any living plant material, do not compost it near watercourses, and do not put cut material in garden waste collections. Eat what you pick on-site or take it home in a sealed bag for cooking. The law concerns spreading the plant, not eating it.

## 199. Larch Needles

**Species Name:** Larch Needles
**Latin Name:** Larix decidua
**Type:** Trees
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** larch-needles
**Seasons:** Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Larch is Britain's only deciduous conifer, and its spring needles, emerging in soft bright green tufts in April, are briefly edible. They have a fresh, citrusy, slightly resinous flavour and can be used to infuse syrups, creams, and spirits.

---

**Full Description:**
Most conifers are evergreen. Larch is not. This is the first thing to understand about a tree that, from a distance in winter, looks dead. By April it has produced something that no other conifer offers in Britain: a spring flush of soft, tender new needles that are genuinely worth eating.

The needles emerge in April in dense rosette-like tufts from the knobbly short shoots on the branches. They are bright acid-green at this stage, soft to the touch, and they have a flavour that is unmistakeably conifer, but fresh and citrus-edged rather than resinous or turpentine-heavy. The window lasts two to three weeks. Once the needles have hardened and darkened, the opportunity is gone until next year.

The fresh needles make excellent infusions. Steeped in hot water or in neutral spirits, they release a fresh, slightly resinous, citrusy flavour that is unlike almost anything else from the British spring. Larch needle syrup is one of the more interesting things you can make from a foraging afternoon in April.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large deciduous conifer, 25–40m. Bark is grey-brown, deeply furrowed on mature trees. Needles are 15–40mm, soft, narrow, and borne in dense clusters of 20–40 on short knobbly spurs along the branches (rather than singly, as in spruce or fir). In spring, the new needles emerge bright acid-green; they deepen through summer and turn golden-yellow before dropping in autumn. Female cones are small (2–4cm), upright, remaining on the tree for years. The deciduous habit and clustered needle arrangement together distinguish larch from all other conifers.

European Larch (*Larix decidua*) and Japanese Larch (*Larix kaempferi*) are both widely planted in Britain and both have edible spring needles.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic conifer in Britain closely resembles larch in its deciduous, clustered-needle form.

Yew (*Taxus baccata*) has flat, dark green needles arranged in two rows along the twig, and is evergreen. The red berry-like fruit contains highly toxic seeds. Do not confuse. Yew needles and seeds are deadly — ingestion causes cardiac arrest. Yew does not have clustered needles on knobbly spurs.

The most important rule with conifers: confirm the species before eating needles from any unfamiliar tree. Larch is distinctive in its deciduous habit and clustered needle rosettes. In spring, no other British conifer produces the acid-green tufts of soft new growth characteristic of larch.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Widely planted throughout Britain in commercial forestry, estate woodland, and ornamental planting. Common in upland areas of Scotland, Wales, and northern England in commercial forests. Also frequently planted in parkland, estate grounds, and roadside planting schemes. Does not naturalise widely in Britain but is a familiar and easily identified plantation tree.

---

**In Season:**
Needles emerge in April, with the precise timing depending on altitude and location. Lowland plantations in southern England go first; upland Scottish forests can be three to four weeks later. The picking window is two to three weeks at most. Pick when the needles are still soft and bright acid-green. Once they have begun to harden and darken, the fresh citrus quality is gone.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Larch needle syrup: pack 50g of fresh needles into a jar, pour over 400ml of hot (not boiling) water, and steep for 12 hours. Strain, add 200g of sugar, and dissolve over low heat. The resulting syrup is pale green-gold, mildly resinous and citrusy. Good over ice cream, in sparkling water, or stirred into cocktails. Larch needle gin: pack needles into a jar of gin for 24 to 48 hours, then strain. The infusion picks up the citrus-resin character quickly. Do not over-steep — the flavour becomes more bitter and turpentine-like with time. Fresh needles can also be steeped in cream for an infused cream to use in panna cotta.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Larch is not a protected species. Picking needles for personal use from woodland and parkland is generally permitted. In commercial forestry, the land is private — seek permission or pick from trees on public access land.

---

## 200. Lemon Balm

**Species Name:** Lemon Balm
**Latin Name:** Melissa officinalis
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** lemon-balm
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A strongly lemon-scented herb of hedgerows, roadsides, and old gardens, naturalised throughout Britain. The fresh leaves make an excellent herbal tea and can be used in cooking wherever lemon zest or lemon herbs are called for. Easy to find, easy to identify, and unmistakeable by smell.

---

**Full Description:**
Lemon balm is not a native British plant, but it has been growing here for long enough that it barely registers as foreign. A garden herb since medieval times, it has escaped over walls and garden fences and now grows in hedgerows, on roadside banks, and on waste ground adjacent to habitation throughout England and Wales.

The smell is the whole story: a clean, intensely lemony scent released immediately on touching any part of the plant. It is one of the most distinctive smells in the British hedgerow and makes identification effortless. Find it once and you will recognise it without hesitation every time after.

The leaves make a simple, pleasant tisane, particularly when fresh. Dried lemon balm loses some of its brightness but still delivers a clean lemon flavour. In the kitchen, fresh leaves can substitute for lemon zest in many applications, adding a lighter, more herbaceous lemon quality to dressings, sauces, and fish dishes. It combines well with other herbs.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A bushy perennial, 40–90cm when in flower. Stems are square in cross-section, lightly hairy. Leaves are 3–7cm long, broadly oval to heart-shaped, with a rounded base, distinctly crinkled surface, and coarsely toothed margins. Mid to bright green. The lemon smell is released immediately and strongly when the leaf is brushed or crushed. Flowers are small (8–12mm), white to pale yellow, tubular with two lips, appearing in whorls at the leaf axils from July to September. Often found in large patches in abandoned garden corners, hedgebank bases, and south-facing roadside banks.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic plant closely resembles lemon balm in its smell and habit.

White Dead-nettle (*Lamium album*) shares the square stem and opposite toothed leaves but smells of nothing in particular when crushed, not of lemon.

Catnip (*Nepeta cataria*) is another naturalised garden herb with square stem and opposite leaves, with a quite different smell — minty and slightly musty, not lemony.

Common Nettle (*Urtica dioica*) stings on contact; lemon balm does not. The lemon smell settles any uncertainty immediately.

The lemon smell is definitive. No plant that smells strongly and unambiguously of lemon when crushed in a British hedgerow setting is going to cause harm.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, hedgebanks, and disturbed ground adjacent to gardens and habitation. A garden escape naturalised throughout England and Wales. Prefers warm, sheltered, south-facing positions on well-drained soil. Particularly common near old kitchen gardens, allotment sites, and cottage garden settings. Less common in Scotland and northern upland areas.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from May through the growing season. Best before the plant flowers, June to July, when the leaves are largest and the flavour freshest. Flowers July to September. After flowering, the leaves become smaller but remain usable. For drying: pick in June or early July before flowering, strip the leaves, and dry quickly in a warm, well-ventilated spot.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Fresh leaf tea: pour hot water over six to eight fresh leaves and steep for five to eight minutes. The flavour is fresh and cleanly lemony — different from dried lemon balm, which is more muted. As a cooking herb: use fresh leaves in a dressing for fish, stirred into Greek yogurt with mint and cucumber, or added to a cold chicken salad. Good with new potatoes and a little crème fraîche. Combine with other herbs — mint, tarragon, basil — for a fresh herb sauce. Use quickly after picking: the leaves wilt fast and lose brightness.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Lemon balm is not protected. Picking for personal use from public land and verges is permitted. Do not pick from private gardens without permission.

## 201. Lime Leaf

**Species Name:** Lime Leaf
**Latin Name:** Tilia × europaea
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** lime-leaf
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
The common lime is a familiar street and parkland tree, and its young leaves in spring are edible, mild, and pleasant in salads. The flowers that follow in July make one of the best wild-flower teas available in Britain. A tree that offers two distinct seasonal finds.

---

**Full Description:**
Common lime is a hybrid tree, planted extensively throughout Britain in avenues, parks, and as a street tree. It is one of the most familiar urban trees in the country, recognisable by its heart-shaped leaves, its midsummer sticky honeydew drip, and the clusters of small hanging flowers that appear in July.

In spring, before the leaves have fully expanded, the young tender leaves emerging from the bud are soft, slightly mucilaginous, and have a mild, pleasant flavour that works well in salads. This is a less well-known use than the flowers, but a worthwhile one. The leaves develop quickly and the window for eating them raw is short — a week to ten days in April or May before they have toughened.

The flowers, appearing in July in drooping clusters with a strap-like bract, make an excellent tisane that is floral, mildly sweet, and calming. Lime flower tea has a long European tradition. In Britain, the flowers are abundant, free, and of good quality from any established lime tree.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A large deciduous tree, 20–40m. Bark grey-brown, furrowed. Leaves are 6–10cm, heart-shaped with a pointed tip and slightly asymmetric base, toothed margins, and small tufts of orange-brown hairs in the vein axils on the underside. Young spring leaves are soft, slightly shiny, and bright green. Flowers appear in July in drooping clusters of 4–10 small (6–10mm) creamy-yellow flowers attached to a distinctive strap-like bract 4–8cm long. The flowers have a sweet, slightly honey-like scent. The combination of heart-shaped leaf, distinctive flower clusters with strap bract, and sticky summer aphid honeydew drip make common lime unmistakeable.

Small-leaved lime (*Tilia cordata*) and large-leaved lime (*Tilia platyphyllos*) are less common but widely planted; both are edible in the same ways.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plant resembles lime at the leaf or flower picking stage.

Hazel (*Corylus avellana*) has similar rounded, toothed leaves but the leaf base is not heart-shaped and it is a shrub rather than a large tree.

Mulberry (*Morus nigra*) has lobed leaves and is a much smaller tree, usually found in old gardens.

No toxic confusion is possible with established lime trees at the sizes they are typically encountered.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Widely planted in parks, avenues, streets, churchyards, and estate grounds throughout Britain. Small-leaved lime is native in ancient woodland on limestone in England and Wales. Common lime and large-leaved lime are planted throughout. Easy to find in most British towns and cities.

---

**In Season:**
Young leaves from late March in the south, April in the north, for a window of one to two weeks before the leaves toughen. Flowers in July, sometimes into August. Pick flowers when they are newly opened and the scent is strong. Both flowers and their bracts are used.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young leaves: add to spring salads for a mild, slightly mucilaginous texture. Good with more assertive flavours — sorrel, dandelion, or a mustardy dressing. The leaves work as a wrap for small pieces of food in the same way as vine leaves, if briefly blanched to soften. Lime flower tea: dry the flowers and bracts in a single layer in a warm, well-ventilated spot for two to three days. Steep a small handful of dried flowers in hot water for five to eight minutes. The flavour is distinctly floral, with a mild sweetness. Fresh flowers can also be steeped in warm milk for a floral infused cream.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Lime trees in parks and streets are generally on public or council land. Picking young leaves or flowers in small quantities for personal use is acceptable. Do not strip branches or pick in quantities that would harm the tree.

## 202. Marjoram

**Species Name:** Marjoram
**Latin Name:** Origanum vulgare
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** marjoram
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Wild marjoram grows on chalk and limestone grassland and at sunny hedgerow edges throughout England and Wales. The flavour is close to the dried oregano sold in every supermarket — warm, aromatic, faintly spicy — but fresher and more complex from the living plant. Good picked and used immediately, or dried for winter.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild marjoram is the same species as the Mediterranean oregano sold in every kitchen shop. Growing on chalk downland and sunny limestone slopes in Britain, it produces the same essential oils — thymol, carvacrol — that give the dried herb its character, though the concentration is lower in the British wild plant than in the sun-baked Mediterranean versions.

On a warm chalk hillside in July, the smell is instantly recognisable: the warm, slightly camphorous, herbal scent of the dried spice jar, but alive and cut through with something greener. The plant is in flower from July into September, the small pink-purple flowers in tight clusters at the tips of the stems, with bees working them constantly.

The leaves are the main culinary target. Dried, they are a reasonable substitute for commercial oregano. Fresh, they have more complexity and a greener edge. On the right patch on the right day, picking marjoram for a summer pasta or a pizza sauce is one of foraging's more immediate pleasures.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A bushy, aromatic perennial, 25–70cm tall. Stems are reddish, softly hairy, square in cross-section. Leaves are 1–3cm, oval, softly hairy, paired, with a faint red-purple colouring at the margins and a strongly aromatic smell when crushed. Flowers are small (3–6mm), pink to pale purple, in tight terminal clusters, July to September. The bracts beneath the flowers are often red-purple, giving the flower heads a colourful appearance. The whole plant smells strongly of oregano when any part is crushed.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Wild marjoram belongs to the labiate family (Lamiaceae), which includes many aromatic herbs. No member of this family in Britain is dangerously toxic.

Thyme (*Thymus* species) is lower-growing and more compact, with smaller leaves and a different, more sharply thymol-forward smell. Edible.

Wild basil (*Clinopodium vulgare*) shares similar chalk grassland habitats and is edible. Its flowers are larger (12–20mm) and its leaves slightly less aromatic.

Wild Mint (*Mentha* species) has a mint smell rather than the oregano-marjoram character.

White Dead-nettle (*Lamium album*) has a similar square stem and opposite leaves but no significant smell.

Betony (*Betonica officinalis*) grows in similar habitats but has longer, more crinkled leaves and deep red-purple flowers. Not dangerous.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Dry, often south-facing chalk and limestone grassland, scrub edges, hedgerow banks, and roadside verges on calcareous soils. Most common in southern England on the chalk and limestone belts: South Downs, North Downs, Chilterns, Cotswolds, Yorkshire Wolds. Less common in Wales and Scotland. Absent from acid heathland and very wet soils.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from June through the growing season. Best before and during early flowering — July and August — when the aromatic oils are at their most concentrated. Flowers July to September. For drying: pick in July on a dry, sunny morning. Strip the leaves from the stems and dry quickly in a warm, well-ventilated spot. Store in a sealed jar out of direct light.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use fresh or dried exactly as you would shop-bought oregano. Fresh leaves in a tomato and garlic sauce for pasta. Dried, crumbled into pizza dough or scattered over a flatbread with olive oil. Good with lamb, roasted vegetables, and grilled fish. Combine with thyme and rosemary for a robust herb salt. A handful of fresh marjoram added to a jar of olive oil and left for a week makes a useful infused oil for cooking. The flavour intensifies with heat, so add fresh leaves at the end of cooking if you want the green note to come through.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Wild marjoram is not a protected species. Picking for personal use from chalk grassland and verges is permitted. Many chalk downland sites are SSSIs or nature reserves — pick sparingly and avoid disturbing the surrounding plant community.

---

## 203. Marsh Mallow

**Species Name:** Marsh Mallow
**Latin Name:** Althaea officinalis
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** marsh-mallow
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, softly hairy plant of coastal saltmarshes and brackish ditches with pale pink flowers and large, velvety leaves. All parts are edible: the leaves, flowers, and roots. The mucilaginous sap of the root is the original ingredient of marshmallow confectionery.

---

**Full Description:**
Marsh mallow is a plant whose history runs directly into the confectionery aisle of every supermarket. The marshmallow sweet was originally made from whipped egg whites and the mucilaginous sap of the root of this plant. The food chemistry of the original and the modern version have nothing in common — but the name persists.

The plant itself is worth knowing for its own merits. The large, soft, velvety leaves are edible raw or cooked, with a mild, slightly mucilaginous texture that makes them useful as a salad leaf or a cooked green. The pale pink flowers, appearing from August into September, are edible and decorative. The roots, dug in late summer or autumn (with landowner permission), contain the highest concentration of mucilage.

It is a coastal plant, found on saltmarsh edges and brackish ditches, particularly in southern England. Finding it requires knowing where to look. It is not a common discovery.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A robust, softly hairy perennial, 60–120cm tall. The whole plant is covered in a dense grey-white felt of soft, star-shaped hairs, giving it a velvety, greyish appearance. Leaves are 3–8cm, broadly triangular to ovate, three to five-lobed with irregular toothing, soft and slightly thick. Flowers are 25–40mm across, five-petalled, pale pink to almost white, with a characteristic mallow structure — a column of stamens fused into a central tube. Flowers appear from August to September. The seed capsule that follows is flat and disc-shaped, divided into sections like a cheese wheel. No other plant in coastal saltmarsh habitats has quite this combination of size, velvety softness, and pale pink mallow flowers.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Common Mallow (*Malva sylvestris*) is smaller (30–90cm), has darker pink flowers with obvious purple veining, and grows on roadsides and waste ground rather than in coastal saltmarsh. Edible in the same ways.

Tree Mallow (*Malva arborea*) is a larger, woody-based plant of coastal cliffs and rocks, with dark pink flowers. Edible.

Hollyhock (*Alcea rosea*), a garden escape, has taller flower spikes and more distinctly lobed leaves. Edible.

All members of the mallow family (*Malvaceae*) found in Britain are edible. No toxic confusion exists with any of them.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Coastal saltmarshes, brackish ditches, sea walls, and rough ground near tidal water. Most common in southern and eastern England — the Thames Estuary, Kent and Essex coasts, and the south coast of Hampshire and Dorset. Absent from upland and inland areas. A plant with a restricted but predictable habitat.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from June through to late summer. Best before flowering when the leaves are at their freshest. Flowers August to September. Roots can be dug in autumn (they are most mucilaginous in late summer and autumn) but require landowner permission as uprooting requires consent.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young leaves: raw in salads, where the mild flavour and slightly mucilaginous texture add substance. Cooked, they work as a green with butter and lemon. The mucilage means they thicken a soup or stew slightly, in the same way as okra. Flowers: eat fresh as a garnish or steep in a small amount of hot water with honey for a simple infusion. Root: peel, slice, and simmer in water for 20 minutes to extract the mucilaginous sap, then strain. The liquid can be used to thicken sauces or whipped with sugar to a foam — the original marshmallow technique. The cooked root itself can be eaten as a vegetable.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Marsh mallow is not nationally protected but grows in habitats that are frequently SSSIs or within coastal nature reserves. Check before picking in any designated area. Do not uproot plants without landowner permission. Pick leaves and flowers only in conservation areas.

---

## 204. Mugwort

**Species Name:** Mugwort
**Latin Name:** Artemisia vulgaris
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** mugwort
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, common roadside herb with deeply cut leaves, dark green above and silvery-white below, and a strong aromatic smell. Used as a culinary herb and beer-flavouring plant in Britain before hops became standard. Do not use in pregnancy.

---

**Full Description:**
Mugwort grows on roadsides, waste ground, and disturbed soil throughout Britain, reaching 80–150cm by midsummer. It is not a subtle plant. The tall, branching stems with their deeply cut leaves, dark green above and densely silvery-white below, are distinctive. The smell when any part is crushed is strong and aromatic, slightly bitter, with a faint sage-like quality.

It was used for centuries in Britain to flavour beer before hops (*Humulus lupulus*) became the universal brewing plant in the 16th century. The bitter, aromatic flavour is real and usable in brewing, in meat cooking, and as a flavouring for fatty foods where its bitterness provides balance.

The culinary tradition around mugwort is more developed in Germany, Japan, and Korea than in Britain, where it is mostly ignored. In Japan, *yomogi* mochi (rice cakes made with pounded mugwort) is a spring classic. The flavour works well in savoury applications, particularly with pork, duck, and oily fish.

One absolute restriction: do not use in any form during pregnancy. Mugwort contains compounds that stimulate uterine contractions.

---

**Identification Notes:**
An upright perennial, 80–150cm, with erect, branching, often reddish-purple stems. Leaves are 5–10cm, deeply pinnately lobed, with pointed lobes and a slightly coarse surface. The underside of the leaf is covered in dense white-grey hairs, giving it a striking two-tone appearance: dark green above, silver-white below. This underside colouration is the single most reliable identifier. The smell when any part is crushed is strong and aromatic, slightly bitter, quite different from the musty unpleasantness of hemlock or the blandness of most leaves. Flowers are tiny, yellowish-brown to red-brown, in small heads clustered in branching spikes, August to September. The plant dies back in winter and regrows from the root.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Ragwort (*Jacobaea vulgaris*) grows in similar habitats and is toxic to livestock, though not acutely dangerous to humans on contact. Its leaves are green on both sides, not silvery-white below, and its flowers are bright yellow daisy-like heads, quite unlike mugwort's small brownish flowers. The smell is unpleasant; mugwort is aromatic.

Wormwood (*Artemisia absinthium*) is a related species found on dry waste ground, more densely silvery-grey throughout, with a very bitter, stronger smell. Edible in small quantities as a flavouring but not for eating raw or in volume. Absent from most of Britain.

Tansy (*Tanacetum vulgare*) has similar habitat and stature but pinnate leaves without the silvery underside, and bright yellow button flowers. Toxic in quantity.

The silvery-white leaf underside and strong aromatic smell together identify mugwort reliably.

Do not use in pregnancy.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, waste ground, riverbanks, hedgerow bases, and disturbed nitrogen-rich soil throughout Britain. One of the most common large herbs of the roadside and field margin. Grows in full sun or partial shade. Very common in England; less abundant in upland Scotland.

---

**In Season:**
Young leaves and growing tips from June, before the plant flowers. Best in June and early July when the growth is fresh and the aromatic compounds are at their peak. Flowers from August to October — at this point the leaves coarsen and the flavour intensifies. Dry the plant in late summer: hang cut stems in a warm, well-ventilated spot and strip the dried leaves for winter use.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as a bitter aromatic herb in small quantities. Good with roast pork: tuck a few sprigs into the cavity of the joint, as you would rosemary or sage. The bitterness balances fatty meat well. In stuffing: mix finely chopped fresh or dried mugwort with breadcrumbs, onion, and butter for a stuffing for goose or duck. For beer flavouring: add a small handful of dried tops to the fermenting vessel in place of or alongside hops. The bitterness and aromatics carry through. Start with less than you think you need — the flavour is strong.

Do not eat in large quantities. Do not use in pregnancy.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Mugwort is not protected. Picking for personal use from public land and verges is permitted.

---

## 205. Nettle Seed

**Species Name:** Nettle Seed
**Latin Name:** Urtica dioica
**Type:** Seeds
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** nettle-seed
**Seasons:** Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
The seeds of common nettles, ripe from August onwards, are a genuine and underused food source. Dried and used as a seasoning or eaten fresh as a green seed, they have a flavour that is earthy, slightly nutty, and intensely green. The sting diminishes as the seeds ripen.

---

**Full Description:**
Most foragers stop at the spring nettle top. The same plant, in late summer and autumn, offers something quite different: seeds. The nettle seed season runs from August into October, when the seed heads on female plants develop from pale green to grey-brown and hang in long, drooping catkins from the leaf axils.

The seeds are small but numerous. Female nettle plants in a good patch can carry hundreds of seed catkins, and picking a handful's worth takes only a few minutes once you have found a productive patch.

The flavour is interesting: earthy, slightly nutty, intensely green, with a quality that some compare to sesame but more complex. Fresh seeds have a mild residual sting that diminishes on drying or toasting. They can be used as a seasoning, scattered over eggs, salads, or grain dishes, or toasted briefly for a deeper, more nutty flavour.

This is a species most foragers know as a spring green and stop thinking about. Coming back in autumn is worth doing.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Female nettles carry seed catkins from July onwards. The catkins are elongated, hanging from the leaf axils on long thin stalks, initially pale green and eventually grey-brown as the seeds ripen. A population of nettles will have both male plants (which carry thinner, more upright pollen-bearing flowers) and female plants (which carry the seed catkins). The sting on mature female plants is real but slightly less fierce than in spring. The seeds are tiny (1–1.5mm), oval, tan to grey-brown when fully ripe. Pick the catkins by stripping them from the hanging stalks into a bag. Wear gloves.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Nettles in seed are as unmistakeable as nettles at any other stage. No dangerous plant closely resembles them.

White Dead-nettle (*Lamium album*) and Red Dead-nettle (*Lamium purpureum*) do not produce catkin-like seed heads. The sting test settles any uncertainty.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Wherever common nettles grow — roadsides, field edges, riverbanks, waste ground. Large patches of well-established female plants in late summer are the most productive source.

---

**In Season:**
Seeds from August, ripening through September and into October. The optimal window is when the catkins have turned from pale green to grey-brown and the seeds are firm and developed. Slightly under-ripe (still green) seeds have more flavour and a fresher, greener taste; fully ripe seeds are more nutty. Do not pick once the seeds are shedding freely — they will scatter before you collect them.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Dry fresh seed catkins in a warm spot for a day or two, then strip the seeds by rubbing the catkins between your palms over a bowl. The sting diminishes on drying. Use as a seasoning: scatter over a fried egg, over hummus, into a grain salad, or over roasted vegetables. Toast briefly in a dry pan for a deeper flavour — 60 to 90 seconds over moderate heat, watching carefully. They darken quickly. Good stirred into a spiced butter and used over fish. Fresh seeds can be blended with oil and salt for a green seed paste. The flavour is strong and a little goes a long way.

---

**Legal Notes:**
As with nettle leaves, picking seeds from public land for personal use is permitted. Nettles are not protected.

---

## 206. Pepper Dulse

**Species Name:** Pepper Dulse
**Latin Name:** Osmundea pinnatifida
**Type:** Seaweed
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** pepper-dulse
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct

---

**Short Description:**
A small, intensely flavoured red-brown seaweed of rocky shores, often found tucked into rock pools and crevices in the lower intertidal zone. The flavour is the most complex of any British seaweed — spicy, umami-rich, and deeply marine. Used as a seasoning rather than a vegetable.

---

**Full Description:**
Pepper dulse is small. On a rock covered in seaweed, it will not catch your eye. It grows low on the shore, in the lower intertidal zone, often tucked under larger fronds or in the shelter of a rock crevice, and it reaches only 3–8cm at most. Finding it requires knowing what to look for: a small, flat, broadly branched, reddish-brown frond with rounded tips.

The reward for identifying it correctly is the flavour. Pepper dulse has a reputation among seaweed enthusiasts for good reason. Raw, it is spicy and intensely marine, with an umami depth that is unlike any other British coastal plant. Dried, the flavour concentrates further. It is used as a seasoning, not eaten in bulk — a pinch of dried pepper dulse in a fish dish, a cream sauce, or a compound butter gives a complexity that no other seaweed matches.

This is a specialist find, not an everyday foraging target. The water quality where it grows matters.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A small (3–8cm) seaweed with a flat, broadly fan-shaped branching structure. Fronds are 2–5mm wide, branching repeatedly in a slightly irregular pattern, with rounded or blunt tips. Colour is red-brown, sometimes purplish-brown, drying to a darker brownish-red. The texture is slightly fleshy and firm, not slimy. Found in the lower mid to lower intertidal zone on clean, well-exposed rocky shores. The strong, distinctive, spicy and umami smell when fresh distinguishes it from other similarly-sized red seaweeds.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Several small red seaweeds occupy similar niches in the lower intertidal zone.

Dulse (*Palmaria palmata*) is larger (10–30cm), deeper red, with broader undivided fronds or fronds divided into large broad lobes. Edible and well-known.

Ceramium species and other red filamentous algae are finer and more thread-like. Not typically eaten.

No toxic seaweed exists in British intertidal habitats. The risk from seaweed foraging is water quality, not species toxicity. Pick only from clean, well-flushed rocky shores, away from sewage outfalls, marinas, industrial discharge points, and heavily contaminated estuaries.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Lower intertidal zone on clean, exposed rocky shores. Most common on west-facing rocky coastlines with good tidal flushing — the coasts of west Scotland, Ireland, Wales, and the south-west of England. Less common on sheltered east-coast shores and in estuaries where water quality is poorer. Look for it at low tide on the lower shore, tucked under the fronds of larger seaweeds or in the shelter of rock crevices.

---

**In Season:**
Available throughout the year on suitable shores but easiest to find from spring to autumn when tidal exposure allows access to the lower shore. Best harvested on spring low tides when the lower intertidal zone is exposed. Quality is good year-round on clean shores.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as a seasoning, not a bulk ingredient. The flavour is too intense for eating in volume. Dried pepper dulse crumbled over a piece of fish before grilling. Stirred into a cream sauce for pasta or risotto at the end of cooking. Mixed into a compound butter with lemon and black pepper and melted over grilled scallops. Ground into a powder and used as a finishing salt with fish and shellfish. Fresh fronds can be added raw to a seafood salad in small quantities. The drying process concentrates the flavour significantly — start with less than you think you need.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Seaweed picking for personal use is generally permitted from the foreshore. Some marine nature reserves have specific restrictions. Commercial harvesting requires a licence. Pick in small quantities and avoid depleting small patches — pepper dulse is not the most abundant seaweed and heavy picking can remove it from a site entirely.

---

## 207. Pignut

**Species Name:** Pignut
**Latin Name:** Conopodium majus
**Type:** Roots
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** pignut
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun

---

**Short Description:**
Pignut grows in dry grassland and open woodland, producing a small underground corm that is edible raw — crisp, nutty, and faintly earthy, often compared to hazelnut. The challenge is identification: this is a member of the carrot family, which demands careful attention. Never dig near wet ground.

---

**Full Description:**
Finding a pignut is a specific kind of satisfaction. The plant is delicate — a slender, branching umbellifer reaching only 25–50cm — and the corm is small, brown, and buried 5–15cm deep at the end of a thin, fragile root that snaps if you pull too hard. Getting it out intact requires patience and a stick or small trowel.

The taste justifies the effort. The corm has a flavour that is immediately pleasant: crisp, nutty, and faintly sweet, with a mild earthiness that some find reminiscent of fresh hazelnut or water chestnut. Eaten raw, straight from the ground after brushing off the thin skin, it is one of the better wild foods available in spring.

The identification requires care. Pignut is a member of the carrot family, and this family contains several species that are dangerously toxic. The habitat distinction is the first test: pignut grows on dry, well-drained grassland and open woodland. The deadly lookalike, Hemlock Water Dropwort, grows in wet ground beside water. If the ground is damp or near a stream, stop.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A slender, wiry perennial, 25–50cm tall. Leaves are finely cut, two to three times pinnate, with very narrow linear segments — much more finely divided than most other umbellifers. Lower leaves wither early, leaving the upper leaves as the main feature. Stem is hollow, very slender (2–4mm), slightly channelled, green. Flowers are white, in a compound umbel 3–7cm across, with no involucral bracts or very few, June and July. The above-ground plant is among the most delicate and slender of the British umbellifers. Below ground: a thin connecting root leads to a small (1–3cm), rounded, brown-skinned corm. Peel the thin skin with a thumbnail to reveal white flesh with a nutty smell. Do not dig near wet ground.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Pignut is a member of the carrot family. This family demands careful identification. Never pick or dig any umbellifer without being certain of what you have.

Hemlock Water Dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) is the critical danger. It has caused multiple deaths in Britain. Its roots are white, fleshy, and clustered in bundles like small parsnips — quite different from the small rounded pignut corm. Hemlock Water Dropwort grows in or beside water, in marshes and wet meadows. Pignut grows on dry, well-drained grassland. If the ground is wet, or if there is a stream or ditch nearby, do not dig for roots.

Fool's Parsley (*Aethusa cynapium*) has distinctive long, pendulous bracts beneath each flower head. Pignut has no such bracts. Fool's Parsley is toxic and causes vomiting.

Rough Chervil (*Chaerophyllum temulum*) has spotted, hairy stems. Pignut stems are smooth and plain green. Rough Chervil is toxic.

The pignut corm is small, rounded, and brown-skinned, with a nutty smell. Hemlock Water Dropwort roots are white, fleshy, and clustered. If what you find underground is white, fleshy, and bundled in clusters: do not eat it.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Dry, well-drained permanent grassland, meadows, and open deciduous woodland on light or sandy soils. Common throughout Britain in suitable habitats. Indicator of old, undisturbed grassland — it does not survive ploughing or herbicide. Absent from waterlogged ground, wet meadows, and any ground near running water.

---

**In Season:**
The above-ground plant is visible from April into June. Pick for the corm during this period while the foliage is still identifiable. By July the above-ground plant has mostly died back and identifying the site becomes difficult. Work in May and June while the plant is at its clearest.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Eat raw, straight from the ground, after brushing off the thin skin. The flavour needs no enhancement. A handful of pignuts eaten while walking is the classic preparation, and one of the better arguments for knowing your way around a British meadow. They can be used in a raw spring salad with sorrel, wood sorrel, and a lemon dressing. Cooked pignuts become softer and less interesting — the raw crunch is the appeal. Rinse, peel lightly with a thumbnail, and eat.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Uprooting plants without landowner permission is illegal under the Theft Act. The pignut corm requires digging, so you need to be on land you own or have explicit permission to forage. On public footpaths, the right of way does not typically include the right to dig. Many common land sites and nature reserves permit foraging on a non-commercial basis, but digging is often restricted. Confirm before digging.

## 208. Pine Needle

**Species Name:** Pine Needle
**Latin Name:** Pinus sylvestris
**Type:** Trees
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** pine-needle
**Seasons:** Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

---

**Short Description:**
Scots pine needles are available year-round and have a distinctive resinous, citrusy flavour that works well in infusions, syrups, and compound butters. Young spring needles are the most tender and aromatic, but the tree provides something usable in every month of the year.

---

**Full Description:**
Scots pine is one of Britain's native trees and the dominant tree of the Caledonian pinewoods in Scotland. It is also widely planted throughout Britain in commercial forestry, parkland, and on heathland. The needles are available in every month of the year, which gives this species a practical usefulness that few other forageable plants share.

The flavour is resinous and citrusy, with a quality that is immediately recognisable as pine but cleaner and more complex in the young spring growth than in older needles. Steeped in hot water, the flavour is mild and distinctive. Steeped in spirits, it concentrates and deepens. Used to make a syrup, it gives something unusual that has no commercial equivalent.

Not everyone finds the flavour appealing. It is specifically and unmistakeably pine, and people who dislike resinous flavours will dislike this too. For those who like it, it is a year-round resource from a tree that grows across most of Britain.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Scots pine (*Pinus sylvestris*) has a distinctive orange-red upper bark visible in the upper crown of mature trees, grey-brown and deeply furrowed at the base. Needles are in pairs, 3–7cm long, stiff, twisted, blue-green to grey-green. Cones are 2–6cm, egg-shaped, green when young, turning grey-brown. The orange-red upper bark is the single most reliable identifier of Scots pine among the pines.

Several other pine species are planted in Britain: Corsican Pine (*Pinus nigra*), Maritime Pine (*Pinus pinaster*), Stone Pine (*Pinus pinea*), and others. The needles of all planted pines are edible but vary in flavour.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic tree in Britain closely resembles Scots pine in its overall habit and needle structure. The main risk comes from confusion with Yew (*Taxus baccata*), which could not be more different: yew has flat, dark green needles arranged in two rows on the twig, not in paired bundles, and produces red berry-like fruit. Yew is deadly. Its needles and seeds cause cardiac arrest. The species are not genuinely similar on inspection, but the warning bears repeating: any tree with needles in a spreading flat arrangement and red berry-like fruit is yew. Do not pick from it.

Spruce (*Picea* species) needles are single, not paired, and are attached to small peg-like projections on the twig that remain after the needle falls. Edible.

Fir (*Abies* species) needles are also single and have circular leaf scars. Edible.

Larch (*Larix* species) has needles in tufts of 20–40 and is deciduous. Edible.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Native Caledonian pinewoods in Scotland and widely planted throughout Britain in commercial forestry, on heathland, and in parkland. Very common on sandy, acid soils. Easily found in the New Forest, Breckland, the Scottish Highlands, and throughout plantation forestry on light soils.

---

**In Season:**
Year-round. Young spring needles (April to May) are the most tender and aromatic. Pick young shoots — the pale green new growth at the tips of the branches — for the best flavour. Older, darker needles are usable but tougher and more intensely resinous.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Pine needle syrup: pack 50g of young needles (remove the brown papery sheath at the base) into a jar with 400ml hot water, steep for 12 hours, strain, and dissolve 200g sugar into the warm liquid. Use over ice cream, in cocktails, or in sparkling water. Pine needle butter: finely chop fresh young needles, mix into softened butter with lemon zest and salt, and chill. Good melted over grilled fish or chicken. Pine needle infusion: steep a small handful in hot water for five minutes, strain, and drink. The flavour is mild and resinous in a pleasant way. Steep in neutral spirits for 24 hours for a pine-infused gin base.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Scots pine is not protected. Picking needles for personal use from public woodland and forestry land is generally permitted. In commercial Forestry Commission plantations, the public right to access usually covers picking for personal use.

## 209. Pineapple Weed

**Species Name:** Pineapple Weed
**Latin Name:** Matricaria discoidea
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** pineapple-weed
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A small, low-growing annual of gateways, compacted paths, and field margins, with yellow-green button flowers that smell unmistakeably of pineapple when crushed. The flower heads make a pleasant pineapple-chamomile tea and can be added to summer drinks and desserts.

---

**Full Description:**
Pineapple weed grows where almost nothing else will: the heavily compacted soil at gateways, the middle strip of unmade tracks, the hard edges of field entrances. In the right place it forms dense mats underfoot. Crush a flower head between your fingers and the smell is immediate, distinctive, and surprising: fresh pineapple, sweet and tropical, from a weed growing in field-gate mud.

The flower heads are the useful part. They make a simple, pleasant infusion with a mild pineapple-chamomile flavour. They can be added to summer drinks and desserts, scattered over a fruit salad, or used in a cold infusion in still water. The flavour is genuinely charming, particularly from fresh flowers.

Pineapple weed is one of those gateway finds that works well for introducing children or beginners to the idea that plants in unlikely places can smell and taste extraordinary.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A small, hairless annual, 5–25cm tall. Leaves are finely divided, two to three times pinnate, with narrow linear segments, bright green, aromatic when crushed. Stems are low-growing and branching from the base. Flower heads are 5–8mm across, hemispherical to cone-shaped, yellow-green, with no ray petals (no white petals surrounding the central disc — this is the key difference from mayweed and chamomile). Flowers from May to September. Crushed flower heads smell strongly of pineapple. Found on compacted, disturbed, bare, or sparsely vegetated ground: gateways, farm tracks, paths, field margins, and waste ground.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Scentless Mayweed (*Tripleurospermum inodorum*) and Scented Mayweed (*Matricaria chamomilla*) have white ray petals around a yellow centre. Pineapple weed has no white petals — the flower head is entirely yellow-green. Scented mayweed smells of chamomile; pineapple weed smells of pineapple. Both are edible.

Feverfew (*Tanacetum parthenium*) has white daisy flowers with yellow centres and a strong, bitter, medicinal smell quite different from pineapple.

No dangerous plant closely resembles pineapple weed, particularly given the diagnostic pineapple smell of the crushed flower heads.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Compacted, bare, or disturbed ground: field gateways, farm tracks, field margins, footpaths, road edges, and waste ground. Tolerates very heavy foot and vehicle traffic, which is why it is the dominant plant at gateways where most other plants would not survive. Found throughout Britain and Ireland.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers from May to September. At their best June to August when fully formed. Pick flower heads when the cones are firm and fully yellow-green. Wilted or browning flower heads have diminished flavour.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Pineapple weed tea: steep a heaped handful of fresh flower heads in hot water for five to eight minutes, strain, and serve. The flavour is mild, sweet, and distinctly pineapple-chamomile. Good with a little honey. Cold infusion: pack flower heads into a bottle of cold still water and refrigerate for six to eight hours. Strain and drink. Lighter and more refreshing than the hot infusion, with more of the fresh pineapple quality. Scatter fresh flower heads over a summer fruit salad or use as a garnish for a dessert. The flavour is mild enough not to dominate.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Pineapple weed is not protected. Picking for personal use from public paths, verges, and gateways is permitted.

## 210. Samphire

**Species Name:** Samphire
**Latin Name:** Crithmum maritimum
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** samphire
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Rock samphire — the original, Shakespeare's samphire — grows on sea cliffs and coastal rocks throughout Britain. The fleshy, aromatic stems and leaves have a sharp, intensely marine, parsley-anise flavour. Different from marsh samphire (glasswort) in every way except the name.

---

**Full Description:**
Rock samphire has one of the most concentrated flavours of any British coastal plant. A small piece, chewed raw, delivers something sharp, aromatic, and intensely marine: part parsley, part anise, part sea air. It is not a flavour for everyone. For those who like it, it is one of the most distinctive things you can pick from the British coast.

Shakespeare knew it: "Half-way down Hangs one that gathers samphire" is the Gloucester cliff scene in King Lear, where the dangerous business of gathering samphire on cliff faces was a real and risky profession. Modern foragers do not need to do anything so dramatic. The plant grows in accessible cliff-face crevices, on rocky shores, and around harbour walls and coastal paths, often in considerable quantity.

Note: this is not the same plant as marsh samphire or glasswort (*Salicornia europaea*), which grows on saltmarshes and is the samphire most commonly sold in fishmongers. That plant is crunchier, saltier, and milder. Rock samphire is sharper, more aromatic, and more intensely flavoured. Both are worth knowing.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A compact, woody perennial, 15–40cm. Stems are branched from the base, somewhat fleshy, and smell strongly aromatic when crushed. Leaves are fleshy, pinnate, with narrow, cylindrical, pointed leaflets (1–3cm), a distinctive bright yellow-green. The fleshy, finger-like leaf segments distinguish it immediately from any other coastal plant. Flowers are small, yellow-green, in compound umbels 2–6cm across, appearing from June to September. The whole plant has a distinctive, strong aromatic smell — part anise, part parsley — immediately on contact.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Rock samphire is a member of the carrot family. The coastal cliff habitat significantly limits confusion with the most dangerous members of this family, but the rule applies: never eat any umbellifer without being certain of the species.

Hemlock Water Dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) grows near freshwater, not on sea cliffs. Rock samphire grows on dry, salt-sprayed coastal rock. The habitats are distinct, but do not pick any carrot-family plant near wet coastal ground or estuaries without checking carefully.

Rock samphire's fleshy, finger-like yellow-green leaflets and strong aromatic smell are distinctive and not closely replicated by any dangerous species in its typical cliff habitat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Sea cliffs, coastal rocks, harbour walls, shingle beaches with rock outcrops, and cliff-base boulders. Found on all British coasts but most common and abundant in the south and west — the coasts of Cornwall, Devon, Wales, Pembrokeshire, and west Scotland. Less common on the flat east coast of England. Grows in crevices and ledges, often in exposed positions with salt spray.

---

**In Season:**
Young stems and leaves from June through to September. Flowers June to September. At their most tender before flowering. Pick the young shoot tips and tender stems.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Rock samphire pickled in vinegar is the classic preparation and the right one: the intensity of the flavour is tamed by the pickling process, and the result is a genuine condiment. Pack clean stems and leaves into a sterilised jar, heat equal parts white wine vinegar and water with salt and a little sugar, pour over the samphire, and seal. Leave two weeks before opening. Good with fish, cold meats, and strong cheeses. Raw in small quantities as a garnish for a seafood dish. Briefly blanched (60 seconds) and dressed with olive oil and lemon juice. The flavour is strong enough that a little goes a long way.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Rock samphire is not protected as a species, but many of the cliff and coastal habitats where it grows are SSSIs or within nature reserves. Pick sparingly and leave the root system intact. Do not climb cliff faces to reach inaccessible plants.

---

## 211. Sea Holly

**Species Name:** Sea Holly
**Latin Name:** Eryngium maritimum
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** sea-holly
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul

---

**Short Description:**
A striking coastal plant of sandy and shingle shores, with metallic blue-green spiny leaves and distinctive thistle-like blue flowers. The young shoots and root are edible, the root having a history as a candied confection (eryngo root). Now rare enough to warrant restraint in picking.

---

**Full Description:**
Sea holly is one of the most visually distinctive plants on the British coast. The blue-grey, spiny, holly-like leaves and the globular, blue, thistle-like flowers have an almost alien quality among the dunes and shingle of a coastal sand system. Finding a good patch is memorable.

It is edible, and has been eaten for centuries. The roots were candied and sold as 'eryngo root', a sweetmeat popular in Elizabethan and Stuart England associated particularly with Colchester, Essex, where the plant grew in abundance on the coast. The candied root reportedly had a flavour described as warm and slightly sweet, not unlike parsnip. The young shoots in spring, before the spines harden, were also eaten.

Sea holly has declined significantly across the British coast as a result of habitat loss and coastal development. On some sites it is rare and legally protected. Pick only where the plant is clearly abundant, take only small quantities of young shoots, and do not dig the root on public land.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial, 20–60cm. Leaves are distinctive: rounded to ovate, leathery, grey-green to metallic blue-green, with prominent white veins and spiny-toothed margins. Young leaves in spring are softer before the spines harden. Flowers are globular, 2–3cm, thistle-like, with a collar of pointed spiny bracts; blue to steel-blue in colour. July to September. Found on coastal sand dunes, sandy shingle, and coastal grassland above the tide line.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No dangerous plant closely resembles sea holly in its coastal sand dune habitat.

Field eryngo (*Eryngium campestre*) is similar and edible, found on dry chalk and limestone grassland (very rare in Britain).

Thistles (*Cirsium* and *Carduus* species) have similar spiny leaves but quite different flowers and habitats. The young stems of many thistles are edible.

No toxic confusion exists with sea holly in its coastal habitat.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Coastal sand dunes, sandy shingle, sandy coastal grassland, and coastal paths above the tideline. Most common on the sandy coasts of East Anglia, Lincolnshire, and the south and west coasts of England and Wales. Local and declining in many areas. Absent from rocky coasts.

---

**In Season:**
Young shoots in May and June, before the spines harden fully. Flowers July to September. On most sites, given the plant's declining status, picking should be minimal and limited to sites where the plant is clearly growing in abundance.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Young shoots before the spines harden: rinse, blanch briefly, and dress with olive oil and lemon. The flavour is mild and slightly sweet, faintly reminiscent of parsnip. Root (only where abundant, only with landowner permission): wash, peel, and slice the root. Boil until tender, about 20 minutes, and dress simply. Or slice thin and candy in a heavy sugar syrup to make eryngo root — a historical preparation that works and is worth trying once on a plant where the root system is undamaged by picking.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Sea holly is not listed on Schedule 8 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 as a protected species nationally. However, it may be locally protected within SSSIs, nature reserves, and dune systems managed for conservation. Do not dig roots on public land or in nature reserves. Check local guidance before picking in any protected coastal area.

## 212. Sea Purslane

**Species Name:** Sea Purslane
**Latin Name:** Atriplex portulacoides
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** sea-purslane
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Sea purslane is among the most dependable and accessible of the saltmarsh plants. The small, fleshy, grey-green leaves are salty, clean, and good both raw and cooked. It grows in dense, reliable patches on saltmarsh edges and estuarine creeks throughout Britain.

---

**Full Description:**
Sea purslane forms dense carpets on the edges of British saltmarshes. The low, spreading plants, with their small, oval, grey-green leaves, are a feature of the middle saltmarsh across most of the British coastline. On a good stretch of east or south coast saltmarsh, you can be in the middle of a continuous mass of sea purslane that stretches further than you can see.

The flavour is the saltmarsh: clean, salty, and slightly mineral. The leaves are fleshy and hold their texture well in cooking. They work raw in a salad with something acidic to offset the salt, or lightly pan-fried in butter, or added to a fish dish where you want salt and coastal flavour without the sharp intensity of rock samphire.

Sea purslane is one of the most user-friendly coastal plants: easy to identify, available in quantity, and genuinely good to eat.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A sprawling, woody-based perennial, 20–80cm. Stems are spreading, woody at the base, with a distinctive mealy-grey coating. Leaves are 1–4cm, elliptical to spoon-shaped, opposite on young growth, alternate on older growth, fleshy, grey-green with a mealy or powdery surface. The mealy coating gives the whole plant a grey-white appearance that is distinctive at a distance. Flowers are inconspicuous, yellowish-green, in dense spikes, August to October. Found on the edges of saltmarshes, estuarine mudflats, and the upper margins of tidal creeks, often forming the dominant plant of the middle saltmarsh.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Annual sea purslane (*Atriplex prostrata*) and other oraches (*Atriplex* species) grow in similar coastal habitats. All are edible and closely related.

Glasswort / marsh samphire (*Salicornia europaea*) grows in similar saltmarsh habitats but looks entirely different: succulent, jointed, bright green, resembling a tiny cactus.

Sea sandwort (*Honckenya peploides*) grows on shingle and sandy shores near sea purslane territory but is smaller, cushion-forming, with white flowers. Edible.

No toxic plants grow in the middle saltmarsh in close proximity to sea purslane. The habitat itself, combined with the grey mealy leaf coating, makes identification reliable.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Middle to upper saltmarsh, the edges of estuarine creeks, and the landward margins of tidal mudflats. Found on all coasts of Britain and Ireland. Most abundant on the protected saltmarshes of east and south England — the Thames Estuary, Essex and Norfolk coasts, and the south coast estuaries of Hampshire and Dorset. Also common in the larger estuaries of Wales, Scotland, and Ireland.

---

**In Season:**
May to September. Young growth from May when the leaves are at their most tender. Quality through summer. Pick the tips and young upper leaves rather than older, tougher growth on the woody lower stems.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Raw in a simple salad with a sharp dressing — lemon or good vinegar — and something with complementary flavour: pickled fish, cold smoked salmon, or crab. Pan-fried in butter for two to three minutes, the salt content means no additional seasoning is needed. Good as a side for grilled or pan-fried fish. Add to a risotto in the last two minutes of cooking in place of salt. Blanch briefly to reduce the saltiness if you want a less briny result. Pickled in mild white wine vinegar for a mild, salty, crunchy condiment.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Sea purslane is not protected. Picking for personal use from saltmarsh is generally permitted. Many saltmarsh habitats are SSSIs or nature reserves — pick in small quantities and avoid trampling the surrounding marsh. Access to saltmarsh can be restricted by incoming tides; check local tide times.

**Full Description:**
Sea sandwort grows just above the tideline on sandy and shingle beaches throughout Britain. ---

---

The leaves can be briefly blanched and used as a cooked green, though the flavour is mild enough that raw is more interesting.

---

**Legal Notes:**

## 214. Sheep's Sorrel

**Species Name:** Sheep's Sorrel
**Latin Name:** Rumex acetosella
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** sheeps-sorrel
**Seasons:** Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A small, low-growing sorrel of acid grassland, heathland, and sandy soils. The leaves have a sharp, lemony sourness from oxalic acid, stronger than common sorrel in small plants. Good in salads and soups in moderate amounts.

---

**Full Description:**
Sheep's sorrel is the smaller, more acidic cousin of common sorrel (*Rumex acetosa*), growing on the kind of thin, acid, nutrient-poor soils that common sorrel tends to avoid. Heathland edges, dry sandy banks, old railway cuttings, and upland grassland are its habitats. On the right patch it grows in quantity, the small arrow-shaped leaves covering the ground in a reddish-green mat in summer.

The flavour is sharply sour, lemony, and assertive. Eaten in small quantities it has the same appeal as common sorrel: that bright, clean acidity that wakes up a salad or lightens a rich sauce. Eaten in large quantities it has the same drawback: the oxalic acid content means significant amounts are not advisable, particularly for people with kidney problems, gout, or rheumatoid arthritis.

For everyone else, a handful of leaves in a spring or summer salad is exactly as good as common sorrel in the same role.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low perennial, 5–30cm. Leaves are 1–4cm, distinctly arrow-shaped with two pointed, horizontally spreading lobes (hastate lobes) at the base — this is the key identifier. Leaf stalk is slender. The leaves are green to yellow-green, sometimes reddish in dry conditions. Flowers are very small, reddish-brown, in slender, branching spikes, May to August. Male and female flowers on separate plants. The whole plant is small and low-growing, typically associated with thin, acid, nutrient-poor soils. The arrow-shaped leaf with the distinctive spreading basal lobes is the reliable identifier.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Wood sorrel (*Oxalis acetosella*) grows in woodland and has trefoil leaves (three heart-shaped leaflets). Edible and with a similar sour flavour.

Common sorrel (*Rumex acetosa*) is larger (20–60cm), with bigger leaves (5–15cm) and less distinctly spreading basal lobes. Edible.

Dock species (*Rumex* species) have larger, broader leaves and are not significantly sour. Edible in similar ways to sorrel, though not worth eating for flavour.

No toxic plant closely resembles sheep's sorrel. The small, arrow-leaved form on acid grassland, combined with the immediate sour taste test, makes it identifiable.

The oxalic acid content is a practical caution rather than a danger in culinary quantities. People with kidney stones, gout, or hyperoxaluria should avoid eating large amounts.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Acid grassland, heathland, and sandy, thin, nutrient-poor soils. Roadsides on sandy or gravelly soils, old railway cuttings, dune grassland, and upland pasture. Common throughout Britain on suitable soils. Absent from chalk, limestone, heavy clay, and fertile arable land.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from April through the summer and into early autumn. Best in April to June when young and freshest. By midsummer on dry ground the leaves can be tough and the flavour becomes more astringent. A second flush of young growth can appear in September after summer dieback on disturbed soils.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use exactly as you would common sorrel. Small quantities raw in a salad with blander leaves — the sourness provides contrast. Sheep's sorrel sauce for fish: melt butter, add a generous handful of leaves, let them wilt and collapse (about 60 seconds), add cream and season. The leaves turn khaki green on contact with heat. Good with salmon or trout. A few leaves added to a simple potato soup, blended in at the end, gives a bright, clean finish. Do not eat more than a handful raw at a sitting.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Sheep's sorrel is not protected. Picking for personal use from public land, verges, and heathland is permitted.

## 215. Silver Birch Sap

**Species Name:** Silver Birch Sap
**Latin Name:** Betula pendula
**Type:** Drinks
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** silver-birch-sap
**Seasons:** Mar, Apr

---

**Short Description:**
Silver birch sap flows for two to three weeks in early spring, before the leaves emerge. The sap is clear, very mildly sweet, and fresh-tasting. Collected by tapping and fermenting into a light country wine, or drunk fresh as a spring tonic, it is one of foraging's more unusual seasonal finds.

---

**Full Description:**
For most of the year, silver birch offers nothing edible. For two to three weeks in March, it offers something quite specific: a clear, slightly sweet sap that rises through the tree before the leaves emerge.

The sap is subtle. Drunk fresh, it is barely sweet, clean, and faintly mineral, with a freshness that comes partly from the fact of its origin and partly from its actual taste. It is not dramatically flavoured, and people expecting something sweet and syrupy like maple sap will find it underwhelming. What it is, at its best, is fresh, clean, and briefly available.

Fermented into birch sap wine, the character becomes more pronounced: a light, slightly hazy, mildly tannic country wine with an earthy, slightly floral quality. Making birch sap wine requires a reasonable quantity of sap and some patience. The resulting wine is distinctive and worth making once.

Tapping must be done correctly and respectfully. A tree is not a resource to exploit. The method below removes only a small quantity of sap from a large tree over a few days.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Silver birch is one of Britain's most recognisable trees: white to pale grey, papery bark, particularly brilliant white in the upper branches of mature trees, with distinctive dark diamond-shaped markings. Leaves (emerging in April) are 2–4cm, triangular to rhombic, with doubly toothed margins. Branches are slender and pendulous (drooping) in mature trees. Male catkins (long, pendulous, yellow) are present from autumn, shedding pollen in early spring. Female catkins are shorter and upright.

Downy birch (*Betula pubescens*) has rounder leaves and downy young twigs. Also edible and in similar habitats. Sap is usable in the same way.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No British tree closely resembles silver birch. The white papery bark is diagnostic and no toxic confusion is possible.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Open woodland, heathland, moorland edges, and disturbed ground throughout Britain. Silver birch is a pioneer tree, one of the first to colonise open ground. Found on acid to neutral soils, most common on sandy, gravelly, and freely draining ground. Widespread throughout Britain and Ireland.

---

**In Season:**
The sap flows for two to three weeks in March and early April, in the brief window between the last cold weeks of winter and the emergence of the leaves. In the south of England this is typically mid-March to early April. In Scotland, it can be mid-April. The window closes fast once the leaves emerge.

---

**How to tap:**
Select a mature tree (at least 30cm trunk diameter) on land you own or have permission to forage. Drill a hole of 10–15mm diameter, 4–5cm deep, into the trunk at a slight upward angle, about 60–90cm above the ground. Insert a clean tube — silicone tubing of the right diameter works well — to direct the sap into a collecting vessel. Collect no more than one to two litres per day from a large tree. Remove the tube after two to three days and seal the hole with a wooden peg or grafting wax. Do not tap the same tree in consecutive years.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Fresh sap: drink within 24 hours of collection, kept cold. The flavour is delicate and fades quickly. The sap begins to ferment naturally within a day or two at room temperature. Birch sap wine: add sugar (500g per litre), juice of two lemons, and wine yeast to the collected sap. Ferment in a demijohn until bubbling has stopped (two to three weeks), then rack and allow to clear before bottling. The resulting wine is dry, light, and distinctive. Birch sap cordial: reduce the sap with sugar over low heat to a light syrup and use diluted.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Tapping a tree constitutes a modification of the tree and requires landowner permission — do not tap trees on land you do not own. In commercial woodland and forestry land, permission must be obtained. Tapping on public footpaths or open access land is not permitted without the landowner's consent.

## 216. Spearmint

**Species Name:** Spearmint
**Latin Name:** Mentha spicata
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** spearmint
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Spearmint is a garden escape naturalised in damp corners throughout Britain. The familiar, sweet mint flavour is sharper and more complex from the living plant than from the dried version. Good for mint sauce, drinks, cooking, and drying for winter.

---

**Full Description:**
Spearmint has been grown in British gardens for so long that its distinction from a truly wild plant is almost academic. It escapes readily from cultivation and grows in damp ditches, streamside vegetation, waste ground near habitation, and along the edges of paths and tracks. In the right spot it forms extensive patches that smell powerfully of mint when any part is brushed.

The flavour is the familiar spearmint: sweet, bright, slightly less menthol-forward than peppermint, and fresher from the living plant than from anything dried or processed. A handful of fresh leaves dropped into a jug of cold water, or steeped briefly in hot water for tea, is simpler and better than any commercial product.

There is nothing complicated about using spearmint. The challenge, if it can be called that, is distinguishing it from other mints growing nearby, which matters less in culinary terms than confirming you have a mint at all.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A spreading perennial, 30–60cm, spreading by underground runners. Stem is square in cross-section, green or sometimes reddish, hairless or nearly so. Leaves are 2–6cm, oval to lance-shaped, smooth or slightly wrinkled, hairless or nearly so, with a sharply toothed margin and no stalk (sessile, or nearly so). The smell when crushed is the classic spearmint, sweet and sharp. Flowers are small, pale lilac to white, in a slender terminal spike (not in whorls along the stem as in corn mint), appearing from June to September.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
All British mints are edible. No mint-smelling plant in Britain is dangerous.

Corn mint (*Mentha arvensis*) has flowers in whorls at the leaf axils rather than in a terminal spike, and a more menthol-forward smell.

Water mint (*Mentha aquatica*) has its flowers in a rounded terminal head rather than a long spike, and grows in wetter habitats.

Peppermint (*Mentha × piperita*) is a hybrid with reddish stems, more oval leaves, and a stronger menthol quality. Also a garden escape; also edible.

Pennyroyal (*Mentha pulegium*): see Corn Mint entry for safety note regarding pregnancy and large doses.

Confirm mint by smell. If it smells of mint, it is a mint. All British mints are edible.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Damp ground near habitation: ditches, roadside drains, the edges of streams, waste ground, and old garden sites. Found throughout England and Wales, less common in Scotland. A garden escape rather than a truly native plant.

---

**In Season:**
May through September. Best before flowering (June) when the leaves are largest and freshest. Pick in the morning before heat drives off the volatile oils.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Mint sauce: finely chop a large handful of fresh leaves, add sugar and white wine vinegar, and leave for 30 minutes. The classic preparation for lamb. Fresh mint in cold drinks: bruise leaves gently before adding to cold water or cocktails. Mint tea: steep fresh leaves in hot water for five minutes. Dry in summer for winter use: strip leaves from stems, dry flat in a single layer in a warm, well-ventilated spot, then store in a sealed jar. Dried spearmint is useful but noticeably less bright than fresh.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Spearmint growing wild is a garden escape and not protected. Picking for personal use from public land and verges is permitted.

## 217. Spruce Tip

**Species Name:** Spruce Tip
**Latin Name:** Picea abies
**Type:** Trees
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** spruce-tip
**Seasons:** Apr, May

---

**Short Description:**
Norway spruce produces new growth in spring — the bright green, soft shoot tips that emerge from the end of each branch. These tips are briefly edible, with a fresh, citrusy, resinous flavour and a short picking window of two to three weeks. Good in syrups, infusions, and butters.

---

**Full Description:**
Norway spruce is the species most commonly used as a Christmas tree in Britain. For most of the year it offers nothing to the forager. In April and May, the new shoot tips emerging from the branch ends offer something brief and genuinely worthwhile: a short window of soft, fragrant, citrus-resin growth that is one of the more distinctive flavours available from the spring countryside.

The tips are recognisable before you pick them: the bright, pale acid-green of fresh growth against the darker older needles, the whole shoot soft and slightly sticky before it hardens. The flavour is fresh and clean, less resinous than older spruce growth, with a citrusy quality that makes it ideal for infusing into syrups and drinks.

The window is measured in days. Once the tips have hardened and taken on the dark green of mature spruce needles, the opportunity is gone.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Norway spruce is a large conical conifer, 20–40m, with reddish-brown bark and pendulous branchlets on the lower branches of mature trees. Needles are single (not in bundles), 10–25mm, dark green, four-angled in cross-section, attached to small peg-like projections on the branch. The new tips in spring are pale to bright acid-green, soft and slightly sticky, easily bent without snapping. Cones are cylindrical, 10–15cm, hanging downwards. Widely planted in commercial forestry throughout Britain; also commonly planted in parkland and as a Christmas tree.

Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*), the most widely planted commercial forestry species in Britain, has flatter, sharper needles with a blue-green upper surface. Spring tips are also edible.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Yew (*Taxus baccata*) has flat, dark green needles in two rows along the twig and red berry-like fruit. Yew is deadly. Do not pick from it.

Spruce tips and yew do not look similar on careful inspection. Yew needles are flat and arranged in two spreading rows; spruce needles are four-angled and project all around the twig. Yew has red fleshy fruit; spruce has long hanging cones.

Do not pick from any conifer with red berries. If there are red berries anywhere on the tree, it is yew. Walk away from it.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Commercial forestry throughout Britain, particularly in Scotland, Wales, northern England, and parts of East Anglia. Widely planted in parkland and as ornamental and Christmas trees. Very common in upland plantation forestry.

---

**In Season:**
New tips emerge in April in sheltered lowland areas, May in upland forests. The picking window is two to three weeks at most. Pick when the new growth is soft and bright acid-green, before it hardens. Check the branch ends from early April.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Spruce tip syrup: pack 50g of fresh tips into a jar with 400ml of hot (not boiling) water, steep for 12 hours, strain, and dissolve 200g sugar into the warm liquid. Good over ice cream, in cocktails, or in sparkling water. Spruce tip butter: finely chop the tips (removing the papery sheath at the base of each needle cluster), mix into softened butter with salt and lemon zest, and chill. Good over fish or chicken. Spruce tip vinegar: pack fresh tips into a jar and cover with mild white wine vinegar. Leave for two weeks, strain. The resulting vinegar has a fresh, citrusy quality.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Norway spruce in commercial forestry is on private land — seek permission before entering. In Forestry Commission woodland with public access rights, picking small quantities of tips for personal use is generally acceptable.

---

## 218. Tansy

**Species Name:** Tansy
**Latin Name:** Tanacetum vulgare
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Expert
**Slug:** tansy
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**⚠️ Safety-flagged species. Expert review required before this entry goes Live. Contains toxic thujone compounds. Culinary use must be explicitly and narrowly defined.**

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, aromatic perennial of roadsides and riverbanks with clusters of bright yellow button flowers. Historically used in small amounts as a bitter herb flavouring. Contains thujone, which is toxic in anything beyond minimal quantities. Expert-only status applies.

---

**Full Description:**
Tansy was once a significant culinary herb in British cooking, used to flavour cakes and puddings at Easter and as a bitter seasoning in meat dishes. The tradition largely died out by the 19th century, and the reason is relevant: tansy contains thujone, the same toxic compound present in wormwood, in quantities that make it genuinely dangerous if used in more than minimal amounts.

The historical culinary uses were real and attested in detail in early English cookery books. They involved small quantities of tansy as a bitter flavouring — not the main ingredient. The flavour is powerfully aromatic and bitter, with a strong, distinctive, camphorous quality that makes it easy to overdo.

This is a species where the margin between flavouring and toxicity is narrow. The toxic dose of thujone is not enormous. Tansy poisoning causes vomiting, seizures, and in significant doses can be fatal. This is not a plant to use casually or in volume. Including it here is for completeness, historical context, and to ensure that people who encounter it understand what they have. Culinary use requires expert guidance.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A tall, upright perennial, 60–120cm. Leaves are 10–20cm, pinnately lobed with deeply toothed segments, bright green, hairless or nearly so, strongly aromatic when crushed. The smell is powerful and distinctive: camphorous, slightly sweet-bitter, quite unlike any other British hedgerow plant. Flowers are small (5–10mm), bright golden-yellow, with no ray petals — like flat yellow buttons clustered in flat-topped corymbs at the tops of the stems. Flowers July to October. Found on roadsides, riverbanks, field margins, and waste ground throughout England and Wales.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Mugwort (*Artemisia vulgaris*) grows in similar habitats and is also aromatic. Its leaves are more deeply pinnate with a distinctive silvery-white underside. Its flowers are small and brownish, not the distinctive yellow buttons of tansy. Mugwort is a usable culinary herb in small quantities; tansy is not.

Ragwort (*Jacobaea vulgaris*) has yellow flowers on similar roadside habitats but its flowers have clear ray petals (daisy-like). Ragwort is toxic.

No other common British plant has tansy's combination of yellow button flowers (no ray petals), the strongly camphorous aromatic smell, and the pinnately lobed leaves.

Tansy contains thujone. Do not eat in volume. Do not use in pregnancy. Do not give to children.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Roadsides, riverbanks, field edges, waste ground, and disturbed soil throughout Britain and Ireland. Common and easily overlooked as a general roadside herb until its flowers open in late summer.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers July to October. The plant is most obvious and most reliably identified when in flower. Do not pick before flowering without a confirmed identification of the whole plant.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Historical culinary use was as a bitter flavouring in small quantities: a single small leaf rubbed around the inside of a pan before frying omelette, or two to three young leaves added to a stuffing mixture for a gamey meat dish. This is the maximum sensible use: flavouring, not eating. Do not use fresh tansy in any preparation where a quantity will be consumed. Do not use the dried plant. Expert guidance is required before any culinary use.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Tansy is not protected. It is the toxicity, not any legal restriction, that governs its use.

## 219. Tufted Vetch

**Species Name:** Tufted Vetch
**Latin Name:** Vicia cracca
**Type:** Greens
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** tufted-vetch
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
A vigorous, climbing plant of hedgerows and grassy areas, with long racemes of blue-violet flowers and many-paired pinnate leaves ending in branching tendrils. The young shoot tips are edible raw, with a mild pea-like flavour. One of Britain's most attractive summer hedgerow plants.

---

**Full Description:**
Tufted vetch is common throughout Britain and easy to find once you know it: the clambering stems scrambling through hedgerows and tall grassland, the neat pinnate leaves with their branching tendrils hooking the plant to any available support, and from June onwards the one-sided racemes of bright blue-violet flowers, ten to thirty of them per spike, all facing the same direction.

The plant belongs to the same family as peas, beans, and lentils, and the young growing tips have the mild, fresh pea-like flavour that family membership predicts. Eaten raw as a trail snack or added to a summer salad, the shoot tips are pleasant in a mild, uncomplicated way. They are not a major culinary find, but they are good, edible, and free.

Like common vetch, the seeds should be cooked rather than eaten raw in large quantities, though small amounts of raw seeds present minimal risk.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A climbing perennial, 60–150cm, scrambling through supporting vegetation. Leaves are pinnate with six to twelve pairs of narrow, linear leaflets (8–20mm long), the tip of each leaf ending in a branching tendril. Stipules at the base of the leaf stalk are narrow and unlobed. Flowers are 10–12mm, blue-violet, in one-sided racemes of 10–40 flowers on a long stalk from the leaf axils. Pods are 10–25mm, smooth, flat, turning black when ripe. The large, many-flowered, one-sided racemes of blue-violet flowers in summer are the most distinctive feature.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Common vetch (*Vicia sativa*) is shorter with only one to two flowers per leaf axil rather than a long raceme. Edible.

Bush vetch (*Vicia sepium*) has dull purple flowers in clusters of two to six. Edible.

Wood vetch (*Vicia sylvatica*) has white flowers with purple veins, growing in woodland. Edible.

Meadow vetchling (*Lathyrus pratensis*) has yellow flowers and only two leaflets per leaf. Edible.

No toxic plant closely resembles tufted vetch in its hedgerow climbing habit with many-flowered blue-violet racemes.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Hedgerows, rough grassland, scrub edges, roadside verges, and meadow margins throughout Britain and Ireland. Common and widespread on neutral to calcareous soils. One of the most frequent climbing plants of the summer hedgerow.

---

**In Season:**
Growing tips from May into August. Flowers June to August, at which point the growing tips remain pickable but the plant's main growth phase has peaked. Flowers in salads from June onwards.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Pick growing tips 5–10cm long for salads or brief cooking. The flavour is mild and pea-like. Good in a summer salad with sorrel, nasturtium, and a good oil dressing. Flowers scattered over a salad add colour and mild sweetness. Seeds from ripe pods: shell and cook as small dried peas, boiling for 15 to 20 minutes. The flavour is earthy and mild.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Tufted vetch is not protected. Picking for personal use from public land is permitted.

**Short Description:**

**Full Description:**
Water avens is not a common foraging target, and most people will encounter it as a wildflower rather than a food plant. The nodding, dusty-pink flowers on damp stream banks in May are distinctive. What most people do not know is that the root smells of chocolate and cloves.

Found in wet woodland, streamsides, damp meadows, and fen margins.

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Common avens (*Geum urbanum*) has small upright yellow flowers and grows in hedgerows and woodland. The root has a similar eugenol quality, less pronounced. Edible in the same way.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Wet woodland, streamsides, marshy meadows, and fen margins throughout Britain, particularly in the north and upland areas. Less common in dry southern England. Often found with marsh marigold, meadowsweet, and water mint.

---

**In Season:**
Root available year-round but the plant is most easily identified when in flower, May to July. The root's flavour is said to be best in spring. Dig only where the plant is clearly abundant and with landowner permission.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Root infusion: scrape clean a small section of root, place in a mug, and pour over hot milk. Leave for five minutes, strain, and sweeten lightly with honey. The result is a warm drink with a faint chocolate-clove quality. Good, though subtle. Root-infused spirits: pack a handful of scraped root pieces into a jar and cover with gin or vodka. Leave for 48 hours, then strain. Use in a cocktail or as a flavouring base. Do not dig the root in nature reserves or SSSIs.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Water avens is not protected nationally. It grows in habitats that are frequently designated — check before digging. Uprooting requires landowner permission.

## 221. Water Pepper

**Species Name:** Water Pepper
**Latin Name:** Persicaria hydropiper
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** water-pepper
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A mid-sized annual of ditches, pond margins, and wet ground with small pinkish flowers and narrow lance-shaped leaves. The leaves have a fierce, burning peppery heat and were used as a pepper substitute and condiment. Use sparingly. Causes skin irritation on prolonged contact.

---

**Full Description:**
Water pepper is named accurately. The heat from a single leaf, chewed, is immediate and fierce: a burning, persistent peppery sensation that takes a while to subside. It was used historically as a pepper substitute across Northern Europe and Asia, where it grows as a weed of wet ground. It is still used in Japanese cooking as a spicy garnish (*tade*), particularly with freshwater fish.

The plant itself is entirely unremarkable in appearance: a slender, branching annual reaching 25–60cm on the margins of ditches and ponds. The tell is in the taste. Nip off a small leaf, chew it, and within a few seconds the heat is unmistakeable.

This is a plant to use as a condiment in small quantities, not as a vegetable. A few leaves ground with salt and vinegar make a genuine peppery condiment for fish. The sap causes skin irritation on prolonged contact, so handle with reasonable care when processing larger quantities.

---

**Identification Notes:**
An annual, 25–75cm, branching from the base, often reddish-tinged. Stem has swollen nodes with a papery sheath (ochrea) at each node — this sheath is the typical feature of the dock-and-knotweed family. Leaves are 3–8cm, lance-shaped, narrowing at both ends, hairless or nearly so, often dotted with tiny yellow-orange glands (visible with a hand lens). Flowers are small (3–4mm), pink-green, in slender, drooping, slightly beaded spikes, July to October. The stem ochrea is fringed with short bristles at the top. The fierce peppery taste on chewing any part is the diagnostic test.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Tasteless Water-pepper (*Persicaria mitis*) grows in identical habitats and looks the same but has no peppery taste. Edible in the same way, but used less because the flavour is absent.

Redshank (*Persicaria maculosa*) is a common weed with a dark central blotch on the leaf, no peppery taste, and erect (not drooping) flower spikes. Edible.

Amphibious bistort (*Persicaria amphibia*) has broader leaves and pink flower spikes above water. Edible.

No toxic plant closely resembles water pepper in its wet habitat. The peppery taste test is definitive.

The sap contains compounds (polygodial and warburganal) that cause contact dermatitis on prolonged skin contact. Do not pack raw leaves tightly against bare skin for extended periods.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Ditches, pond margins, streambanks, wet meadows, and shallow water on muddy or sandy substrates. Common throughout England and Wales, less so in Scotland. Often found in quantity in drainage ditches alongside water mint and common reed.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from July through the autumn. Flowers July to October. Best in July and August before the plant flowers and the leaves begin to toughen.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Water pepper condiment: finely chop fresh leaves with salt, a little sugar, and rice wine vinegar or white wine vinegar. The resulting paste is fiercely hot and genuinely good with grilled fish, particularly freshwater species. Use a very small amount. In Japan, young stems and leaves are used as a garnish for sashimi and ayu (sweetfish). A few finely shredded leaves over a cold seafood dish add an unexpected heat. Do not eat in volume.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Water pepper is not protected. Picking for personal use is permitted from public land.

---

**Short Description:**
A tall, robust umbelliferous plant of damp woodland, streamsides, and wet meadows, with large white flower heads and distinctively purple-tinged, inflated stem sheaths. The young stems can be candied or cooked. The lookalikes in its family include two of Britain's most toxic plants.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild angelica is an imposing plant. It reaches 1–2m and more, the stout hollow stems sometimes purple-flushed, the large bipinnate leaves with their distinctive inflated sheaths, and the flat white flower heads up to 20cm across. On a damp woodland path in July it stops you.

The plant has culinary value: the young stems can be candied (the same way as cultivated angelica), used to aromatise spirits, or cooked as a vegetable. The stems have a sweet, aromatic, slightly musky flavour. The stems of the garden angelica used in confectionery (*Angelica archangelica*) are essentially the same preparation, and the wild British species can be used in the same way.

The lookalike situation requires total clarity. Wild angelica is a large umbelliferous plant in a family that contains Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) and Hemlock Water Dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*). Both are deadly. Both can reach similar heights in similar habitats. The identification of wild angelica must be confirmed on multiple features before any part of the plant is used.

This is an Expert-rated species. Do not use without a confirmed, multi-feature identification.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Stems are hollow, stout, often purple-tinged, with fine white hairs. The distinctive feature is the leaf sheath: where each leaf joins the stem, the base of the leaf stalk is inflated into a conspicuous pale purple to green sheath or pouch, which clasps the stem. This inflated sheath is the most reliable identifier. Leaves are large (40–60cm), bipinnate, with broad, oval to lance-shaped, toothed leaflets (4–8cm). Flowers are white, in large compound umbels (15–25cm across), July to September. The plant smells pleasantly aromatic when any part is crushed — not musty or unpleasant.

The inflated, clasping stem sheath is the feature that most reliably distinguishes wild angelica from its toxic lookalikes. Check it on every plant before picking.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) grows in similar damp, disturbed habitats and can reach similar heights. Hemlock stems have distinctive purple-red blotching (irregular spots and streaks), not the solid purple flush of angelica. Hemlock smells musty and unpleasant; wild angelica smells pleasantly aromatic. Hemlock stems do not have the inflated leaf sheaths. Hemlock leaves are more finely cut, similar to the carrot family broadly. All parts of hemlock are deadly toxic.

Hemlock Water Dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*) grows in or beside water. Wild angelica grows in damp ground but not typically in standing or running water. Hemlock Water Dropwort has smaller, more finely cut leaves and white fleshy parsnip-like roots that are the most toxic part of the plant. Multiple UK deaths are attributed to this species.

Giant hogweed (*Heracleum mantegazzianum*): Wild angelica does not have the massively enlarged size (3–5m), the very broad white-haired stems, or the huge leaf size of giant hogweed. If uncertain: do not touch. Giant hogweed sap causes severe chemical burns.

Check the inflated stem sheath. Check the pleasant aromatic smell. Check the habitat (damp woodland or meadow, not beside running water). Check the stems for purple blotching (any blotching = stop, do not pick).

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Damp woodland, streamside scrub, wet meadows, fen margins, and damp roadsides. Common throughout Britain and Ireland. Particularly abundant in northern England, Scotland, Wales, and Ireland. Less common in dry southern chalk areas.

---

**In Season:**
Young stems in June, before the plant flowers, when the stems are at their most tender and aromatic. Flowers July to September — the plant is most identifiable when in flower, so use this as the time to locate and confirm the species, then return the following year for the young stems.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Candied angelica stems: cut young stems into 5cm pieces, blanch in boiling water for one minute, peel the outer fibres, then simmer in heavy sugar syrup (1:1 sugar to water) for 20 minutes. Remove, dry on a rack, and coat in caster sugar. The result is intensely sweet and aromatic. Good as a cake decoration or in a fruit compote. Angelica-infused spirits: pack cleaned young stem pieces into gin or vodka for 24 hours. The flavour is sweet-musky and distinctive. Used in making Chartreuse, Bénédictine, and other herbal liqueurs commercially.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Wild angelica is not protected. It grows in habitats that are frequently SSSIs. Pick sparingly.

## 223. Wild Basil

**Species Name:** Wild Basil
**Latin Name:** Clinopodium vulgare
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** wild-basil
**Seasons:** Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A small, softly hairy perennial of chalk and limestone grassland and scrub edges, with whorls of pink-purple flowers from July to September. The leaves have a mild, pleasant aromatic flavour — milder than marjoram, less mint-like than thyme. Good as a fresh herb and for drying.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild basil is not closely related to the cultivated sweet basil (*Ocimum basilicum*) of the kitchen. The name reflects a superficial similarity in habit rather than botanical relationship. What wild basil is, is a pleasant, mild aromatic herb of chalk scrub edges and limestone grassland, with a flavour that is gently warm and herby without the assertive quality of marjoram or the sharpness of thyme.

The leaves are usable fresh in cooking and in salads where a mild herbal note is welcome. They dry reasonably well, though the flavour is lighter than most aromatic herbs and does not require careful preservation to be useful.

It is a plant worth knowing in chalk country, where it grows alongside marjoram, wild thyme, and common knapweed on the same south-facing slopes. On a warm August afternoon on a chalk hillside, it adds to the general aromatic character of the vegetation.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A bushy, softly hairy perennial, 20–60cm. Stem is square in cross-section. Leaves are 1–4cm, broadly oval, slightly toothed, softly hairy on both surfaces, paired, with a faint aromatic smell when crushed — warm and herby, but mild. Flowers are 12–20mm, pink-purple to rose-pink, tubular with two lips, in dense rounded whorls at the leaf axils, July to September. The flower whorls sit in a cluster of narrow, bristle-like bracts. The whole plant is noticeably hairy, more so than marjoram.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Wild marjoram (*Origanum vulgare*) grows in similar habitats and is more aromatic, with smaller flowers (3–6mm) in tighter terminal clusters rather than large-flowered whorls. Edible.

Wild thyme (*Thymus polytrichus*) is lower-growing, more compact, with smaller leaves. Edible.

All labiate family members in British chalk and limestone habitats are edible. No toxic confusion exists in this plant community.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Chalk and limestone grassland, scrub edges, roadside banks on calcareous soils, and old quarry edges. Most common in southern England on the chalk and limestone belts. Less common in Wales and Scotland. Often grows alongside wild marjoram and wild thyme on the same sunny south-facing slopes.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from late May through the growing season. Flowers July to September. Best quality leaves before and during early flowering.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use fresh in salads, in herb oils, or added to a slow-cooked dish. The mild flavour works well blended with the more assertive marjoram or thyme. Good in a cream sauce for fish or chicken where a gentle herbal note is wanted. Dry for winter use by stripping the leaves from harvested stems and drying in a warm, well-ventilated spot.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Wild basil is not protected. Picking from chalk grassland for personal use is permitted. Many chalk grassland sites are SSSIs — pick lightly.

## 224. Wild Chamomile

**Species Name:** Wild Chamomile
**Latin Name:** Matricaria chamomilla
**Type:** Flowers
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** wild-chamomile
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
Scented mayweed, also known as wild chamomile, grows on arable margins and disturbed ground throughout Britain. The flower heads, which smell distinctly of chamomile when crushed, make an excellent herbal tea. Easy to identify by scent and an accessible annual find.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild chamomile is the same species as the chamomile tea you can buy in any supermarket, or close enough in flavour and chemistry to be interchangeable. It grows on disturbed ground, field margins, and bare arable soil, sometimes in considerable quantity, and its flower heads make exactly the same mild, apple-honey infusion.

The identification is made entirely by scent. Crush a flower head and the chamomile smell is immediate, warm, and distinctive. No other common British flower produces this combination of daisy-like appearance and chamomile scent.

Scentless mayweed (*Tripleurospermum inodorum*) grows in identical habitats, looks the same, and is not toxic but has no smell and is not used medicinally or culinarily. Check by smell every time.

---

**Identification Notes:**
An annual, 15–60cm. Leaves are finely divided, feathery, two to three times pinnate with narrow linear segments, bright green. Flowers are 12–25mm across, with white ray petals (6–18, sometimes drooping backwards when old) and a domed yellow central disc. The disc is hollow when the flower head is pressed from below (receptacle is hollow) — this distinguishes it from other mayweeds. Flowers June to August. The chamomile smell from crushed flowers is the definitive identifier.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Scentless mayweed (*Tripleurospermum inodorum*) is identical in appearance but has no smell and a solid (not hollow) receptacle. Not toxic; not useful for tea.

Corn chamomile (*Anthemis arvensis*) is similar with a slight chamomile scent; receptacle has pointed scales. Edible.

Stinking chamomile (*Anthemis cotula*) has an unpleasant, slightly different smell and a scaly receptacle. Causes contact dermatitis. Avoid.

Smell is definitive. If the crushed flower head smells of chamomile: it is chamomile or a close edible relative. If it smells unpleasant: do not pick.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Arable margins, disturbed soil, roadsides, waste ground, and sandy or chalky soils. Common throughout England, Wales, and parts of southern Scotland. Often found alongside black medick, field pansy, and other arable weed species. More common in eastern and southern England on light soils.

---

**In Season:**
Flowers June to August. Pick flower heads when fully open and the central yellow disc is well formed. Dry for later use by spreading on a clean cloth in a warm, well-ventilated spot for two to three days.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Chamomile tea: steep a heaped teaspoon of fresh or dried flower heads per cup in just-boiled water for five minutes. Strain and drink. Sweeten lightly with honey if preferred. The flavour is mild, slightly apple-honey, and warming. Good in the evening. Dried flowers keep well in a sealed jar for up to a year. Fresh flowers can be added to a summer salad as garnish.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Wild chamomile is not protected. Picking for personal use from public land and arable margins is permitted.

---

## 225. Wild Fennel

**Species Name:** Wild Fennel
**Latin Name:** Foeniculum vulgare
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** wild-fennel
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Wild fennel grows on coastal cliffs, road verges, and disturbed ground, with feathery thread-like leaves and flat yellow flower heads. The anise flavour is intense in every part. The leaves, seeds, and pollen are all edible. The lookalike warning for Hemlock is serious and must be read.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild fennel grows large and conspicuous: the tall, glaucous-blue stems, the thread-fine feathery leaves, and the flat-topped yellow flower heads are a feature of coastal cliffs, roadside cuttings, and disturbed ground around southern Britain from summer through autumn.

The flavour is unmistakeably anise. Every part of the plant carries it — the leaves, the pollen, the seeds, the stem — and the smell when any part is crushed is the definitive identifier. In culinary terms it is essentially the same as the garden fennel bulb variety, minus the swollen stem base. The leaves, used fresh or dried, flavour fish and salads. The pollen, collected from the flower heads in July, is one of the more extraordinary flavourings available from the British countryside: intensely sweet and anise-scented, it transforms a simple fish dish.

This is also a family with serious dangers. The smell test is the most important thing in the Lookalikes section. Never pick a plant that smells of fennel from near water unless the identification is confirmed on multiple features.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A tall, branching perennial, 1–2m. Stems are hollow, glaucous blue-green, with a slight powdery bloom. Leaves are finely divided into long, thread-like segments, 3–4mm across. The whole plant smells strongly of anise when any part is crushed. Flowers are yellow, in large flat-topped compound umbels 5–10cm across, July to September. Seeds are oval-cylindrical, 4–6mm, with prominent ribs. The combination of thread-like leaves, glaucous stems, and overpowering anise scent makes wild fennel distinctive.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) is the critical lookalike. Both are tall umbellifers with divided leaves and flat-topped white or yellow flower heads, found on roadside and disturbed ground. The distinction is the smell: fennel smells powerfully and unambiguously of anise. Hemlock smells musty and unpleasant. Crush a leaf and smell it. If there is any anise smell: likely fennel. If it smells musty or unpleasant or of nothing: stop. Hemlock stems have distinctive purple-red blotching. Wild fennel stems are plain glaucous-green with no blotching. Hemlock leaves are broader in segment, with a more leaf-like texture; fennel leaves are thread-like filaments.

Hemlock Water Dropwort (*Oenanthe crocata*): wild fennel grows in dry habitats — coastal cliffs, roadsides, dry chalk slopes. Hemlock Water Dropwort grows in or beside water. Habitat distinction is critical. If you are near a stream or ditch and find a tall umbellifer: assume Hemlock Water Dropwort until proven otherwise.

The smell test is the most important single check. An anise smell does not confirm fennel with certainty, but the absence of an anise smell should stop you picking immediately.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Coastal cliffs, roadside cuttings, chalky slopes, waste ground, and disturbed soil in southern England. Most common on southern coastal cliffs from Dorset to Kent and throughout the south-facing chalk and limestone slopes of southern England. Absent from very wet ground and running water habitats.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from June through the growing season. Pollen available from July when the flower heads open. Seeds ripen from August to October. Pick pollen by cutting flower heads over a bag or tray. Seeds when they have dried on the plant but before shedding.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Fresh leaves: chop finely and add to fish dishes, sauces, and salads. Good with salmon, sea bass, and mackerel. Fennel pollen: the most intensely flavoured part of the plant. Sprinkle a pinch over a piece of fish before grilling, or over pasta with olive oil. A very small amount transforms a simple dish. Seeds: use whole or ground in the same way as commercial fennel seeds. Good in bread, sausage-making, and spice mixes. Fennel-infused oil: pack a jar of leaves with a neutral oil, leave for a week, strain, and use over fish or vegetables.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Wild fennel is not protected. Picking for personal use from public verges, coastal paths, and public land is permitted.

---

## 226. Wild Mint

**Species Name:** Wild Mint
**Latin Name:** Mentha arvensis
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** wild-mint
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
A catch-all entry for the mints found growing wild across Britain. The square stem and mint smell are diagnostic. All common British wild mints are edible. Water mint, corn mint, and spearmint are the most frequently encountered species and all are good in the kitchen.

---

**Full Description:**
Mint is one of the easiest plant groups to identify in Britain. The square stem, opposite leaves, and mint smell together form an identification that requires no further confirmation at the genus level. Getting to the precise species matters less for culinary purposes than knowing you have a mint at all.

Water mint (*Mentha aquatica*) is the most common truly wild British mint, found on streambanks and wet ground. It has a slightly coarse, almost musty mint character compared to spearmint or corn mint, but it is usable in all the same ways.

Corn mint (*Mentha arvensis*) grows on arable land and damp disturbed ground, with a clean, slightly menthol-forward character.

Spearmint (*Mentha spicata*) is a garden escape naturalised throughout Britain, with the classic sweet mint flavour.

Peppermint (*Mentha × piperita*) is a hybrid escape, stronger and more menthol-heavy, also naturalised.

All are edible. All are useful in the kitchen. The note on pennyroyal below is the one precaution in this genus.

---

**Identification Notes:**
Square stem in cross-section. Opposite leaves, aromatic when crushed with a clear mint smell. Flowers in whorls at the leaf axils (corn mint and water mint) or in a terminal spike (spearmint). All mints are found in damp or disturbed habitats. The mint smell itself is the primary identifier — it is unmistakeable and immediately apparent.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Pennyroyal (*Mentha pulegium*) is a small, creeping mint (5–20cm) of damp grassland and heath, with a very strong, slightly different pennyroyal-menthol smell. It contains pulegone, which is hepatotoxic in large doses and acts as an abortifacient. Do not use in pregnancy and do not consume in large amounts. In culinary quantities (a few leaves in a dish) it is not dangerous, but it is much stronger than other mints and should be used more sparingly than spearmint or corn mint.

White dead-nettle (*Lamium album*) has a similar square stem and opposite leaves but does not smell of mint. Not dangerous; edible.

The single reliable check: if it smells of mint, it is a mint. Confirm with square stem. All British mints except pennyroyal are usable freely.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Water mint: streambanks, pond margins, marsh, and wet meadows throughout Britain.
Corn mint: arable margins, damp cultivated ground, and disturbed grassland.
Spearmint: damp hedgerow bases, ditches, and waste ground near habitation.
Peppermint: similar to spearmint.

---

**In Season:**
All species from June through the growing season. Best before and during early flowering.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
See Spearmint and Corn Mint entries for detailed culinary guidance. The preparation for all common wild mints is identical: fresh in sauces, teas, drinks, and cooking; dried for winter use.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Wild mints are not protected. Picking for personal use is permitted from public land.

---

## 227. Wild Sage

**Species Name:** Wild Sage
**Latin Name:** Salvia verbenaca
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Slug:** wild-sage
**Seasons:** May, Jun, Jul, Aug

---

**Short Description:**
Wild clary, commonly called wild sage, grows on dry chalk grassland, coastal turf, and sandy ground. The leaves have a mild, aromatic, sage-like smell and flavour, usable as a cooking herb. Less intensely flavoured than cultivated sage but genuinely useful on a chalk hillside walk.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild clary (*Salvia verbenaca*) is the closest thing to a native wild sage in Britain. It is not quite the same as the large-leaved, powerfully aromatic cultivated sage (*Salvia officinalis*), which is a Mediterranean plant not native to Britain. But it has a genuine if milder sage-like quality that makes it worth knowing.

The plant grows on dry, south-facing chalk and limestone turf, on coastal cliffs and sandy ground, and occasionally in old churchyards and roadside banks. It is low-growing and often overlooked. In flower from May to September, the small blue-violet flowers in whorls on upright stems are distinctive.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A perennial, 20–60cm. Basal leaves are oval to oblong, 4–10cm, with a crinkled surface, wavy margins, and a rounded base, softly hairy and sage-scented when crushed. Stem leaves are smaller. Flowers are small (5–8mm), blue-violet, in whorls of two to six on upright, branching stems. The calyx (outer whorl below the flower) has a distinctive glandular, sticky surface. Root leaves are persistently rosulate. The sage smell when leaves are crushed confirms identification.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
Meadow clary (*Salvia pratensis*) is rare but similar, with larger, more showy flowers. Edible.

Wild basil (*Clinopodium vulgare*) has similar flowers and habitat but lacks the sage smell. Edible.

No toxic plant closely resembles wild clary in its dry chalk grassland setting with sage-scented leaves.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Dry chalk and limestone grassland, coastal cliffs, sandy turf, and old churchyards. Most common in southern and eastern England. Local and declining in some areas — pick sparingly.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves from May through summer. Flowers May to September. Best leaf quality in May and June before the plant flowers.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as a mild sage substitute: in stuffings, with pork or chicken, in butter sauces, or dried and crumbled into bread dough. The flavour is lighter than cultivated sage and requires slightly more quantity to achieve the same effect. A few fresh leaves in a pan with butter and chicken makes a simple and good pan sauce.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Wild clary is not nationally protected. Many of its chalk grassland habitats are SSSIs — pick very sparingly and leave the rosettes intact.

## 228. Wild Thyme

**Species Name:** Wild Thyme
**Latin Name:** Thymus polytrichus
**Type:** Herbs
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Slug:** wild-thyme
**Seasons:** Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep

---

**Short Description:**
Wild thyme forms mats of tiny aromatic leaves on chalk downland, moorland, and coastal turf throughout Britain. The flavour is thyme — clean, warm, herbal — and it is used exactly as you would use cultivated thyme. The plant's low-growing habit and brilliant pink-purple flowers in summer make it easy to spot.

---

**Full Description:**
Wild thyme is one of the few wild plants that delivers exactly the same flavour as its cultivated counterpart without any qualification. The thyme smell from a crushed leaf is the same thyme smell from the plant in a pot on a kitchen windowsill, though a little softer and greener in the wild plant.

It grows in dense mats, the woody stems hugging the ground across chalk downland, moorland, and coastal cliff turf. In June and July the pink-purple flower heads appear above the mat, and on a warm summer afternoon the combined aromatic effect of a stretch of chalk grassland with thyme, marjoram, and basil all in bloom is considerable.

Pick a few sprigs, use them as you would use cultivated thyme, and leave the mat intact. The plant is low-growing and slow to recover from heavy picking.

---

**Identification Notes:**
A low-growing, mat-forming perennial, 5–10cm. Stems are woody, creeping, and spreading, with upright flowering shoots. Leaves are tiny (3–8mm), oval, flat, paired, with slightly inrolled margins and a hairy underside. The smell when crushed is classic thyme. Flowers are small (3–5mm), pink to purple, in compact rounded clusters at the tips of upright stems, June to September. Bracts beneath the flowers are often purple-tinged. The low, mat-forming habit, tiny thyme-scented leaves, and pink flower clusters in short grassland are distinctive.

Breckland thyme (*Thymus serpyllum*) is similar, confined to Breckland in East Anglia, and edible.

Large thyme (*Thymus pulegioides*) is slightly larger and more strongly scented; also edible.

---

**Lookalikes & Dangers:**
No toxic plant closely resembles wild thyme in its low-growing mat habit on chalk or moorland turf.

Pennyroyal (*Mentha pulegium*), also low-growing on damp grassland, has larger leaves and a pennyroyal-mint smell rather than thyme. See Wild Mint entry for the note on pennyroyal and pregnancy.

The thyme smell settles identification in almost all cases.

If in doubt, leave it out.

---

**Habitat:**
Chalk and limestone downland, moorland and heathland on acid soils, coastal cliff turf, old sandy grassland, and rocky upland ledges. Found throughout Britain from sea level to mountain summits. Most abundant on south-facing chalk in southern England and on limestone grassland throughout Wales and northern England.

---

**In Season:**
Leaves available throughout the year from the mat. Best flavour in June to August when the plant is in active growth and the aromatic oils are at their peak. Pick fresh sprigs in summer for immediate use or for drying. Dry by tying small bundles and hanging in a warm place for a week — the leaves strip easily from dry stems.

---

**Culinary Uses:**
Use as cultivated thyme in all applications: in stocks, sauces, roasted vegetables, grilled meat, and slow-cooked dishes. The flavour holds well through extended cooking. Wild thyme salt: strip dried leaves from the stems, blend with sea salt in a 1:4 ratio (thyme to salt), and store in a sealed jar. Good as a finishing salt for meat and vegetables. Wild thyme oil: steep fresh sprigs in good olive oil for a week. The flavour infuses well. A simple and useful kitchen staple from a summer afternoon's picking.

---

**Legal Notes:**
Wild thyme is not protected. Picking for personal use from chalk downland, moorland, and coastal paths is permitted. On protected downland SSSIs, pick lightly and avoid disturbing the surrounding vegetation.

---
