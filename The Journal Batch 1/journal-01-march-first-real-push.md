# March: The First Real Push of Spring

*In Season / March 2026*

---

February gives you almost nothing. A few persistent leaves of sorrel if you know where to look. Velvet shank on dead wood if the winter has been wet. But the ground is mostly bare, the hedgerows stripped, and the idea of picking anything feels optimistic at best.

March changes that. Not all at once, and not everywhere equally, but by the middle of the month the shift is unmistakable. Things are growing. The year has started.

## Wild garlic

The first sign is the smell. You walk into a damp stretch of woodland and catch it before you see anything. The leaves are still small, tight scrolls pushing through leaf litter, often no more than 10–15cm tall. They double in size over the next three weeks.

Pick the leaves now and they are at their most tender. Mild, clean, without the sharpness that develops closer to flowering. By April the flavour intensifies. By May, once the white flowers open, the leaves toughen and the taste coarsens. March is the beginning of a roughly six-week window, and the early weeks are the best of it.

Wild garlic (*Allium ursinum*) grows in damp, shaded woodland across most of Britain. In the south it starts in early March. In Scotland and the north, mid to late March is more typical. You will not struggle to find it. The colonies are vast and the plant is abundant where it grows.

One note: the leaves resemble lily of the valley (*Convallaria majalis*) before either plant flowers. Crush a leaf. Wild garlic smells unmistakably of garlic. Lily of the valley does not. If there is no smell, do not pick it. Lily of the valley is toxic and causes cardiac problems. If in doubt, leave it out.

## Nettles

Common nettles (*Urtica dioica*) are everywhere by mid-March, and the youngest growth is exactly what you want. Shoots up to about 15cm, the top four to six leaves, picked with gloves and blanched for thirty seconds. That is the entire method.

Nettles do not stay good for long. Once they grow past about 30cm the leaves become coarse, and after flowering in summer they develop cystoliths that can irritate the kidneys. The window for good eating is spring and early spring only. March and April are the months.

You already know where nettles grow. Every hedgerow base, field margin, garden edge, churchyard, and patch of waste ground in the country has them. This is the most accessible species in British foraging, free, absurdly common, and good to eat.

## Alexanders

If you live near the coast, or in the south of England, Alexanders (*Smyrnium olusatrum*) are at their peak now. The stems are thick and upright, the leaves glossy and divided into groups of three. The whole plant smells somewhere between celery and parsley, with a resinous, myrrh-like note underneath.

Alexanders were a kitchen staple before celery replaced them. They grow on roadsides, hedgerows, and waste ground within a few miles of the coast, and increasingly inland in the south. The stems, leaves, and flower buds are all edible. Peel the stems and braise them. Use the leaves raw in small quantities, or wilt them like spinach.

The plant bolts and flowers in April and May, and after that the leaves become bitter and the stems woody. March is the last full month of good picking.

## Three-cornered leek

Three-cornered leek (*Allium triquetrum*) is in full flow in the south and southwest by March. The drooping white flowers and strong garlic-onion scent make it easy to identify. The stems have a distinctive triangular cross-section, which gives the plant its name.

Originally from the Mediterranean, it now covers hedgerows, verges, and shaded banks in Cornwall, Devon, south Wales, and much of southern England. It grows in dense stands and spreads aggressively. You will not damage the population by picking it. In many areas it is considered invasive.

The leaves, stems, and flowers are all good to eat. The flavour sits between garlic and spring onion. Use it raw in salads, or wilt the leaves briefly in butter.

## Cleavers

Cleavers (*Galium aparine*), the sticky, scrambling plant that clings to your clothes, starts appearing in hedgerows and along fences in March. Most people know it as a weed. Few know the young tips are edible.

Pick the top 10cm of the growing shoots while they are still soft and pale green. Once the stems toughen and the plant starts setting its round, burr-like seeds, the eating window has closed. The flavour is mild, green, vaguely pea-like. Not spectacular, but useful chopped into a salad or added to a soup at the last moment.

## What else is stirring

Sorrel (*Rumex acetosa*) has been around since late winter but the new spring leaves are sharper and brighter now. Hairy bittercress (*Cardamine hirsuta*) is everywhere, tiny and peppery, easy to miss and easy to pick in quantity from garden beds and paths. Dandelion leaves are mild and worth eating before the plant flowers and the bitterness increases.

Towards the end of March, depending on where you are, you may start to see the first elderflower buds forming, though they will not open until May. Hawthorn leaf buds are beginning to break. The hedgerow is waking up.

## The month ahead

March is the month when the year starts making sense again. The ground is no longer empty. There are things to find, things to pick, things to cook. April will bring more, and by May the list will be long. But this is where it begins, and the species available now, wild garlic and nettles above all, are as good as anything the year produces.

Go outside. Pay attention. The season has started.
