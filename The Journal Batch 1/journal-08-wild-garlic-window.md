# The Wild Garlic Window

*Wild Table / March 2026*

---

Wild garlic has a deadline. The leaves appear in March. The flowers open in late April or May. After flowering, the leaves yellow, the flavour coarsens, and the plant dies back to its bulb. The whole above-ground life of wild garlic (*Allium ursinum*) lasts about eight weeks. The period when the leaves are genuinely worth picking is shorter than that. Six weeks, roughly. Sometimes less.

This is the defining fact about cooking with wild garlic, and it changes the way you think about it.

## The timeline

In the south of England, the first leaves appear in late February or early March. They are small, tightly rolled, pale green. At this stage they are tender and mild. The garlic flavour is present but gentle. These earliest leaves are the finest, and there are very few of them.

By mid-March the leaves are up properly. Fifteen to twenty centimetres tall, broad, bright green. The flavour has sharpened. This is peak season. The leaves are large enough to pick in quantity and the taste has its full character without being harsh.

In April the flower stems push up. The buds are tight, green, and worth eating in their own right. Chopped and fried in oil, or pickled, they have a concentrated garlic punch. Once the buds open into white, star-shaped flowers, the leaves start to toughen. The flavour becomes sharper and slightly acrid. You can still use them, but the quality is declining.

By mid to late May, in most of Britain, it is over. The leaves have yellowed. The stems have gone limp. The woodland floor that was a carpet of green a month ago is empty. The garlic season is finished, and it will not come back until next March.

## What scarcity does

Supermarket garlic is available every day of the year. It sits in a net bag on a shelf and waits. There is no urgency to it. You buy it when you need it. You do not think about its season because it does not have one, or rather, its season has been engineered away by global supply chains and cold storage.

Wild garlic does not work like this. You have six weeks. If you miss them, you wait a year. If you pick too early, the leaves are small and the yield is thin. If you leave it too late, the flowers have opened and the flavour has turned.

This deadline changes the way you cook. You do not plan wild garlic meals a month in advance. You plan them a week in advance, based on what the leaves look like right now. You make the pesto this weekend because the leaves will not be this good next weekend. You use it generously because the supply is temporary.

The scarcity is part of the pleasure. Eating wild garlic in March means something precisely because you cannot eat it in September. It marks a point in the year. It connects you to a specific set of weeks. This is what seasonal eating actually means, not the vague idea of eating strawberries in summer, but the hard fact of a plant that exists for six weeks and then is gone.

## In the kitchen

The best use of wild garlic leaves is the simplest. Tear them into a salad. The raw leaf has a clean garlic hit that cooked garlic cannot replicate. It is fresh and sharp without being aggressive.

Pesto is the obvious preservation method. Leaves, oil, hard cheese, a handful of nuts, salt, blended. It freezes well. A batch made in late March will still be good in August. This is how you extend the window, by converting fresh leaves into something stable.

Wild garlic butter is even simpler. Chop the leaves finely, work them into softened butter with salt, roll in cling film, freeze. You will use this on steaks, on bread, on baked potatoes in July, and it will taste like spring in a way that nothing else does.

The leaves wilt quickly in heat. Add them to pasta in the last thirty seconds. Stir them through risotto off the heat. Lay them over fish before roasting. They do not want long cooking. The flavour dulls and the colour turns from vivid green to a murky olive.

The flower buds, picked before they open, deserve more attention than they usually get. Pickled in cider vinegar they become something like a wild caper. Tight, punchy, acidic. A jar of pickled wild garlic buds in the cupboard is a useful thing to have in June when the fresh leaves are long gone.

## The calendar

Late February to mid-March: first leaves. Tender, mild. Pick sparingly.

Mid-March to mid-April: peak. Full-flavoured, abundant. This is the time to pick in quantity, make pesto, make butter, eat it fresh every day.

Late April: flower buds forming. Pick and pickle the buds. Leaves still good but declining.

May: flowers open. Leaves coarsening. Season ending. Last chance for pesto if you have not made it already.

Late May: over.

## The point

Wild garlic is the ingredient that teaches you what a season actually is. Not a loose category on a restaurant menu. A fixed window. A real deadline. The leaves are good and then they are gone, and there is nothing you can do about that except be ready when they arrive.

The window is open. Six weeks. Use it.
