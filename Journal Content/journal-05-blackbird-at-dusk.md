# The Blackbird at Dusk

*The Land / March 2026*

---

Stand outside at six o'clock on an evening in mid-March. Anywhere in Britain. A garden. A park. The edge of a car park. A residential street with a few trees. Stand still for two minutes.

You will hear a blackbird.

## The song

The blackbird's song is not like other birdsong. It is not repetitive in the way a great tit's is, the same two-note phrase hammered out fifty times. It is not rapid and complex in the way a wren's is, an overwhelming blast of noise from a bird you can barely see. The blackbird sings in phrases. Each phrase is different. Each one is clear, melodic, and delivered at a pace you can follow. There are pauses between them, as though the bird is thinking about what comes next.

It sings from a high, exposed perch. The peak of a roof. The top of a bare tree. A TV aerial. The bird is easy to find because it wants to be heard, and it positions itself accordingly.

The song starts in earnest in late February or early March, depending on the weather, and continues through to July. But it is in March and April that it is most noticeable. The evenings are getting longer. Other birds have gone quiet for the day. The blackbird keeps singing into the dusk, often the last voice you hear before dark.

## Why now

The song is territorial. A male blackbird holding a breeding territory sings to tell other males where the boundaries are. He also sings to attract a mate, though by March most pairs are already established.

The reason it starts in late winter and builds through March is photoperiod. The lengthening days trigger hormonal changes that stimulate singing behaviour. It is a response to light, the same light that pushes wild garlic leaves through the woodland floor and breaks open the hawthorn buds in the hedgerow.

Everything in the natural year is connected to the same clock. The blackbird sings because the days are getting longer. The plants grow because the days are getting longer. You notice both because you are outside and paying attention. Which is, in the end, the only thing this site has ever asked you to do.

## The bird

The male blackbird is entirely black with an orange-yellow bill and a narrow yellow ring around the eye. The female is dark brown, often with a slightly mottled breast, and a darker bill. Juveniles are brown and speckled. You already know what a blackbird looks like, even if you have never consciously identified one.

It is the most common breeding bird in Britain. The population is somewhere around five million pairs. It lives in every habitat that has trees, shrubs, or hedgerows, from deep woodland to city centres. It feeds on the ground, turning over leaf litter with quick, deliberate flicks of the bill, looking for earthworms, beetles, and larvae. In autumn it eats berries. You will see it on lawns, in flower beds, under hedges, on paths, and on rooftops.

It is not exotic. It is not rare. Nobody puts it on a list. And it produces a sound that, once you start listening for it on March evenings, becomes the marker by which you measure the turn of the year.

## Listening

There is no technique to this. You do not need binoculars. You do not need an app. You do not need to know the difference between a song thrush and a blackbird, though once you do, you will notice the thrush too. The thrush repeats each phrase two or three times. The blackbird does not.

Go outside at dusk. The song carries. In a quiet street you can hear it from 200 metres. In a garden you will hear multiple birds, each on its own perch, each singing its own sequence, the territories overlapping in sound.

March evenings have a particular quality. The air is cool. The light is going. The day is longer than it was a month ago but still short enough that you notice the difference week by week. The blackbird singing from a rooftop at ten past six, still going at twenty past, quiet by half past. Next week it will still be singing at six thirty-five. The week after, quarter to seven.

This is how spring arrives. Not in a single moment, but in increments you can measure if you stand outside often enough.

## A small thing

This piece is not about foraging. There is nothing to pick here. But The Foragers has always been about paying attention to the outdoors, and the blackbird at dusk in March is one of those things that rewards attention out of all proportion to the effort involved.

Five million pairs. Every garden in Britain. Every evening from now until midsummer. You already know the sound. You just might not have been listening for it.

Tonight, listen.
