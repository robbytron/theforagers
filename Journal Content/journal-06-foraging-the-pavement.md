# Foraging the Pavement

*The Field / March 2026*

---

Most British foragers live in cities. This is not a contradiction. It is just a fact that gets overlooked because the imagery around foraging, the wicker baskets, the woodland paths, the rolling countryside, has nothing to do with how most people actually start.

They start on their doorstep. A patch of nettles behind the bins. Dandelion leaves pushing through a crack in the paving. Three-cornered leek covering a railway embankment. The city is full of edible plants, and March is the month when they become impossible to ignore.

## A walk

I walked a loop of about three miles through a city in the south of England on a Saturday morning. Not a park. Not a nature reserve. Streets, pavements, car parks, back lanes, and the edges of retail units. The kind of ground that most people cross without looking down.

Within the first ten minutes I had passed nettles, dandelions, chickweed, and cleavers. All of them growing in the strip of bare earth between a brick wall and a pavement. The nettles were about 12cm tall, perfect. The chickweed was in flower, small white stars held open in the weak March sun.

Chickweed (*Stellaria media*) grows everywhere in cities. Pavement edges, raised beds, flowerpots, cracks in tarmac, bare soil around street trees. It is small, mild-flavoured, and entirely edible. A good salad leaf with a clean, green taste. Most people pull it up as a weed without knowing they could eat it.

## What grows where

Cities generate heat. Buildings store it. Pavements radiate it. The result is that urban plants are often two or three weeks ahead of the same species in the countryside. Wild garlic in a sheltered urban park may be fully leafed out while the same plant in a woodland five miles away is barely through the soil.

Walls and south-facing edges are the warmest spots. This is where you find the earliest nettles, the first dandelion flowers, the quickest growth. Railway embankments, canal towpaths, and the backs of industrial estates are productive in cities for the same reason waste ground is productive everywhere. Disturbed soil, decent light, minimal competition.

Three-cornered leek (*Allium triquetrum*) has colonised urban and suburban areas across the south of England. In Bristol, parts of London, and much of the southwest, it grows in dense stands along residential streets, on road verges, and along park boundaries. The white flowers and strong garlic-onion smell make it easy to identify. It is abundant, spreading, and good to eat.

Dandelions (*Taraxacum* spp.) grow in every city in Britain. The young leaves, picked before the plant flowers, are mild and slightly bitter, good in a salad or wilted in a pan. Once the flowers open the leaves become more bitter, though still edible. The flowers themselves can be used in cooking. Pick from areas you are confident have not been sprayed.

## The rules

Urban foraging has two additional concerns that do not apply in the countryside.

The first is contamination. Plants growing on road verges absorb heavy metals and particulate pollution from traffic. The closer to a busy road, the higher the contamination. Pick from quiet residential streets, back lanes, parks, and away from main roads. A good rule: if you would not eat a sandwich sitting next to it, do not pick from it.

Dog fouling is the other obvious issue. Avoid picking anything below knee height from ground-level plants in areas with heavy dog traffic. Parks where dogs are exercised regularly, popular walking routes, and pavement edges near residential areas are higher risk. Washing helps but does not eliminate the problem. Use judgment. Pick higher up. Pick from spots with less foot traffic.

The second is legality. Foraging in public parks and council-managed land is generally permitted for personal use under the same common law right that applies elsewhere, but some local authorities have bylaws restricting it. Check before you pick. Private land, including corporate grounds and residential gardens, requires the landowner's permission regardless.

Herbicide spraying is a real concern. Councils spray verges, path edges, and around street furniture. You can often see the results: strips of dead, yellowed vegetation alongside otherwise green growth. Do not pick from any area that shows signs of spraying. If the vegetation looks artificially dead or patchy, move on.

## What you can actually find in March

A realistic urban March foraging list for most of England:

Nettles. Everywhere. The youngest growth is the best. Pick the top four to six leaves of shoots under 15cm. Blanch for thirty seconds.

Dandelion leaves. Best before flowering. Pick from clean ground away from roads and dogs.

Chickweed. Small, common, overlooked. A handful in a salad adds a fresh, mild green note.

Cleavers (*Galium aparine*). The scrambling, sticky plant growing along fences and through hedges. Pick the top 10cm while still soft.

Three-cornered leek (south and southwest mainly). Unmistakable by smell. Leaves, stems, and flowers all edible.

Wild garlic (where urban woodland or shaded parks exist). The same plant as in the countryside, often earlier in the city.

Hairy bittercress (*Cardamine hirsuta*). Tiny, peppery, abundant in bare soil, flower beds, and path edges. Easy to miss, easy to pick in quantity once you spot it.

## The point

Urban foraging does not need justifying as a novelty or a statement. It is foraging. The plants do not know they are growing in a city. They grow where the conditions suit them, and cities offer disturbed ground, warmth, shelter, and neglect in abundance.

The difference is attention. You have walked past these plants every day. They have been there every March, growing in the same cracks, along the same walls, in the same strips of bare earth between the pavement and the fence.

You just were not looking. Start looking.
