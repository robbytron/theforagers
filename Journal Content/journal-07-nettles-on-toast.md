# Nettles on Toast

*Wild Table / March 2026*

---

Nobody is excited about nettles. That is part of the problem. Nettles are the foraging equivalent of a bus. They are everywhere, they are not glamorous, and because they sting, most people's childhood experience of them was negative. The word itself sounds unappealing. Nettle. It does not promise pleasure.

But blanch a handful of young nettles in boiling water for thirty seconds, drain them, press out the liquid, drop them onto hot buttered toast with salt and a scrape of something sharp, lemon juice, a few drops of vinegar, and what you have is better than spinach. It is better because it has more flavour, more texture, and more character. Spinach is mild and obliging. Nettles have an opinion.

## The flavour

Young nettles, the top four to six leaves of a shoot under 15cm, taste green and mineral. There is an iron edge that spinach gestures at but never fully delivers. Underneath that, something earthy and almost savoury, closer to a dark leafy green like cavolo nero than to the watery mildness of baby spinach.

Blanching kills the sting entirely. The formic acid breaks down in seconds. What you are left with is a soft, dense leaf with a slight chew to it. Not slimy. Not collapsing to nothing the way spinach does. Nettles hold their shape. They hold butter. They work on toast in a way that feels substantial.

The flavour intensifies when you cook them in fat. Butter is the obvious choice. A minute in a hot pan after blanching, with butter, a pinch of salt, and you have something that tastes richer and deeper than any cultivated green you could buy.

## The comparison

Spinach has been the default green for so long that it has become a kind of background. It goes in everything. It completes every supermarket salad bag. It wilts to nothing and tastes of very little unless you dress it heavily.

Nettles are not a substitute for spinach. They are what spinach would be if spinach had more to say. The mineral content is higher. The flavour is more assertive. The texture, once blanched, is firmer and more interesting. They pair better with strong flavours: aged cheese, eggs, garlic, anchovy, lemon.

Nettles on toast with a poached egg and a shaving of hard cheese is a meal. Spinach on toast is a snack at best.

## The window

Nettles are only good for eating in spring. March and April are the peak months. Once the plant grows past about 30cm and begins to flower, the leaves become coarse and develop cystoliths, tiny calcium carbonate crystals that can irritate the urinary tract. Nobody wants that.

The window is short. Six to eight weeks of genuinely good eating, starting when the first shoots appear in early March and closing by late April or early May. After that, leave them alone until next year.

This is part of what makes them worth paying attention to. A vegetable you can buy all year round has no urgency. A green that is only good for six weeks, free, growing within walking distance of your front door, and better than the thing it replaces, that has something.

## Where to find them

You know where nettles grow. Every hedgerow. Every field margin. Every patch of waste ground, churchyard, garden edge, and canal bank in Britain. They favour nitrogen-rich soil, which is why they grow so well near habitation, on compost heaps, and in ground that has been fertilised or disturbed.

Pick with gloves. Thick rubber kitchen gloves work better than thin gardening gloves. Pinch the top of the shoot and take the top four to six leaves. Leave the rest. The plant will regrow.

A carrier bag full of nettle tops, picked in fifteen minutes, blanches down to enough for four servings. The conversion rate is similar to spinach. A lot of raw leaf becomes a modest amount of cooked green. Plan accordingly.

## How to cook them

Blanch in boiling water for thirty seconds. No longer. Drain. Squeeze out the water firmly, using the back of a spoon or your hands once they are cool enough. Chop roughly.

From there, the simplest thing is the best thing. Hot pan. Butter. Nettles. Salt. Thirty seconds. Onto toast. A squeeze of lemon if you want brightness. That is it.

They also work in any recipe that calls for cooked spinach. Stirred through pasta with ricotta and lemon. Folded into an omelette. Blended into a soup with potato and onion. Mixed into a filling for ravioli.

But start with toast. Toast is honest. It does not hide anything. If the nettles are good, you will know immediately.

## The argument

Nettles are free. They are abundant. They grow within walking distance of almost every person in Britain. They require no cultivation, no packaging, no transport, no refrigeration. They taste better than the cultivated green they most closely resemble.

They are available for six weeks a year. You are in those six weeks right now.

Go and pick some. Put them on toast. Then decide whether spinach was ever the right answer.
