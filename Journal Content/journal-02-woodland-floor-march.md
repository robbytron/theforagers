# A Woodland Floor in March

*The Field / March 2026*

---

There is a beech wood I walk through most weeks between October and April. It sits on a chalk slope above a valley in the south of England, the kind of place that appears on no list and has no particular reputation. It is not ancient woodland, or if it is, nobody has made a case for it. The beeches are tall and well-spaced. The canopy in summer is dense enough to kill almost everything underneath. But in March, the canopy is still bare, and the floor belongs to whatever can use the light while it lasts.

I went on a Tuesday morning. It had rained overnight. The path was soft underfoot and the air had that specific March quality, cold in the shade, warm where the sun hit.

## The smell first

You smell the wild garlic before you see any of it. It arrives as a faint thread, metallic and green, carried from somewhere deeper in the wood. You follow the path and it gets stronger. Within fifty metres the air is thick with it.

The leaves are still young. Tight, rolled, pushing up through the leaf litter in loose groups. Some are fully open, maybe 15cm tall, a vivid green against the brown of last year's beech leaves. Others are barely through the soil surface. Give it two weeks and this will be a carpet. Right now it is a scatter. The colonies are marking out their territory but have not yet filled it.

I picked a small handful of the youngest leaves and put them in my jacket pocket. That was enough for what I needed.

## What is not there

No bluebells yet. Not even the leaf tips. They come later, mid-April usually, and by then this wood will look entirely different. The wild garlic and the bluebells share the same strategy, grow and flower before the canopy closes, but the bluebells run a few weeks behind.

No wood anemone either, though this is a site where it grows. The anemones will come in the next fortnight. Small, white, single flowers held on thin stems above divided leaves. They track the sun. On overcast days they stay half-closed.

Dog's mercury (*Mercurialis perennis*) is already up, thick stands of it along the path edge. It flowers early, small green spikes that nobody notices. Dog's mercury is poisonous. There is no reason to pick it, but it is worth knowing because it is one of the most common woodland floor plants in Britain and you will walk past it every time you enter a wood.

## Light

The light in a leafless deciduous wood in March is particular. It reaches the ground at a low angle. It moves through the trunks in clean lines. The shadows are long and precise because there are no leaves to diffuse anything. You can see the full shape of every tree.

This light lasts a few weeks at most. Once the buds break and the first leaves unfurl, the canopy starts to close. By late April the character of the wood changes completely. The floor goes dark. The ground flora that depends on spring light finishes its work and dies back or goes dormant.

The timing is the thing. Everything happening on this woodland floor right now is a response to that brief window of open canopy. Wild garlic, wood anemone, lesser celandine, bluebell. All of them racing to photosynthesise, flower, and set seed before the lights go off overhead.

## The small things

Moss on a fallen trunk, vivid in the damp. Bracket fungi on a standing dead beech, the fruiting bodies old and leathery from last autumn. A hazel on the wood edge with catkins still hanging, though they are past their best now and shedding pollen onto the path.

A wren somewhere in the understorey, fast and mechanical, absurdly loud for its size. A great tit repeating the same two-note call from a high branch. Nothing unusual. Nothing rare. Just a wood in early spring doing exactly what it does every year.

## What this is for

I am not writing a foraging guide here. The species pages do that. This is about the thing that happens before the picking, the noticing. The walk that teaches you where things grow, when they appear, and what the wood looks like at each stage of the year.

If you visit the same wood once a month, by the end of a year you understand it. You know which corners get the first garlic. You know where the ground stays wet. You know when the canopy closes and what disappears underneath it.

The wood does not change much from one year to the next. But your ability to read it does. March is a good month to start paying attention, because there is just enough happening to reward it, and not so much that you are overwhelmed.

Pick a wood. Go back to it. The rest follows.
