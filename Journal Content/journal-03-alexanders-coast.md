# The Alexanders Coast

*The Field / March 2026*

---

There is a plant that lines half the coastal footpaths in southern England, grows waist-high by March, smells strong enough to catch from a passing car, and most people have no idea it exists.

Alexanders (*Smyrnium olusatrum*) is not rare. It is not subtle. It is not hiding. It dominates road verges, car park edges, field boundaries, and waste ground within a few miles of the coast from Cornwall to Kent and up into East Anglia. In some stretches it is the single most visible plant on the roadside. And almost nobody picks it.

## A walk along the estuary

The stretch I walk runs along the south side of an estuary in Devon. The path is a mixture of tarmac lane and muddy track, hedgerow on one side, salt marsh on the other. Alexanders grows along the full length of it.

In January the rosettes of dark, glossy leaves sit low to the ground, easy to miss among the dead grass and dock stems. By February the flower stems start to push up. By mid-March the plants are 60–80cm tall, thick-stemmed and vigorous, with dense clusters of pale yellow-green flower buds just beginning to open.

The smell hits you before you are close enough to touch. It is hard to describe accurately. Celery is the nearest reference, but there is something else underneath, a warm, resinous note, almost like myrrh. Some people find it unpleasant. I think it smells exactly like what it is: a strong-flavoured edible plant that wants to be noticed.

## The history

Alexanders is not native to Britain. It arrived with the Romans, who grew it as a pot herb. For centuries it was a common kitchen plant, used the way we now use celery. It grew in monastery gardens and around settlements. When celery became widely available in the eighteenth century, Alexanders dropped out of the kitchen and into the hedgerow. It never left.

Its distribution tells this story. The densest populations are coastal and peri-urban, clustered around old settlements, ports, monasteries, and roadsides. You find it in exactly the places where people once lived and grew food. It spread from cultivation into the wild and stayed.

The Latin name is a giveaway. *Smyrnium* comes from the Greek for myrrh, a reference to the smell. *Olusatrum* is from the Latin for pot herb. A scented kitchen plant. That is what it was, and what it still could be, if anyone paid attention.

## How to see it

The leaves are the first clue. They are divided into groups of three, each leaflet broadly toothed with a glossy surface. They look nothing like most other hedgerow plants. In winter and early spring, when the surrounding vegetation is brown and dead, the dark green rosettes stand out clearly.

By March the stems are the dominant feature. They are thick, round, hollow, and grooved, often with a purple tint at the base. The flower heads are umbels, flat-topped clusters of tiny yellow-green flowers. The whole plant has a robust, structural quality. It does not look delicate.

The smell confirms it. Crush a leaf between your fingers. If it smells like celery with a background of something aromatic and slightly bitter, it is Alexanders. Nothing else in the hedgerow smells like this in March.

One caution: Alexanders is an umbellifer, and the umbellifer family contains both excellent edible species and lethally poisonous ones. Hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) grows in similar habitats. The differences are clear, hemlock has finely divided, feathery leaves, purple-blotched stems, and a foul, mousy smell, but you need to learn them. Do not pick any umbellifer until you can reliably tell the edible species from the dangerous ones. If in doubt, leave it out.

## The eating

The stems are the prize. Pick them while the flower buds are still tight, before the plant bolts into full flower. Peel away the outer layer to reveal a pale green, slightly translucent core. This can be eaten raw, though the flavour is strong. Braised in butter with salt, it becomes something very good, tender and aromatic, somewhere between celery and fennel.

The leaves are usable in small quantities. Raw, they are punchy. Wilted briefly in a pan they mellow, though they never become mild. The flower buds, picked in tight clusters, can be fried in batter or used as a garnish.

By May the plant flowers fully, sets seed, and dies back. The window for good eating runs from about February to mid-April. After that the stems turn woody and the flavour becomes unpleasantly strong. This is a plant with a season, and you are in it right now.

## What walking past looks like

I counted on a recent walk. In a two-mile stretch of lane, I passed Alexanders growing in at least thirty separate stands. Some were single plants on a verge. Others were dense colonies, ten or twenty plants thick, occupying a full metre of hedgerow base.

Two joggers passed me. A woman walking a dog. A cyclist. None of them glanced at the plants. This is not a criticism. Most people have no reason to look at roadside vegetation. But once you know what Alexanders is, you cannot unsee it. It is everywhere. It is good to eat. It has been here for two thousand years, waiting for someone to remember what it is for.
