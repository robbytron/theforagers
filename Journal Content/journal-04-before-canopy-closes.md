# Before the Canopy Closes

*The Land / March 2026*

---

A deciduous woodland runs on a clock. The canopy opens in autumn when the leaves fall. It closes again in late April or early May when the new leaves unfurl. Between those two points, light reaches the ground. After the canopy closes, it mostly does not.

The whole ecology of a British woodland floor depends on this cycle. And the most critical period, the weeks when the most happens in the least time, is right now. Late March into early April. The last stretch of open canopy before the lights go off.

## The logic

A beech leaf absorbs roughly 90% of the light that hits it. A full beech canopy in summer lets through about 2–5% of available sunlight. For any plant growing underneath, summer is effectively darkness.

The solution, if you are a woodland floor plant, is to do everything before the canopy closes. Push up. Photosynthesise. Flower. Set seed. Store energy. Die back or go dormant. All of this in a window that lasts, depending on the tree species overhead, somewhere between eight and twelve weeks.

Oak canopy closes later than beech. Ash later than oak. The timing varies from wood to wood and year to year, pushed around by temperature, rainfall, and geography. But the principle is always the same. The floor plants are racing the trees, and the trees always win.

## The cast

Walk into almost any lowland deciduous wood in the last two weeks of March and you will see this race in progress.

Wild garlic (*Allium ursinum*) is the most visible player. The leaves are up, covering patches of ground in bright green. In warm years the first flowers appear by the end of March. In cold years, not until mid-April. Either way, the bulk of the plant's annual energy capture happens in this period. By June the leaves yellow and collapse. The bulb sits underground until next spring.

Lesser celandine (*Ficaria verna*) flowers earlier than almost anything else on the woodland floor. The bright yellow, star-shaped flowers open in sunshine and close in cloud. They are already out in most of southern England by mid-March. The plant stores energy in small tubers and is gone by May.

Wood anemone (*Anemone nemorosa*) follows a week or two behind the celandine. Single white flowers on slender stems, the petals faintly pink on the underside. Wood anemone is a marker of old woodland. It spreads slowly, a metre or two per century, and its presence tells you the wood has been here a long time.

Wood sorrel (*Oxalis acetosella*) appears in the deeper shade, often at the base of trees or on mossy banks. The leaves are a pale, almost translucent green, each divided into three heart-shaped leaflets that fold down at night. The flavour is sharp and citric. A few leaves are a clean, bright addition to a salad. Do not eat large quantities. The oxalic acid that gives the sourness can cause problems in excess, though you would need to eat far more than any reasonable person would.

Bluebells (*Hyacinthoides non-scripta*) are the last of the spring floor plants to peak. The leaves are up by late March but the flowers will not open until mid to late April. When they do, the effect is well documented. Entire woods turn blue. It lasts about three weeks, and then the leaves die back and the bulbs rest until next year.

## The underground economy

What you see on the surface is only part of the story. Below ground, these plants are managing energy reserves with precision.

A wild garlic bulb in January contains enough stored energy to push a leaf shoot through several centimetres of soil and leaf litter. The first leaves are powered entirely by last year's reserves. Only once they reach the light and begin photosynthesising does the plant start running on current income.

Bluebell bulbs work the same way. The carbon stored last spring funds the next spring's growth. A bluebell that gets shaded out, by a building, a fence, or an evergreen shrub, cannot replenish its reserves and will weaken year on year. This is why picking bluebell leaves damages the plant more than picking flowers. The leaves are the solar panels.

## What the trees are doing

The trees are not idle during this period. The buds are swelling. Sap is rising. In a warm March, you can sometimes see the first green of an oak or hazel leaf breaking through the bud scales by the final week.

But the trees are slower. They invest in structure, wood, bark, root systems that take years to build. The floor plants invest in speed. A wild garlic leaf can go from soil surface to full photosynthetic capacity in less than two weeks. A beech tree takes four to six weeks from bud break to full canopy.

That gap is the whole story. Every spring flower on the woodland floor exists because of it.

## Closing time

By the end of April in most of southern England, the canopy is closing. The quality of light on the ground changes first. It goes from direct to dappled. Then the dappled patches shrink. By mid-May, under a beech wood in full leaf, the floor is dim and green.

The wild garlic flowers. The bluebells peak and fade. The wood anemones close and disappear. The celandine is already gone. Within a few weeks the floor is quiet, a carpet of dead leaves with shade-tolerant species, ferns, mosses, dog's mercury, holding on through the summer months.

The wood will not look like this again for six months. The next time the floor gets light, the leaves will be falling, and the cycle will reset.

This is the week to see it. The race is on.
